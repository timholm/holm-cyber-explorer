D16-013 — User Onboarding & Training Pathways

Document ID
D16-013
Domain
16 -- Interface & Navigation
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D16-003 (Navigation Structures), D16-004 (Search System Design), D16-014 (Component Catalog)
Depended Upon By
D9 (Education & Training Domain), D20-003 (Oral History Capture -- onboarding as knowledge transfer)

Purpose

This article defines how new users learn to use the institution. In a fifty-year institution, the definition of "new user" encompasses at least three distinct scenarios: a person encountering the institution for the first time during its founding era, a person inheriting the institution from a previous generation of operators, and a person discovering the institution decades after its founding with no prior knowledge of its existence or purpose. Each of these users needs a pathway from ignorance to competence, and the institution must provide that pathway through the interface itself -- not through external training, not through word-of-mouth, and not through the assumption that someone will be available to explain things in person.

The air-gapped, off-grid nature of the institution makes onboarding particularly critical. There is no online help community. There are no tutorial videos hosted externally. There is no customer support service. The institution must teach itself to its users, using only the resources it contains. If the onboarding system fails, the user is alone with a system they cannot understand.

This article is addressed to every person who designs the user's first experience with the institution, creates training materials, writes help text, or decides how much the interface should explain itself. It is equally addressed to the person who, thirty years from now, must redesign the onboarding pathway for a user population that has different technological literacy, different expectations, and different starting knowledge than the founding generation.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The classification of user types and their distinct onboarding needs.
  • The structure of the onboarding pathway: what users learn, in what order, and through what mechanisms.
  • First-run experience design: what happens the first time a user encounters the institution.
  • Progressive disclosure: how the interface reveals complexity gradually.
  • Contextual help and in-context documentation.
  • Self-assessment mechanisms: how users can gauge their own competence.
  • Retraining pathways for users after major interface changes (per D16-010).
  • The relationship between onboarding and the institution's broader educational mission.

This article does not cover:

  • Formal educational curricula delivered through the institution (covered by Domain 9).
  • Operator training for system administration and maintenance (covered by OPS-001 and Infrastructure domains).
  • Import operator certification (covered by D18-014).
  • The visual design of onboarding components (covered by D16-007 and D16-014).

Background

The Three Generations of New Users

The institution must plan for three fundamentally different onboarding scenarios:

Generation 1: Founding-Era Users. These users are present during the institution's creation. They may have participated in its design. They understand the institutional context, the technology choices, and the philosophical foundations. Their onboarding needs are minimal for navigation and operation but may be significant for specific features they did not help design.

Generation 2: Successor Users. These users inherit the institution from Generation 1. They did not build the system but may have received verbal or written guidance from its builders. They understand the general concept of a digital institution but may not understand the specific design rationale behind this one. Their onboarding needs centre on understanding "why things are the way they are," not just "how to use them."

Generation 3: Discovery Users. These users encounter the institution with no prior context. They may not know what the institution is for. They may not know who built it. They may be using hardware and software paradigms that differ significantly from the founding era. Their onboarding needs are the most comprehensive: they need to understand the institution's purpose, its structure, its interface conventions, and its operational requirements, all from scratch.

The onboarding system must serve all three generations. It cannot assume any prior knowledge, but it must not bore users who already have knowledge. This requires a progressive disclosure model that starts with the essentials and reveals depth on demand.

The Self-Teaching Institution

A conventional software product can rely on external onboarding resources: documentation websites, video tutorials, community forums, paid support. The institution has none of these. Every onboarding resource must be contained within the institution itself, stored locally, and accessible through the institution's own interface. This is both a constraint and a strength: the onboarding materials cannot become separated from the system they describe. They are always available, always local, and always in the same context as the system itself.

The Danger of Onboarding Neglect

In most software projects, onboarding is an afterthought. The system is built for expert users, and a thin layer of tutorials is applied afterward. This approach fails for a fifty-year institution because the ratio of new users to experienced users will shift continuously. There will be periods when all users are new (after a generational transition). There will be periods when the last person who understood the system has departed and the new operators must learn entirely from what the system tells them. The onboarding system is not a convenience feature. It is a survival mechanism.

System Model

ONBOARDING PATHWAY ARCHITECTURE
==================================

+-------------------+     +-------------------+     +-------------------+
|  FIRST ENCOUNTER  | --> |  GUIDED PATHWAY   | --> |  SELF-DIRECTED    |
|  (Who are you?    |     |  (Structured       |     |  EXPLORATION      |
|   What do you     |     |   learning path    |     |  (Contextual help |
|   need?)          |     |   with milestones) |     |   always available)|
+-------------------+     +-------------------+     +-------------------+


USER TYPE CLASSIFICATION
==========================
Type        Starting Knowledge              Priority Learning
----        ------------------              -----------------
Explorer    Knows nothing; needs purpose    What is this? Why does it exist?
                                            How is it organized?
Operator    Knows purpose; needs mechanics  How do I navigate? Search? Find
                                            specific content?
Maintainer  Knows mechanics; needs depth    How do I maintain? Configure?
                                            Troubleshoot? Modify?
Inheritor   Knows nothing; needs everything All of the above, plus: What
                                            decisions were made and why?


PROGRESSIVE DISCLOSURE MODEL
===============================
Level 0: ORIENTATION
  - What is this institution?
  - What does it contain?
  - How is it organized?
  - "You are here" indicator

Level 1: BASIC NAVIGATION
  - How to move between sections
  - How to use the table of contents
  - How to use breadcrumbs
  - How to return to the starting point

Level 2: SEARCH AND DISCOVERY
  - How to search for specific content
  - How to browse by topic
  - How to use faceted navigation
  - How to follow cross-references

Level 3: ADVANCED FEATURES
  - How to use information density controls
  - How to configure locale preferences
  - How to access the decision log and institutional memory
  - How to interpret system status indicators

Level 4: MAINTENANCE OPERATIONS
  - How to perform system maintenance tasks
  - How to interpret degraded-mode indicators
  - How to execute recovery procedures
  - How to update documentation


CONTEXTUAL HELP SYSTEM
========================
Every interface component provides:
  1. A tooltip (brief, 5-10 words)
  2. An inline help expansion (paragraph-length, accessible via "?" icon)
  3. A link to the relevant article (full documentation context)

Help is never modal. It appears adjacent to the element being explained.
Help is never forced. It appears only when requested.
Help is always current. It is generated from the same source as the
  interface itself and cannot fall out of sync.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D16-13-01: Zero-Knowledge Start. The onboarding pathway MUST be usable by a person who has never seen the institution, does not know its purpose, and has no one to ask. This is the Generation 3 "Discovery User" scenario. If the onboarding system requires any knowledge that is not provided within the institution itself, it has failed.

  • R-D16-13-02: No Mandatory Tutorials. Onboarding MUST be available but MUST NOT be forced. A user who already knows the system MUST be able to skip or dismiss onboarding materials without being impeded. A first-run experience may be offered, but it MUST be dismissible within one action (a single keypress or click). Users who skip onboarding MUST be able to access the same materials later through a clearly discoverable help section.

  • R-D16-13-03: Self-Contained Onboarding. All onboarding materials MUST be stored within the institution's local file system. No onboarding resource may depend on external networks, services, or content. This follows directly from the air-gap mandate (CON-001) and from the principle that the institution must be self-sufficient.

  • R-D16-13-04: Contextual Over Global. Contextual help (explanations that appear near the element they describe) is preferred over global help (a separate help section that the user must navigate to). When a user needs help with a specific element, the help should be available at the point of need, not in a distant location that requires the user to leave their current context.

  • R-D16-13-05: Onboarding Must Track Interface Changes. When the interface changes (per D16-010), the onboarding materials MUST be updated in the same release cycle. If the interface says "click the blue button" and the button is now green, the onboarding has failed. Onboarding accuracy MUST be verified as part of every interface change review.

  • R-D16-13-06: Multiple Modalities. Onboarding materials MUST be available in at least two modalities: text (for reading) and structured walkthroughs (step-by-step guided paths through the actual interface). Additional modalities (audio, video if storage permits, printed quick-reference cards) SHOULD be provided where practical. No onboarding information may be available in only one modality.

  • R-D16-13-07: Competence Self-Assessment. The onboarding system MUST provide mechanisms for users to assess their own competence. This may take the form of checklists ("Can you do these five things?"), practice exercises, or guided tasks with verification. The assessment MUST NOT be gated or scored in a way that prevents access to the institution. It is a tool for the user, not a barrier.

  • R-D16-13-08: Retraining After Changes. After any moderate or major interface change (per D16-010), a retraining pathway MUST be available that highlights what changed and how to accomplish previously familiar tasks in the new interface. This pathway MUST be accessible from the interface change notification and from the general help section.

Failure Modes

  • Onboarding-interface desynchronization. The interface evolves but the onboarding materials do not. New users follow instructions that do not match what they see. Likelihood: very high without strict enforcement of R-D16-13-05. Severity: critical (undermines trust in the entire onboarding system). Detection: automated testing that verifies onboarding walkthroughs against the actual interface; any walkthrough step that cannot be completed indicates desynchronization.

  • Expert blindness. Onboarding materials are written by experienced users who cannot accurately gauge what a novice does not know. Instructions skip "obvious" steps that are not obvious to newcomers. Terminology is used without definition. Likelihood: very high (expert blindness is universal). Severity: high (new users are lost at the first gap). Detection: usability testing with genuine novices; every onboarding pathway must be tested by someone who has not previously used the system.

  • Onboarding avoidance. The onboarding experience is so lengthy, condescending, or intrusive that users dismiss it immediately and never return to it. They then struggle with the interface indefinitely, having formed a negative association with the help system. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: medium (users eventually learn through trial and error, but at much higher cost). Detection: tracking of onboarding dismissal rates; if more than 50% of new users dismiss onboarding within 10 seconds, the onboarding is failing to engage.

  • Generational gap. The onboarding materials assume knowledge that was common in the founding era but is not common in a later era. References to "scrolling," "clicking," or "typing" assume interaction paradigms that may not be the primary paradigms of future users. Likelihood: high over 50 years. Severity: medium (concepts may be understood even if terminology is dated). Detection: periodic review of onboarding language and interaction assumptions by members of the current user generation.

  • Single-path rigidity. The onboarding system provides one fixed pathway that does not accommodate different user types, learning speeds, or prior knowledge. Expert users are forced through basic tutorials. Novice users are overwhelmed by options. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: medium. Detection: user feedback and completion-rate analysis by user type.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If onboarding materials are out of date: Immediately audit all onboarding walkthroughs against the current interface. Flag every discrepancy. Prioritise fixing walkthroughs for Level 0 (Orientation) and Level 1 (Basic Navigation), as these are used by every new user. Update remaining levels within the current release cycle. Establish automated walkthrough verification to prevent future desynchronisation.

  2. If new users are failing to complete onboarding: Observe (or simulate observing) new users attempting the onboarding pathway. Identify the specific steps where they abandon the process. Determine whether the cause is length (too long), tone (too condescending or too technical), pacing (too fast or too slow), or relevance (not teaching what the user needs). Redesign the failing sections. Test again. Iterate.

  3. If a generational transition has made onboarding ineffective: Recruit users from the current generation to review and revise all onboarding materials. Do not assume that the problem is merely linguistic; the entire pedagogical approach may need to change. Users who learned technology through touchscreens and voice commands may not respond to the same instructional patterns as users who learned through keyboards and command lines. Record the revision and its rationale in the D20 decision log.

  4. If the Generation 3 "Discovery User" scenario actually occurs: This is the ultimate test of the onboarding system. If a person encounters the institution with no prior context and can successfully understand what it is, navigate its contents, and begin using it -- the onboarding system has succeeded. If they cannot, conduct an emergency review. The Discovery User scenario is not a thought experiment; it is the institution's succession plan. Treat any failure in this scenario as a critical institutional defect.

  5. If expert blindness has compromised the materials: Institute a mandatory novice review for all onboarding content. No onboarding material may be published without being tested by someone who meets the target user profile for that material. If no genuine novice is available, the author must wait a minimum of 72 hours after writing the material, then attempt to follow it as literally as possible, questioning every assumption and every skipped step.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Build the onboarding framework with all four levels of progressive disclosure. Write the initial onboarding content for the founding-era interface. Test with available novice users. Establish the contextual help system. Generate the first printed quick-reference materials. This is the easiest period to write onboarding materials because the system is small and the author understands it fully.

  • Years 5-15: The first Generation 2 users may arrive. Observe their onboarding experience carefully. Where they struggle, the onboarding materials have gaps. Update accordingly. Begin tracking which onboarding pathways are most and least used. Consider whether the Level 4 (Maintenance Operations) pathway needs expansion as the system accumulates more operational complexity.

  • Years 15-30: A full generational review of onboarding materials is needed. The technology assumptions, linguistic conventions, and cultural references of the founding era may be losing their effectiveness. This is not a failure of the original materials; it is the natural consequence of time. Revise with sensitivity to the current user population's starting assumptions.

  • Years 30-50: The Generation 3 scenario becomes increasingly likely. Ensure that the Level 0 (Orientation) onboarding pathway can serve a person who has no context whatsoever. This pathway should explain not just how to use the institution, but what it is, why it exists, and what principles govern it. In the most extreme scenario, the onboarding system is the institution's first and only representative to a completely new community. It must be worthy of that role.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The Generation 3 "Discovery User" is the person I think about most when writing this article. This is a person who finds the institution in a closet, on a hard drive, in a building they have inherited. They have no one to call. They have no manual other than what the institution itself provides. If the onboarding system works, this person becomes the institution's next generation of stewards. If it fails, the institution dies in that closet.

I am deliberately keeping the onboarding system lightweight. Heavy onboarding systems -- long tutorial sequences, mandatory courses, certification gates -- solve the problem of user incompetence by adding institutional overhead. They work for large organisations with training departments. They do not work for a small, self-sovereign institution that may have only one or two operators. The onboarding must be woven into the interface itself, not layered on top of it. The best onboarding is an interface that explains itself.

The "no mandatory tutorials" rule (R-D16-13-02) will seem counterintuitive to some. Would it not be better to require new users to complete a basic orientation? No. It would not. A mandatory tutorial trains the user to dismiss the tutorial. An optional tutorial that appears useful trains the user to seek it out. The difference is between compliance and curiosity. The institution needs curiosity.
