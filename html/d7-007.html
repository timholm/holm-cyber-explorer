D7-007 — Opportunity Recognition Framework

Document ID
D7-007
Domain
7 -- Intelligence & Analysis
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D7-001, D7-002, D7-003, D7-004, D7-005, D7-006, D7-009, ETH-001, CON-001, GOV-001, OPS-001
Depended Upon By
D7-008, D7-010, D7-012, D10-002. All articles that depend on proactive institutional positioning.

Purpose

This article establishes the institution's formal framework for identifying, evaluating, and acting upon favorable conditions, emerging possibilities, and windows for action. It is the positive mirror of D7-006 (Threat Assessment Procedures). Where threat assessment asks "what could harm us?", opportunity recognition asks "what could benefit us, and are we positioned to capture that benefit?"

Institutions that survive over decades do not merely defend against threats. They also recognize moments when conditions align to permit advances that were previously impossible or impractical -- a new technology becomes available for import, a resource becomes accessible at favorable terms, a shift in the external environment opens a window for infrastructure improvement, or accumulated internal capability enables a project that was once beyond reach. An institution that only thinks defensively will survive but will not thrive. It will gradually fall behind as the world evolves and its own systems age.

The intelligence philosophy in D7-001 emphasizes disciplined curiosity. This article operationalizes the positive dimension of that curiosity. It provides structured methods for recognizing opportunities, evaluating their genuine merit versus wishful thinking, assessing the institution's readiness to exploit them, and deciding whether and when to act. The framework is deliberately conservative -- the institution's primary obligation is survival, and opportunities that jeopardize survival are not opportunities at all. But within that constraint, the institution must remain capable of recognizing and seizing favorable conditions when they arise.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The opportunity recognition cycle: how favorable conditions are identified, evaluated, and acted upon.
  • Categories of opportunity relevant to the institution: technological, resource, knowledge, capability, environmental, and relational.
  • The opportunity evaluation framework: a structured method for assessing benefit, feasibility, risk, alignment, and time-sensitivity.
  • The opportunity register: a living inventory of identified opportunities with current assessments.
  • Decision criteria for when to act on an opportunity and when to defer or decline.
  • Integration with threat assessment to ensure opportunities are not pursued at the expense of security.
  • The annual opportunity review: a companion to the annual threat review.

This article does not cover:

  • The intelligence philosophy underlying opportunity recognition (see D7-001).
  • The intelligence cycle that generates opportunity-related information (see D7-002).
  • The collection methods used to discover opportunities (see D7-003).
  • The analytical techniques applied during evaluation (see D7-004).
  • Environmental monitoring that may surface opportunity indicators (see D7-009).
  • Threat assessment procedures, though the relationship between threats and opportunities is addressed (see D7-006).
  • The decision-making process for acting on opportunities (see D7-010, GOV-001).

Background

3.1 The Asymmetry of Threat and Opportunity

Human cognition is biased toward threat detection. Evolutionary pressures favored organisms that noticed predators over organisms that noticed ripe fruit in a distant tree. This bias persists in institutional thinking. Threat assessment is urgent, visceral, and naturally motivating. Opportunity recognition requires deliberate effort -- the analyst must actively look for favorable conditions rather than waiting for them to demand attention.

In an air-gapped, security-conscious institution, this asymmetry is amplified. The institution's entire posture is defensive. Its founding documents emphasize sovereignty, integrity, and resilience. The threat assessment machinery in D7-006 is detailed and mandatory. Without an equally formal opportunity recognition framework, the institution will devote all its analytical capacity to threats and none to possibilities. Over a 50-year horizon, this produces an institution that is well-defended but stagnant -- safe, but increasingly irrelevant as the world changes around it.

3.2 What Constitutes an Opportunity?

An opportunity, in this institution's usage, is a condition, event, or development that creates the possibility of advancing the institution's mission, strengthening its capabilities, or improving its resilience in ways that were not previously possible or practical. Opportunities are not aspirations or wish lists. They are specific, identifiable favorable conditions that can be acted upon within a defined window.

Opportunities differ from routine improvements. Routine improvements are planned, budgeted, and scheduled -- they are part of normal operations. Opportunities arise from changed circumstances: something has become possible that was not possible before, or something has become cheaper, easier, or more urgent than it was before. The opportunity framework exists to catch these changes and evaluate whether they warrant deviation from the standard plan.

3.3 The Danger of Opportunity Bias

Just as threat assessment can suffer from threat inflation, opportunity recognition can suffer from opportunity enthusiasm. The analyst may become excited about a new technology, a novel approach, or an apparently favorable development, and overestimate its benefit while underestimating its cost and risk. This is especially dangerous for a single-analyst institution where there is no skeptical colleague to challenge enthusiasm.

The evaluation framework in this article is designed to counteract opportunity bias. Every opportunity must be evaluated against the same structured criteria, including an explicit assessment of what could go wrong. The devil's advocate requirement from D7-001 applies: for any opportunity the analyst recommends pursuing, they must formally argue the case against pursuit before the recommendation is final.

System Model

4.1 Opportunity Categories

The institution recognizes six categories of opportunity:

Technological Opportunities. A new technology becomes available that could improve the institution's infrastructure, tools, or capabilities. Examples: a new storage medium with superior longevity, a more efficient backup methodology, a better encryption algorithm, improved hardware at favorable cost. These opportunities are typically identified during import cycles or through environmental monitoring per D7-009.

Resource Opportunities. A resource becomes available at favorable terms -- whether through price reduction, increased availability, a gift, or a discovered surplus. Examples: discounted hardware, free access to a knowledge resource, unexpected budget surplus, donated equipment. These opportunities often have narrow windows and require rapid evaluation.

Knowledge Opportunities. New information becomes available that significantly improves the institution's understanding or capability. Examples: a newly published technical reference relevant to the institution's systems, a discovered archive of historical data relevant to the institution's mission, access to expertise that was previously unavailable. Knowledge opportunities are identified through the intelligence cycle and import processes.

Capability Opportunities. The institution's own internal development has reached a point where a previously impossible project becomes feasible. Examples: accumulated storage allows a new archival initiative, improved documentation coverage enables a new analytical capability, a tested backup procedure enables a more ambitious disaster recovery posture. These opportunities emerge from internal monitoring and the institution's own progress tracking.

Environmental Opportunities. Changes in the external environment create favorable conditions for institutional action. Examples: a favorable regulatory change, improved local infrastructure (better power grid, improved internet access for import), reduced external threat levels freeing resources for development, a climate shift that extends equipment lifespan or reduces cooling costs.

Relational Opportunities. Changes in the institution's relationships -- with suppliers, with communities of practice, with potential allies or partners -- create possibilities for collaboration, exchange, or mutual support. These opportunities are relevant even for an air-gapped institution, which must interact with the external world for procurement, knowledge import, and succession planning.

4.2 The Five-Component Evaluation Framework

Every opportunity is evaluated against five dimensions. This mirrors the five-component threat assessment in D7-006, providing a parallel and complementary structure.

Component 1: Benefit Assessment. What does the institution gain if this opportunity is successfully exploited? Benefit is evaluated on two axes: magnitude (how significant is the improvement?) and durability (how long will the benefit last?).

ScoreLevelDescription
1MarginalMinor improvement. Convenience rather than capability. Benefit unlikely to be noticed after initial period.
2ModestMeaningful improvement to a specific function. Noticeable benefit for months to a year.
3SignificantSubstantial improvement to institutional capability. Benefit persists for years.
4MajorTransforms a major institutional function. Benefit persists for a decade or more.
5StrategicFundamentally advances the institution's mission or survival posture. Generational benefit.

Component 2: Feasibility Assessment. Can the institution actually exploit this opportunity given current resources, skills, and constraints?

ScoreLevelDescription
1InfeasibleFar beyond current capability. Would require resources or skills the institution cannot obtain.
2DifficultPossible but would strain the institution significantly. Major trade-offs required.
3AchievableWithin capability but requires dedicated effort. Some trade-offs required.
4StraightforwardWell within capability. Modest effort required. Minimal trade-offs.
5TrivialCan be done with negligible effort and no meaningful trade-offs.

Component 3: Risk Assessment. What could go wrong if the institution pursues this opportunity? Risk includes resource expenditure that yields no benefit, disruption to existing systems, security exposure during implementation, and opportunity cost of resources devoted to pursuit instead of maintenance or defense.

ScoreLevelDescription
1High RiskSignificant probability of harmful outcomes. Failure could damage critical systems or deplete essential resources.
2Elevated RiskMeaningful probability of setback. Failure would waste significant resources or create operational disruption.
3Moderate RiskSome chance of setback. Failure would waste modest resources. Reversible with effort.
4Low RiskLow probability of negative outcome. Failure is easily absorbed.
5Minimal RiskNegligible downside. Even failure costs very little.

Component 4: Alignment Assessment. How well does this opportunity align with the institution's mission, values, and current priorities? An opportunity that is beneficial but misaligned is a distraction.

ScoreLevelDescription
1MisalignedContradicts or distracts from institutional mission and current priorities.
2TangentialLoosely related to mission but not clearly advancing it.
3CompatibleConsistent with mission and current priorities but not directly advancing a stated goal.
4AlignedDirectly advances a stated institutional goal or priority.
5CoreDirectly advances the institution's primary mission or addresses a critical capability gap.

Component 5: Time-Sensitivity Assessment. How long is the window for action? Some opportunities persist indefinitely; others vanish quickly.

ScoreLevelDescription
1Expired or ClosingWindow is nearly closed or closing within days. Immediate action or decline required.
2UrgentWindow closes within weeks. Rapid evaluation needed.
3Time-LimitedWindow closes within months. Standard evaluation is possible.
4ExtendedWindow persists for a year or more. No urgency, but conditions may change.
5PersistentThe opportunity is structural rather than transient. Available indefinitely.

4.3 Opportunity Score and Decision Criteria

Opportunity Score = (Benefit + Feasibility + Risk + Alignment) / 4

This produces a score between 1.0 and 5.0. Time-sensitivity does not factor into the score but determines the urgency of the decision.

Score RangeRecommendationAction
1.0 - 2.0DeclineLog in the opportunity register. No further action unless circumstances change materially.
2.1 - 3.0MonitorLog in the opportunity register. Revisit during next review cycle. Pursue only if conditions improve.
3.1 - 4.0Pursue (conditional)Develop a specific action plan. Verify that pursuit does not compromise current threat mitigations. Present to decision function per GOV-001.
4.1 - 5.0Pursue (priority)Act promptly. This is a high-value, well-aligned, feasible opportunity with low risk. Delay reduces the expected benefit.

4.4 The Opportunity Register

The opportunity register is the companion document to the threat register. It maintains a living inventory of identified opportunities with their current evaluations.

Register format:

Opp IDDescriptionCategoryBenefitFeasibilityRiskAlignmentScoreTime-SensitivityStatusLast Assessed
OP-[YYYY]-[NNN][Description][Category][1-5][1-5][1-5][1-5][Calc][1-5][Active/Pursued/Declined/Expired][Date]

Each register entry also has an associated detail page containing the full five-component evaluation, evidence and reasoning for each score, any devil's advocate analysis performed, the action plan if pursued, the outcome if completed, and the review history.

4.5 The Annual Opportunity Review

The annual opportunity review is conducted as a companion to the annual threat review per D7-006. It follows a parallel four-phase structure:

Phase 1: Preparation (Week 1, concurrent with threat review). Gather all inputs: current opportunity register, past year's monitoring data, intelligence products, import logs, internal progress reports. Review the previous annual opportunity review. Note which opportunities were pursued, which succeeded, and which failed or were abandoned.

Phase 2: Opportunity Identification (Week 2). For each opportunity category, brainstorm current and foreseeable opportunities. Review imported information for external developments that create favorable conditions. Review internal progress for capability thresholds that have been crossed. Update the register with new opportunities and retire expired ones.

Phase 3: Scoring (Week 3). Evaluate each active opportunity using the five-component framework. Apply the bias checklist per D7-005 -- specifically checking for optimism bias, sunk cost bias (continuing to pursue an opportunity because effort has already been invested), and novelty bias (overvaluing new opportunities relative to existing ones). For any opportunity recommended for priority pursuit, apply devil's advocate analysis.

Phase 4: Production (Week 4). Produce the Annual Opportunity Assessment. Structure: executive summary of top opportunities and recommendations; opportunity-by-opportunity evaluation; trend analysis showing how the opportunity landscape has shifted; alignment check against the institution's current strategic priorities; specific action recommendations with resource requirements and timelines.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D7-07-01: The Annual Opportunity Review must be conducted concurrently with the Annual Threat Review per D7-006. Threat and opportunity must be assessed together so that the institution can balance defensive and offensive resource allocation.
  • R-D7-07-02: Every opportunity evaluation must use the five-component framework (benefit, feasibility, risk, alignment, time-sensitivity). Evaluations that skip components are incomplete and must not inform resource allocation decisions.
  • R-D7-07-03: No opportunity may be pursued if doing so would reduce the institution's threat mitigations below the acceptable threshold defined in the current Annual Threat Assessment. Security comes before advancement. If the analyst believes the opportunity justifies accepting increased risk, this must be escalated as a Tier 2 decision per GOV-001.
  • R-D7-07-04: Devil's advocate analysis per D7-001 R-D7-02 is mandatory for any opportunity recommended for priority pursuit (score 4.1 or higher). The analyst must formally argue against pursuit before the recommendation is final.
  • R-D7-07-05: Opportunity evaluations must state their assumptions explicitly per D7-001 R-D7-03. The benefit assessment in particular depends on assumptions about future conditions that may prove false.
  • R-D7-07-06: The opportunity register must be maintained alongside the threat register. New opportunities must be logged within 14 days of identification. The register must be backed up per D6-002.
  • R-D7-07-07: Pursued opportunities must be tracked to outcome. When an opportunity is exploited, the actual result must be documented and compared to the expected benefit. This data feeds calibration tracking per D7-011 and D7-005.
  • R-D7-07-08: Resource opportunities with time-sensitivity scores of 1 or 2 may receive an abbreviated evaluation if a full evaluation cannot be completed within the window. The abbreviated evaluation must still address all five components, but with shorter rationale. The abbreviated nature must be documented.

Failure Modes

  • Opportunity blindness. The institution fails to recognize favorable conditions because its entire analytical capacity is devoted to threat monitoring. Opportunities pass unnoticed or are recognized only after the window has closed. Mitigation: the annual opportunity review is mandatory and concurrent with the threat review, ensuring that at least once per year the analyst is formally required to look for opportunities. Environmental monitoring per D7-009 includes opportunity indicators alongside threat indicators.

  • Opportunity enthusiasm. The analyst becomes excited about a possibility and overestimates its benefit while underestimating its cost and risk. Resources are diverted from defense to pursuit, and the pursuit fails or yields less benefit than expected. Mitigation: the structured evaluation framework forces explicit scoring of risk and feasibility alongside benefit. The devil's advocate requirement for priority recommendations provides a formal check on enthusiasm. R-D7-07-03 prevents pursuit from compromising security.

  • Analysis paralysis on opportunities. The evaluation framework is so thorough that by the time the institution decides to act, the window has closed. Mitigation: the time-sensitivity assessment triggers accelerated evaluation for time-critical opportunities per R-D7-07-08. The scoring system produces a clear recommendation that reduces deliberation time.

  • Sunk cost pursuit. The institution continues to invest in a pursued opportunity because it has already committed resources, even when new information indicates the opportunity is less valuable than originally assessed. Mitigation: R-D7-07-07 requires tracking to outcome. If interim indicators show the actual benefit is significantly below the projected benefit, the analyst must re-evaluate and potentially recommend abandonment. Abandoning a failing pursuit is not a failure of the framework; it is the framework working correctly.

  • Register neglect. The opportunity register is created during the annual review but not consulted between reviews. Conditions change, new opportunities emerge, and the register does not reflect reality. Mitigation: the register is reviewed quarterly as part of the intelligence requirements review per D7-001 R-D7-05. Monitoring per D7-009 may surface new opportunities that trigger ad hoc evaluation.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If opportunity blindness is detected (the institution realizes it has missed significant favorable conditions): Conduct an immediate opportunity scan across all six categories. Review imported information from the past 12 months for favorable developments that were noticed but not evaluated. Determine whether the oversight was due to analytical overload, threat fixation, or a gap in monitoring. Address the root cause: if threat fixation, review the balance of analytical effort; if monitoring gap, update D7-009 indicators; if overload, consider whether automation per D8 can assist with routine opportunity scanning.

  2. If an opportunity was pursued and failed: Conduct a post-action review. Determine whether the failure was due to inaccurate evaluation (the scoring was wrong), changed circumstances (the evaluation was correct but conditions shifted), or execution failure (the evaluation was correct but implementation was flawed). Feed findings into the calibration tracking. Update the evaluation framework if systematic scoring errors are identified.

  3. If sunk cost pursuit is suspected: Immediately re-evaluate the opportunity using current information, not the original evaluation. If the re-evaluation score falls below 3.0, recommend discontinuation. Document the loss and the lesson. Sunk costs are sunk -- they must not influence the forward-looking decision.

  4. If the Annual Opportunity Review has been missed: Conduct a compressed review within 30 days. Flag all current opportunity register entries as "UNREVIEWED -- POTENTIALLY STALE." Do not commit new resources to opportunities based on stale evaluations.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The opportunity register will be sparse initially as the institution focuses on establishment and survival. This is appropriate -- foundational security and operational stability are prerequisites for opportunity pursuit. However, the framework must be exercised even during this period to build the analytical habit. The first annual reviews will identify more opportunities than the institution can pursue; the scoring framework helps prioritize ruthlessly.

  • Years 5-15: As the institution's defensive posture matures and operational stability is achieved, more analytical capacity becomes available for opportunity recognition. The register should grow. Calibration data from pursued opportunities should reveal whether the scoring framework accurately predicts value. Adjust the rubrics if systematic biases are detected -- most likely optimism bias on benefit scores and underestimation of implementation effort on feasibility scores.

  • Years 15-30: The institution should have a substantial track record of pursued and declined opportunities. This history itself becomes an intelligence resource -- revealing which categories of opportunity have historically yielded the most benefit, which have the most unpredictable outcomes, and which the institution consistently misjudges. Use this meta-analysis to refine the framework. If succession is approaching, the successor must understand not just the evaluation procedure but the institutional history that informs scoring calibration.

  • Years 30-50+: The categories of opportunity will have shifted as technology and the external environment evolve. New categories may emerge that do not fit the current framework. The evaluation methodology -- structured, multi-dimensional, bias-checked scoring -- should remain stable even as the specific categories and examples change completely. The opportunity archive spanning decades provides unique strategic perspective on how favorable conditions recur, evolve, and can be anticipated.

  • Signpost for revision: If the institution consistently declines opportunities that, in retrospect, would have been highly beneficial, or consistently pursues opportunities that yield little value, the evaluation framework and its scoring rubrics need recalibration. The annual opportunity review must include a retrospective assessment of past decisions to detect this pattern.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: This is the article I almost did not write. The institution's founding posture is defensive. Its reason for existing is to protect something -- data, knowledge, sovereignty. When you are in a defensive posture, thinking about opportunities feels like a luxury, or worse, a distraction. The threat register feels urgent. The opportunity register feels optional.

But I have studied institutions that failed over decades, and many of them did not fail because a threat overwhelmed their defenses. They failed because the world moved on and they did not move with it. They were so focused on protecting what they had that they never improved, adapted, or grew. Their defenses held, but what they were defending became irrelevant.

The scoring framework in this article is deliberately parallel to D7-006's threat framework. This is intentional. I want the opportunity evaluation to feel as serious, as structured, and as mandatory as threat assessment. If opportunity recognition is treated as a casual, informal activity -- "wouldn't it be nice if" thinking -- it will never compete with the urgency of threat response for the analyst's attention. By giving it the same formal structure, the same annual review requirement, and the same scoring rigor, I am declaring that the institution's positive future matters as much as its defensive present.

The hardest part of this framework will be the risk assessment for opportunities. When you are excited about a possibility, risk feels abstract. It is not. Every opportunity pursued is resources not spent on defense, time not spent on maintenance, attention not given to monitoring. The risk score must be honest. R-D7-07-03 exists because I know the temptation: "this opportunity is so good that it is worth accepting more threat exposure." Maybe. But that is a governance decision, not an intelligence decision, and it must be made explicitly with full awareness of the trade-off.
