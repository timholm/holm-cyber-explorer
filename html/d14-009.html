D14-009 — Peer Review and Validation Process for Research Outputs

Document ID
D14-009
Domain
14 -- Research & Theory
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
ETH-001, CON-001, GOV-001, D14-001, D14-002, D14-004, D14-008
Depended Upon By
D14-010. All Domain 14 articles that produce findings requiring validation before integration into operational practice. Referenced by all domains that consume validated research findings.

Purpose

This article defines how research findings are reviewed, challenged, and validated before being accepted into the institutional knowledge base. In the broader scientific community, peer review is performed by independent experts who were not involved in the research. They bring different perspectives, different expertise, and different biases. The confrontation between the researcher's claims and the reviewer's skepticism is what gives peer-reviewed findings their authority.

This institution cannot perform peer review in the traditional sense. There are no independent experts available. The "reviewer" is the same person who conducted the research. This limitation is acknowledged honestly per ETH-001, Principle 6. But the function that peer review serves -- the systematic challenge of research conclusions before they are accepted as institutional knowledge -- is too important to abandon simply because the ideal mechanism is unavailable.

This article defines an adapted validation process that extracts as much value as possible from solo review while being honest about the limitations of self-review. The process is structured to force the operator into adversarial engagement with their own conclusions, to create temporal separation between the roles of researcher and reviewer, and to leave a documented record that allows future operators to assess whether the validation was genuine.

Scope

In scope: - The adapted peer review process for a single-operator institution. - The validation checklist: mandatory checks for all research findings before acceptance. - The adversarial review protocol: structured self-challenge for high-confidence findings. - The temporal review mechanism: using time separation to approximate reviewer independence. - The Validation Record: documentation format for the review process and its findings. - Review requirements for different confidence levels. - Integration with the knowledge classification system (D14-004).

Out of scope: - General experimental methodology (D14-002). - Knowledge classification definitions (D14-004, though classification depends on validation). - Integration of validated findings into operational practice (D14-010). - Replication procedures (D14-012, though replication is one form of validation).

Background

3.1 What Peer Review Actually Does

Peer review is often misunderstood as a quality stamp -- a certification that a finding is "correct." It is not. Peer review is a filter for the most obvious errors, a check on methodological soundness, and a challenge to unsupported claims. It catches gross mistakes: arithmetic errors, logical fallacies, undeclared conflicts, and conclusions that the data does not support. It does not guarantee truth. It guarantees that a qualified person other than the researcher has examined the work and found it plausible.

When adapting peer review for a solo environment, it is the functions of peer review -- not its social form -- that must be preserved. The functions are: error detection, methodological verification, logical scrutiny, and adversarial challenge. These functions can be partially performed by a single person if the process is structured to compensate for the absence of an independent reviewer.

3.2 The Self-Review Problem

Self-review is inherently weaker than independent review because the reviewer shares all of the researcher's biases, blind spots, and assumptions. The researcher who made an error is the least likely person to catch it, because the same cognitive process that produced the error will reproduce it during review. The researcher who holds a bias is the least likely person to identify it, because the bias is invisible to the person who holds it.

These limitations cannot be fully overcome. They can be partially mitigated through three mechanisms: structured checklists that force the reviewer to examine specific aspects of the work regardless of whether they seem problematic; temporal separation that introduces cognitive distance between the researcher and reviewer roles; and adversarial framing that explicitly requires the reviewer to argue against the conclusion rather than for it.

3.3 The Validation Spectrum

Not all research findings require the same level of validation. A speculative observation that "the backup seems to take longer on Tuesdays" requires minimal validation -- it is recorded as an observation, classified as speculative, and may or may not be investigated further. A verified finding that "ZFS scrub reliably detects and corrects single-bit errors on our hardware" requires extensive validation because operational decisions will be based on it.

This article defines validation requirements that scale with the confidence level being assigned. The higher the confidence, the more rigorous the validation. This is both practical (rigorous validation is time-consuming) and epistemological (higher confidence claims require stronger evidence).

System Model

4.1 The Validation Checklist

Every research finding undergoes a validation checklist before being classified in the knowledge registry (D14-004). The checklist is performed by the operator in the explicit role of reviewer, not researcher. The distinction is psychological but important: the reviewer's job is to find problems, not to defend the work.

The checklist contains three tiers, corresponding to the three non-deprecated confidence levels:

Tier 1 (Speculative classification):

  • Is the observation or hypothesis clearly stated?
  • Is the evidence base described, including its limitations?
  • Is the confidence level honestly assessed?
  • Has the finding been documented in the appropriate format?

Tier 2 (Provisional classification): All Tier 1 checks, plus:

  • Was the investigation conducted according to D14-002 methodology?
  • Was the experimental design pre-registered?
  • Was raw data collected and preserved per D14-008?
  • Do the conclusions follow logically from the data?
  • Are there alternative explanations that were not considered?
  • Are the limitations of the investigation honestly stated?
  • Has the negative results registry (D14-005) been checked for relevant prior work?

Tier 3 (Verified classification): All Tier 1 and Tier 2 checks, plus:

  • Has the finding been replicated through at least one adapted method per D14-012?
  • Has the adversarial review protocol (Section 4.2) been completed?
  • Has the temporal review (Section 4.3) been completed?
  • Is the chain of custody for all supporting data complete per D14-008?
  • Has the finding been tested against the strongest plausible counter-argument?
  • Could a competent future operator, reading only the documentation, reach the same conclusion?

4.2 The Adversarial Review Protocol

The adversarial review, introduced in D14-001 Section 4.2, is operationalized here as a structured protocol. It is mandatory for any finding being classified as "verified" and recommended for any finding being classified as "provisional."

The protocol has five steps:

Step 1: State the conclusion. Write the conclusion in a single, clear sentence. This forces precision and prevents the vagueness that makes conclusions unfalsifiable.

Step 2: Identify assumptions. List every assumption that the conclusion depends on. Include assumptions about the experimental setup, the data collection, the analysis method, the environmental conditions, and the representativeness of the sample. Be exhaustive. The assumptions you do not list are the ones most likely to be wrong.

Step 3: Attack the weakest assumption. For each assumption, assess its vulnerability. Select the weakest one -- the one most likely to be wrong or most consequential if wrong. Develop a specific argument for why this assumption might be invalid. Search the data for evidence that contradicts it. If no contradicting evidence is found, explain why: is the assumption genuinely robust, or is the available evidence simply insufficient to test it?

Step 4: Consider alternative explanations. Generate at least three alternative explanations for the observed results. For each alternative, assess: is this explanation consistent with the data? If so, what additional evidence would be needed to distinguish between the original conclusion and this alternative? If the alternatives cannot be ruled out with the available data, the finding's confidence level should reflect this ambiguity.

Step 5: Document the review. The adversarial review is documented in a Validation Record (Section 4.4) that includes: the conclusion, the assumption list, the attack on the weakest assumption, the alternative explanations, and the reviewer's honest assessment of whether the conclusion survived the review. "I could not find any problems" is an acceptable outcome only if the review was genuinely rigorous. The documentation must show the work -- the specific alternatives considered, the specific evidence sought, the specific arguments made.

4.3 The Temporal Review Mechanism

Temporal review introduces time separation between the researcher and reviewer roles. The operator conducts the research, documents the findings, and then waits a minimum period before performing the validation review. The waiting period allows the operator's emotional investment in the outcome to fade, their memory of the specific details to soften (forcing reliance on the documentation rather than recall), and their perspective to shift as they engage with other work.

Minimum waiting periods:

  • Speculative classification: No waiting period required.
  • Provisional classification: Minimum 7 days between completing the investigation and performing the validation review.
  • Verified classification: Minimum 30 days between completing the investigation and performing the validation review. The 30-day period ensures meaningful cognitive distance.

During the waiting period, the operator should engage with unrelated work. The goal is to return to the findings with fresh eyes -- not the eyes of the researcher who produced them, but the eyes of a reviewer encountering them for the first time.

4.4 The Validation Record

Every validation produces a Validation Record (VR) containing:

  • VR ID: Format VR-YYYY-NNN.
  • Date of review: When the validation was performed.
  • Research reference: Experiment log ID, TDR ID, or other reference to the work being validated.
  • Proposed confidence level: The level being evaluated for.
  • Checklist results: The completed validation checklist for the proposed tier.
  • Adversarial review results: If applicable, the documented adversarial review per Section 4.2.
  • Temporal review compliance: Confirmation that the minimum waiting period was observed.
  • Issues identified: Any problems, concerns, or weaknesses found during the review.
  • Resolution of issues: How each identified issue was resolved (or acknowledged as unresolved).
  • Final classification decision: The confidence level assigned, with rationale if it differs from the proposed level.
  • Reviewer's honest assessment: A brief statement of the reviewer's overall confidence in the finding, separate from the formal classification. This is where the operator records their gut feeling, which may differ from what the checklist produced.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-PRV-01: No research finding may be classified in the knowledge registry (D14-004) without a completed Validation Record. The VR is the evidence that the finding has been reviewed, not just produced.
  • R-PRV-02: The adversarial review protocol (Section 4.2) is mandatory for all findings proposed for "verified" classification. The protocol must be documented in full, including the specific alternative explanations considered and the evidence sought. A blank adversarial review is worse than no review -- it creates a false appearance of rigor.
  • R-PRV-03: The temporal review waiting periods (Section 4.3) are mandatory. Findings reviewed before the minimum waiting period has elapsed may not be classified above "provisional" regardless of the checklist results.
  • R-PRV-04: The Validation Record must include an honest assessment of review quality. If the operator suspects their review was not genuinely rigorous -- if they rushed through the checklist, if they did not seriously consider alternatives, if they simply confirmed what they already believed -- this must be noted. Dishonest validation records are more dangerous than no validation records because they create false confidence.
  • R-PRV-05: Validation Records must be stored permanently in the research archive alongside the research they validate. Future operators must be able to assess not just whether a finding was validated but how it was validated.
  • R-PRV-06: If a validation review identifies significant issues that cannot be resolved, the finding must be classified at a lower confidence level or flagged for further investigation. Findings with known unresolved issues must not be classified as "verified."
  • R-PRV-07: Validation requirements may not be waived for convenience or urgency. If a finding must be acted upon before validation is complete (e.g., during an emergency), the action may proceed but the finding must remain classified as "speculative" or "provisional" until proper validation is performed.

Failure Modes

  • Validation theater. The operator completes the validation checklist and adversarial review as formalities, checking boxes without genuine intellectual engagement. The review process exists on paper but not in practice. Mitigation: R-PRV-04 requires honest assessment of review quality. The Validation Record's adversarial review section must show substantive work -- specific alternatives considered, specific evidence sought. The annual classification audit (D14-004, R-KCT-05) should review VRs for evidence of genuine engagement.
  • Temporal review erosion. The operator gradually reduces the waiting period, conducting "validation" the day after completing the research. The cognitive distance that the waiting period provides is lost. Mitigation: R-PRV-03 makes the waiting periods mandatory. The VR records the dates of both research completion and validation, making compliance auditable.
  • Confirmation-biased review. The operator conducts the adversarial review but cannot escape their own biases. The "alternative explanations" they generate are weak straw men, easily dismissed. The "weakest assumption" they identify is not actually weak. Mitigation: this is the fundamental limitation of self-review and cannot be fully overcome. The mitigations are: the structured protocol forces engagement even if the engagement is imperfect; the temporal separation provides some cognitive distance; and the documentation allows future operators to assess the quality of the review from the outside.
  • Validation bottleneck. The validation process becomes so time-consuming that findings pile up unvalidated. The operator has a backlog of research that has been conducted but not formally validated, creating a body of un-classified knowledge. Mitigation: the tiered validation system scales effort with confidence level. Speculative findings require minimal validation. Only findings proposed for "verified" status require the full protocol. If the bottleneck persists, the operator may be attempting to validate too many findings at "verified" level.
  • Classification without validation. The operator skips validation entirely, classifying findings directly in the knowledge registry without producing a Validation Record. The registry's confidence levels become meaningless. Mitigation: R-PRV-01 requires a VR for every classification. The annual classification audit should verify that every registry entry has a corresponding VR.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If validation has been treated as theater: Select the three most consequential "verified" findings in the knowledge registry. Re-conduct the adversarial review for each with explicit commitment to genuine engagement. Generate at least three substantive alternative explanations per finding. If the re-review reveals that the original validation was insufficient, downgrade the classification and document the reason.
  2. If temporal review periods have been shortened: Reinstate the full waiting periods immediately. For findings validated with shortened periods, assess whether the shortened review materially affected the quality. If there is any doubt, schedule a re-validation at the proper interval.
  3. If findings have been classified without Validation Records: Conduct retroactive validation for all findings lacking VRs. Prioritize "verified" and "provisional" findings, as these carry the most operational weight. Create VRs using the current validation process. Accept that retroactive validation is weaker than timely validation because the temporal separation has been artificial (the operator has lived with these findings for months or years).
  4. If the validation backlog has grown unmanageable: Triage. Identify findings that are most consequential for operational decisions and validate those first. For lower-priority findings, accept classification at "speculative" until validation capacity is available. Investigate why the backlog grew -- is too much research being produced relative to validation capacity? Should the research pace be reduced, or should the validation process be streamlined?
  5. If confidence in the entire validation process has eroded: Return to this article and D14-001. Re-read the philosophical foundation. Conduct a validation process review: is the checklist still appropriate? Are the waiting periods sufficient? Is the adversarial review protocol producing genuine challenge? Revise the process if needed, but do not abandon it -- an imperfect validation process is vastly better than no validation process.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The validation process is being practiced and calibrated. The operator is learning to separate the researcher and reviewer roles, to take the adversarial review seriously, and to use the temporal separation productively. Early Validation Records may be rough. The process will be refined based on experience with its strengths and weaknesses.
  • Years 5-15: The accumulated VR archive provides data on the validation process itself. How often did the adversarial review catch genuine problems? How often did temporal review change the assessment? Has any "verified" finding been subsequently contradicted, and if so, did the VR for that finding show signs of weak validation? These meta-questions improve the process.
  • Years 15-30: If succession occurs, the VR archive gives the successor a window into the predecessor's intellectual rigor. The successor can read the adversarial reviews and assess for themselves whether the challenges were genuine. They may choose to re-validate findings they consider suspect -- which is their prerogative and may produce genuinely independent review for the first time in the institution's history.
  • Years 30-50+: The validation process has been tested across multiple operators and decades of practice. Its effectiveness can be assessed by comparing "verified" findings against subsequent experience. The process itself has evolved, documented through revisions to this article. The VR archive is a record of the institution's commitment to intellectual honesty over its lifetime.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: I am my own peer reviewer, and I know exactly how unreliable that makes me. I cannot surprise myself. I cannot bring a perspective I do not already have. I cannot catch blind spots that are invisible to me by definition. The validation process in this article is my best attempt to compensate for these limitations, and I want to be honest about how far the compensation goes.

The adversarial review is the most valuable component, and also the most difficult to do genuinely. The temptation is powerful: I have just spent weeks on an investigation, I have a conclusion I believe in, and now I am supposed to argue against myself? The natural response is to generate weak counterarguments that I can easily dismiss, declare the review complete, and move on. I have to resist that temptation every time, and I will not always succeed.

The temporal review is my secret weapon against myself. After 30 days away from a piece of work, I return to it almost as a stranger. The emotional investment has faded. The details have blurred. I am forced to rely on the documentation rather than my memory. In that state, I am closer to an independent reviewer than at any other time. It is not enough. But it is something.
