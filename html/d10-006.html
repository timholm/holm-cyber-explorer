D10-006 — Operational Logging Standards

Document ID
D10-006
Domain
10 -- User Operations
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
OPS-001, D10-002, D6-001, D6-008, GOV-001
Depended Upon By
D10-003 (incident log), D10-004 (maintenance log), D10-005 (classification records), D10-013 (post-incident review records), D7 (logs as intelligence source).

Purpose

This article defines the standards for all operational logging within the holm.chat Documentation Institution. It specifies what gets recorded, when, in what format, where, and for how long. It transforms the general principle of "document everything" from OPS-001 into specific, implementable logging standards that produce useful records rather than ceremonial noise.

Operational logs are the institution's memory of what actually happened. The documentation articles describe what should happen. The governance decisions describe what was decided. But the operational logs describe what happened in practice -- the daily reality of running the institution, with all its deviations, anomalies, discoveries, and routines. Over decades, the operational log becomes the most valuable historical record the institution possesses, because it is the most honest one.

This article exists because logging without standards produces one of two failure modes: either the logs are so sparse that they record nothing useful, or they are so verbose that the useful information is buried in noise. The standards defined here aim for the middle ground: structured enough to be searchable, detailed enough to be useful, brief enough to be sustainable.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The types of operational logs maintained by the institution.
  • The format and structure requirements for each log type.
  • Minimum and maximum logging requirements for daily operations.
  • The relationship between operational logs and other institutional records (decision log, incident log, maintenance log).
  • Log storage, preservation, and retention policies.
  • Log review procedures and their integration with the operational tempo.
  • Minimum viable logging for a solo operator under time pressure.

This article does not cover:

  • System-generated logs (syslog, application logs) -- those are governed by Domain 5 and Domain 8 articles.
  • The decision log format (see GOV-001).
  • Metadata standards for log entries as data artifacts (see D6-008).
  • The incident log format in detail (see D10-003).

Background

3.1 The Operational Log as Institutional Memory

Institutions that endure keep records. Ship captains maintain logbooks. Hospital staff write patient charts. Air traffic controllers record every instruction. The common thread is that these records are not created for pleasure or curiosity but because the institution's survival depends on knowing what happened, when, and why. When something goes wrong, the log is the first thing consulted. When responsibility transfers, the log is the briefing document. When years have passed and memory has faded, the log is the only reliable witness.

The holm.chat operational log serves all of these functions. It is the daily record of the institution's operational life. It records what was checked, what was found, what was done, and what was deferred. It is the bridge between one day's operations and the next, between one operator's knowledge and their successor's understanding, between the institution's current state and its historical trajectory.

3.2 The Problem of Sustainable Logging

Logging has a cost. Every minute spent writing a log entry is a minute not spent operating. For a solo operator with a 15-30 minute daily cycle, the logging overhead must be minimal. But minimal logging that records nothing useful is worse than no logging, because it creates the illusion of a record without the substance.

The solution is structured logging with templates. The operator does not compose prose from scratch each day. Instead, they fill in a structured template that prompts for the essential information, allows for narrative when something noteworthy occurs, and can be completed in two to five minutes. The structure ensures consistency; the narrative space ensures richness.

3.3 The Relationship Between Logs

The institution maintains several types of logs, each serving a different function. The operational log is the daily diary. The incident log records events that caused or threatened harm. The maintenance log records the performance and results of scheduled maintenance. The decision log records governance decisions. These logs are related but separate: they serve different audiences and different purposes. The operational log is the connective tissue that ties them together, because it references incidents, maintenance activities, and decisions as they occur in the flow of daily operations.

4.2 Operational Log Format

Each daily operational log entry contains the following fields:

Header:

  • Date (ISO 8601 format: YYYY-MM-DD).
  • Operator identifier.
  • Operational cycle type (routine, abbreviated, degraded).

Health Check Summary:

  • Systems status: all nominal, or list of deviations.
  • Storage utilization: current usage and trend (stable, growing, shrinking).
  • Backup status: most recent backup date, status, and verification result.
  • Security status: any alerts, anomalies, or access events.

Scheduled Work:

  • Maintenance tasks performed (reference Task IDs from D10-004).
  • Documentation updates made.
  • Follow-up items from previous entries addressed.

Observations:

  • At least one genuine observation per entry. This is the narrative section. "All systems nominal" is not an observation. "CPU temperature 2 degrees higher than last week; still within range; monitoring" is an observation. "Noticed the UPS battery indicator has moved from green to amber since last Thursday" is an observation.

Deviations and Anomalies:

  • Any deviations from the daily checklist, with classification per D10-002 Section 4.4.
  • Any anomalies detected during the health check, with initial assessment.
  • Cross-references to incident log entries if an incident was opened.

Follow-up Items:

  • Items requiring attention in subsequent operational cycles.
  • Items carried forward from previous entries that remain unresolved.

Closing:

  • Time spent on today's operational cycle (actual, in minutes).
  • Overall assessment: routine, notable, or concerning.

4.3 Change Log Format

The change log records every modification to the institution's systems, configurations, or procedures. Each entry contains:

  • Date and time of the change.
  • System or component affected.
  • Description of the change (what was changed and how).
  • Rationale for the change (why it was made).
  • Governance authorization (which decision log entry authorized this change, if applicable).
  • Rollback procedure (how to undo the change if it causes problems).
  • Verification (how the change was tested and confirmed successful).

4.4 Minimum Viable Logging

On days when the operator is under extreme time pressure or operating in degraded mode per D10-010, the following minimum viable log entry is acceptable:

  • Date.
  • One-line status: "All systems operational" or brief description of any issue.
  • Flag: "Abbreviated entry -- [reason]."

This minimum entry preserves the daily logging streak and records whether the institution was healthy on this date. It is not sustainable as a routine practice -- it provides insufficient detail for pattern analysis and follow-up. But it is infinitely better than a missing entry, which creates ambiguity about whether the institution was checked at all.

4.5 Storage and Preservation

The operational log is Tier 1 data per D6-001. It is subject to all Tier 1 preservation requirements: multiple copies, integrity verification, and archival-quality storage. The operational log is never purged. Entries are never deleted or modified; corrections are recorded as new entries referencing the original, consistent with the append-only principle in D10-002 Section 4.3.

The log should be stored in a format that is human-readable without specialized software. Plain text with structured formatting is preferred over binary formats, database formats, or proprietary formats. The log must be readable in fifty years with tools that exist today: a text editor, a file system, and eyes. Metadata per D6-008 standards must accompany the log files.

4.6 Log Review Integration

Operational logs are reviewed at the following frequencies:

  • Weekly: Review the past week's entries for unresolved follow-up items, recurring observations, and patterns. Part of the OPS-001 weekly operations cycle.
  • Monthly: Review the past month's entries for trends. Count deviations by category. Identify any observations that require investigation. Part of the OPS-001 monthly cycle.
  • Quarterly: Review the quarter's entries alongside the incident log and maintenance log. Feed findings into the D10-005 quarterly pattern review.
  • Annually: Read the full year's log. Summarize the year's operational history. This summary becomes part of the institutional record and informs the annual strategic planning process.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D10L-01: The operational log must be updated every day the institution is operational. Minimum viable logging (Section 4.4) is acceptable under duress; a missing entry is not acceptable.
  • R-D10L-02: Every operational log entry must contain at least one genuine observation per D10-002 Section 4.3. Perfunctory "all passed" entries defeat the purpose of logging.
  • R-D10L-03: The operational log is append-only. Entries must never be deleted or modified. Corrections are recorded as new entries. This rule preserves the log's integrity as an honest record.
  • R-D10L-04: The operational log must be stored in a human-readable, non-proprietary format. No specialized software should be required to read it.
  • R-D10L-05: Every system or configuration change must be recorded in the change log with the fields specified in Section 4.3. Undocumented changes are treated as incidents per D10-003.
  • R-D10L-06: Log entries must use ISO 8601 date format (YYYY-MM-DD) for consistency and searchability across decades of records.
  • R-D10L-07: The operational log and change log are subject to the backup and integrity verification requirements of their respective data tiers per D6-001.

Failure Modes

  • Log stagnation. Entries become rote, repeating the same "all nominal" without genuine observation. The log becomes a checklist of checkboxes rather than a living record. Mitigation: R-D10L-02 requires at least one genuine observation. The weekly review should check for stagnation and prompt more attentive logging.

  • Log verbosity. The operator writes extensive narrative entries that take 20 minutes to compose. The logging overhead consumes the entire daily cycle. Mitigation: the structured format in Section 4.2 constrains the entry. Narrative is appropriate for anomalies and observations; routine items use the structured fields.

  • Log fragmentation. Operational information is scattered across multiple logs without cross-references. An incident is recorded in the incident log but not referenced in the operational log. A maintenance task is recorded in the maintenance log but the finding is not noted in the operational log. Mitigation: the operational log serves as the connective tissue. All incidents, maintenance events, and decisions that occur during an operational cycle are at minimum referenced (by ID) in that day's operational log entry.

  • Format decay. Over time, the operator stops following the structured format. Entries become informal notes that lack the fields necessary for searchability and analysis. Mitigation: use a template. The template enforces structure without requiring the operator to remember the format from memory. Review format adherence during quarterly reviews.

  • Storage failure. The log is lost due to hardware failure or data corruption. Decades of operational history vanish. Mitigation: Tier 1 preservation requirements per D6-001. The operational log is backed up with the same rigor as any other critical institutional data.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If logging has lapsed: Begin today. Record the gap duration in the first new entry. Do not attempt to reconstruct entries for missed days unless specific events during the gap are known and documented elsewhere (incident log, change log). A gap in the log is honest; fabricated entries are not.

  2. If log format has decayed: During the next weekly review, recommit to the structured format. Create or update the logging template. For the next 30 days, use the template deliberately until the habit is re-established.

  3. If logs are fragmented: During the quarterly review, reconcile the operational log with the incident log, maintenance log, and decision log. Add cross-references where they are missing. Going forward, establish the practice of referencing other log entries by ID in the operational log as events occur.

  4. If log storage is compromised: This is a data loss incident. Invoke D10-003 and D6-002. Restore from the most recent backup. The gap between the backup and the loss is irrecoverable; record the gap and its extent in the restored log.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The logging format is being tested against daily reality. Expect adjustments to the template as the operator discovers which fields are useful daily, which are useful only occasionally, and which are never useful. The first year's log will be inconsistent as the format stabilizes. That inconsistency is acceptable and expected.

  • Years 5-15: The log format should be stable. The weekly, monthly, and quarterly reviews should be producing insights from log analysis. The operator should be able to search the log effectively for patterns and historical information. The log should be large enough that a table of contents or index mechanism becomes useful.

  • Years 15-30: The operational log is now a historical document spanning more than a decade. It records the institution's entire operational biography. A successor reading the log can understand not just the institution's current state but how it arrived there. The log's value as institutional memory now exceeds its value as a daily operational tool.

  • Years 30-50+: Decades of operational logs. The early entries may seem quaint or primitive compared to current practice. They must be preserved exactly as they are. They are the institution's founding testimony, and their value increases with age.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The single most important sentence in this article is: "At least one genuine observation per entry." Everything else is structure. The observation is the soul. A log full of "all nominal" for 365 days tells me nothing except that I went through the motions 365 times. A log with one sentence of genuine observation each day tells me 365 things about my institution that I would otherwise have forgotten.

I have chosen plain text for the log format because I cannot predict what software will be available in fifty years, but I can predict that plain text will be readable. Databases are more powerful but more fragile. Spreadsheets are more structured but more format-dependent. Plain text with consistent formatting is the least powerful option and the most durable one. That trade-off is correct for this institution.

The minimum viable logging provision is a concession to reality. There will be days when I am sick, distracted, overwhelmed, or simply do not want to spend five minutes writing about my backup status. On those days, one line is enough. One line preserves the streak, records the date, and tells future me that on this day, I at least looked at the systems. That is the floor. It is never acceptable to have no entry at all.
