GOV-012 — Governance Amendment Process

Document ID
GOV-012
Domain
2 -- Governance & Authority
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
ETH-001, CON-001, GOV-001, GOV-007, GOV-008, GOV-010
Depended Upon By
None (terminal article). This article governs the amendment of all other governance articles.

Purpose

This article defines how governance structures themselves change. Every other governance article defines rules, procedures, and frameworks. This article defines how those rules, procedures, and frameworks are modified, extended, or retired. It is the meta-governance article: governance about governance.

Every institution must balance two competing needs: stability and adaptability. A governance structure that cannot be changed will eventually become obsolete, its rules no longer matching the reality they were designed to govern. A governance structure that changes too easily provides no stability, its rules shifting with every passing mood or preference. The amendment process is the mechanism that mediates between these two needs.

This article exists because the founder of this institution knows -- with the certainty that comes from watching other institutions -- that the governance structures written today will need to change. Technology will change. Circumstances will change. The institution's membership may change. The world will present challenges that the founder could not anticipate. The governance framework must be able to evolve in response to these challenges without losing its essential character.

But evolution must be deliberate. It must be documented. It must be constrained by the principles in ETH-001 and the mandate in CON-001. And it must be difficult enough that amendments represent genuine institutional will rather than momentary whim. This article calibrates that difficulty.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The amendment process for governance articles (GOV-001 through GOV-014).
  • The amendment process for root documents (ETH-001, CON-001, GOV-001) -- which is a special case of the general process, with additional requirements.
  • The process for creating new governance articles.
  • The process for retiring governance articles that are no longer needed.
  • Version control for governance documents.
  • The protection of immutable principles: elements of the governance framework that may not be amended under any circumstances.

This article does not cover amendments to non-governance articles (those follow the decision processes in GOV-008 as applied within their respective domains). It does not cover the creation of new domains (which is an Existential Decision per GOV-008). It covers only changes to the governance framework itself.

Background

Constitutional amendments are among the most carefully guarded processes in any governance system. The United States Constitution has been amended only 27 times in over 230 years. The difficulty of amendment is not a flaw; it is the source of the Constitution's stability and authority. If the Constitution could be changed by a simple majority vote, it would be changed constantly, and its function as a stable foundation for governance would be lost.

At the same time, the ability to amend is essential. An unamendable governance framework is a time bomb. As circumstances change and the framework becomes increasingly mismatched with reality, pressure builds. Eventually, the framework either breaks catastrophically (revolution) or is simply ignored (institutional decay). Neither outcome is acceptable.

This institution's governance framework is designed to be amendable but resistant to casual amendment. The resistance is proportional to the importance of what is being amended. Changing a procedural detail in GOV-013 (Meeting and Deliberation Protocols) is relatively easy. Changing a structural element of GOV-001 (Authority Model) is relatively hard. Changing a principle in ETH-001 is the hardest thing the institution can do.

This graduated resistance is the central design principle of the amendment process. It ensures that small corrections can be made efficiently while fundamental changes receive the scrutiny they deserve. It also ensures that the governance framework does not ossify: the path to change always exists, even if the path is steep for the most important changes.

System Model

4.1 Amendment Categories

Amendments to governance documents are classified into three categories based on what is being changed:

Category A: Clarification Amendments. Changes that do not alter the substance of a governance rule but improve its clarity, correct typographical errors, update references to other articles, or add examples. The governance rule itself is unchanged; only its expression is improved.

Process: Significant Decision process (GOV-008). 7-day waiting period. The amendment must be documented in the decision log and in the article's Commentary Section, noting that it is a clarification, not a substantive change.

Category B: Procedural Amendments. Changes that alter the procedures, thresholds, time limits, or operational details of a governance rule without changing the rule's fundamental purpose or scope. Examples: changing a waiting period from 30 days to 21 days, adding a new field to a decision template, modifying the frequency of a review cycle.

Process: Critical Decision process (GOV-008). 30-day waiting period. The amendment proposal must include a rationale for why the current procedure is inadequate and an assessment of how the change affects other governance articles. The proposal is reviewed during the waiting period against the governance health assessment data (GOV-007) to confirm that the change addresses a real need rather than a perceived inconvenience.

Category C: Structural Amendments. Changes that alter the fundamental purpose, scope, or principles of a governance article, or that amend a root document (ETH-001, CON-001, GOV-001). Examples: redefining the authority tiers, changing the decision taxonomy, altering the ethical principles, modifying the founding mandate.

Process: Existential Decision process (GOV-008). 90-day waiting period. The amendment proposal must include all elements of the Existential Decision Template, plus: a detailed analysis of how the change affects every other governance article, a transition plan for moving from the current governance structure to the amended one, and an explicit statement of what institutional value is being sacrificed and what institutional value is being gained.

4.2 The Amendment Proposal

Every amendment begins with a formal proposal. The proposal format varies by category but always includes:

  • The specific text to be changed. Quote the current text exactly. Show the proposed replacement text exactly. This is not a summary; it is the actual before and after.
  • The category of the amendment (A, B, or C) with justification for the category assignment.
  • The rationale. Why is this change needed? What problem does the current text create? What evidence supports the need for change?
  • The impact assessment. What other articles, processes, or practices are affected by this change?

4.3 The Amendment Review

During the waiting period, the amendment undergoes review:

For Category A: The operator reads the amended text in context during the waiting period and confirms that the clarification does not inadvertently change the substance. If it does, the amendment is escalated to Category B.

For Category B: The operator reviews the amendment at least twice during the 30-day waiting period. Each review is documented. The second review should specifically assess whether the initial rationale still holds after two or more weeks of consideration. Additionally, the operator should mentally simulate three scenarios involving the amended procedure to test whether it performs as intended.

For Category C: The operator reviews the amendment at least four times during the 90-day waiting period, at roughly equal intervals. Each review is documented. The reviews should progressively deepen: the first review checks the rationale, the second checks the impact assessment, the third checks the transition plan, and the fourth is a final comprehensive review. If a successor has been designated, they must be notified and given the opportunity to comment.

4.4 The Amendment Record

When an amendment is ratified, the following records are created:

  • The amended article is updated with the new text. The version number is incremented (see Section 4.6).
  • An entry is added to the amended article's Commentary Section noting the amendment: date, category, summary, and rationale.
  • An entry is added to the decision log with the full amendment proposal and review documentation.
  • If the amendment affects other articles, those articles receive Commentary Section entries noting the change and its implications.

4.5 Creating and Retiring Governance Articles

Creating a new governance article is a Category B amendment to the governance framework as a whole. It requires:

  • A proposal that defines the article's purpose, scope, and relationship to existing articles.
  • Confirmation that the article's subject is not already adequately covered by an existing article.
  • A draft of the article itself, following the standard 10-section structure.
  • Critical Decision process (30-day waiting period).

Retiring a governance article is also a Category B amendment. It requires:

  • A proposal explaining why the article is no longer needed.
  • Confirmation that no other article depends on the article being retired (or that those dependencies have been addressed).
  • A record of the retired article is preserved in the institutional archive; retirement means the article is no longer active, not that it is destroyed.
  • Critical Decision process (30-day waiting period).

4.6 Version Control

Governance articles follow semantic versioning: Major.Minor.Patch.

  • Patch increment (e.g., 1.0.0 to 1.0.1): Category A amendments (clarifications).
  • Minor increment (e.g., 1.0.1 to 1.1.0): Category B amendments (procedural changes).
  • Major increment (e.g., 1.1.0 to 2.0.0): Category C amendments (structural changes).

The version history of every governance article is maintained in the decision log. Previous versions are not destroyed; they are archived. Any operator should be able to trace the evolution of any governance article from its initial version to its current state.

4.7 Immutable Principles

Certain elements of the governance framework are designated as immutable: they may not be amended under any circumstances. These are:

  • The append-only nature of the decision log. The decision log may never be made editable or deletable. This is the bedrock of institutional accountability.
  • The requirement that governance amendments follow a documented process. Governance may not be changed by fiat, regardless of who holds authority. There must always be a proposal, a waiting period, and a record.
  • The right of any member to exercise a constitutional veto (GOV-010). The ability to block a decision that conflicts with root documents may not be removed.

These immutable principles exist because some safeguards are so fundamental that removing them would destroy the institution's character as a governed entity rather than an arbitrary exercise of personal will. They are the governance equivalent of physical constants: they define the framework within which everything else operates.

If the institution ever concludes that one of these immutable principles is genuinely wrong, the appropriate response is not to amend the principle but to dissolve the institution and reconstitute it under a new governance framework. This is an extreme measure, and it is meant to be. The immutable principles are the institution's identity. Changing them changes the institution into something fundamentally different.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-GOV-12-01: Every governance amendment must be categorized (A, B, or C) and must follow the process defined for that category. Amendments that skip the required process are invalid regardless of their substance.
  • R-GOV-12-02: Category assignments may be escalated (A to B, or B to C) at any point during the review process. They may not be de-escalated for Category C amendments: once classified as structural, an amendment remains structural.
  • R-GOV-12-03: The immutable principles in Section 4.7 may not be amended. Any proposal that would modify these principles is automatically rejected. This rule is itself immutable.
  • R-GOV-12-04: Previous versions of governance articles must be preserved in the institutional archive. No version may be destroyed. The complete history of governance evolution must be available to future operators.
  • R-GOV-12-05: Amendments to this article (GOV-012) are always Category C, regardless of how minor they appear. The process for changing governance is itself a structural matter.
  • R-GOV-12-06: No more than three Category C amendments may be ratified in any twelve-month period. If institutional circumstances require more fundamental governance changes than this limit allows, the operator should consider whether a comprehensive governance overhaul (itself a Category C process) would be more appropriate than piecemeal structural amendments.
  • R-GOV-12-07: The governance amendment process must be reviewed every five years as part of the sunset clause review defined in the Domain 2 maintenance plan. The review assesses whether the process is functioning as intended: are amendments happening at an appropriate rate? Are the categories well-calibrated? Is the process sufficiently accessible for necessary changes while sufficiently resistant to unnecessary ones?

Failure Modes

  • Amendment avoidance. The operator recognizes that a governance rule needs updating but avoids proposing an amendment because the process feels burdensome. The governance framework gradually drifts out of alignment with institutional reality. Mitigation: the three-category system ensures that minor corrections (Category A) have a lightweight process. The quarterly health check (GOV-007) provides a scheduled opportunity to identify needed amendments.
  • Amendment cascade. One structural amendment triggers the need for amendments to multiple other articles, which in turn trigger further amendments. The governance framework enters a period of instability where multiple articles are simultaneously being revised. Mitigation: the transition plan requirement for Category C amendments, which should identify downstream effects before the amendment is ratified. R-GOV-12-06, which limits the rate of structural amendments.
  • Category manipulation. The operator classifies a structural amendment as procedural to avoid the more rigorous process. Over time, this erodes the integrity of the amendment categories. Mitigation: the escalation-only rule for Category C (R-GOV-12-02). The governance health check should assess whether recent amendments were appropriately categorized.
  • Ossification. The amendment process is so rigorous that no amendments are ever proposed, even when the governance framework needs updating. Mitigation: the five-year sunset review (R-GOV-12-07), which explicitly asks whether the process itself has become an impediment. If it has, that is itself a Category C amendment to propose.
  • Immutable principle challenge. A future operator genuinely believes that one of the immutable principles in Section 4.7 is wrong and is frustrated by the lack of a mechanism to change it. Mitigation: the dissolution-and-reconstitution path described in Section 4.7. It is extreme because it is meant to be. But it exists, and its existence ensures that the institution is never truly trapped by its own rules.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If governance amendments have been made without proper process: Review all amendments in the decision log. For each that lacks the required proposal, review, and documentation, initiate a retroactive review. The amendment may stand if the retroactive review confirms it, but the procedural violation must be documented and the amendment record completed.
  2. If the amendment categories have been misapplied: Review all recent amendments for correct categorization. For any that were under-categorized (substantive changes classified as clarifications), escalate them retroactively. This may require re-initiating the review process for the higher category.
  3. If an amendment cascade has destabilized the governance framework: Pause all pending amendments. Conduct a comprehensive assessment of the governance framework's current state. Identify which amendments are essential and which can be deferred. Resume the amendment process with the essential amendments only, in a deliberate sequence that minimizes further disruption.
  4. If version control has been neglected: Reconstruct the version history from the decision log. Each amendment entry in the log corresponds to a version increment. Assign version numbers retroactively and update the current version of each affected article.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Few amendments are expected. The governance framework is new and largely untested. Most changes will be Category A clarifications as the operator discovers ambiguities in the initial drafting. The amendment process itself should be lightly used but strictly followed, to establish the habit of formal governance change.
  • Years 5-15: Category B amendments become more likely as operational experience reveals procedural improvements. The five-year sunset review (the first one) provides a structured opportunity to assess the entire governance framework. This is the period where the amendment process proves its value or reveals its inadequacy.
  • Years 15-30: If succession has occurred, the new operator may propose amendments that reflect their different perspective. This is healthy and expected. The amendment process ensures that these changes are deliberate and documented, not impulsive. Category C amendments may be proposed as the institution adapts to its second generation.
  • Years 30-50+: The governance framework has been amended multiple times. The version histories of governance articles tell the story of the institution's evolution. The amendment process should itself have been refined through at least one or two Category B amendments. The Commentary Sections of governance articles contain reflections from multiple operators, providing context that no single version of the text can capture.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators. Each entry should include the date, the author's identifier, and the context for the commentary.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: This is the last governance article to be written, and it is the one that makes me most nervous. Every other governance article defines rules. This article defines how those rules change. If I get this wrong -- if the amendment process is too easy, or too hard, or poorly calibrated -- the error will compound over decades. Too easy, and the governance framework will be rewritten by every new operator until it bears no resemblance to its original intent. Too hard, and the framework will become a straitjacket that future operators cannot escape.

I have placed my trust in the three-category system. Minor fixes are easy. Procedural changes are moderately difficult. Structural changes are hard. And a small number of things are immutable. This gradient feels right today. Whether it will feel right in twenty years, I cannot know. But I have built in a mechanism for reassessment (the five-year review), and the amendment process can be applied to itself (though always at Category C). The framework is self-modifying, within limits. That is the best I can do.
