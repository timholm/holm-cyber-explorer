D17-010 — Resource Allocation Framework

Document ID
D17-010
Domain
17 -- Scaling & Federation
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D17-001 (Federation Philosophy), D17-002 (Node Identity), D17-009 (Topology Models)
Depended Upon By
D17-011 (Capacity Planning), D17-015 (Federation Health Monitoring). Referenced by OPS-001 (operational complexity budget).

Purpose

This article defines the framework by which resources are identified, measured, allocated, and balanced across the federation. Resources in the context of an air-gapped digital institution are finite and non-trivial: compute capacity (processor time, memory), storage capacity (disk space, physical media), power (electrical supply, battery reserves, solar capacity), human attention (operator time, courier effort, maintenance hours), and physical materials (replacement hardware, transport media, packaging). Each of these resources is consumed by every node and by the federation itself as a coordinating entity.

A single-node institution manages its resources locally. Federation introduces resource interdependencies: a hub node in a star topology bears more transport burden than a spoke. A node that hosts a large shared archive consumes more storage. A node that processes reunion sync packages after prolonged partition consumes a burst of compute and operator attention. Without a framework for understanding and managing these resource flows, federation creates invisible debts and unsustainable imbalances that surface as failures years or decades after they begin.

This article is addressed to every node operator in a federation, with particular attention to those whose nodes bear disproportionate resource burdens due to their topological position, their archive size, or their role in governance and administration.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The taxonomy of resources relevant to air-gapped federation.
  • Resource measurement methods: how to quantify compute, storage, power, human-time, and material consumption.
  • The resource budget model: how to plan and track resource expenditure at the node and federation level.
  • Allocation fairness principles: how to distribute shared resource burdens equitably across nodes.
  • Scarcity protocols: what happens when a resource is insufficient at a node or across the federation.
  • Resource reporting: how nodes communicate their resource status to the federation.

This article does not cover:

  • Long-term forecasting and growth planning (see D17-011 Capacity Planning).
  • Specific hardware provisioning or replacement procedures (see Infrastructure Domains).
  • Power generation or distribution systems (see D4-series Infrastructure articles).
  • The governance process for federation-level resource decisions (see D17-008).
  • Health metrics derived from resource data (see D17-015).

Background

3.1 Resource Asymmetry in Voluntary Federation

The federation is voluntary. Nodes join because they find value in participation. But the costs of participation are not evenly distributed. A hub node in a star topology processes twice as many sync packages as a spoke node. A node with a large, well-curated archive attracts more synchronization traffic than a new node with little content. A node with a skilled operator spends more human-time on maintenance and support than a node with a less experienced operator. These asymmetries are natural and often beneficial -- they reflect the diverse strengths of different nodes. But if they are not acknowledged and managed, they become sources of resentment and eventual withdrawal.

The resource allocation framework does not attempt to make all nodes equal. It attempts to make the costs visible, to ensure that no node bears unsustainable burdens without acknowledgment, and to provide mechanisms for redistribution when imbalances become harmful.

3.2 Resources as Constraints, Not Abstract Numbers

In a conventional IT environment, resources are fungible and elastic. Need more compute? Spin up another server. Need more storage? Buy another disk. Need more bandwidth? Upgrade the link. In an air-gapped, off-grid institution, every resource is constrained by physical reality. Compute is limited by the hardware you have. Storage is limited by the drives you possess. Power is limited by your generation capacity. Human attention is limited by the number of hours in a day and the number of competent operators available. These constraints are hard. They cannot be wished away by better software or more efficient algorithms.

The resource framework therefore treats resources not as quantities to be optimized but as constraints to be respected. The question is not "how do we get more?" but "how do we live within what we have?"

3.3 The Federation Tax

Federation is not free. It imposes a resource overhead -- a "federation tax" -- on every participating node. This tax includes: the compute and storage cost of maintaining sync package infrastructure, the human-time cost of managing trust relationships and governance participation, the transport cost of physically moving sync media between nodes, and the attention cost of reviewing and integrating incoming content from other nodes. This tax must be accounted for in every node's resource budget. If a node cannot afford the federation tax, it should not federate -- or should federate at a reduced level (fewer sync partners, less frequent sync, limited content sharing).

System Model

4.1 Resource Taxonomy

The federation recognizes five resource categories, each with distinct measurement units and allocation characteristics:

Compute. Processor time, working memory, and temporary storage used for active operations (sync package processing, conflict resolution, search indexing, content validation). Measured in processing-hours per quarter. Compute is consumed in bursts (during sync processing) and at background levels (routine maintenance). Compute is non-transferable between nodes -- each node must have sufficient local compute.

Storage. Persistent data capacity for the institution's archive, sync package staging, quarantine areas, logs, and institutional memory. Measured in gigabytes. Storage is consumed monotonically -- it grows over time as content accumulates. Storage can be partially offloaded through selective replication policies (a node may choose not to replicate all content from all peer nodes). Storage media has finite lifespan and must be budgeted for replacement.

Power. Electrical energy for running compute hardware, storage devices, and peripheral equipment. Measured in watt-hours per day. For off-grid nodes, power is generated (solar, wind, generator) and stored (batteries). Power is the most immediate constraint: when power runs out, everything stops. Power budgets must account for seasonal variation (solar output varies), equipment degradation (battery capacity declines), and peak demand (sync processing is power-intensive).

Human attention. Operator time for maintenance, administration, courier duties, governance participation, content curation, and troubleshooting. Measured in person-hours per quarter. Human attention is the scarcest resource in most federations. It is also the most variable: an operator who is energized and engaged produces far more useful work than one who is burned out. Human attention budgets must include recovery time -- no operator should be scheduled at 100% capacity.

Physical materials. Transport media (USB drives, external hard drives, SD cards), packaging materials, replacement hardware components, and consumables (paper for printed backups, printer supplies). Measured in inventory counts and replacement intervals. Physical materials are consumed and degraded through use and must be replenished through procurement -- which, for an air-gapped institution, may be infrequent and logistically complex.

4.2 The Node Resource Budget

Each node MUST maintain a resource budget that accounts for its local consumption and its federation contribution. The budget is structured as follows:

NODE RESOURCE BUDGET
====================
Node ID: [identifier]
Budget Period: [quarter and year]

CATEGORY        | CAPACITY  | LOCAL USE | FED. TAX  | RESERVE  | AVAILABLE
----------------|-----------|-----------|-----------|----------|----------
Compute (hrs)   |           |           |           |          |
Storage (GB)    |           |           |           |          |
Power (Wh/day)  |           |           |           |          |
Human (hrs)     |           |           |           |          |
Materials       |           |           |           |          |

CAPACITY: Total available resource for the period.
LOCAL USE: Resource consumed by local (non-federation) operations.
FED. TAX: Resource consumed by federation-related operations.
RESERVE: Resource held in reserve for emergencies (minimum 15%).
AVAILABLE: CAPACITY - LOCAL USE - FED. TAX - RESERVE.

A node whose AVAILABLE column shows negative values in any category is operating beyond its means and must either reduce consumption, increase capacity, or reduce federation participation.

4.3 Federation Resource Reporting

Each node includes a resource summary in its quarterly governance sync (D17-008). The summary contains:

  • The node's current resource budget (as above).
  • Any resource categories where AVAILABLE is below 10% of CAPACITY (a warning threshold).
  • Any resource categories where the federation tax exceeds 30% of CAPACITY (an imbalance indicator).
  • A qualitative assessment of the operator's capacity and wellbeing (critical but often omitted in technical frameworks).

This reporting enables the federation to detect emerging imbalances before they become crises. A node whose storage is nearly full, whose operator is approaching burnout, or whose power budget is strained by seasonal changes can signal this early and receive support -- whether through reduced sync frequency, temporary exemption from governance participation, or redistribution of hub responsibilities.

4.4 Allocation Fairness Principles

Resource allocation across the federation follows these principles:

Proportionality. Federation costs should be distributed in proportion to federation benefits. A node that receives more content from the federation should bear a proportionally higher share of sync processing costs. A node that contributes more content should have its contribution acknowledged in resource accounting.

Voluntarism. No node may be compelled to contribute resources beyond its stated capacity. Resource commitments are voluntary and may be renegotiated at any quarterly review. A node that reduces its resource contribution may receive reduced federation benefits (less frequent sync, lower priority in conflict resolution) but may not be penalized or expelled for resource scarcity alone.

Sustainability. Resource allocations must be sustainable over the long term. A resource commitment that an operator can maintain for one quarter but not for a year is not a valid commitment. Burnout is a resource failure. The framework must prevent it, not just respond to it.

Transparency. All resource budgets and federation tax assessments are shared openly within the federation. No node should be surprised by the resource costs of federation. Hidden costs erode trust.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D17-010-01: Resource Budget Is Mandatory. Every federated node MUST maintain a resource budget updated at least quarterly. The budget template in Section 4.2 is the minimum required format. Nodes that fail to submit a resource budget for two consecutive quarters SHOULD be flagged for a health review per D17-015.

  • R-D17-010-02: Minimum Reserve of 15%. Every node MUST maintain a minimum reserve of 15% of capacity in every resource category. This reserve is for emergencies: unexpected sync volumes, hardware failures, power disruptions, or operator illness. A node that consistently operates below the 15% reserve threshold is in a pre-failure state.

  • R-D17-010-03: Federation Tax Must Not Exceed 40%. The federation tax -- the total resources consumed by federation-related operations -- SHOULD NOT exceed 40% of any node's capacity in any resource category. If it does, the node is spending more resources on federation than on its primary institutional mission. The federation must work with the node to reduce its federation burden (through topology changes, reduced sync frequency, or redistribution of roles).

  • R-D17-010-04: No Coerced Resource Contribution. No governance decision, federation agreement, or peer pressure may compel a node to contribute resources it has not voluntarily committed. Resource sovereignty follows from institutional sovereignty (D17-001, R-D17-01). A node that cannot or will not meet a resource request must not be treated as deficient.

  • R-D17-010-05: Scarcity Triggers Graceful Degradation. When a node experiences resource scarcity (AVAILABLE drops below zero in any category), it MUST implement graceful degradation in the following priority order: (1) reduce non-essential federation activities first (content sharing, discovery), (2) reduce sync frequency, (3) reduce governance participation to voting only, (4) request partition status (temporary withdrawal from active synchronization). At no point should local institutional operations be sacrificed for federation obligations.

  • R-D17-010-06: Human Attention Is a First-Class Resource. Human attention MUST be tracked in the resource budget with the same rigor as compute and storage. An operator who is overcommitted is a single point of failure for the node. The resource budget must explicitly account for operator rest, training, and non-institutional obligations.

Failure Modes

  • Resource accounting neglect. Nodes stop maintaining their resource budgets because the process feels bureaucratic. Resource imbalances accumulate silently. By the time a crisis surfaces (a node runs out of storage, an operator burns out), the federation has no data to diagnose the cause or predict future occurrences. Likelihood: high without cultural reinforcement. Severity: medium individually, high systemically. Detection: missing resource reports in quarterly governance syncs.

  • Invisible federation tax. The costs of federation participation are not tracked separately from local operations. Operators feel overworked but cannot articulate why. The federation appears to demand nothing while actually consuming significant resources. Trust erodes because the costs are felt but not named. Likelihood: high in early federations without established accounting. Severity: medium. Detection: operator dissatisfaction; inability to explain resource consumption.

  • Resource imbalance between nodes. One node has abundant resources while another is severely constrained. The abundant node does not increase its federation contribution because no one has asked. The constrained node does not reduce its federation burden because it fears losing standing. The imbalance persists until the constrained node fails. Likelihood: medium. Severity: high if the constrained node holds unique content. Detection: comparison of resource reports across nodes during governance review.

  • Operator burnout. Human attention budget is exceeded consistently. The operator compensates by working longer hours, skipping rest, deferring personal obligations. Performance degrades. Errors increase. The operator eventually withdraws, taking institutional knowledge with them. Likelihood: high in single-operator nodes with hub responsibilities. Severity: critical -- potentially fatal to the node. Detection: declining quality of sync packages; increasing processing delays; operator self-report in resource summary.

  • Scarcity cascade. One node's resource scarcity triggers increased burden on neighboring nodes (e.g., a hub node reduces sync frequency, forcing spoke nodes to compensate with direct links to each other). The compensating nodes may themselves approach scarcity, triggering a cascade. Likelihood: low to medium. Severity: high if it affects multiple nodes simultaneously. Detection: multiple nodes reporting resource warnings in the same quarter.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If resource accounting has lapsed: Conduct a resource census across the federation. Each node prepares a current-state resource budget within 30 days. The governance lead compiles a federation-wide resource report. Identify any nodes in pre-failure states (below reserve thresholds). Prioritize support for those nodes. Re-establish the quarterly reporting cadence with explicit calendar reminders.

  2. If an imbalance is detected: Quantify the imbalance using resource reports. Determine the cause (topological burden, archive size, governance role, operator capacity). Propose a redistribution plan through the governance process. Options include: redistributing hub responsibilities, adjusting sync frequency for overburdened nodes, sharing courier duties, or lending physical materials. The plan must be voluntary -- redistribution is a request, not a demand.

  3. If operator burnout occurs or is imminent: Immediately reduce the operator's federation obligations to the minimum (voting only, no courier duties, no sync processing beyond incoming packages). If possible, identify a temporary assistant or substitute operator. Review the resource budget to determine which obligations exceeded the operator's capacity. Adjust permanently, not just temporarily. An operator who recovers from burnout and returns to the same unsustainable workload will burn out again.

  4. If a scarcity cascade is developing: Declare a federation-wide resource emergency. All non-essential sync activity is suspended. Each node operates independently, prioritizing local stability. The governance lead coordinates a resource assessment. The federation resumes sync activity only when resource budgets across all affected nodes show AVAILABLE above zero in all categories.

  5. If a node cannot sustain federation participation: The node transitions to minimal federation mode: it receives sync packages but does not process or distribute them until resources permit. If the condition persists beyond two quarters, the node should consider voluntary partition (temporary withdrawal) per D17-006, with a planned return when resources recover.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Resource accounting will feel like overhead for a small federation. Do it anyway. The habit of tracking resources prevents the slow accumulation of invisible debts. For single-node institutions preparing for future federation, begin tracking the baseline resource budget so that the federation tax can be measured accurately when federation begins.

  • Years 5-15: The federation tax becomes visible and potentially contentious. The fairness principles in Section 4.4 will be tested by real imbalances. This is the period when the resource framework proves its value -- or when its absence causes preventable failures.

  • Years 15-30: Resource patterns stabilize. Seasonal variations are well-understood. Operator succession has been planned with resource budgets in mind. The primary risk shifts from resource scarcity to resource complacency -- nodes have enough resources and stop tracking carefully, setting up a future surprise.

  • Years 30-50+: Hardware has been replaced multiple times. Power systems have been upgraded or rebuilt. The resource framework must have evolved with the technology while preserving its core function: making resource consumption visible and federation costs accountable. Future operators will read this document to understand why resource accounting exists, not just how to do it.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The most important resource in this framework is human attention, and it is the one most likely to be neglected in accounting. Technical people tend to measure what is easy to measure -- gigabytes, processor cycles, watt-hours -- and to treat human effort as an inexhaustible input. It is not. Every node in this federation is operated by a person or a small group of people who have lives, obligations, and limits. The resource framework must honor those limits explicitly, not just in principle.

I debated whether to include a mechanism for resource transfers between nodes -- a node with excess storage lending drives to a node that needs them, for example. I decided against it for the initial framework because resource transfers create dependencies and debts that complicate sovereignty. A node that depends on another node for storage is no longer fully sovereign. If resource transfers become necessary, they should be governed by explicit, time-limited agreements documented in institutional memory, not by the informal goodwill that erodes under pressure.

The 15% reserve requirement will feel conservative to operators who are accustomed to running systems at near-full capacity. In a resilient institution, spare capacity is not waste. It is the margin that absorbs the unexpected. An institution running at 100% capacity is an institution one surprise away from failure.
