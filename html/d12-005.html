D12-005 — Backup Strategy and Data Protection

Document ID
D12-005
Domain
12 -- Disaster Recovery
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D12-001, D12-004, D6-002, SEC-003, D11-001, CON-001
Depended Upon By
D12-006, D12-003, D12-008, D12-011. All recovery procedures that depend on backup availability.

Purpose

This article defines the institution's comprehensive backup strategy from the disaster recovery perspective. While D6-002 (Backup Doctrine) defines the operational mechanics of creating backups, this article defines the strategic architecture: what is backed up, why, how often, in how many copies, at what geographic separation, on what media, and with what verification schedule.

The distinction matters. D6-002 answers "how do I perform a backup." This article answers "what must the backup system accomplish to ensure the institution can survive every failure class identified in D12-004." The backup strategy is the single most important protective measure the institution possesses. Redundant hardware can be replaced. Damaged facilities can be relocated. Lost personnel can be succeeded. But lost data -- data that existed in no backup, or only in corrupted backups -- is gone permanently. The backup strategy is the barrier between recoverable failure and permanent loss.

This article is written with the assumption that the reader has already internalized D12-001's principle that a backup which has not been tested is not a backup but a hope. Every element of this strategy includes verification requirements.

Scope

In scope:

  • The three-location backup architecture and its rationale.
  • Backup frequency by data tier (Tier 1, Tier 2, Tier 3).
  • Media selection criteria for backup storage across decades.
  • Geographic separation requirements and off-site storage protocols.
  • The backup-of-backups principle: protecting against backup system failure.
  • Retention policies and rotation schedules.
  • Integration with the rebuild kit (D12-003).

Out of scope:

  • Backup creation procedures and command-level instructions (D6-002).
  • Backup verification procedures (D12-006).
  • Encryption key management for encrypted backups (SEC-003).
  • Hardware procurement for backup media (D11-002).

Background

3.1 The 3-2-1 Principle and Beyond

The standard backup rule is 3-2-1: three copies of data, on two different media types, with one copy off-site. This institution adopts 3-2-1 as a minimum and extends it to account for the specific constraints and risks of air-gapped, fifty-year operation.

The extensions are: geographic separation sufficient to survive a site-destroying event (not just "off-site" but "at a distance that makes simultaneous destruction implausible"), media diversity sufficient to survive the obsolescence of any single media type, and temporal depth sufficient to recover from corruption that propagates across multiple backup cycles before detection.

3.2 The Temporal Dimension of Backup

Most backup strategies focus on spatial redundancy: multiple copies in multiple places. This strategy adds temporal redundancy: multiple backup generations retained over time. The reason is D12-004's Compound C scenario -- silent corruption that propagates into backups before detection. If the institution keeps only the most recent backup and that backup contains corrupted data, the clean data is lost. If the institution retains older backup generations, it can recover clean data from before the corruption occurred, at the cost of losing changes made between the last clean backup and the discovery of corruption.

The retention policy in this article is designed to ensure that at least one clean backup generation survives even if corruption goes undetected for months.

3.3 Media Longevity and the Fifty-Year Problem

No currently manufactured storage medium has a proven lifespan of fifty years. Hard drives are rated for three to five years of continuous operation. SSDs retain data for one to ten years without power, depending on technology and temperature. Optical media (pressed, not burned) can last decades but has limited capacity. Magnetic tape can last fifteen to thirty years under ideal conditions. Flash media degrades without regular refresh.

This means the institution cannot create a backup today and expect it to be readable in 2076. The backup strategy must include regular media refresh: copying data to new media before the old media degrades. This is an operational commitment that spans the institution's entire lifetime.

System Model

4.1 The Three-Location Architecture

The institution maintains backup copies at three physically separated locations:

Location A (On-site, Zone 1): The primary backup. Stored on dedicated backup media within the institution's primary secure zone. Updated most frequently. Provides the fastest recovery time. Vulnerable to any event that destroys the primary site.

Location B (Near off-site, Zone 2 or separate structure): The secondary backup. Stored in a physically separate location from the primary site but within reasonable transport distance (same building but different room, separate outbuilding, or a nearby secure location). Updated less frequently than Location A. Provides protection against localized events (fire in the server room, water pipe burst) that do not affect the entire site.

Location C (Distant off-site, Zone 3): The tertiary backup. Stored at a geographic distance sufficient to survive any event that destroys the primary site (different building, different neighborhood, different city if practical). Updated least frequently. Provides protection against site-destroying events. This is the backup that feeds the rebuild kit (D12-003).

4.2 Backup Frequency by Tier and Location

Data TierLocation ALocation BLocation C
Tier 1 (Institutional memory: docs, logs, governance records)DailyWeeklyMonthly
Tier 2 (System config, active projects, automation)WeeklyMonthlyQuarterly
Tier 3 (Reference materials, research archives)MonthlyQuarterlyAnnually

4.3 Retention Policy

Retention ensures temporal depth for corruption recovery:

LocationRetention Schedule
Location ADaily backups: retain 7 days. Weekly backups: retain 4 weeks. Monthly backups: retain 6 months.
Location BWeekly backups: retain 8 weeks. Monthly backups: retain 12 months. Quarterly backups: retain 2 years.
Location CMonthly backups: retain 24 months. Quarterly backups: retain 5 years. Annual backups: retain indefinitely (or until media refresh cycle).

This retention policy means that if silent corruption is discovered, the institution can roll back to a backup from up to five years ago at Location C. The data created between that backup and the discovery of corruption may be lost, but the institution's core integrity is preserved.

4.4 Media Selection and Rotation

Backup media is selected based on four criteria: capacity (sufficient for the institution's current data volume plus growth margin), durability (rated lifespan under the institution's storage conditions), availability (can replacement media be procured over the coming decade), and compatibility (can the media be read without specialized equipment that may become unavailable).

Current recommended media:

  • Location A: Dedicated internal or external hard drive. Replaced every three years or at first sign of degradation (SMART monitoring).
  • Location B: External hard drive or high-quality USB solid-state drive. Stored powered-off when not in use. Refreshed every two years.
  • Location C: Two copies on different media types: one on external hard drive, one on high-capacity optical media (M-DISC or equivalent archival-grade) if data volume permits, or on a second hard drive of a different manufacturer. Refreshed annually during the annual operations cycle.

The dual-media requirement at Location C protects against media-type-specific failures: a manufacturing defect in a hard drive batch, a firmware vulnerability in a specific SSD model, or degradation patterns specific to a media technology.

4.5 Media Refresh Cycle

All backup media is subject to a refresh cycle: the data is read from the existing media, verified, and written to new media. The old media is securely wiped per SEC-003 and decommissioned. The refresh cycle runs on this schedule:

  • Location A: every 3 years (aligned with hardware replacement cycle).
  • Location B: every 2 years.
  • Location C: annually during the annual operations cycle.

4.6 Encryption and Access Control

All backup media is encrypted at rest per SEC-003. Location A uses the institution's primary disk encryption. Locations B and C use separate encryption keys (Tier B and Tier C keys per SEC-003) so that compromise of one location's key does not compromise the others. The encryption keys for off-site backups are stored separately from the backup media, per SEC-003 Section 4.3.

4.7 Integration with the Rebuild Kit

The Location C backup is the data component of the rebuild kit defined in D12-003. The rebuild kit also contains the encryption keys, printed documentation, and installation media needed to use the backup for a full institutional rebuild. The backup strategy and the rebuild kit must be maintained in tandem: any change to the backup format, encryption scheme, or media type at Location C must be reflected in the rebuild kit documentation and the printed recovery procedures.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D12-05-01: The institution must maintain at least three copies of all Tier 1 data at all times: one at each of the three locations. Temporary reduction to two copies (during media refresh or transport) is permitted for no more than 48 hours.
  • R-D12-05-02: At least one backup copy must be stored at a geographic distance sufficient that no single event (fire, flood, earthquake, storm) can destroy both the primary site and the backup location simultaneously.
  • R-D12-05-03: Backup media must be refreshed on the schedule defined in Section 4.5. Media past its refresh date is considered unreliable and must be refreshed immediately or replaced.
  • R-D12-05-04: Every backup must be encrypted at rest. The encryption keys for each location must be managed independently per SEC-003.
  • R-D12-05-05: Retention policies must be enforced. Old backups may only be purged when newer backups have been verified as intact and the retention period has expired.
  • R-D12-05-06: Changes to backup format, media type, or encryption scheme must be documented in the operational log and reflected in the rebuild kit (D12-003) within 7 days.
  • R-D12-05-07: The backup strategy must be reviewed during the biennial Domain 12 review cycle and after any real recovery event that relied on backups.

Failure Modes

  • Single-event multi-location loss. A disaster large enough to destroy both the primary site and Location B. This is why Location C exists at geographic distance. Mitigation: R-D12-05-02 requires geographic separation.
  • Media degradation undetected. Backup media at Location B or C degrades between refresh cycles. When needed, the backup is unreadable. Mitigation: the media refresh cycle (Section 4.5) and the backup verification procedures in D12-006.
  • Corruption propagation through backup generations. Silent data corruption on the primary system is faithfully backed up to all three locations, overwriting clean data with corrupted data. Mitigation: the retention policy (Section 4.3) preserves older generations. D6-003 integrity verification provides early detection.
  • Encryption key inaccessibility. The backup exists but the encryption key is lost or inaccessible. The data is intact but unreadable. Mitigation: SEC-003 defines tiered key backup with independent storage. Key recovery is tested in D12-002 Scenario 5.
  • Backup schedule drift. The operator falls behind on the backup schedule. Daily backups become weekly. Monthly transports to Location C become quarterly. Recovery point objectives silently degrade. Mitigation: the operational checklist (D10-002) includes backup verification. The quarterly drill cycle (D12-002) tests backup recency.
  • Media format obsolescence. The media type used for backups becomes unreadable because compatible hardware is no longer available. Mitigation: the media refresh cycle replaces media before obsolescence. D13-002 (Technology Migration) tracks media format viability.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If Location A backup is lost or corrupted: Restore from Location B. Verify the restored data against Location B checksums. Create a new Location A backup immediately from the current (verified) primary data or from Location B. Resume the normal backup schedule.
  2. If Location B backup is lost or corrupted: Create a new Location B backup from the current primary data and Location A backup. Verify. Store at Location B. Resume the normal schedule.
  3. If Location C backup is lost or corrupted: This is a high-priority emergency. Location C is the rebuild safety net. Create a new Location C backup from the most recent verified data. Transport it to the off-site location. Update the rebuild kit. Document the incident.
  4. If all three locations are compromised: This is the catastrophic scenario. If any backup generation at any location is still readable, restore from it. Accept the data loss between that generation and the present. If no backup is readable, the institution has suffered total data loss. Follow D12-003 recovery procedure 1 (rebuild from surviving knowledge and printed materials).
  5. If the backup schedule has drifted: Immediately perform a full backup at all three tiers. Update all three locations. Reset the schedule. Conduct a post-mortem: why did the schedule drift? Address the root cause (operator overload, broken automation, motivational failure).

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The backup strategy is established alongside the institution's infrastructure. Early challenges include selecting reliable media, establishing the off-site location, and integrating backups into the daily operational tempo. The first annual media refresh provides the first real test of media longevity assumptions.
  • Years 5-15: The strategy is routine. Lessons from the first media refresh cycles inform media selection. Data volume growth may require media capacity upgrades. The retention policy accumulates a deep history of backup generations, providing strong temporal depth.
  • Years 15-30: Media technology transitions become the dominant challenge. The media types selected at founding may no longer be manufactured. The media refresh cycle must bridge the gap: transitioning data from legacy media to current media without loss. This is the period where the backup strategy interacts most intensely with D13-002 (Technology Migration).
  • Years 30-50+: Multiple media technology transitions have occurred. The backup architecture (three locations, tiered frequency, temporal retention) remains stable even as the specific media change. The accumulated experience of managing decades of backups is itself institutional knowledge of high value.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The backup strategy is the article I revised most before finalizing. Every time I thought it was complete, I imagined another scenario: what if the house burns down while I am on vacation? (Location C.) What if bit rot corrupts a file and I do not notice for six months? (Retention policy.) What if the USB drive at Location B silently fails? (Media refresh cycle.) What if the encryption key at Location C is lost? (SEC-003 tiered key backup.)

Each question produced another layer of protection, and at some point I had to acknowledge that perfect protection is impossible. Some residual risk always remains. The goal is not zero risk but manageable risk: risk that is small enough that the institution can survive the worst realistic scenario with acceptable losses. "Acceptable" here means: the institution continues to exist and can rebuild what was lost, even if the rebuilding takes months.

The operational burden of this strategy is real. Daily Tier 1 backups, weekly rotations, monthly off-site transports, annual media refreshes. It is tedious work that produces no visible benefit in years when nothing goes wrong. The temptation to skip it will be strong. D12-001 warned about this temptation. The answer is the same: preparation is a daily practice, not a one-time event.
