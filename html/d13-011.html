D13-011 — Technology Horizon Scanning Without Internet Access

Document ID
D13-011
Title
Technology Horizon Scanning Without Internet Access
Domain
13 -- Evolution & Adaptation
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
ETH-001, CON-001, SEC-001, D13-001, D13-003, D13-010, D18-001
Depended Upon By
D13-003, D13-005. Referenced by D14-001 for research methodology in air-gapped environments.

Purpose

This article defines how the institution evaluates emerging technologies when it operates behind an air gap without persistent internet access. Technology horizon scanning -- the systematic monitoring of the technology landscape for changes that may affect the institution -- is essential to preventing the stagnation trap described in D13-001. But the air gap, which is the institution's primary security control, also limits access to information about the outside world. This article resolves that tension by establishing structured procedures for gathering, quarantining, evaluating, and acting on technology intelligence within the constraints of air-gapped operation.

Horizon scanning is not casual browsing. It is a disciplined intelligence-gathering activity with defined targets, defined schedules, defined evaluation criteria, and defined outputs. The institution does not need to know everything about the technology landscape. It needs to know whether any technology it depends on is approaching obsolescence, whether any superior alternative has matured to the point of viability, and whether any paradigm shift is emerging that could affect its foundational assumptions. These are specific, answerable questions that can be addressed through periodic, focused information acquisition.

Scope

In scope: - The horizon scanning cycle: planning, acquisition, quarantine, evaluation, and reporting. - Information sourcing strategies for air-gapped institutions. - The technology landscape report: format, content, and maintenance. - Evaluation frameworks for assessing emerging technologies against institutional requirements. - Controlled experimentation with new technologies within the air gap. - The relationship between horizon scanning and the hardware roadmap (D13-003). - Scanning frequency and resource allocation.

Out of scope: - Detailed procurement procedures (D11-001). - Import quarantine procedures (D18-001 handles the security aspects). - Research methodology for deep evaluation of specific technologies (D14-001). - Paradigm shift assessment (D13-005, though horizon scanning may trigger it).

Background

3.1 The Air-Gap Information Constraint

In a connected institution, technology awareness is ambient. News, updates, community discussions, and vendor announcements flow continuously through the internet. An air-gapped institution receives none of this ambient awareness. The technology landscape outside the air gap changes invisibly. Without deliberate scanning, the operator's knowledge of that landscape freezes at the moment the air gap was established and decays from there.

This decay is the primary risk that horizon scanning addresses. An operator who does not know that their storage technology has been superseded by something better and cheaper cannot plan a migration. An operator who does not know that their encryption algorithm has been compromised cannot assess their security posture. An operator who does not know that their operating system's community has fragmented cannot assess long-term support viability.

3.2 Information as a Controlled Import

In an air-gapped institution, information about the outside world is a controlled import, subject to the same principles as any other material crossing the air gap. D18-001 establishes the quarantine framework for imports. This article specifies what to import, when to import it, and how to evaluate it once it has been imported and cleared.

3.3 The Signal-to-Noise Problem

The technology landscape produces vastly more noise than signal. Marketing announcements, opinion pieces, hype cycles, and premature product launches vastly outnumber genuine technological advances. Horizon scanning must be disciplined enough to separate signal from noise, focusing on technologies that are relevant to the institution's needs, mature enough to be viable, and significant enough to warrant attention. The evaluation framework (Section 4.4) provides this discipline.

System Model

4.1 The Horizon Scanning Cycle

Horizon scanning operates on a semi-annual cycle with five phases:

Phase 1: Planning (1-2 weeks before external interaction). Before any information-gathering session, the operator reviews the scanning priorities. Priorities are derived from: the hardware roadmap (D13-003 Section 3), the format watch list (D13-008), the assumption registry (D13-010 Section 4.4), and any outstanding scanning tasks from the previous cycle. The output is a scanning brief: a short document listing the specific questions to be answered and the specific technology categories to be investigated.

Phase 2: Acquisition (during external interaction). During the scheduled external interaction (the controlled moment when the operator interacts with external systems), information is gathered according to the scanning brief. Sources include: vendor documentation downloaded to transfer media, independent review sites, technical specifications, community health indicators (commit frequency, contributor count, forum activity), and pricing and availability data. All collected material is placed on quarantine media per D18-001.

Phase 3: Quarantine and import (1-2 weeks after acquisition). The acquired material passes through the standard import quarantine process. Executable content is excluded. Document content is sanitized per D18-001 procedures. Cleared material is transferred to the institutional library.

Phase 4: Evaluation (2-4 weeks after import). The cleared material is evaluated against the institutional requirements using the framework in Section 4.4. Each technology category in the scanning brief receives an assessment: current landscape status, relevance to the institution, maturity level, and recommended action.

Phase 5: Reporting (at end of evaluation). The evaluation results are recorded in the technology landscape report (Section 4.3). Recommended actions are translated into tasks: updates to the hardware roadmap, additions to the format watch list, initiation of technology migration evaluations, or triggering of the paradigm shift assessment framework.

4.2 Information Sourcing Strategies

The following information sources are appropriate for air-gapped horizon scanning:

  • Vendor release notes and roadmaps: Downloaded as PDF or plain text during external interactions. Provide authoritative information about specific products.
  • Independent review aggregators: Sites that compile reviews from multiple sources. Provide broader perspective than vendor materials alone.
  • Open-source project metrics: For open-source technologies, project health indicators (commit frequency, release cadence, contributor count, issue resolution time) are publicly available and can be captured during external interactions.
  • Technical specification documents: Standards bodies publish specifications for data formats, protocols, and interfaces. These are primary sources for format assessment.
  • Printed technical publications: Books, magazines, and technical papers that can be physically transported across the air gap without any digital quarantine concerns.
  • Trusted human sources: Conversations with knowledgeable individuals during external interactions. Findings from such conversations should be recorded in writing promptly.

4.3 The Technology Landscape Report

The technology landscape report is a living document, updated after each scanning cycle. It is organized by technology category (compute hardware, storage, operating systems, data formats, encryption, power systems) and for each category contains:

  • Current institutional choice: What the institution currently uses in this category.
  • Landscape status: What alternatives exist, what the current state of the art is, what trends are visible.
  • Institutional relevance: How relevant changes in this category are to the institution. High, medium, or low.
  • Risk assessment: Is the institution's current choice approaching obsolescence? Is supply chain continuity at risk?
  • Recommended action: Continue monitoring, begin evaluation, initiate migration planning, or trigger paradigm shift assessment.
  • Last updated: Date of the most recent assessment for this category.

4.4 The Technology Evaluation Framework

When a specific technology is identified as a candidate for adoption (either as a replacement for an existing technology or as a new capability), it is evaluated against institutional requirements using these criteria:

CriterionWeightAssessment Method
Reliability and maturityCriticalYears in production, known failure modes, community size
Open format / open sourceCriticalLicense review, specification availability
Air-gap compatibilityCriticalCan it be installed, configured, and maintained without internet?
Power efficiencyHighWattage under load, idle power consumption
MaintainabilityHighDocumentation quality, spare parts availability, community support
Migration path from currentHighDoes a clear migration path exist from the current technology?
Longevity projectionHighExpected market presence, backward compatibility track record
Operator skill requirementsMediumLearning curve, availability of training materials
PerformanceMediumMeets operational requirements (not necessarily best-in-class)
CostLowWithin institutional budget (D11-001)

Technologies that fail any critical criterion are disqualified. Technologies that meet all criteria are ranked by their combined assessment and recommended for further evaluation or adoption.

4.5 Controlled Experimentation

When horizon scanning identifies a promising technology, controlled experimentation may be conducted within the air gap. Controlled experimentation means installing the technology on an isolated, non-production system and testing it against specific evaluation criteria. The experiment is documented with: objective, setup procedure, test cases, results, and recommendation. Controlled experiments must not affect production systems and must follow D18-001 quarantine procedures for any imported software or firmware.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-THS-01: Horizon scanning must be conducted at least twice per year, aligned with scheduled external interactions. Each scanning cycle must have a documented scanning brief prepared in advance.
  • R-THS-02: The technology landscape report must be updated after each scanning cycle. Categories not updated in the current cycle must retain their previous assessment with an unchanged "last updated" date.
  • R-THS-03: All information acquired during scanning must pass through the D18-001 quarantine process before entering the institutional library. No exceptions.
  • R-THS-04: Controlled experiments (Section 4.5) must be conducted on isolated, non-production systems. Experimental software must not be installed on production hardware.
  • R-THS-05: If horizon scanning has not produced any outputs (no landscape report updates, no recommended actions) for 18 months, this is a decay signal requiring immediate remediation per D13-001 Section 7.4.
  • R-THS-06: Scanning priorities must include at minimum: every technology category where the current institutional choice has a lifecycle status of "deprecated" or worse, and every category flagged on the format watch list (D13-008).
  • R-THS-07: The technology evaluation framework (Section 4.4) must be used for all candidate technologies. Evaluations must be documented and preserved as Tier 2 data artifacts.

Failure Modes

  • Scanning starvation. External interactions are consumed by other priorities. Scanning is deferred cycle after cycle. The technology landscape report becomes stale. Mitigation: R-THS-01 mandates twice-yearly scanning. R-THS-05 detects scanning decay at 18 months.
  • Information overload. The operator collects too much material during acquisition, overwhelming the evaluation phase. Material sits unreviewed. Mitigation: the scanning brief (Phase 1) focuses acquisition on specific questions. Better to answer three questions well than to collect material for thirty questions and evaluate none.
  • Hype susceptibility. The operator is swayed by marketing materials and evaluates a technology more favorably than it deserves. Mitigation: the evaluation framework (Section 4.4) weights reliability and maturity as critical criteria. A technology with impressive specifications but limited production track record will fail these criteria.
  • Stale landscape report. Categories in the report are not updated for years. The assessments become misleading. Mitigation: R-THS-02 requires the report to be updated each cycle. Categories with stale assessments are visually flagged by their "last updated" date.
  • Experimentation escape. Experimental software accidentally contaminates production systems. Mitigation: R-THS-04 mandates physical isolation of experimental systems. D18-001 quarantine procedures apply to experimental software.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If scanning has been starved for more than 18 months: Dedicate the next external interaction entirely to scanning. Focus on critical categories first: any technology approaching end-of-life, any category on the format watch list, and any category where the assumption registry indicates a foundational assumption may be weakening. Accept that the first recovery scan will be incomplete and schedule follow-up scans at shorter intervals until the landscape report is current.
  2. If the technology landscape report is severely outdated: Do not attempt to update all categories at once. Prioritize by risk: categories where the institution's current choice is oldest or where the technology landscape is most volatile. Update the highest-risk categories first and schedule the remainder for subsequent scanning cycles.
  3. If a hype-driven evaluation led to a poor adoption decision: This becomes a technology migration issue. If the adopted technology is already in production, assess whether it can be stabilized and maintained or whether it should be sunset per D13-004. Document the evaluation failure and update the evaluation framework to better filter for hype in future assessments.
  4. If experimental software has contaminated production: Treat as a security incident per SEC-001. Isolate the affected systems. Assess whether data integrity has been compromised. If production data is at risk, initiate disaster recovery procedures per D12-001. Document the contamination path and reinforce the isolation requirements for future experiments.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Horizon scanning establishes baseline awareness. The technology landscape report is populated for the first time. The institution's information sourcing channels are identified and tested. Controlled experiments are conducted on founding-era alternatives to build evaluation experience.
  • Years 5-15: Horizon scanning has detected the first significant changes in the technology landscape. Some categories have shifted substantially from the founding baseline. The scanning process has been refined based on experience -- the operator knows which sources are most reliable, which evaluation criteria are most discriminating, and what pace of scanning is sustainable.
  • Years 15-30: The technology landscape report has become a historical document as well as a planning tool, showing how each technology category has evolved over time. The information sourcing strategies may have changed as external information channels evolve. The evaluation framework has been refined by decades of assessments.
  • Years 30-50+: Horizon scanning is a mature, routine institutional function. The operator draws on decades of landscape reports and evaluation records. The patterns of technology evolution -- how quickly categories turn over, which criteria best predict longevity, what sources provide the most reliable intelligence -- are documented institutional knowledge that informs ever-better scanning and evaluation.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The air gap is a security blessing and an information curse. It protects the institution from external threats but also blocks the continuous technology awareness that connected institutions take for granted. Horizon scanning is my compromise: deliberate, periodic peeks outside the wall, with specific questions and structured evaluation. I am particularly conscious of the hype susceptibility failure mode. When you only look at the technology landscape twice a year, everything seems exciting and new. The discipline of the evaluation framework is my defense: reliability and maturity outweigh features and marketing. I would rather adopt a boring technology that has been in production for five years than an exciting technology that launched last month. The boring technology has proven itself. The exciting technology is a promise.
