D4-007 — Hardware Selection Criteria

Document ID
D4-007
Domain
4 -- Infrastructure & Power
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D4-001, CON-001, SEC-001
Depended Upon By
D4-004 (Hardware Lifecycle), D4-012 (Spare Parts), D4-010 (Decommissioning), Domain 5 (Platform runs on selected hardware)

Purpose

This article defines the criteria by which all hardware is evaluated, selected, and approved for use within the holm.chat Documentation Institution. Hardware is the physical substrate of the institution. Every file stored, every service running, every backup maintained depends on physical components that were chosen by someone, at some point, for some reason. This article ensures those reasons are explicit, defensible, and aligned with the institution's fifty-year horizon.

The institution's relationship with hardware is fundamentally different from a conventional organization's. A conventional organization replaces hardware on a three-to-five year cycle driven by vendor support timelines and performance benchmarks. This institution replaces hardware when it fails or when it can no longer perform its function adequately. The difference is not merely economic. It is philosophical: the institution values understanding over novelty, repairability over performance, and longevity over specification sheets.

This article is written for the operator who must decide what to buy, when to buy it, and how to evaluate whether a given piece of hardware belongs inside the institution's physical perimeter. It provides concrete evaluation criteria, a scoring framework, and a set of absolute disqualifiers that no amount of performance can override.

Scope

This article covers:

  • Evaluation criteria for all categories of hardware: computing (servers, workstations), storage (drives, media), networking (switches, cables, transceivers), power (UPS units, inverters, charge controllers), and environmental (sensors, cooling equipment).
  • The hierarchy of selection priorities: repairability, understandability, availability, longevity, and then performance.
  • Absolute disqualifiers: characteristics that exclude hardware from consideration regardless of other merits.
  • The procurement process: how hardware is researched, evaluated, approved, and acquired.
  • Supply chain considerations: provenance, trustworthiness, and the avoidance of single-source dependencies.
  • Documentation requirements for every hardware acquisition.

This article does not cover:

  • Specific hardware models or brands (those are operational decisions that change over time; this article provides the criteria for making those decisions).
  • Software or firmware that runs on the hardware (see Domain 5).
  • Hardware lifecycle management after acquisition (see D4-004).
  • Physical site requirements for housing hardware (see D4-001).

Background

The consumer electronics industry optimizes for a three-year replacement cycle. Laptops are designed to be difficult to repair. Servers are designed to be replaced as units. Storage drives are designed with planned obsolescence in firmware update policies. This model is incompatible with a fifty-year institution.

The institution cannot control what the hardware industry manufactures. But it can control what it purchases. By establishing rigorous selection criteria and adhering to them without exception, the institution builds a hardware estate that is understandable, maintainable, and resilient. The cost of discipline at the point of purchase is repaid many times over during the decades of operation that follow.

Historical precedent supports this approach. The most reliable computing installations in history -- early IBM mainframe sites, military hardened installations, scientific observatories in remote locations -- all shared a common trait: they selected hardware for longevity and maintainability, not for peak performance. The institution follows this tradition deliberately.

CON-001 establishes that the institution must be self-built, meaning the operator understands every component. This mandate directly constrains hardware selection: if the operator cannot understand how a piece of hardware works, it cannot be used, regardless of its specifications. SEC-001 further constrains selection by requiring that hardware not introduce unverifiable trust dependencies -- no black-box components whose behavior cannot be audited.

System Model

7.1 The Selection Priority Hierarchy

When evaluating hardware, the following priorities apply in strict order. A higher-priority criterion always overrides a lower-priority one:

  1. Repairability. Can the operator diagnose and fix common failures without returning the unit to the manufacturer? Are replacement parts available from multiple sources? Can individual components (fans, power supplies, memory, drives) be replaced independently? Hardware that must be replaced as a unit when any component fails is scored lower than hardware with modular, replaceable components.
  2. Understandability. Is the hardware's design documented? Are schematics, block diagrams, or at least detailed technical specifications available? Does the operator understand what every component does and why it is there? Hardware with proprietary, undocumented subsystems is scored lower than hardware with transparent designs. Open-hardware designs (where schematics are publicly available) receive the highest scores.
  3. Availability. Can the hardware (or compatible replacements) be obtained from multiple vendors? Is it a standard form factor that will remain available for years? Hardware that is available from a single vendor, or that uses a proprietary form factor, is scored lower. Standard form factors (ATX, standard rack-mount, standard drive bays) are preferred.
  4. Longevity. What is the expected operational lifespan? Is the hardware designed for continuous operation (server-grade) or intermittent use (consumer-grade)? Server-grade and industrial-grade components are preferred over consumer-grade equivalents. Components with longer mean time between failures (MTBF) ratings are preferred.
  5. Performance. Does the hardware meet the institution's functional requirements? Performance is evaluated last because the institution's workload -- storing, indexing, serving, and backing up text documents -- is not computationally demanding. Almost any modern server-grade hardware vastly exceeds the institution's performance requirements. Performance is relevant only when all higher-priority criteria are satisfied equally by competing options.

7.2 Absolute Disqualifiers

The following characteristics disqualify hardware from use within the institution, regardless of any other merits:

  • Unremovable wireless capability. Any hardware with a wireless radio (WiFi, Bluetooth, cellular, NFC) that cannot be physically removed or permanently disabled at the hardware level is disqualified. Software-disable is not sufficient (per R-D4-02 and SEC-001).
  • Proprietary storage interfaces. Hardware that stores institutional data on media that can only be read by that specific hardware (or by software from a single vendor) is disqualified. All storage must use standard interfaces and standard formats readable by multiple vendors' hardware.
  • Cloud dependency. Hardware that requires an internet connection to function, to be configured, or to receive firmware updates is disqualified. The institution is air-gapped; hardware that cannot function without phoning home is incompatible by definition.
  • Non-replaceable critical components. Hardware where the failure of a soldered-on, non-replaceable component (such as soldered RAM or soldered storage) renders the entire unit unusable is disqualified for primary institutional roles. Such hardware may be used in non-critical, easily replaceable roles only.
  • Tamper-evident seal requirements. Hardware that voids its functionality (not merely its warranty) when opened for inspection or repair is disqualified. The operator must be able to inspect and repair any hardware without the hardware retaliating.

7.3 Category-Specific Criteria

Computing hardware (servers and workstations):

  • ECC (Error-Correcting Code) memory is required for all systems that store or process institutional data. Non-ECC memory is acceptable only for the quarantine machine and non-critical workstations.
  • Standard motherboard form factors (ATX, Micro-ATX, or standard server form factors) are required. Proprietary form factors are disqualified.
  • The BIOS/UEFI must be configurable to disable all wireless interfaces, all remote management interfaces not needed by the institution, and all boot options except the intended boot device.
  • Preference is given to hardware that supports open-source firmware (such as coreboot or Libreboot), though this is not an absolute requirement.

Storage hardware (drives and media):

  • Enterprise-grade or NAS-grade drives are required for all storage roles. Desktop-grade drives are acceptable only as temporary replacements while enterprise-grade replacements are procured.
  • Standard interfaces (SATA, SAS, NVMe with standard M.2 or U.2 form factors) are required.
  • Drives must support S.M.A.R.T. monitoring for health tracking.
  • For archival storage, media with documented longevity characteristics is required. Optical media (M-DISC or equivalent) is preferred for cold archives due to its physical stability over decades.

Networking hardware (switches, cables):

  • Managed switches are preferred over unmanaged switches for their monitoring and diagnostic capabilities.
  • Switches must not have wireless capability of any kind.
  • Standard Ethernet cabling (Cat6 or Cat6a) is required. Proprietary cabling systems are disqualified.
  • Switches with open or well-documented management interfaces are preferred over those requiring proprietary management software.

Power hardware (UPS, inverters, charge controllers):

  • UPS units must use replaceable batteries. UPS units with non-replaceable battery packs are disqualified.
  • Pure sine wave output is required for all UPS units powering computing equipment. Modified sine wave UPS units are disqualified for computing loads.
  • Charge controllers and inverters for solar systems must use standard communication protocols for monitoring (e.g., Modbus, CAN bus). Proprietary-only monitoring interfaces are discouraged.

7.4 The Procurement Process

All hardware acquisitions follow this process:

  1. Need identification. The operator identifies a hardware need: a new capability, a replacement for aging hardware, or a spare for inventory. The need is documented with a justification.
  2. Research. The operator researches available options. At least three candidate products are identified for any significant acquisition. For each candidate, the operator documents: specifications, repairability assessment, availability of spare parts, vendor reputation, and price.
  3. Evaluation against criteria. Each candidate is evaluated against the Selection Priority Hierarchy (Section 7.1) and checked against the Absolute Disqualifiers (Section 7.2). The evaluation is documented.
  4. Decision. The operator selects the candidate that best satisfies the criteria. If no candidate passes the Absolute Disqualifiers, the procurement is deferred until a qualifying product is found, or the need is re-evaluated.
  5. Acquisition. The hardware is purchased. The purchase is recorded in the hardware register (see D4-013) with: date, vendor, model, serial number, cost, intended role, and evaluation summary.
  6. Verification. On receipt, the hardware is inspected for physical damage, tested for basic functionality, and verified against the documented specifications. Wireless hardware is physically disabled or removed before the unit enters the institutional environment.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D4-10: No Black-Box Hardware. Every piece of hardware in the institution must have sufficient documentation for the operator to understand its function, diagnose common failures, and perform component-level repairs where physically possible. Hardware whose internal operation is undocumented and opaque is prohibited for critical institutional roles.
  • R-D4-11: Multi-Source Availability. For every hardware component in a critical role, there must be at least two sources from which a compatible replacement can be obtained. Single-source hardware is permitted only in non-critical roles and only when documented as a known risk.
  • R-D4-12: Pre-Acquisition Evaluation Required. No hardware may be introduced into the institutional environment without completing the procurement process defined in Section 7.4. Emergency acquisitions (replacing a failed component from the nearest available source) must be retroactively evaluated within 30 days and replaced if they do not meet criteria.
  • R-D4-13: Standard Interfaces Only. All data-carrying interfaces must use open, documented standards. Proprietary interfaces for data storage, data transfer, or power delivery are prohibited in critical roles.
  • R-D4-14: Wireless Removal Before Deployment. All wireless radios must be physically removed or permanently disabled at the hardware level before any hardware enters the institutional network. This verification is part of the acquisition verification step and is documented in the hardware register.

Failure Modes

  • Criteria drift. Over time, the operator relaxes the selection criteria because qualifying hardware becomes harder to find or more expensive. The institution gradually accumulates hardware that is harder to repair, less well-documented, or dependent on single sources. Mitigation: the criteria in this article are mandatory, not advisory. Every acquisition is evaluated in writing. Deviations require a formal exception documented in the decision log with a remediation plan.
  • Supply chain disruption. A critical hardware component becomes unavailable from all sources. The institution cannot replace a failed unit. Mitigation: the spare parts inventory (D4-012) maintains at least one spare for every critical component. The annual hardware review identifies components approaching end-of-availability and triggers pre-emptive stockpiling.
  • Vendor obsolescence. A hardware vendor discontinues a product line, ending availability of spare parts and documentation. Mitigation: the preference for standard form factors and standard interfaces means that vendor-specific dependencies are minimized. When a vendor exits, the institution transitions to alternative vendors whose products meet the same criteria.
  • Specification mismatch. Hardware that met all selection criteria proves inadequate for its intended role in practice (performance too low, compatibility issues, unexpected failure patterns). Mitigation: all new hardware types undergo a 30-day evaluation period in a non-critical role before being assigned to a critical function. Issues discovered during evaluation are documented and the hardware is replaced if necessary.
  • Counterfeit components. Hardware purchased from third-party sources contains counterfeit components (fake capacitors, relabeled drives, refurbished units sold as new). Mitigation: purchase from authorized distributors when possible. Verify serial numbers against manufacturer databases. Inspect hardware on receipt for signs of refurbishment or tampering. Test drives against their rated specifications before trusting them with institutional data.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If critical hardware fails and no qualifying replacement is available: Deploy the spare from inventory (per D4-012). If no spare is available, identify the closest qualifying hardware available for immediate purchase. If no qualifying hardware is available at all, deploy the best available non-qualifying hardware as a temporary measure, document it as an exception, and begin an intensive search for qualifying replacement hardware. The exception must be reviewed monthly until resolved.
  2. If hardware is discovered to have an undisclosed wireless capability: Immediately isolate the hardware from the institutional network. Physically inspect the unit to locate and remove or disable the wireless component. If the wireless component cannot be physically disabled, the hardware is decommissioned and replaced. Document the incident and update the evaluation for that hardware model to prevent recurrence.
  3. If a supply chain is found to be compromised (counterfeit components): Identify all hardware acquired through the compromised source. Test each unit against its rated specifications. Replace any unit that fails testing. Add the compromised source to a prohibited vendors list. Document the incident and the remediation in the decision log.
  4. If the procurement process was bypassed (emergency acquisition): The retroactive evaluation must be completed within 30 days. If the hardware does not meet criteria, a replacement acquisition is initiated immediately. The non-qualifying hardware is either reassigned to a non-critical role (if it passes the Absolute Disqualifiers) or decommissioned (if it does not).

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The initial hardware estate is established. The operator learns which manufacturers, distributors, and product lines best meet the selection criteria. The evaluation process is refined based on practical experience. A trusted vendor list emerges organically from successful acquisitions.
  • Years 5-15: The first generation of hardware reaches end-of-life. The replacement cycle tests the selection criteria against a changed marketplace. New hardware categories may emerge (new storage technologies, new processor architectures). The criteria in Section 7.1 remain stable; the category-specific criteria in Section 7.3 are updated to reflect new technologies while preserving the priority hierarchy.
  • Years 15-30: Major technology transitions may occur. Current standard interfaces (SATA, standard Ethernet) may be superseded. The institution must navigate these transitions without losing access to existing data or creating vendor lock-in. The principle of standard interfaces ensures that transitions happen gradually, with bridge hardware available during overlap periods.
  • Years 30-50: The hardware landscape will be unrecognizable compared to the founding era. The specific criteria in Section 7.3 will have been revised many times. The priority hierarchy in Section 7.1 -- repairability, understandability, availability, longevity, then performance -- will not have changed. These are not technology-specific values. They are institutional values expressed through hardware choices, and they endure because the institution's nature endures.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry:

The hardest part of hardware selection is not finding good hardware. It is saying no to hardware that is almost good enough. A server with soldered RAM that otherwise has perfect specifications. A switch with excellent management features but also an embedded wireless radio that "can just be disabled in software." A drive with outstanding performance ratings but available from only one vendor.

Each of these represents a small compromise. And each small compromise becomes a dependency that the institution carries for years or decades. The soldered RAM fails in year seven and the entire motherboard must be replaced. The switch's wireless radio is re-enabled by a firmware update that was applied without checking. The sole-source drive vendor exits the market and there is no compatible replacement.

The criteria in this article are strict because the consequences of lax criteria compound over time. A conventional organization can absorb these compromises because it replaces its entire hardware estate every few years. This institution cannot. Every piece of hardware the institution acquires may be in service for a decade or more. Choose accordingly.
