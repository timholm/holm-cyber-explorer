GOV-011 — External Relations Policy

Document ID
GOV-011
Domain
2 -- Governance & Authority
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
ETH-001, CON-001, GOV-001, SEC-001
Depended Upon By
SEC-004. Referenced by any article involving communication with entities outside the institution.

Purpose

This article defines the governance of any interaction between the holm.chat Documentation Institution and entities outside the institution. It answers the questions: Who may authorize external contact? What information may be shared? Under what circumstances? What records must be kept?

An air-gapped, single-operator institution might seem to have no external relations. This is an illusion. The operator exists in the world. They purchase hardware from vendors. They may consult with technical contacts when facing problems. They interact with estate planners regarding succession. They exist within legal jurisdictions that may compel disclosure. Each of these interactions is an external relation, and each carries risk.

The risk is not paranoia. It is a practical concern about information boundaries. The institution's security model (SEC-001) depends on controlled information flow. Every external interaction is a potential information leak -- not through malice, necessarily, but through the natural tendency to share context when explaining a problem, to mention capabilities when seeking help, to reveal architecture when purchasing components. These small disclosures accumulate. Over years, they can erode the information boundaries that the security model depends upon.

This article establishes a framework for managing external interactions that balances the institution's need for isolation with its practical need to exist in the world. Complete isolation is impossible and undesirable. But undisciplined external interaction is dangerous. The framework finds the middle ground.

Scope

This article covers:

  • Categories of external interaction: commercial (purchases, services), advisory (technical consultation), legal (compliance, estate planning), social (conversations about the institution), and compelled (legal demands for information).
  • Information classification for external sharing: what may be shared freely, what may be shared with caution, and what may never be shared.
  • Authorization requirements for each category of interaction.
  • Record-keeping requirements for external interactions.
  • The relationship between external relations policy and the security model.
  • Protocols for handling unsolicited external contact (inquiries about the institution from unknown parties).

This article does not cover the technical implementation of information boundaries (Domain 3), the physical security of the institution against external intrusion (Domain 4), or the specific protocols for importing external data into the institution (Domain 18). It covers the governance layer: who decides what is shared, and how those decisions are made and recorded.

Background

Every institution that values its privacy eventually discovers that privacy is not a state but a practice. You do not have privacy; you maintain it. Every interaction with the outside world is an opportunity for privacy to erode, and the erosion is almost always gradual and initially imperceptible.

Consider a simple example: the operator purchases a replacement hard drive. The vendor knows nothing about the institution. But the purchase record reveals that someone at a particular address bought a particular model of drive on a particular date. Over years, repeated purchases reveal a pattern: the type of hardware, the frequency of upgrades, the scale of storage. To a determined adversary, this pattern is informative. To a casual observer, it is meaningless. The external relations policy must protect against both, with appropriate proportionality.

The more consequential interactions are advisory and legal. When the operator consults a technical expert about a storage problem, the natural inclination is to describe the problem in full context. But the full context includes information about the institution's architecture, its data volume, its redundancy model, and its failure history. Each of these details, shared individually, is benign. Shared in aggregate, they form a reasonably complete picture of the institution's infrastructure.

This article does not require the operator to live in paranoid isolation. It requires them to think before sharing. It provides a framework for that thinking: categories of information, rules for each category, and a habit of recording what was shared with whom. The goal is not zero external contact. The goal is deliberate external contact, where every interaction is a conscious choice rather than an unconsidered reflex.

System Model

4.1 Information Classification for External Sharing

All institutional information falls into one of three sharing categories:

Category Green: Shareable. Information that may be shared with external parties without specific authorization. This includes: the existence of the institution (in general terms), the operator's role as someone who maintains a personal data archive, general technical preferences (e.g., preference for a particular operating system or filesystem), and publicly available technical information that happens to be relevant to the institution's operations.

Category Amber: Share with Caution. Information that may be shared with specific external parties for specific purposes, but only with deliberate consideration and documentation. This includes: the institution's storage capacity (approximate, not exact), the types of data stored (categories, not specifics), the general architecture (e.g., "I use a redundant storage system" without specifying the exact configuration), and the institution's physical location (to the extent necessary for deliveries or services, but not more precisely than needed).

Amber information requires the operator to pause before sharing and ask: "Do I need to share this specific detail? Would a less specific version serve the same purpose?" If a less specific version would suffice, use it.

Category Red: Never Share. Information that must not be shared with any external party under any non-compelled circumstances. This includes: the institution's specific security model and configuration, the contents of the decision log, the specific contents of any institutional data, the identity of any designated successor (without the successor's consent), encryption keys, passwords, or any security credentials, and the exact physical configuration of institutional hardware.

Red information is shared only under legal compulsion (see Section 4.5), and even then, the minimum necessary disclosure principle applies.

4.2 Categories of External Interaction

Commercial Interactions. Purchases, subscriptions, warranty claims, and service arrangements. These are routine and require no specific authorization beyond the operator's normal authority. The operator should use Category Green information only and should minimize the amount of context provided. When purchasing hardware, the operator does not need to explain what the hardware will be used for.

Record-keeping: maintain a log of significant purchases (hardware, software licenses) including date, vendor, item, and any information shared beyond the basic transaction.

Advisory Interactions. Consultations with technical experts, fellow enthusiasts, or online communities about problems or techniques relevant to the institution. These require conscious information management. The operator may share Category Green information freely and Category Amber information when necessary to receive useful advice.

Record-keeping: when Category Amber information is shared in an advisory interaction, record the date, the recipient (by name or description), the specific information shared, and the purpose. This record is not about distrust; it is about maintaining awareness of the institution's information footprint.

Legal Interactions. Consultations with attorneys, estate planners, accountants, or other professional advisors. These often require sharing Category Amber information and, in some cases, information that approaches Category Red (estate planning may require disclosure of what the institution contains and how to access it). Legal interactions should be conducted with professionals bound by professional confidentiality obligations.

Record-keeping: document the interaction, the professional's identity, the scope of information shared, and any confidentiality agreements in place. Legal interactions involving succession planning should be cross-referenced with GOV-004.

Social Interactions. Casual conversations with friends, family, or acquaintances about the institution. These are the most common and the most difficult to govern, because they happen informally and without planning. The operator should default to Category Green information in social contexts. The temptation to explain or show off should be resisted.

Record-keeping: no formal record-keeping is required for social interactions that stay within Category Green. If Category Amber information is shared socially (it happens), the operator should note this in their next decision log entry as a matter of awareness.

Compelled Interactions. Legal demands for information, including subpoenas, court orders, regulatory inquiries, or law enforcement requests. These are governed by the legal jurisdiction in which the institution operates and may require disclosure of Category Red information.

Record-keeping: all compelled disclosures must be fully documented in the decision log, including: the specific legal authority cited, the scope of information demanded, the scope of information actually provided, any legal counsel consulted, and the date and manner of disclosure.

4.4 Unsolicited External Contact

If an unknown party contacts the operator specifically about the institution (rather than about the operator personally), the following protocol applies:

  1. Do not confirm or deny the institution's existence in specific terms.
  2. Assess the nature of the inquiry. Is it casual curiosity, commercial solicitation, legal process, or something else?
  3. If legal process: consult legal counsel before responding.
  4. If any other type: respond with Category Green information only, or decline to respond.
  5. Record the contact in the decision log: date, nature of contact, information provided (if any), and assessment of risk.

4.5 Compelled Disclosure Protocol

When faced with a legal demand for institutional information:

  1. Do not panic. Legal demands have timelines. You have time to respond properly.
  2. Verify the demand. Confirm that it is genuine, properly served, and within the jurisdiction's authority.
  3. Consult legal counsel. This is one of the few situations where seeking external professional advice is not optional but required.
  4. Apply minimum necessary disclosure. Provide what is legally required and nothing more. Do not volunteer context, explanation, or additional information.
  5. Document everything. The decision log entry for a compelled disclosure should be the most detailed entry the institution ever creates.
  6. Assess the impact. After the compelled disclosure, review the security implications. Has the information disclosed compromised any aspect of the security model? If so, initiate appropriate remediation through SEC-001 procedures.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-GOV-11-01: Category Red information may not be shared voluntarily with any external party. Sharing Red information is permitted only under legal compulsion or through the Critical Decision process (GOV-008) when the sharing is necessary for succession planning or similar institutional purposes.
  • R-GOV-11-02: All sharing of Category Amber information must be deliberate, not incidental. The operator must consciously decide to share before sharing, not realize afterward that they shared more than intended.
  • R-GOV-11-03: Compelled disclosures must be preceded by consultation with legal counsel. If legal counsel is not immediately available, the operator should request the maximum permissible time extension from the compelling authority.
  • R-GOV-11-04: The external relations log must be reviewed during the quarterly governance health check (GOV-007). The review should assess whether information sharing patterns have changed, whether any Amber disclosures were unnecessary in retrospect, and whether the information classification categories need adjustment.
  • R-GOV-11-05: No external party may be granted access to institutional systems, whether physical or digital, except through the import/quarantine procedures in Domain 18 (for data) or the emergency provisions in GOV-005 (for crisis assistance).
  • R-GOV-11-06: The operator's personal identity and the institution's identity must be kept as separate as practically possible. The operator is a person who does many things. The institution is one of those things. External parties should not be given reason to equate the two beyond what is necessary for a specific interaction.

Failure Modes

  • Information creep. Over time, the operator shares small amounts of Amber information with many different parties, each individual disclosure being insignificant but the aggregate forming a substantial picture. Mitigation: the quarterly review of the external relations log (R-GOV-11-04), which provides a periodic opportunity to assess the cumulative effect of individual disclosures.
  • Social pressure leaks. Friends or family ask about the institution, and the operator shares more than Category Green to satisfy curiosity or to impress. Mitigation: advance preparation. The operator should have a prepared Category Green description of the institution that is accurate, satisfying, and complete enough to end casual inquiry. Practice it.
  • Vendor over-sharing. When seeking technical support or warranty service, the operator provides more context than necessary. "My RAID array is degraded" becomes a detailed description of the entire storage architecture. Mitigation: conscious discipline. Before contacting a vendor, decide what information is necessary for the interaction and share only that.
  • Legal panic disclosure. Faced with a legal demand, the operator provides everything demanded without consulting counsel, out of fear or a desire to comply quickly. Mitigation: R-GOV-11-03, which makes legal counsel consultation mandatory, and the compelled disclosure protocol in Section 4.5, which emphasizes that legal demands have timelines.
  • Isolation extremism. The operator becomes so protective of institutional information that they refuse to engage in necessary external interactions (purchasing hardware, consulting experts, planning succession). The institution's operations suffer from the operator's excessive caution. Mitigation: the information classification system itself, which explicitly identifies what can be shared freely. Not everything is sensitive. The framework should liberate as much as it constrains.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If Category Red information has been inadvertently disclosed: Assess the scope of the disclosure. Determine who received the information and what they could do with it. If the disclosure compromises the security model, initiate SEC-001 remediation procedures. Record the incident in the decision log. Revise the external relations practices to prevent recurrence.
  2. If the external relations log has not been maintained: Reconstruct what can be reconstructed. For the past twelve months, identify all significant external interactions and document them retroactively. Going forward, integrate the logging habit into the operator's routine. Consider adding external interaction logging to the weekly operations checklist.
  3. If information creep is discovered: Conduct a comprehensive review of all documented external interactions. Map the aggregate picture that an attentive external party could assemble. If the aggregate picture is unacceptably revealing, consider whether any operational changes are needed (using different vendors, reducing advisory consultations, or changing communication patterns).
  4. If a compelled disclosure has been handled improperly: Consult legal counsel retroactively. Assess whether additional disclosure is required or whether the improper handling has legal consequences. Document the incident and the corrective actions. Revise the compelled disclosure protocol if the failure reveals a gap.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: External relations are simple: the operator is the only point of contact, and interactions are limited to purchases, occasional advisory consultations, and estate planning. The information classification categories should be tested against real interactions to confirm they are well-calibrated.
  • Years 5-15: The external relations landscape may become more complex as the institution matures. Succession planning may introduce new external contacts (estate attorneys, designated successors). The legal environment may change. The policy should be reviewed during each annual governance cycle.
  • Years 15-30: Technology changes may alter the nature of external interactions. New purchase channels, new communication methods, new legal obligations may emerge. The policy's principles (deliberate sharing, minimum disclosure, documentation) should remain stable even as the specifics evolve.
  • Years 30-50+: The institution may exist in a very different external environment than when this policy was written. Regulatory frameworks, technology landscapes, and social norms will have changed. The next generation of operators should review this article with fresh eyes, preserving the principles while updating the specifics for their era.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators. Each entry should include the date, the author's identifier, and the context for the commentary.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The hardest discipline in external relations is not refusing to share information with strangers. It is refusing to share information with friends. The person who asks about your project over dinner is not a threat. They are curious, and curiosity is flattering. The temptation is to explain, to demonstrate, to share the thing you have built. This temptation is natural and healthy in most contexts. In the context of an institution that depends on information boundaries, it must be managed.

I chose to include a specific category for social interactions because I know myself well enough to recognize this as my primary vulnerability. I will not accidentally share Red information with a hardware vendor. But I might, over a long dinner with a trusted friend, gradually share enough Amber information to construct a fairly detailed picture of the institution. The framework does not forbid this. It asks me to notice it, to document it, and to periodically assess whether the cumulative picture I have shared is acceptable. That awareness is the discipline. Not silence, but deliberate speech.
