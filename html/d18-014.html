D18-014 — Import Operator Training & Certification

Document ID
D18-014
Domain
18 -- Import & Quarantine
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
All other D18 articles (this is a capstone article)
Depended Upon By
D19-014 (QA Tooling -- certification audit procedures), D9-001 (Education & Training foundations)

Purpose

This article defines who is authorized to operate the import pipeline, what training they must receive, how they are certified, and how their certification is maintained over time. The import pipeline is the institution's immune system, and its operators are the immunologists. An untrained operator is a vulnerability: they may admit harmful content through ignorance, reject valuable content through excessive caution, or damage the quarantine architecture through procedural error.

This is a capstone article -- it depends on all other Domain 18 articles because an import operator must understand the entire import system, not just their immediate task. The operator must understand the philosophy (D18-001), the quarantine architecture (D18-006), the validation pipeline (D18-002), the format recognition system (D18-007), the trust scoring framework (D18-008), the rejection and appeal procedures (D18-009), the integrity verification methods (D18-012), the logging requirements (D18-013), and the specialized procedures for batch imports (D18-010), inter-node imports (D18-011), dangerous content (D18-003), format conversion (D18-004), and provenance tracking (D18-005).

This article is addressed to the people who will train import operators, to the operators themselves, to governance authorities who must ensure the institution always has certified operators available, and to future maintainers who must keep the training program current as the import system evolves.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The qualifications required before an individual may begin import operator training.
  • The training curriculum: what topics are covered and in what order.
  • The certification process: how competence is assessed and certification is granted.
  • Certification tiers: levels of authorization corresponding to different import operations.
  • Recertification: how certification is maintained over time.
  • Minimum staffing requirements: how many certified operators the institution must maintain.
  • Training delivery methods appropriate for an air-gapped institution.

This article does not cover:

  • General institutional training and onboarding (see Domain 9, Education & Training).
  • Security-specific training (see Security Domain articles), though import operator training includes security-relevant content.
  • The content of the import pipeline procedures themselves (see other D18 articles); this article covers how operators learn those procedures.

Background

Why Certification Matters

The import pipeline involves decisions with lasting consequences. Content admitted to the institution becomes part of its permanent record. Content rejected is potentially lost to the institution. A security breach caused by a careless import can compromise the entire system. These are not tasks for casual volunteers or untrained personnel. The institution must know, with documented confidence, that every person operating the import pipeline has the knowledge and judgment to do so safely.

The Apprenticeship Model

Import operator training follows an apprenticeship model rather than a classroom model. The trainee learns by working alongside a certified operator, performing increasingly complex import operations under supervision, until the trainer is confident the trainee can operate independently. This model was chosen because import operations are primarily judgment-based: the operator must assess situations, weigh evidence, and make decisions that no checklist can fully capture. Judgment is learned through practice, not lecture.

The Succession Imperative

D18-001's maintenance plan requires at least two certified operators at all times. If the institution has only one certified operator and that person becomes unavailable, import operations halt entirely. Training and certification is therefore not just a quality measure -- it is a continuity requirement. The institution must always be training the next operator before the current one departs.

System Model

Certification Tiers

Training Curriculum

Module 1: Import Philosophy & Overview (4 hours)

  • Reading: D18-001 (Import Philosophy) in full.
  • Discussion: The immune system analogy. The tension between protection and nourishment.
  • Overview of the complete import pipeline and all Domain 18 articles.
  • Assessment: Oral discussion demonstrating understanding of import principles.

Module 2: Quarantine Architecture & Safety (4 hours)

  • Reading: D18-006 (Quarantine Architecture).
  • Hands-on: Physical tour of the quarantine zone. Identification of all components.
  • Practice: Quarantine station startup, shutdown, and rebuild from known-good image.
  • Assessment: Trainee can independently rebuild a quarantine station within the time limit.

Module 3: Format Recognition & Integrity Verification (6 hours)

  • Reading: D18-007 (Format Recognition), D18-012 (Integrity Verification).
  • Hands-on: Using format identification tools. Navigating the Format Registry.
  • Practice: Identifying formats of 20 sample files including deceptive and unknown formats.
  • Practice: Computing hashes, comparing against origin hashes, interpreting results.
  • Assessment: Correctly identify format and verify integrity for 15 of 20 test files.

Module 4: Validation Pipeline & Trust Scoring (6 hours)

  • Reading: D18-002 (Validation Pipeline), D18-008 (Trust Scoring), D18-005 (Provenance Tracking).
  • Hands-on: Running content through the complete validation pipeline.
  • Practice: Assessing trust scores for 10 import scenarios with varying provenance.
  • Discussion: Borderline cases where judgment is required.
  • Assessment: Correctly assess trust scores and pipeline routing for 7 of 10 test scenarios.

Module 5: Rejection, Appeal & Dangerous Content (4 hours)

  • Reading: D18-009 (Rejection & Appeal), D18-003 (Dangerous Content Handling).
  • Practice: Documenting rejection decisions. Reviewing mock appeal submissions.
  • Practice: Handling simulated dangerous content (test malware, corrupted files).
  • Discussion: The ethics of rejection. Communicating rejection to carriers.
  • Assessment: Correctly handle 5 rejection scenarios with proper documentation.

Module 6: Logging, Batch Import & Inter-Node (4 hours)

  • Reading: D18-013 (Import Logging), D18-010 (Batch Import), D18-011 (Inter-Node Import).
  • Hands-on: Creating complete log entries. Querying the import log.
  • Practice: Processing a simulated small batch import (50 items) using sampling.
  • Discussion: Inter-node trust dynamics and the federation import relationship.
  • Assessment: Complete a simulated batch import with proper logging and sampling.

Module 7: Practical Assessment (8 hours)

  • The trainee independently processes 5 import scenarios of increasing complexity under observation by a T3 or T4 operator.
  • Scenarios include: a standard single-item import, a rejected import with documentation, an import with integrity failure, a small batch import, and a deliberately deceptive or dangerous import.
  • The assessor evaluates: procedural correctness, judgment quality, documentation completeness, quarantine safety, and communication.
  • Pass/fail criteria: The trainee must handle all 5 scenarios without a safety-critical error. Procedural errors are acceptable if recognized and corrected. Failure to detect the dangerous import is an automatic fail.

Advanced Module (for T4 certification): Training & Pipeline Management (8 hours)

  • How to train a new operator using the apprenticeship model.
  • Pipeline configuration: adding validation checks, adjusting thresholds, updating the Format Registry.
  • Complex appeal adjudication with case studies.
  • Inter-node trust level assessment and modification.
  • Assessment: Conduct a mock training session observed by another T4 operator.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D18-C01: Only Certified Operators May Operate the Pipeline. No person may operate a quarantine station, make admission/rejection decisions, or perform any import pipeline function beyond observation without the appropriate certification tier. Violations are treated as security incidents.

  • R-D18-C02: Minimum Two T3 Operators. The institution MUST maintain at least two operators certified at T3 or above at all times. If the number drops below two, training a replacement becomes the highest operational priority for the import domain. Import operations continue with the remaining certified operator but at reduced throughput.

  • R-D18-C03: Certification Records Are Permanent. All certification records -- training completion, assessment results, certification grants, recertification events, and lapses -- MUST be recorded in the institutional record and retained permanently.

  • R-D18-C04: Lapsed Certification. If an operator's certification lapses (recertification period expires without recertification), the operator reverts to the next lower tier. A T3 operator whose certification lapses reverts to T2 and may not make independent decisions until recertified. A T2 operator whose certification lapses reverts to T1 (observer only).

  • R-D18-C05: Training Materials Are Maintained. The training curriculum MUST be reviewed and updated whenever any Domain 18 article is revised. Outdated training materials are a systemic risk: operators trained on old procedures will apply old procedures. The T4 operator responsible for training MUST verify training material currency before beginning any training program.

  • R-D18-C06: No Self-Certification. An operator MUST NOT assess or certify themselves. Assessment requires a different certified operator. If the institution has only one qualified assessor, external assessment arrangements must be made (e.g., a T4 operator from a federated node).

  • R-D18-C07: Training Is Documentation-Driven. All training MUST be based on the written Domain 18 articles. Oral tradition, "how we've always done it," and undocumented shortcuts are explicitly excluded from training. If a practice is not documented in a D18 article, it is not part of the training and should not be taught. If a useful practice exists that is not documented, document it first, then teach it.

Failure Modes

  • Training neglect. The institution has only two T3 operators and neither prioritizes training a third. If either becomes unavailable, the institution is one departure from an import capability crisis. Likelihood: Moderate -- training competes with operational duties for time. Severity: High. Detection: Quarterly staffing review. Governance alerts when certified operator count approaches the minimum.

  • Training drift. The training curriculum diverges from actual procedures. Articles are updated but training materials are not. New operators learn outdated practices. Likelihood: Moderate over years. Severity: Medium. Detection: R-D18-C05 requires currency review before each training program. QA audit of training material alignment with current articles.

  • Certification without competence. The assessment is too lenient, or personal relationships influence the assessment. An operator is certified who lacks the judgment to operate safely. Likelihood: Low if assessments are rigorous. Severity: High. Detection: Post-certification observation by QA. Import log review for anomalous decisions by newly-certified operators. Mitigation: R-D18-C06 prevents self-certification; assessment rubrics are documented and objective where possible.

  • Sole remaining operator burnout. With only two certified operators, one departs. The remaining operator handles all import operations alone while also training a replacement. Workload is unsustainable. Quality degrades. Likelihood: Moderate over the institution's lifetime. Severity: High. Detection: Operator self-report. Governance observation. Mitigation: R-D18-C02 makes replacement training the highest priority. Reduce import throughput to sustainable levels during the gap.

  • Knowledge concentration in T4 operators. Over time, T4 operators accumulate undocumented knowledge about pipeline quirks, common issues, and workarounds. If all T4 operators depart, this knowledge is lost. Likelihood: Moderate. Severity: Medium. Detection: R-D18-C07 mitigates this by requiring all training to be documentation-driven. But undocumented knowledge still accumulates in practice. Mitigation: Periodic "knowledge extraction" sessions where T4 operators are asked to identify any practices they use that are not in the documentation. These are then documented.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If certified operator count drops below minimum: Declare an import capability alert. Reduce import operations to essential imports only (inter-node sync, critical acquisitions). Identify the most qualified T2 operator and begin accelerated T3 training. If no T2 operator exists, identify the most suitable institutional member and begin the full training program. Request T4 assistance from a federated node if available. Do not allow the sole remaining operator to burn out -- sustainable pace is more important than maintaining full import throughput.

  2. If training materials are found to be outdated: Suspend new training until materials are updated. Current certified operators are not affected (they have operational experience), but no new certifications may be granted against outdated materials. Assign a T4 operator to update the curriculum. Resume training after the update is complete and verified.

  3. If an operator is found to be incompetent despite certification: Suspend the operator's certification pending review. Examine the assessment records: was the assessment conducted properly? Examine the operator's import log: what decisions did they make, and were they appropriate? If the assessment was deficient, address the assessment process. If the operator's skills have degraded, require recertification with additional supervised imports. If the operator's judgment is consistently poor, revoke certification. Document the entire process.

  4. If all T4 operators depart: This is a critical knowledge loss event. Before departure (if planned), conduct knowledge extraction per D20-003 (Oral History Capture). The most experienced remaining T3 operator assumes T4-equivalent responsibilities under governance authorization. They cannot self-certify but can be granted provisional T4 authority by governance. A formal T4 certification process should be established as soon as practical, potentially involving assessment by a T4 operator from a federated node.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The founding operators are self-trained from the documentation. They certify each other through the practical assessment process. The training curriculum is untested and will require significant adjustment as real training sessions reveal gaps. The first generation of operators creates the training culture that subsequent generations will inherit.

  • Years 5-15: The training program has been refined through multiple operator generations. Common misunderstandings, frequent questions, and typical beginner mistakes have been identified and addressed in the curriculum. The case study library (from Module 7 scenarios) grows from real import events, making the training increasingly grounded in institutional experience.

  • Years 15-30: The training program itself is institutional knowledge. New trainers learn from the program's accumulated case studies and from the experience of previous trainers. The curriculum may need structural revision to accommodate changes in import technology, format landscapes, and federation complexity. The apprenticeship model should remain unchanged -- the judgment component of import operation does not become automatable.

  • Years 30-50+: The training program has been handed down through multiple generations of operators. Its longevity depends on two things: the documentation being kept current (R-D18-C05), and the apprenticeship model preserving the judgment dimension that documentation alone cannot capture. If both are maintained, the institution can train competent import operators indefinitely. If either breaks down, operator quality degrades within one generation.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The founding operators face a bootstrapping problem: who certifies the first operators when no certified operators yet exist? The answer is that the founding operators certify each other through the practical assessment. This is explicitly a pragmatic compromise. The founding certifications should be noted as "founding certification -- mutual assessment" in the certification records. Subsequent certifications should follow the standard process with assessment by a previously-certified operator.

The apprenticeship model was chosen over classroom instruction because import operation is a craft, not an academic subject. You learn to assess trust by assessing trust under guidance. You learn to handle a dangerous import by handling one (with supervision). You learn to make judgment calls by making judgment calls and discussing them with your mentor. No amount of reading D18-008 (Trust Scoring) teaches you the gut feeling that something about a particular import is not quite right. That instinct develops through experience.

The minimum requirement of two T3 operators is a floor, not a target. A healthy institution should aim for three or four, providing coverage for illness, leave, and training duties without straining any individual. The minimum of two exists because below two, the institution is one person away from losing import capability entirely. Above two, each additional operator provides diminishing but real additional resilience.

Open question: should the institution establish a formal mentorship relationship between each T3 operator and a T4 operator? The current model allows any T4 to train any candidate, but a dedicated mentorship might produce better outcomes by creating a sustained relationship. This should be evaluated after the first few operator training cycles.
