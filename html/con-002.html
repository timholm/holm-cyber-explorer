CON-002 — Core Principles and Non-Negotiable Values

Document ID
CON-002
Domain
1 -- Constitution & Philosophy
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Draft

Purpose

This article enumerates, defines, and explains the foundational principles upon which the entire holm.chat Documentation Institution operates. Where CON-001 defines what the institution is and why it exists, this document defines what the institution believes -- the irreducible commitments that shape every decision, every design, and every refusal the institution will ever make.

These are not aspirations. They are not goals to be achieved when convenient. They are constraints that bind the institution from its first day to its last. Every article in every domain is subordinate to the principles stated here. When an operational need conflicts with a principle defined in this document, the principle prevails. When a technical optimization would violate a value articulated here, the optimization is rejected. This is not inefficiency. It is architecture.

This article is written for every person who will ever make a decision on behalf of this institution. It is written with the understanding that the person reading it may have never met the person who wrote it, may live in a different era, and may face circumstances the founder could not have imagined. The principles must therefore be stated with enough clarity and reasoning that they can be applied to situations that do not yet exist.

A principle without reasoning is a dogma. A value without explanation becomes a ritual. This document provides both the principles and the reasoning, so that future stewards can apply them with intelligence rather than mere obedience.

Scope

This article applies to every domain, every article, every system, and every decision within the institution. There is no carve-out, no exception, and no domain that operates outside the principles stated here.

Specifically, this article governs:

  • All architectural and design decisions across every technical domain.
  • All governance structures and their operation.
  • All security policies and their implementation.
  • All documentation standards and their enforcement.
  • All evolution and adaptation decisions.
  • The evaluation of any proposed change, addition, or removal of institutional capability.

This article does not define specific implementations of these principles. Implementation is the responsibility of the relevant domain. This article defines the principles that every implementation must satisfy.

When another article in any domain makes a claim about institutional values, the claim must be traceable to a principle defined here. If it is not, the claim lacks authority and must be either grounded in this document through amendment or withdrawn.

Background

Every institution encodes values, whether or not it states them explicitly. Commercial cloud providers encode the value of growth, monetization, and user acquisition -- values that are never stated in their terms of service but are visible in every design decision they make. Social media platforms encode the value of engagement and attention capture. Corporate IT departments encode the values of compliance, liability reduction, and cost containment.

This institution chooses to state its values explicitly, in writing, as a binding constraint. It does this for three reasons.

First, because unstated values drift. When values live only in the minds of the people operating a system, they shift with mood, motivation, and personnel changes. Written values drift too, but they drift more slowly, and the drift is visible. You can compare what you are doing today against what the document says and measure the gap.

Second, because future operators deserve honesty. A person inheriting this institution should know what they are inheriting -- not just the hardware and the software, but the commitments. These principles are part of the inheritance. They are non-optional.

Third, because principles without documentation are indistinguishable from preferences. A preference is something you follow when it is convenient. A principle is something you follow when it is costly. The act of writing these principles down, with reasoning and consequences, is the act of committing to them even when they are inconvenient.

The principles that follow were derived from the founding experience described in CON-001 and the ethical framework established in ETH-001. They are not arbitrary. Each one exists because its absence would produce a specific, identifiable failure mode in the institution's long-term operation.

System Model

The principles are organized into three tiers, reflecting their relationship to the institution's survival and identity.

Tier 1: Existential Principles

These principles define what the institution is. If any of these principles is abandoned, the institution has ceased to exist in any meaningful sense, even if its hardware still runs and its files are still intact.

Principle 1: Sovereignty. The institution exists to serve the absolute sovereignty of its operator over their digital life. Sovereignty means the operator decides what data exists, where it lives, who accesses it, and when it is destroyed. No external entity -- no corporation, government, service provider, or other party -- has any claim on the data, the systems, or the decisions of this institution. Sovereignty is not privacy alone, though it includes privacy. It is not security alone, though it requires security. It is the total, practical authority of the operator over every aspect of their digital infrastructure. Every design decision must preserve or enhance sovereignty. Any decision that diminishes sovereignty, no matter how small the diminishment, requires explicit justification and documentation.

Principle 2: Durability. The institution is designed to operate for a minimum of fifty years. This is not a marketing claim. It is a design constraint. Every hardware choice, every software choice, every data format, every documentation practice must be evaluated against the question: will this still work, or be replaceable, in fifty years? Durability does not mean stasis. It means that every component of the institution can be replaced, upgraded, or migrated without loss of data, capability, or institutional knowledge. The institution endures not by resisting change but by being designed for change from the beginning.

Principle 3: Integrity. What the institution says is true must actually be true. Backups that claim to be complete must be complete. Logs that claim to be accurate must be accurate. Documentation that describes a system must describe the system as it actually exists, not as it was planned or as it once was. Integrity is the foundation of trust, and trust is the foundation of operation. An operator who cannot trust their own systems is worse off than an operator with no systems at all, because they will make decisions based on false information. Every system must be verifiable. Every claim must be auditable. Integrity is not a feature. It is a prerequisite.

Tier 2: Architectural Principles

These principles define how the institution is built. They can theoretically be modified without destroying the institution's identity, but doing so would fundamentally alter its character and risk compromising the Tier 1 principles they support.

Principle 4: Autonomy. The institution must be capable of operating indefinitely without any external service, supply chain, or support structure. This does not mean it must always operate in isolation -- it means it must always be capable of operating in isolation. Every external dependency is a potential point of failure, and every point of failure is a threat to sovereignty and durability. Autonomy requires that the institution can generate its own power, store its own data, run its own software, and maintain its own documentation without any external input. Where external resources are used for convenience, a documented plan must exist for their removal.

Principle 5: Simplicity. The institution prefers simple solutions over complex ones, proven technologies over novel ones, and understandable systems over powerful-but-opaque ones. Simplicity is not an aesthetic preference. It is a survival strategy. Complex systems fail in complex ways. Simple systems fail in predictable ways. On a fifty-year horizon, the ability to understand, diagnose, and repair a system matters more than its elegance or efficiency. Every layer of complexity must justify its existence. Every abstraction must earn its place. The institution's complexity budget is finite, and every addition must be weighed against everything else that is already present.

Principle 6: Transparency. Every system within the institution must be fully understandable by the person operating it. This means open source software, documented configurations, explained design decisions, and no black boxes. Transparency is the operational expression of sovereignty: you cannot be sovereign over a system you do not understand. Transparency also serves durability: a system that can only be operated by its creator is a system that dies with its creator. Transparency is the bridge between the person who built the system and the person who will maintain it.

Tier 3: Operational Principles

These principles guide day-to-day decisions. They are the most likely to be refined over time, but they must always remain consistent with the principles in Tiers 1 and 2.

Principle 7: Privacy. The institution collects no data beyond what is necessary for its stated functions. Data that is no longer needed is destroyed, not archived. The default state of any data collection mechanism is off. Privacy within the institution means that the operator's data is never exposed to any entity the operator has not explicitly authorized, and that the institution itself does not accumulate data about its operator beyond what is operationally required.

Principle 8: Opacity to Outsiders. While the institution is transparent to its operator, it is opaque to everyone else. The existence, contents, capabilities, and configuration of the institution are not disclosed to any external party. This is not secrecy for its own sake. It is a security posture. Information about the institution's architecture is information that could be used to compromise it. The institution practices security through obscurity as one layer of a defense-in-depth strategy, not as its sole defense.

Principle 9: Deliberate Evolution. The institution changes only through deliberate, documented decisions. No change is made "temporarily" without a documented reversion plan. No capability is added without a documented justification. No component is deprecated without a documented migration path. Evolution is not resisted -- it is governed. The institution embraces change, but it demands that change be intentional, recorded, and reversible.

Principle 10: Sustainable Operation. The institution is designed to be maintained by one person without heroic effort. Operational procedures must be sustainable indefinitely. If a procedure requires more time, energy, or attention than one person can sustainably provide, the procedure must be simplified, automated, or eliminated. The institution must never depend on the operator's best days. It must function on their worst.

Rules and Constraints

The following rules are derived directly from the principles above and are binding on all institutional decisions:

  • R-CON-002-01: Any proposed change to the institution must identify which principles it serves and must demonstrate that it does not violate any principle of equal or higher tier.
  • R-CON-002-02: When two principles conflict in a specific situation, the higher-tier principle prevails. When principles of the same tier conflict, the resolution must be documented with reasoning and added to this article's commentary section as a precedent.
  • R-CON-002-03: No external dependency may be introduced without a documented removal plan, a timeline for removal, and a designated person responsible for executing the removal.
  • R-CON-002-04: Every system must have a documented complexity assessment that explains what the system does, why it is necessary, and what would be lost if it were removed. Systems that cannot justify their complexity must be simplified or removed.
  • R-CON-002-05: The operational burden of any new system or procedure must be assessed before deployment. If the total operational burden of the institution exceeds what one person can sustainably manage, the least essential systems must be simplified or deprecated until sustainability is restored.
  • R-CON-002-06: Tier 1 principles may not be amended. They may only be reinterpreted through commentary, and reinterpretation must preserve the original intent as described in this article's reasoning sections. If the institution ever determines that a Tier 1 principle must be abandoned, the institution should be dissolved and reconstituted under a new mandate rather than hollowed out by amendment.
  • R-CON-002-07: Tier 2 principles may be amended through the process defined in CON-011, requiring a 180-day deliberation period and documented reasoning that explains why the amendment does not compromise any Tier 1 principle.
  • R-CON-002-08: Tier 3 principles may be amended through the standard amendment process defined in CON-011, requiring a 90-day deliberation period.

Failure Modes

Principle inversion. The institution begins to serve its principles rather than the principles serving the institution's mission. Rules are followed mechanically without understanding their purpose. This produces absurd outcomes that technically comply with the letter of the principles while violating their spirit. Mitigation: the reasoning sections of each principle exist precisely to prevent mechanical application. When a principle produces an absurd result, the reasoning should be consulted to determine whether the situation is truly covered by the principle or whether it represents a gap in the principle's formulation.

Tier collapse. The distinction between tiers erodes, and all principles are treated as equally mutable or equally immutable. If all principles are treated as immutable, the institution becomes rigid and unable to adapt. If all principles are treated as mutable, the institution loses its identity. Mitigation: the tier structure must be respected in every amendment discussion. The question "which tier is this principle in?" must precede the question "should we change it?"

Principle proliferation. New principles are added without sufficient rigor, diluting the authority of the existing principles. If everything is a principle, nothing is. Mitigation: new principles may only be added through the amendment process, which includes a deliberation period and a requirement to demonstrate that the new principle is not already covered by an existing one.

Silent abandonment. A principle is never formally amended but is quietly ignored in practice. The documentation says one thing; the institution does another. This is the most dangerous failure mode because it is invisible until a crisis exposes the gap. Mitigation: annual principle audits in which every principle is tested against current practice and discrepancies are documented.

Contextual obsolescence. A principle was written for a world that no longer exists. The words remain, but their meaning has shifted so far that applying them faithfully produces outcomes the author would not have intended. Mitigation: the Commentary Section accumulates interpretive guidance over time. The Evolution Path section below provides a framework for recognizing and addressing contextual change.

Recovery Procedures

If a principle audit reveals that one or more principles have been violated or abandoned:

  1. Document the violation. Record precisely which principle was violated, when the violation began (or when it was discovered), and what the current state of affairs is. Be factual. Do not minimize or rationalize.
  2. Assess impact. Determine what systems, data, or institutional capabilities have been affected by the violation. Determine whether the violation has compromised any higher-tier principles.
  3. Determine root cause. Was the violation deliberate (a conscious decision to deviate)? Was it accidental (a consequence of decisions that did not consider the principle)? Was it systemic (the result of a process or structure that made violation inevitable)?
  4. Design correction. If the principle should be restored: define specific, measurable steps to bring the institution back into compliance, with deadlines. If the principle should be amended: initiate the amendment process appropriate to the principle's tier.
  5. Execute and verify. Implement the correction. After implementation, verify through independent audit that the principle is now being respected.
  6. Record the precedent. Add an entry to this article's Commentary Section describing the violation, the correction, and the lessons learned. This entry becomes part of the institution's interpretive record.

Evolution Path

Years 0-5: These principles are being tested against reality for the first time. Expect to discover edge cases where principles conflict, situations the principles do not clearly address, and interpretive questions that require deliberation. The Commentary Section should accumulate rapidly during this period. Amendments should be rare and limited to Tier 3.

Years 5-15: The principles should be stable. The focus shifts from establishing the principles to building an interpretive tradition around them. Commentary entries become the primary mechanism for evolution, providing guidance on how the principles apply to specific situations without changing their text.

Years 15-30: Succession becomes the dominant concern. The principles must be transferable -- understandable by someone who did not write them and did not participate in the founding. The Commentary Section serves as the bridge between founding intent and successor interpretation. If a successor finds the principles incomprehensible or inapplicable, this is a documentation failure, not a successor failure.

Years 30-50+: The world in which these principles were written may be unrecognizable. Technology, society, governance, and even language may have changed in ways the founder could not have predicted. The principles must be evaluated for continued relevance. Tier 1 principles, by design, should be abstract enough to survive any technological or social change. Tier 2 and Tier 3 principles may require amendment. The amendment process ensures that change is deliberate and documented.
