GOV-009 — The Continuity Protocol

Document ID
GOV-009
Domain
2 -- Governance & Authority
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
ETH-001, CON-001, GOV-001, GOV-004, SEC-001
Depended Upon By
SEC-008. Referenced by Domain 12 (Disaster Recovery) for governance continuity during catastrophic events.

Purpose

This article defines what happens when the institution is reduced to its minimum viable state. Specifically, it addresses three scenarios that GOV-004 (Succession Planning) does not fully cover: the institution reduced to a single member who is not the founder, the institution reduced to zero active members, and the institution being reconstituted after a period of dormancy.

GOV-004 handles the transfer of authority from one person to another. This article handles the edge cases: what governance structures remain when the institution is at its most fragile? How does a sole operator govern when there is nobody else to check their work, no quorum to consult, no predecessor to advise? How does someone reconstitute an institution they inherit from documentation alone, with no living connection to its founders?

These are not theoretical concerns. Over a 50-year horizon, the probability that the institution will pass through at least one period of single-operator fragility, one period of dormancy, or one reconstitution event is high. An institution that has not planned for these contingencies will not survive them. An institution that has planned for them poorly -- with vague instructions or aspirational language -- will survive them in name only, losing its character and purpose in the process.

This article is therefore specific, procedural, and uncompromising about what must be preserved. It defines the minimum viable governance structure: the smallest set of rules, records, and practices that allow the institution to function as an institution rather than degenerating into a personal project without memory or discipline.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The definition of minimum viable governance: the irreducible set of governance practices required for the institution to remain an institution.
  • Single-operator continuity: governance for an institution with exactly one member who is not the founder.
  • Dormancy protocols: what happens when the institution has zero active operators, including how systems are preserved during dormancy.
  • Reconstitution procedures: how a new operator activates a dormant institution.
  • The continuity archive: a self-contained package of documents and credentials that enables reconstitution.
  • Degraded governance: which governance practices can be simplified or suspended during minimum-viable-state operations, and which cannot.

This article does not cover technical disaster recovery (Domain 12), security credential management for succession (SEC-001), or the normal succession process (GOV-004). It covers the governance layer that sits above all of those: the human decision-making structure that must survive even when everything else is strained.

Background

Institutions die in two ways. The dramatic way is sudden destruction: a fire, a legal action, a catastrophic failure. The quiet way is gradual abandonment: an operator who loses interest, a successor who never fully engages, a period of inactivity that stretches from weeks to months to years until the institution exists only as dormant hardware and forgotten files.

The quiet death is far more common and far harder to reverse. A destroyed institution can be rebuilt from backups. An abandoned institution has no clear moment of failure, no obvious point at which recovery should begin. It drifts. Its documentation becomes stale. Its systems continue running unattended, accumulating errors that nobody notices. By the time someone thinks to check on it, the gap between the institution's documented state and its actual state may be insurmountable.

This article is designed to prevent the quiet death, or at least to make recovery possible when it threatens. It does so by defining clear states (active, degraded, dormant) with clear transitions between them, so that the institution always knows -- or can determine -- where it stands. It also defines a continuity archive: a sealed, self-contained package that contains everything a competent person needs to understand, access, and operate the institution without any external assistance.

The continuity archive is the institution's insurance policy against its own mortality. It is the answer to the question: if every person associated with this institution disappeared tomorrow, and a stranger found the archive five years later, could they bring the institution back to life? The answer must be yes.

System Model

4.1 Institutional States

The institution exists in one of four governance states at any given time:

State A: Full Operations. The institution has at least one active operator who is fully onboarded, has read all root documents, and is following the governance procedures in GOV-001 and GOV-008. All governance tiers and decision categories are available. This is the normal operating state.

State B: Degraded Operations. The institution has exactly one operator who is not fully onboarded, or who has been operating without proper governance discipline (missed reviews, incomplete decision logs, lapsed maintenance). The institution is functioning but its governance integrity is compromised. State B triggers a mandatory governance restoration process (Section 4.5).

State C: Minimal Operations. The institution has exactly one operator who is maintaining essential functions only: hardware monitoring, backup verification, and basic security. No new development, no architectural changes, no policy modifications. The operator may lack full understanding of the institution's history and rationale. State C is a holding pattern, sustainable for months but not for years.

State D: Dormancy. The institution has no active operator. Systems may or may not be running. No governance is being exercised. The institution persists only as hardware, data, and documentation. Dormancy is not death; the continuity archive (Section 4.3) is designed to make reconstitution possible from this state.

4.2 State Transitions

Transitions between states follow defined protocols:

A to B (Degradation): Occurs when governance discipline lapses. Detected by the quarterly health check (GOV-007) or by self-assessment. The operator should initiate governance restoration (Section 4.5) within 30 days of recognizing the degradation.

B to A (Restoration): The operator completes the governance restoration process, which includes: re-reading all root documents, auditing the decision log for gaps, retroactively documenting undocumented decisions, and conducting a full governance health check. Restoration is confirmed by a decision log entry.

A or B to C (Reduction): Occurs when the current operator cannot maintain full or degraded operations, typically during a health crisis, extended absence, or the early phase of an unplanned succession. The operator (or successor) records the transition to Minimal Operations in the decision log and restricts activity to essential maintenance only.

C to A or B (Expansion): The operator completes the onboarding process defined in GOV-004 and demonstrates readiness through 90 days of supervised or self-assessed operation. If the operator can reach State A directly (full governance compliance from the start), they may skip State B.

Any to D (Dormancy): Occurs when the last operator ceases activity. This transition may be planned (the operator records the transition and activates dormancy protocols) or unplanned (the operator simply stops). In the unplanned case, dormancy is recognized retroactively when a new operator arrives.

D to C (Reconstitution): A new operator locates the continuity archive, follows the reconstitution procedures (Section 4.4), and begins Minimal Operations. This is the most critical transition in the continuity protocol and is detailed extensively below.

4.3 The Continuity Archive

The continuity archive is a self-contained package, stored in a physically secure location separate from the institution's primary systems, that contains everything needed to reconstitute the institution from dormancy. It is updated at least annually and after every Tier 1 or Tier 2 decision.

The archive contains:

  • All root documents in their current ratified versions: ETH-001, CON-001, GOV-001, and this article (GOV-009).
  • The complete decision log from the institution's founding to the archive date.
  • Security credentials in a sealed, encrypted format with the decryption key stored separately (see SEC-001 for credential management).
  • A system inventory: what hardware exists, where it is located, how to access it, and its current state.
  • A reconstitution guide: a step-by-step document written for a technically competent person who has no prior knowledge of the institution. This guide covers: how to gain physical access, how to power on systems, how to verify data integrity, how to access the documentation, and how to begin Minimal Operations.
  • Contact information for any designated successor, advisory contacts, or legal representatives.
  • A letter from the most recent operator explaining the institution's current state, any pending issues, and any advice for the reconstituting operator.

The archive must be verifiable: it includes checksums for all digital contents and a dated signature from the operator who assembled it. It must be discoverable: its location must be known to the designated successor, recorded in the decision log, and (if possible) referenced in the operator's personal estate planning.

4.4 Reconstitution Procedures

A person who discovers or inherits the continuity archive and wishes to reconstitute the institution follows these steps:

  1. Read the reconstitution guide. Do not touch any systems until you have read the entire guide.
  2. Verify the archive's integrity. Check the checksums. Confirm that the archive date is consistent with expectations. If the archive is significantly out of date, proceed with caution: the institution's actual state may differ from the documented state.
  3. Read the root documents. Read ETH-001, CON-001, GOV-001, and this article in their entirety. If you cannot commit to the principles in these documents, do not reconstitute the institution. Preservation of the data and hardware without reconstitution is preferable to reconstitution by someone who does not share the institution's values.
  4. Access the systems. Follow the guide to gain physical access. Power on systems. Verify that hardware is functional. Check data integrity using the procedures in Domain 12.
  5. Record the reconstitution. Create a decision log entry: "RECONSTITUTION FROM DORMANCY -- [date] -- [your name] -- Institution was dormant since approximately [date of last known activity]. Reconstitution initiated using continuity archive dated [archive date]."
  6. Begin Minimal Operations (State C). For the first 90 days, restrict activity to essential maintenance. Do not make architectural or policy changes. Use this period to read the complete decision log, understand the institution's history, and assess the gap between documented state and actual state.
  7. Assess and plan. After 90 days, draft a governance restoration plan. This plan should identify what needs to be updated, what has drifted during dormancy, and what the operator's priorities are. Record this plan as a Significant decision (GOV-008).
  8. Transition to full operations. Implement the restoration plan. When governance compliance is achieved, record the transition to State A in the decision log.

4.5 Governance Restoration Process

When the institution is in State B (Degraded) and needs to return to State A (Full Operations), the operator completes the following:

  1. Re-read all root documents. Note any points of confusion or disagreement in the Commentary Sections.
  2. Audit the decision log for the previous twelve months. Identify any gaps. For decisions that were made but not logged, create retroactive entries marked "RECONSTRUCTED."
  3. Review all governance articles (GOV-001 through GOV-014) for currency. Update any that reference obsolete procedures or systems.
  4. Conduct a full governance health assessment per GOV-007.
  5. Record the restoration in the decision log: "GOVERNANCE RESTORED TO STATE A -- [date] -- [summary of what was corrected]."

4.6 Minimum Viable Governance

During State C (Minimal Operations), the following governance practices are mandatory:

  • The decision log must be maintained. Even in Minimal Operations, every action that changes the state of any system must be recorded.
  • ETH-001 remains in full effect. Ethical principles are not degraded by operational reduction.
  • Backup verification continues. Data preservation is the highest priority during Minimal Operations.
  • No Tier 1 or Tier 2 decisions may be made. The institution is in conservation mode.

The following practices may be simplified during State C:

  • Waiting periods for Tier 3 decisions may be reduced from 7 days to 48 hours, to allow necessary operational adjustments.
  • The quarterly health check (GOV-007) may be reduced to a brief monthly assessment.
  • Documentation reviews may be suspended until the institution returns to State B or A.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-GOV-09-01: The continuity archive must be updated at least annually and within 30 days of any Tier 1 or Tier 2 decision. Failure to maintain the archive is a governance violation.
  • R-GOV-09-02: A person reconstituting the institution from dormancy must read all root documents before accessing any systems. This is not optional. The order matters: understanding before action.
  • R-GOV-09-03: During State C (Minimal Operations), no Tier 1 or Tier 2 decisions may be made. If a situation arises that would normally require a Tier 1 or Tier 2 decision, the operator must either transition to State A first or invoke the emergency governance protocols in GOV-005.
  • R-GOV-09-04: Dormancy (State D) must not be entered deliberately without first updating the continuity archive and recording the transition in the decision log. Deliberate dormancy without these steps is an abandonment, not a transition.
  • R-GOV-09-05: A reconstituting operator who cannot commit to the principles in ETH-001 and CON-001 must not assume governance authority. They may preserve the hardware and data in place, but they must not make governance decisions for an institution whose values they do not share.
  • R-GOV-09-06: The reconstitution guide in the continuity archive must be tested at least once every five years. The test consists of having someone unfamiliar with the institution read the guide and report whether they could follow it. The test results are recorded in the decision log.

Failure Modes

  • Archive decay. The continuity archive is not updated, and over time it diverges so far from the institution's actual state that it becomes useless for reconstitution. Mitigation: R-GOV-09-01, which mandates annual updates and updates after significant decisions. The governance health check (GOV-007) should include archive currency as an assessment item.
  • Undiscoverable archive. The archive exists and is current, but nobody can find it. The operator dies, and the archive's location dies with them. Mitigation: the archive location must be recorded in the decision log, communicated to the designated successor, and (if possible) referenced in the operator's estate planning.
  • Unqualified reconstitution. Someone reconstitutes the institution without the technical competence or institutional commitment needed to operate it. They follow the steps mechanically but do not understand the principles. The institution resumes in name but loses its character. Mitigation: R-GOV-09-05 requires commitment to the root documents, and the 90-day Minimal Operations period provides time for the reconstituting operator to assess their own readiness honestly.
  • Permanent State C. The operator remains in Minimal Operations indefinitely, never completing the transition to full governance. The institution survives but does not develop. Mitigation: State C is designed to be sustainable for months, not years. The governance health check, even in its reduced monthly form, should flag a prolonged State C as an issue requiring attention.
  • Unrecognized dormancy. The operator gradually reduces activity without formally transitioning to any degraded state. The institution drifts into de facto dormancy while nominally remaining active. Mitigation: the institutional states have defined criteria. If no decision log entries have been made in six months and no maintenance has been performed, the institution is dormant regardless of what the operator believes.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If the continuity archive is out of date: Update it immediately. If the gap is more than two years, conduct a full audit of the archive against the institution's current state. Replace all contents. Test the reconstitution guide. Record the update in the decision log.
  2. If the archive is lost or destroyed: Reconstruct it from the institution's primary systems. This is a Tier 2 decision (it affects institutional resilience). Assemble all required contents, verify them, seal the archive, and store it in the designated secure location. Document the loss and reconstruction in the decision log.
  3. If reconstitution fails (systems are non-functional): Proceed to Domain 12 (Disaster Recovery) procedures. Governance reconstitution cannot proceed until there are functional systems to govern. The reconstituting operator should maintain a detailed log of all recovery actions, which will become part of the decision log when the institution is restored.
  4. If the reconstituting operator discovers that the institution has been compromised during dormancy: Follow SEC-001 procedures for security incident response before proceeding with governance reconstitution. Security must be established before governance can be exercised. Document the compromise, the response, and the outcome.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The continuity archive is assembled and tested. The reconstitution guide is written and validated. The institutional states are defined but likely untested by real events. The founder should simulate a State C period (one month of Minimal Operations only) to test the procedures.
  • Years 5-15: The archive has been updated multiple times. If succession planning (GOV-004) is active, the continuity archive should be shared with the designated successor as part of the onboarding process. The reconstitution guide should be tested by the successor.
  • Years 15-30: The institution may have experienced its first real State B or State C period. The protocols should be refined based on that experience. The Commentary Section should contain lessons learned. The archive format may need updating as technology changes.
  • Years 30-50+: The institution is likely on its second or third generation. The continuity protocols have been tested by real succession events. The archive may need to be adapted for storage media that did not exist when this article was written. The fundamental principles -- discoverability, self-containment, and the requirement for values alignment -- should remain constant even as the implementation evolves.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators. Each entry should include the date, the author's identifier, and the context for the commentary.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The scenario I find most haunting is not the dramatic one -- not the fire or the flood, but the quiet one. The slow drift. I stop maintaining the institution because life gets busy. Months pass. Then a year. Then five years. The hardware sits in a closet, gathering dust. The documentation sits on drives that nobody reads. And one day someone finds it all -- a family member, an executor, a curious stranger -- and they have to decide what to do with it.

This article is my attempt to make that moment recoverable. The continuity archive is a letter to that future person. It says: here is what this was, here is why it mattered, here is how to bring it back if you want to. And if you do not want to, that is also acceptable. Preservation of the data without reconstitution is a legitimate outcome. What is not acceptable is reconstitution without understanding. An institution operated by someone who does not understand its principles is worse than a dormant institution, because it will make decisions that corrupt what the institution was meant to preserve.
