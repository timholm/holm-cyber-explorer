D11-007 — Record-Keeping Standards and Archival Procedures

Document ID
D11-007
Domain
11 — Administration
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Draft

Purpose

This article defines what administrative records the institution must keep, in what format, for how long, how they are indexed, and how they are eventually retired. Administrative records are distinct from the documentation corpus (Domain 6) and from governance records (GOV-001). They are the operational memory of the institution's material life: purchase histories, maintenance logs, inventory audits, budget reconciliations, personnel records, and scheduling histories.

Without record-keeping standards, administrative records accumulate in inconsistent formats, in scattered locations, with no indexing and no retention policy. The result is an archive that is technically comprehensive but practically useless — the information exists but cannot be found when it is needed. This article establishes the standards that make administrative records findable, readable, and useful across decades.

Scope

In scope:

  • Categories of administrative records and retention requirements for each.
  • Format standards for administrative records (file formats, naming conventions, structural requirements).
  • Indexing and cataloguing requirements.
  • Storage locations and backup requirements for administrative records.
  • Record retirement: when and how records are moved from active to archival status.
  • Record destruction: circumstances under which records may be permanently deleted.
  • Integration with the institutional documentation management system (Domain 6).

Out of scope:

  • Technical documentation standards (Domain 6 articles).
  • Governance decision records (GOV-001 decision log).
  • Security-sensitive records (Domain 3, which defines its own handling requirements).
  • Specific form templates (D11-009).

Background

Records as Institutional Memory

D11-001 establishes that administrative records are the institution's memory of its physical self. This article operationalizes that principle by defining exactly how that memory is organized. The analogy to human memory is instructive: a person with perfect recall but no organizational structure for their memories — no categories, no associations, no ability to retrieve a specific memory on demand — is functionally no better than a person who has forgotten everything. Institutional records have the same property. The act of recording is necessary but not sufficient. The act of organizing, indexing, and maintaining those records is what transforms raw data into usable memory.

The 50-Year Readability Requirement

The institution's founding mandate requires a 50-year operational horizon. This has direct implications for record formats. Any record stored in a format that depends on proprietary software, specific hardware, or a particular operating system may become unreadable within a decade. The history of computing is littered with dead formats: WordStar documents, Lotus 1-2-3 spreadsheets, FileMaker databases, and countless others that are now accessible only through heroic digital archaeology.

The record-keeping standards defined here therefore mandate formats that are human-readable without specialized software. Plain text, CSV, and simple HTML are the primary formats. These formats have survived every computing era since their invention and are as close to universal as any digital format can be. The cost of this constraint is reduced formatting elegance. The benefit is guaranteed readability across decades.

The Volume Problem

Over 50 years, even a modestly administered institution will generate thousands of records: purchase receipts, maintenance logs, audit reports, budget documents, personnel records, and correspondence. Without a systematic approach to organization and retention, this volume becomes unmanageable. The record-keeping standards must therefore address not just how records are created but how the record archive itself is managed as it grows.

System Model

Record Categories and Retention Periods

Administrative records are organized into six categories, each with its own retention period:

CategoryExamplesActive PeriodArchive PeriodTotal Retention
Financial RecordsBudget documents, purchase receipts, expense logs, audit reportsCurrent year + 2 years20 years23 years minimum
Asset RecordsAsset registry entries, maintenance logs, decommission recordsLife of asset + 2 yearsPermanentPermanent
Personnel RecordsOnboarding records, skills inventories, succession plans, exit recordsDuration of association + 5 yearsPermanentPermanent
Operational RecordsScheduling histories, procurement logs, vendor correspondenceCurrent year + 1 year10 years12 years minimum
Audit RecordsSelf-audit reports, reconciliation records, variance analysesCurrent cycle + 1 cycle15 years18 years minimum
Institutional RecordsFounding documents, charter amendments, major decision recordsPermanentN/APermanent

Format Standards

All administrative records must conform to the following format requirements:

  • Text Records: Plain text (UTF-8 encoding), Markdown, or simple HTML. No word processor formats. No PDF unless accompanied by a plain-text equivalent.
  • Tabular Data: CSV (comma-separated values) with a header row. No spreadsheet formats. Complex tables may use TSV (tab-separated values) when commas appear in data fields.
  • Structured Data: JSON or YAML for machine-readable records. Both are human-readable with a text editor and do not require specialized software.
  • Images: JPEG or PNG for photographs and scans. SVG for diagrams. No proprietary image formats.
  • Naming Convention: All record files follow the pattern: [CATEGORY]-[YYYY]-[MM]-[DD]-[brief-description].[ext]. Example: FINANCIAL-2026-02-17-q1-budget-review.txt. This convention ensures chronological sorting and category identification from the filename alone.

Indexing

The administrative record archive maintains a master index: a single CSV file that lists every record with the following fields: filename, category, date created, date archived (if applicable), brief description, related records (cross-references), and retention expiry date. The master index is updated whenever a record is created, archived, or retired. It is the primary tool for finding records and must be kept current.

The master index is backed up alongside the records themselves. A copy of the master index is maintained in both digital and printed form. The printed form is updated quarterly and serves as a backup in case digital access is compromised.

Storage Architecture

Administrative records are stored in a dedicated directory structure within the institutional storage system:

/admin-records/
  /active/
    /financial/
    /assets/
    /personnel/
    /operational/
    /audit/
    /institutional/
  /archive/
    /[year]/
      /[category]/
  master-index.csv

Active records reside in category subdirectories. When a record's active period expires, it is moved to the archive directory, organized by year and category. The master index is updated to reflect the move. Administrative records are included in the institution's standard backup procedures (Domain 6) and are treated as critical data.

Record Retirement and Destruction

When a record's total retention period expires, it enters the retirement process:

  1. Review: The record is reviewed to confirm that its retention period has genuinely expired and that no ongoing reference to it exists.
  2. Historical Assessment: If the record has potential historical or institutional value beyond its retention period, it may be reclassified as an Institutional Record (permanent retention). This is a judgment call made by the operator and documented in the master index.
  3. Destruction: Records that pass review and are not reclassified are permanently deleted. The deletion is recorded in the master index with the date and reason (retention period expired). Destroyed records cannot be recovered; this is intentional. The retention periods are designed to be conservative, and permanent storage of all records would eventually create an unmanageable archive.

Rules and Constraints

  • R-REC-01: Every administrative action that creates or consumes institutional resources must generate a record. No purchase, no maintenance event, no personnel change, and no audit may occur without a corresponding record.
  • R-REC-02: All records must conform to the format standards defined in this article. Records in non-compliant formats must be converted at or before archival.
  • R-REC-03: The master index must be updated within 48 hours of any record creation, archival, or retirement. An index that is more than one week out of date is in violation and must be corrected immediately.
  • R-REC-04: No record with a "Permanent" retention classification may be destroyed under any circumstances except by a Tier 1 governance decision (GOV-001) with documented justification.
  • R-REC-05: Records containing sensitive information (personnel evaluations, security-related procurement details, access credentials) must be stored in the secure records area defined by Domain 3 and must follow Domain 3 handling requirements in addition to the standards defined here.
  • R-REC-06: A printed backup of the master index must be maintained and updated at least quarterly. This printed backup ensures that the record archive can be navigated even if all digital systems are unavailable.
  • R-REC-07: Record-keeping procedures must be reviewed annually for adherence to the Lightness Principle (D11-001). If record-keeping is consuming disproportionate time relative to its value, procedures should be simplified — but the simplification must not compromise the ability to find and read records when needed.
  • R-REC-08: Cross-references between related records must be maintained. When a procurement record references a budget line item, the budget record should reference the procurement record. Bidirectional linking makes the record archive navigable rather than merely stored.

Failure Modes

  • Index rot. The master index gradually falls out of sync with the actual records. Records exist that are not indexed; index entries point to records that have been moved or deleted. The index becomes unreliable and is eventually ignored. Mitigation: R-REC-03's 48-hour update requirement. Quarterly reconciliation between the index and the actual directory contents. Automated checksums where feasible.
  • Format drift. Over time, new records are created in formats that violate the standards — a quick note in a word processor, a spreadsheet instead of CSV, a scanned image without OCR text. The archive becomes a mix of formats that require multiple tools to read. Mitigation: R-REC-02 is enforced at archival. Records in non-compliant formats are converted before being accepted into the archive.
  • Over-retention. No records are ever retired because the operator is afraid of deleting something that might be needed. The archive grows without bound, becoming increasingly difficult to navigate and maintain. Mitigation: the retention periods defined in this article are designed to be conservative. Records that have expired should be retired through the defined process. The historical assessment step (Section: System Model) provides a safety valve for genuinely valuable records.
  • Under-recording. Administrative actions occur without generating records because the operator is too busy or considers the action too minor to document. Over time, significant institutional history exists only in memory. Mitigation: R-REC-01 requires a record for every resource-affecting action. The forms defined in D11-009 make record creation quick and standardized.
  • Archive inaccessibility. Records exist and are well-organized, but the storage medium becomes unreadable (drive failure, filesystem corruption). Mitigation: administrative records are included in the institution's backup procedures. The printed master index provides a map to the archive even if digital access is temporarily lost.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If the master index is lost or corrupted: Reconstruct the index from the directory structure. Walk through every file in the administrative records directory, recording its filename, category (from the directory location and naming convention), date, and a brief description. This is labor-intensive but straightforward if the naming convention has been followed. The reconstructed index may lack cross-references, which should be rebuilt over time as records are accessed.
  2. If records have been stored in non-compliant formats: Conduct a format audit. Identify all non-compliant records. Prioritize conversion by category: financial and asset records first (permanent or long retention), operational records last (shorter retention). Convert each record to a compliant format and update the master index. If the original format cannot be read, this is a data loss event and should be documented as a lesson learned.
  3. If significant recording gaps exist: Identify the period and categories affected. For financial records, reconstruct from bank statements, receipts, and memory. For asset records, conduct a physical audit and create current-state records even if historical records cannot be recovered. For operational records, accept the gap and document it. Focus on preventing future gaps by simplifying recording procedures if the gap was caused by excessive burden.
  4. If the archive storage has failed: Restore from backup (Domain 6 procedures). If backup is also unavailable, the printed master index identifies what was lost. Reconstruct what can be reconstructed from surviving documents, memory, and external records (vendor correspondence, bank records). Document the loss and adjust backup procedures to prevent recurrence.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: The record-keeping system is being established. The master index is small. Format compliance is easy because the archive is young. The primary challenge is building the habit of recording every administrative action. The naming convention and directory structure should be established from the first record and maintained consistently.
  • Years 3-10: The archive is growing. The master index may reach hundreds of entries. Cross-referencing becomes increasingly valuable as the web of connections between records grows. The first records may be approaching their active-period expiry and moving to archive status. Retention policies get their first real exercise.
  • Years 10-25: The archive is substantial. The first records are approaching retirement eligibility. The retirement process is exercised for the first time. Storage format evolution may require attention: are the formats chosen in Year 0 still readable with current tools? They should be, if the standards have been followed, but verification is prudent.
  • Years 25-50+: The archive spans decades. It is a significant institutional asset in its own right. Successor operators may find the early records invaluable for understanding the institution's material history. The archive management procedures must have scaled gracefully from dozens of records to thousands. The master index is the key: if it is current and accurate, the archive is navigable regardless of its size.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 — Founding Entry: The temptation with record-keeping is to go to one of two extremes: record nothing (because it feels like overhead) or record everything in elaborate detail (because it feels thorough). Both extremes fail. Recording nothing creates institutional amnesia. Recording everything creates an archive so vast that finding anything requires more effort than recreating the information from scratch. The standards in this article are my attempt at the middle path: record the things that matter, in formats that last, organized in ways that make them findable. I expect to struggle most with the master index — updating it within 48 hours of every record is a discipline I do not yet have. But the index is the spine of the entire system. Without it, the records are a pile. With it, they are a library.
