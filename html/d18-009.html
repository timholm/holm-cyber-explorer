D18-009 — Rejection, Appeal & Disposition

Document ID
D18-009
Domain
18 -- Import & Quarantine
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D18-002 (Validation Pipeline), D18-008 (Trust Scoring)
Depended Upon By
D18-010 (Batch Import), D18-014 (Operator Training)

Purpose

This article defines what happens when content fails the import validation pipeline -- the procedures for rejection, the mechanisms for appeal, and the disposition of rejected content. D18-001 established that rejection must be documented (R-D18-04). This article operationalizes that principle into a complete lifecycle for rejected content.

Rejection is not the end of a process. It is a decision with consequences that must be managed. Rejected content must be disposed of properly. The reasons for rejection must be recorded so that the institution can learn from its decisions. There must be a mechanism for challenging a rejection decision, because import operators are human and make mistakes, and because the institution's standards evolve over time -- content rejected today might be acceptable under tomorrow's criteria.

This article is addressed to import operators who make rejection decisions, to the individuals or systems that may wish to appeal those decisions, and to future maintainers who must understand why the institution rejected what it rejected.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The categories of rejection and their definitions.
  • The documentation requirements for every rejection decision.
  • The appeal process: who may appeal, on what grounds, and how appeals are adjudicated.
  • Re-submission procedures: when and how rejected content may be re-submitted.
  • Permanent exclusion criteria: when content is barred from re-submission.
  • Physical disposition of rejected content: destruction, return, or archival of rejected media.
  • Metrics and monitoring for rejection rates.

This article does not cover:

  • The validation checks that produce pass/fail results (see D18-002, Validation Pipeline).
  • Trust scoring that influences rejection thresholds (see D18-008, Trust Scoring).
  • Dangerous content handling, which has its own disposition procedures (see D18-003).
  • The admission process for content that passes validation (covered by the pipeline's terminal stages).

Background

Why Rejection Must Be a First-Class Process

In many systems, rejection is an afterthought -- a discard pile where things go without ceremony or record. This is dangerous for an institution that must learn from its import decisions over decades. Every rejection is a data point. Patterns of rejection reveal whether the pipeline is well-calibrated or over-restrictive. Individual rejections reveal whether the institution's standards are clear to the people submitting content. The record of rejections is as valuable as the record of admissions, because it documents the institution's boundaries -- what it chose not to include and why.

The Appeal Imperative

Any system that makes judgments must have a mechanism for challenging those judgments. Without appeal, rejection is absolute and potentially arbitrary. An operator having a bad day might reject content that another operator would admit. A validation check might have a false-positive rate that disproportionately affects certain types of content. An appeal process does not undermine the import pipeline's authority -- it strengthens it by providing a self-correction mechanism.

Disposition Is a Security Concern

Rejected content does not simply vanish. The physical media on which it arrived must be dealt with. Content that was rejected because it contained malware cannot be left in the quarantine zone indefinitely. Content that was rejected for format reasons may be returned to the carrier for re-submission in a different format. The disposition of rejected content has security implications (malware must be destroyed), logistical implications (media must be tracked), and diplomatic implications (a carrier whose content is rejected should understand why and what they can do about it).

Rejection Archive

The institution maintains a rejection archive -- a record (not necessarily the content itself) of every rejected item. The archive contains: the rejection record (category, reason, date, operator), the provenance record, the trust score assessment, and a content summary (sufficient to identify the content without retaining the full content). For non-security rejections, the rejection archive MAY also retain a sample or representative portion of the content for reference.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D18-R01: Every Rejection Documented. Every rejection MUST be documented in the import log with: the rejection category code, a human-readable explanation of the reason, the operator's identity, the date and time, and the trust score assessment at the time of rejection. Rejection without documentation is a procedural violation.

  • R-D18-R02: Appeal Right Exists. Every rejection (except REJ-SEC and REJ-POL) carries an implicit right of appeal. The appeal window is 90 days from the date of rejection. The existence of an appeal right MUST be communicated to the carrier at the time of rejection.

  • R-D18-R03: Appeal Reviewed by Different Operator. An appeal MUST be reviewed by an operator other than the one who made the original rejection decision. If only one operator is available, the appeal MUST be deferred until a second operator is available or escalated to governance authority.

  • R-D18-R04: Permanent Exclusions Are Rare. Permanent exclusion (no re-submission) SHOULD be applied only for security rejections (REJ-SEC) and policy rejections (REJ-POL). All other rejection categories SHOULD permit re-submission after the basis for rejection has been addressed.

  • R-D18-R05: Rejection Rate Monitoring. The institution MUST track rejection rates by category on a quarterly basis. If the overall rejection rate exceeds 40% of all import attempts, or if any single category exceeds 25%, a pipeline review MUST be triggered to determine whether the pipeline is over-restrictive or whether submitters need better guidance.

  • R-D18-R06: Secure Destruction for Security Rejections. Content rejected under REJ-SEC MUST be securely destroyed per SEC-001 disposal procedures. The media on which the content arrived MUST be destroyed or sanitized. No copy of security-rejected content may be retained in any institutional system, including quarantine. The rejection record (without the content) is retained.

  • R-D18-R07: Disposition Within 30 Days. Rejected content MUST be disposed of (destroyed, returned, or archived) within 30 days of rejection. Content that remains in quarantine past 30 days after rejection is a housekeeping failure and MUST be escalated for immediate disposition.

Failure Modes

  • Rejection without documentation. An operator rejects content but fails to record the reason, creating a gap in the import log. The institution loses the ability to learn from the decision or to support an appeal. Likelihood: Moderate, especially under time pressure. Severity: Medium. Detection: Audit of import logs for rejection records with missing fields. Automated completeness checking at the time of rejection entry.

  • Appeal process bottleneck. Appeals accumulate because the second-operator review requirement creates scheduling conflicts. Content remains in limbo. Carriers lose confidence in the institution's fairness. Likelihood: Moderate if import volume is high and operator count is low. Severity: Medium. Detection: Track appeal resolution times. Escalate if any appeal exceeds 30 days without review.

  • Excessive rejection rate. The pipeline rejects so much content that the institution effectively stops growing. The rejection rate may be justified (genuinely poor-quality submissions) or unjustified (over-restrictive pipeline). Likelihood: Moderate over time as security-conscious operators err on the side of caution. Severity: High if sustained. Detection: R-D18-R05 rejection rate monitoring.

  • Rejection archive grows unmanageably. Over decades, the rejection archive accumulates thousands of records, many for content types, sources, or formats that are no longer relevant. The archive becomes difficult to search and maintain. Likelihood: High over a 50-year horizon. Severity: Low (the archive is reference material, not operational). Detection: Periodic archive review and compaction.

  • Permanent exclusion misapplied. Content is permanently excluded when it should have been given a re-submission path. The institution loses access to potentially valuable content with no recourse. Likelihood: Low if the category definitions are followed. Severity: Medium. Detection: Governance review of all permanent exclusion decisions annually.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If a rejection was made in error: The appeal process is the primary recovery mechanism. If the appeal is sustained, the content re-enters the validation pipeline. If the content has already been destroyed or returned, the rejection record is annotated with the error finding, and the carrier is notified that re-submission is welcome. The original operator should be debriefed (not punished) to understand the cause of the error.

  2. If rejection documentation is missing: Attempt to reconstruct the basis for rejection from the validation pipeline logs, quarantine station logs, and operator memory (if the rejection is recent). Document the reconstruction. If the basis cannot be reconstructed, annotate the record as "documentation gap -- basis for rejection unknown." If the content is still in quarantine, re-validate before disposing of it.

  3. If the appeal process is backlogged: Temporarily authorize senior operators to conduct self-review of their own rejections (documented as self-review, not a standard appeal). Prioritize appeals by content value and time in queue. If the backlog is structural (insufficient operators), escalate to governance for resource allocation.

  4. If the rejection rate indicates pipeline over-restriction: Conduct a calibration review. Examine a random sample of recent rejections. For each, ask: "Was this rejection justified? Would a second operator have reached the same conclusion?" If more than 20% of sampled rejections are questionable, the pipeline requires adjustment. Identify the specific validation checks or trust thresholds that are producing excessive rejections and recalibrate.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Rejection procedures are established and refined through practice. Expect the rejection category definitions to be adjusted as real-world edge cases reveal gaps in the initial taxonomy. The appeal process may be underutilized at first simply because the institution is small and decisions are made collaboratively. Formalize the process anyway -- it will be needed as the institution grows.

  • Years 5-15: The rejection archive becomes a valuable analytical resource. Pattern analysis of rejections reveals: common reasons for failure (guiding submitter education), pipeline checks with high false-positive rates (guiding pipeline tuning), and shifts in the content landscape (guiding format recognition updates). The appeal process should be functioning smoothly, with clear precedents guiding consistent decisions.

  • Years 15-30: Rejection precedents accumulate into an informal case law. The institution may benefit from a "rejection digest" -- a periodically compiled summary of notable rejection and appeal decisions that helps new operators understand how the pipeline's boundaries have been interpreted in practice.

  • Years 30-50+: The rejection archive spans decades. Historical rejections may be revisited as standards evolve -- content rejected in 2030 for format reasons may be admissible in 2060 once format conversion tools have improved. The archive becomes a source of institutional self-awareness: what did we refuse, and were we right?

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The most important thing about rejection is that it must not feel arbitrary. A carrier who brings content to the institution and has it rejected must understand why. If the explanation is "trust score too low," that is insufficient. The operator must be able to explain which dimension was low and what the carrier could do to improve it. "Your source is unknown to us; if you can provide evidence of the author's identity and credentials, we can re-assess" is a useful rejection. "Trust score 1, rejected" is not.

The 90-day appeal window is a compromise. Shorter windows (30 days) were considered but rejected because content carriers may not be in regular contact with the institution (especially in an air-gapped context where communication is infrequent). Longer windows (180 days, 1 year) were considered but rejected because they leave rejection decisions unresolved for too long and create uncertainty about quarantine storage obligations.

Permanent exclusion for security rejections (REJ-SEC) is intentionally strict. Content that contained malware or active exploits should never be re-submitted because even a "cleaned" version may retain hidden threats. This strictness may occasionally exclude content that was contaminated without the source's knowledge and could be re-submitted clean. The institution accepts this cost. The risk of re-admitting once-hostile content is greater than the risk of losing potentially valuable content from a source whose media was once compromised.
