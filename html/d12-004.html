D12-004 — Failure Taxonomy and Threat Model

Document ID
D12-004
Domain
12 -- Disaster Recovery
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D12-001, SEC-001, CON-001, ETH-001, D4-001, D5-002
Depended Upon By
D12-005 through D12-014. All Domain 12 articles derive their scope from this taxonomy. D13-002 (fragility data for migration planning).

Purpose

This article classifies every credible failure that the holm.chat Documentation Institution may face over a fifty-year operational horizon. It is the threat model for disaster recovery -- the comprehensive enumeration of what can go wrong, how likely it is, how severe its consequences would be, and which recovery procedures address it.

D12-001 established the certainty of catastrophe as a foundational premise. This article gives that certainty a structure. Without a taxonomy, disaster recovery is reactive improvisation. With a taxonomy, it becomes a systematic discipline: every failure class has an assessed probability, a defined severity, a known blast radius, and a mapped recovery path.

The taxonomy is deliberately pessimistic. If there is uncertainty about whether a failure mode is credible, it is included. The cost of preparing for a failure that never occurs is low. The cost of failing to prepare for a failure that does occur can be terminal. This article errs on the side of inclusion.

This is the largest and most frequently revised article in Domain 12. As the institution evolves -- as hardware changes, as new systems are added, as the environment shifts -- the threat model must evolve with it. A threat model that describes the institution as it existed five years ago is worse than no threat model at all, because it creates false confidence.

Scope

In scope:

  • Classification of all failure types into seven primary categories: hardware, software, data, personnel, facility, supply chain, and knowledge.
  • Probability assessment for each failure class over 1-year, 10-year, and 50-year horizons.
  • Severity assessment using a five-level impact scale.
  • Blast radius analysis: which systems and functions are affected by each failure class.
  • Mapping between failure classes and recovery procedures defined in other Domain 12 articles.
  • Compound and cascade failure scenarios.

Out of scope:

  • Specific recovery procedures (those belong in D12-005 through D12-014).
  • Security-specific threat modeling for adversarial attacks (SEC-001; this article covers accidental and environmental failures).
  • Backup strategy design (D6-002; this article identifies what needs protection, not how to protect it).
  • Research into novel failure modes (Domain 14).

Background

3.1 The Seven Failure Domains

Every failure the institution can experience falls into one or more of seven categories. These categories are not arbitrary; they correspond to the fundamental dependencies of the institution: the physical substrate (hardware, facility), the logical substrate (software, data), the human substrate (personnel, knowledge), and the external substrate (supply chain).

Most real-world failures are multi-domain events. A power surge (facility) damages a hard drive (hardware) causing data corruption (data) that is not immediately detected (software/knowledge). The taxonomy treats each domain separately for analysis, but Section 4.6 addresses compound failures explicitly.

3.2 Probability and Severity Calibration

Probability estimates in this article are based on publicly available reliability data, industry failure statistics, and the specific constraints of this institution's air-gapped, single-operator environment. They are not precise predictions. They are calibrated estimates intended to guide prioritization.

The probability categories are: Rare (less than 5% over 50 years), Unlikely (5-25% over 50 years), Possible (25-50% over 50 years), Likely (50-90% over 50 years), and Near-Certain (greater than 90% over 50 years).

The severity categories are: Negligible (no data loss, no service interruption exceeding one hour), Minor (no data loss, service interruption of hours to one day), Moderate (potential minor data loss, service interruption of days), Severe (potential significant data loss, service interruption of weeks), and Catastrophic (potential total data loss, institutional survival threatened).

3.3 The Air-Gap Amplifier Revisited

D12-001 Section 3.3 introduced the air-gap amplifier: the principle that the institution's isolation amplifies the impact of many failures. This article applies that principle concretely. Every failure class is assessed both in general terms and with the air-gap amplification factor considered. A failure that would be Minor for an internet-connected system may be Moderate or Severe for this institution, because the remediation resources available to connected systems (online documentation, vendor support, cloud failover, rapid parts procurement) are unavailable here.

4.7 Compound Failure Scenarios

The most dangerous scenarios involve multiple simultaneous failures. These compound events are individually unlikely but collectively not improbable over fifty years. The following scenarios are specifically modeled:

  • Compound A: Hardware failure + backup corruption. The primary storage fails, and the most recent backup is discovered to be unreadable. Recovery depends on the next-oldest backup. Probability over 50 years: Possible. Severity: Severe. Recovery: D12-006 Section 7.
  • Compound B: Facility loss + operator absence. The site is destroyed while the operator is away or incapacitated. Recovery depends entirely on the off-site rebuild kit and successor readiness. Probability over 50 years: Unlikely. Severity: Catastrophic. Recovery: D12-003, GOV-004.
  • Compound C: Data corruption + detection failure. Silent corruption propagates through multiple backup generations before being detected. All recent backups contain the corrupted data. Recovery requires finding the last clean backup, which may be months or years old. Probability over 50 years: Possible. Severity: Severe-Catastrophic. Recovery: D12-008 Section 7, D6-003.
  • Compound D: Supply chain collapse + hardware failure. A critical component fails and cannot be replaced because the manufacturer has ceased production and no compatible alternative exists. The institution must migrate to entirely different hardware. Probability over 50 years: Likely. Severity: Severe. Recovery: D13-002, D12-003.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D12-04-01: Every failure mode in this taxonomy must map to at least one recovery procedure in another Domain 12 article. A failure mode without a recovery procedure is an unaddressed vulnerability.
  • R-D12-04-02: This taxonomy must be reviewed and updated every two years as part of the scheduled Domain 12 review cycle (Section 12.7.1 of domain-12.html). It must also be updated after any real incident or after any significant system change.
  • R-D12-04-03: Probability estimates must be recalibrated after every real failure event. A failure that was estimated as Rare but has occurred moves to at least Possible until evidence supports a different assessment.
  • R-D12-04-04: Any failure mode assessed as both Near-Certain in probability and Severe or Catastrophic in severity must have its recovery procedure tested via drill (D12-002) at least annually.
  • R-D12-04-05: Disputes about whether a failure mode is credible are resolved by inclusion. A failure mode may only be removed from the taxonomy by unanimous agreement that it is no longer credible, documented in the decision log.
  • R-D12-04-06: The compound failure scenarios in Section 4.7 must be included in the DR drill rotation (D12-002). At least one compound scenario must be drilled per year.

Failure Modes

  • Taxonomy incompleteness. The taxonomy fails to include a failure mode that subsequently occurs. The institution is unprepared for a predictable event. Mitigation: the pessimistic inclusion principle (R-D12-04-05). The biennial review requirement (R-D12-04-02). The post-incident review process (D12-013) which identifies new failure modes from real events.
  • Probability miscalibration. The probability estimates are systematically too optimistic or too pessimistic. If too optimistic, the institution under-prepares. If too pessimistic, it over-invests in preparation at the expense of operations. Mitigation: calibrate against industry data where available. Update after real events (R-D12-04-03). Accept that estimates are approximate and focus on relative ranking rather than absolute precision.
  • Taxonomy staleness. The taxonomy describes the institution as it existed years ago, not as it exists today. New systems have been added without being included in the failure analysis. Old systems have been decommissioned but still appear in the taxonomy. Mitigation: R-D12-04-02 requires updates after significant system changes. The annual drill cycle (D12-002) provides a regular opportunity to discover discrepancies.
  • False completeness. The existence of the taxonomy creates a false sense that all risks are accounted for. The operator stops thinking creatively about what could go wrong because the taxonomy "already covers everything." Mitigation: the commentary section of this article should note novel threat observations. The adversarial red-team review (Section 12.6 of domain-12.html) actively challenges the taxonomy's completeness.

Recovery Procedures

The recovery procedures for this article address failures of the taxonomy itself -- the meta-failure of having an inadequate threat model.

  1. If a failure occurs that is not in the taxonomy: First, manage the immediate crisis using the most applicable existing recovery procedure. Second, after the crisis is resolved, add the new failure mode to the taxonomy with a post-incident analysis of its probability and severity. Third, map it to an existing or new recovery procedure. Fourth, conduct a broader review: if this failure was missed, what other failures might be missing?
  2. If probability estimates prove systematically wrong: Conduct a full recalibration. For each failure mode that has actually occurred, compare the predicted probability against the observed frequency. Adjust estimates accordingly. For failure modes that have never occurred, consider whether the original assessment is still valid given changes to the institution's environment and technology.
  3. If the taxonomy has become stale: Conduct a top-to-bottom audit. For each failure mode, verify: does the referenced system still exist? Is the probability estimate still reasonable? Is the recovery procedure still valid? Remove decommissioned systems. Add new systems. Update probability estimates. Complete the audit within 30 days.
  4. If compound scenarios are discovered that are not modeled: Add them to Section 4.7. Assess their probability and severity. Map them to recovery procedures. Include them in the drill rotation.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The initial taxonomy is written against the founding infrastructure. It will be incomplete. The first few years of operation will reveal failure modes that were not anticipated. Every real incident and every drill finding should feed back into the taxonomy. Expect heavy revision during this period.
  • Years 5-15: The taxonomy stabilizes as the institution's infrastructure matures. New entries slow. The focus shifts to maintaining accuracy: updating probability estimates, reflecting hardware changes, and keeping recovery procedure mappings current. The compound failure scenarios become more important as the institution's complexity grows.
  • Years 15-30: Major technology transitions force significant taxonomy revision. Hardware categories that were central become legacy. New hardware categories appear. Supply chain failures move from theoretical to actual. The taxonomy's historical data -- which failures actually occurred and at what frequency -- becomes the best calibration tool for future estimates.
  • Years 30-50+: The taxonomy has been through multiple complete revisions. The accumulated history of actual failures is more valuable than any theoretical probability model. The taxonomy's value is no longer in its predictions but in its structure: the discipline of systematically enumerating, assessing, and mapping failures to recovery procedures.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: Building a threat model for fifty years feels like hubris. The world in 2076 will contain threats I cannot imagine, running on technology I cannot predict. And yet the exercise is valuable precisely because it forces honesty about what is fragile.

The failure modes I worry about most are the slow ones. A house fire is dramatic but obvious -- you know the institution has been destroyed. Bit rot is invisible. Configuration drift is invisible. Knowledge decay is invisible. These slow failures can erode the institution's integrity for years before anyone notices. The taxonomy reflects this concern: the data failures and knowledge failures sections are given equal weight to the more dramatic hardware and facility failures, even though they are less emotionally compelling.

I have deliberately included personnel failures that are uncomfortable to contemplate -- my own death, my own abandonment of the project. These are real risks over a fifty-year horizon and pretending otherwise would violate ETH-001 Principle 6: honest accounting of limitations.
