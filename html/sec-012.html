SEC-012 — Data Integrity and Provenance

Document ID
SEC-012
Domain
3 -- Security & Integrity
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
SEC-010 (Cryptographic Principles), SEC-003 (Key Management), ETH-001 (Ethical Foundations)
Depended Upon By
SEC-008 (Security Audit Framework), Domain 5 (Platform), Domain 6 (Data & Archives)

Purpose

This article defines how the holm.chat Documentation Institution ensures the integrity and provenance of its data. Integrity means that data has not been altered -- accidentally or deliberately -- since its last verified state. Provenance means that the origin, authorship, and chain of custody of every piece of data is known and documented.

These two properties are the foundation upon which the institution's value rests. A documentation institution whose documents cannot be trusted to be accurate is worthless. A documentation institution whose documents have no verifiable origin is unreliable. The institution exists to preserve knowledge over decades; if that knowledge can be silently corrupted or its origin obscured, the institution has failed its core purpose regardless of how well its other systems function.

This document establishes the mechanisms -- hash chains, digital signatures, integrity verification procedures, and provenance records -- that make tampering detectable and origins traceable. It does not assume that tampering will be attempted maliciously. The far more likely threats to data integrity are bit rot, software bugs, operator error, and storage media degradation. The mechanisms defined here protect against all of these, not merely adversarial attacks.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The integrity verification model: how the institution detects unauthorized or accidental changes to data.
  • Hash chain construction: how individual file integrity checks are linked into a chain that provides tamper-evident history.
  • Digital signatures for authorship attribution and document authentication.
  • Provenance records: how the origin and modification history of every document is tracked.
  • Integrity verification schedules: how often verification is performed and what constitutes a failure.
  • The relationship between data integrity and backup verification.

This article does not cover the specific cryptographic algorithms used for hashing and signing (those are defined in SEC-010), the key management procedures for signing keys (those are in SEC-003), or the backup and archival storage procedures (those are in Domain 6 and OPS-001). This article defines the integrity and provenance framework. Other articles provide the specific tools and procedures.

Background

3.1 The Silent Threat of Data Corruption

The most dangerous data corruption is the kind that is not detected. A file that is obviously corrupted -- garbled text, unreadable format, missing content -- is a nuisance but not a disaster, because the corruption is visible and can be addressed by restoring from backup. A file that has been subtly altered -- a changed number, a flipped bit in a critical value, a silently truncated record -- may not be detected for months or years, during which time the corrupted version propagates to backups and the uncorrupted original is overwritten or aged out.

This is why integrity verification must be proactive, automated, and comprehensive. The operator cannot be expected to manually review every file for subtle changes. Cryptographic hash functions provide the mechanism: a hash computed at the time of creation or last verified modification serves as a fingerprint. If the file changes by even a single bit, the hash changes. Comparing the current hash to the stored hash reveals corruption instantly, regardless of how subtle.

3.2 Provenance in a Single-Author Institution

In a multi-author institution, provenance is primarily about identifying who wrote or modified a document. In a single-operator institution, the "who" is almost always the same person. But provenance remains critical for a different reason: it establishes when a document was created or modified, what its state was at each point in time, and how it arrived at its current state.

This temporal provenance is essential for a fifty-year institution. Thirty years from now, the operator (or a successor) may need to understand not just what a document says today, but what it said ten years ago, why it was changed, and whether the change was deliberate. Without provenance records, these questions are unanswerable. The document exists in its current form, and all history is lost.

3.3 Hash Chains as Institutional Memory

A hash chain is a sequence of hash values where each entry includes the hash of the previous entry, creating a tamper-evident chain. If any entry in the chain is modified, all subsequent entries become invalid, making the modification detectable. Hash chains are the same fundamental mechanism that underlies blockchain technology, but the institution uses them for a simpler and more focused purpose: creating an immutable record of data states over time.

The institution's hash chain serves as a cryptographic audit trail. It records not just the current state of each document, but the sequence of states through which each document has passed. This chain is stored in Zone A (the Archive Core) and is the ultimate reference for resolving disputes about what a document contained at any point in the past.

System Model

4.1 The Integrity Verification Model

Every file in the institution's canonical archive has an associated integrity record consisting of:

  • The file's path and name.
  • The file's size in bytes.
  • A SHA-3-256 hash of the file's contents.
  • A SHA-256 hash of the file's contents (dual-hash per SEC-010).
  • The timestamp of the hash computation.
  • The identity of the operator who computed the hash (relevant after succession).

These integrity records are stored in a manifest file that is itself hashed and included in the hash chain. The manifest is the ground truth for what the institution's data should look like. Any discrepancy between a file's current hash and the hash recorded in the manifest is an integrity failure that must be investigated per the incident response framework (SEC-011).

4.2 Hash Chain Construction

The institution maintains a hash chain that records the state of the archive at regular intervals and at every modification event. Each entry in the chain contains:

  • A sequence number (monotonically increasing, never reused).
  • The timestamp of the entry.
  • The hash of the current archive manifest.
  • A description of the event (e.g., "routine verification," "document SEC-012 created," "quarterly integrity check").
  • The hash of the previous chain entry.
  • A digital signature over the entire entry using the operator's signing key.

The chain is append-only. Entries are never modified or deleted. If an error is discovered in a previous entry, a new corrective entry is appended that references and corrects the error; the original erroneous entry remains in the chain as part of the historical record. This append-only property is enforced through the chaining mechanism: modifying any entry would invalidate all subsequent entries, making the modification detectable.

4.3 Provenance Records

Every document in the institution carries provenance metadata that records:

  • The document's original creation date.
  • The identity of the original author.
  • The method of creation (drafted by the operator, imported from external source, generated by automated process).
  • For imported documents: the external source, the date of import, and the quarantine verification record.
  • A complete modification history: the date, author, and summary of each modification.
  • The hash chain entry that corresponds to each state of the document.

Provenance metadata is stored both within the document itself (in the metadata section of each article, as visible in this document's header) and in a separate provenance database that provides a centralized view of all document histories. The two records must be consistent; discrepancies between in-document metadata and the provenance database are treated as integrity findings.

4.4 Verification Schedules

Integrity verification is performed on three schedules:

Event-driven verification. Whenever a document is created, modified, or restored from backup, its hashes are computed and recorded in the manifest and the hash chain. This provides a verified baseline for the new state.

Periodic full verification. Once per quarter, every file in the archive is rehashed and compared against the manifest. Any discrepancy is a finding. This catches silent corruption (bit rot, media degradation) that would not be detected by event-driven verification alone.

Spot-check verification. During each operational session, a random sample of files (at least 5% of the archive, or 20 files, whichever is greater) is verified against the manifest. This provides a statistical early warning system for emerging corruption patterns between full verifications.

4.5 Backup Integrity Correlation

Integrity verification and backup verification are complementary but distinct processes. The integrity system verifies that the current live data matches its expected state. The backup system verifies that backup copies match the live data (or a known-good historical state). The correlation between these two systems provides the institution's strongest defense against data loss: if the live data is corrupted, the backup provides recovery. If the backup is corrupted, the live data provides the reference. If both are corrupted, the hash chain reveals the last known-good state, and the operator can determine which version to trust or whether recovery from a more distant backup is necessary.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-DI-01: Every file in the institutional archive must have a corresponding integrity record in the manifest. Files without integrity records are unverified and must not be treated as authoritative.
  • R-DI-02: Integrity records must use the dual-hash scheme defined in SEC-010 (SHA-3-256 and SHA-256). Single-hash records are insufficient because they do not provide defense against the compromise of a single hash function.
  • R-DI-03: The hash chain must be append-only. No mechanism for modifying or deleting chain entries may exist in the system. If the underlying storage does not enforce append-only semantics natively, the chain's integrity must be verified by checking the chaining relationship (each entry's previous-hash field must match the hash of the preceding entry) at every full verification.
  • R-DI-04: Every modification to a document must result in a new hash chain entry. Documents must not be modified without updating the chain. This is not a guideline; it is a hard requirement. An unrecorded modification is indistinguishable from tampering.
  • R-DI-05: Full archive verification must be performed at least quarterly. Spot-check verification must be performed at every operational session. These schedules may be made more frequent but never less frequent.
  • R-DI-06: Any integrity discrepancy detected during verification must be treated as a security incident per SEC-011 and classified at Severity 2 or higher until the cause is determined. Integrity discrepancies are never dismissed without investigation.
  • R-DI-07: Provenance metadata must be maintained for every document. Documents without provenance records are considered to have unknown origin and must be treated with the same caution as external imports (quarantine and verification per Domain 18).
  • R-DI-08: The hash chain and archive manifest must be stored in Zone A (Archive Core) with the highest protection level. Copies may exist in other zones for operational convenience, but the Zone A copy is authoritative.

Failure Modes

  • Silent corruption undetected between verifications. Bit rot or media degradation corrupts a file between quarterly verifications, and the corrupted version is backed up before the corruption is detected. Mitigation: spot-check verifications between full verifications provide probabilistic early detection. The dual-hash scheme means that corruption must defeat two independent hash functions to go undetected (vanishingly unlikely for random corruption). Geographic distribution of backups means that at least one backup should contain the uncorrupted version.
  • Hash chain divergence. Two copies of the hash chain (e.g., the live copy and a backup copy) diverge due to a failure to propagate updates. Mitigation: the chain contains sequence numbers and timestamps that allow divergence to be detected and the authoritative copy to be identified. The Zone A copy is always authoritative.
  • Provenance record loss. The provenance metadata for a document is lost or corrupted, leaving the document's history unknown. Mitigation: provenance is stored in two locations (in-document and in the provenance database). Loss of one copy can be recovered from the other. Loss of both is an integrity incident requiring investigation.
  • Verification fatigue. The operator begins to treat verification as a routine chore rather than a critical security operation. Failures are dismissed or deferred. Mitigation: R-DI-06 mandates that all discrepancies are treated as incidents. The audit framework (SEC-008) includes verification compliance as an audit checkpoint.
  • Manifest corruption. The manifest itself becomes corrupted, making it impossible to verify the archive. Mitigation: the manifest is included in the hash chain, so its expected state is recorded. The manifest is also backed up with the same redundancy as the archive data. Recovery from manifest corruption requires restoring the manifest from a verified backup and re-verifying the entire archive against it.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If an integrity discrepancy is detected during verification: Immediately classify as a Severity 2 incident. Determine whether the discrepancy is in the file or in the manifest (compare both hashes; if both hashes disagree with the file but agree with each other, the file has changed; if the hashes disagree with each other, the manifest may be corrupted). If the file has changed, compare against the most recent backup to determine whether the change was recent. Restore from the most recent backup whose hash matches the manifest. Update the hash chain to record the incident and the restoration.
  2. If hash chain corruption is detected: Identify the point at which the chain diverges from expected values. Restore the chain from a backup copy. If no backup copy is available, reconstruct the chain from the current archive state, recording the reconstruction as a chain entry. The reconstructed chain will have a gap in its history corresponding to the period of corruption. Document this gap.
  3. If provenance records are lost: Reconstruct as much provenance information as possible from the hash chain (which records creation and modification events), from in-document metadata, from backup copies of the provenance database, and from the operator's memory and notes. Document the reconstruction process and note any provenance gaps as permanent uncertainties in the institutional record.
  4. If the manifest is corrupted: Restore the manifest from the most recent verified backup. Re-verify the entire archive against the restored manifest. Any files that fail verification against the restored manifest must be investigated individually -- they may have been legitimately modified after the backup was taken, or they may be corrupted. Use the hash chain to resolve ambiguities.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-2: Establish the integrity verification infrastructure. Compute initial hashes for all existing data. Initialize the hash chain. Build the provenance database. These are foundational tasks that must be completed before the institution can claim to have verified data integrity.
  • Years 2-5: Refine the verification schedules based on operational experience. If spot-check verifications consistently find no issues, the schedule may be adequate. If discrepancies are found, increase verification frequency. Automate as much of the verification process as possible to reduce the burden on the operator and the risk of human error.
  • Years 5-15: The archive will have grown significantly. Verification times will increase. Optimize the verification process for larger archives. Consider incremental verification strategies that focus on recently modified files while still performing periodic full verification. The hash chain will be long enough to provide meaningful historical analysis.
  • Years 15-30: Storage media generations will have changed. Data will have been migrated to new media, potentially multiple times. Each migration is an integrity event that must be verified through the hash chain. The provenance records will be deep enough to provide a rich history of the institution's documentary evolution.
  • Years 30-50+: The hash chain itself becomes a historical artifact of considerable value. It is the institution's cryptographic memory -- an unbroken record of every state the archive has occupied since its founding. Preserving the chain's integrity is as important as preserving the data it protects.

Founder Commentary

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: Data integrity is the promise that makes everything else meaningful. If you cannot trust that the documents in this institution say what they said when they were written, then the institution is not a knowledge repository. It is a collection of files of uncertain content. The difference between those two things is the difference between a library and a pile of paper.

The hash chain is the simplest and most powerful tool in this entire security framework. Each entry takes seconds to compute and bytes to store. But the chain, in aggregate, provides something extraordinary: a mathematical proof of the archive's history. Twenty years from now, if someone asks "was this document modified since 2026?", the chain can answer that question with cryptographic certainty. No human memory, no organizational policy, no filing system can provide that level of assurance. Only mathematics can.

Future maintainers: never let the chain break. Never skip a verification. Never dismiss a discrepancy. The chain is the institution's memory of itself, and it is more reliable than yours.
