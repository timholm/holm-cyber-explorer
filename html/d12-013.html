D12-013 — Post-Incident Review and Learning

Document ID
D12-013
Domain
12 -- Disaster Recovery
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D12-001, D12-002, D12-004, ETH-001, GOV-001
Depended Upon By
All Domain 12 articles (post-incident findings update all procedures). D14-001 (incident data as research input). D20-001 (incident history as institutional memory).

Purpose

This article defines how the institution learns from its failures. Every real incident -- every hardware failure, data corruption event, backup problem, or operational error -- contains information about the institution's actual resilience. The post-incident review process extracts that information, analyzes it, and feeds it back into the institution's procedures, threat model, and preparedness posture.

D12-001 R-DR-07 requires a post-incident review within 30 days of any real disaster event, with findings integrated into recovery procedures within 90 days. This article defines the methodology for that review: how to conduct it, what questions to ask, how to identify root causes, how to generate actionable improvements, and how to ensure that the lessons learned actually change institutional behavior.

The post-incident review is blameless. In a single-operator institution, blame is meaningless -- the operator made the mistake, the operator suffered the consequences, and the operator must fix the problem. Self-blame is not only unproductive; it actively harms the review process by discouraging honesty. The review asks "what happened and why" and "what should change," never "whose fault was this."

Scope

In scope:

  • The post-incident review process and timeline.
  • Root cause analysis methodology adapted for a single-operator institution.
  • The Post-Incident Report template and required content.
  • Integration of findings into the institution's documentation and procedures.
  • Incident classification and severity assessment.
  • Learning from near-misses, not just actual incidents.

Out of scope:

  • Incident response procedures (those are defined in D12-007 through D12-012).
  • Drill after-action reports (D12-002 Section 4.5 defines the AAR for drills; this article covers real incidents).
  • Research into novel resilience techniques (Domain 14).

Background

3.1 The Learning Loop

The institution's disaster recovery capability is not static. It improves through a learning loop: prepare -> practice (drill) -> experience (real incident) -> review -> improve -> prepare again. Without the review step, the loop is broken. The institution experiences failure but does not learn from it. The same failure mode can recur, the same procedural gaps can persist, the same assumptions can go unchallenged.

The post-incident review is the mechanism that closes the loop. It converts raw experience into structured knowledge that can be preserved, communicated, and acted upon.

3.2 The Challenge of Self-Review

In a multi-person organization, post-incident reviews benefit from multiple perspectives: someone who was not involved in the incident can ask questions that the incident responder would not think to ask. In a single-operator institution, this external perspective is absent. The operator must review their own actions, their own decisions, and their own mistakes.

This is psychologically difficult. The structured methodology in this article compensates for the absence of external reviewers by providing a systematic series of questions that force the operator to examine the incident from multiple angles, including angles that are uncomfortable.

3.3 Near-Misses Are Incidents

A near-miss -- an event that could have caused damage but did not, due to luck or timely intervention -- is treated as an incident for review purposes. The near-miss revealed a vulnerability that still exists. The only difference between a near-miss and a real incident is luck, and luck is not a resilience strategy. Near-misses are reviewed using the same methodology as real incidents, with the same expectation of actionable findings.

System Model

4.1 Incident Classification

4.2 Root Cause Analysis Methodology

The root cause analysis uses the "Five Whys" method adapted for technical systems. Starting from the observable failure, the operator asks "why" repeatedly until they reach a root cause -- a cause that, if addressed, would have prevented the incident.

Process:

  1. Document the observable failure. What happened? When? What was the first symptom? What was the impact?
  2. Ask "Why did this happen?" Identify the immediate cause. (Example: "The backup was corrupted." Why?)
  3. Ask "Why?" again. Identify the cause of the cause. (Example: "The source data was corrupted before the backup was made." Why?)
  4. Continue asking "Why?" until a root cause is reached. A root cause is typically a missing safeguard, an assumption that was wrong, a procedure that was not followed, or a design weakness. (Example: "The integrity monitoring scan was overdue by two weeks, so the corruption was not detected before it was backed up." Root cause: integrity monitoring schedule was not enforced.)
  5. Verify the root cause. Ask: if this root cause had been addressed, would the incident have been prevented or detected earlier? If yes, it is a valid root cause. If no, continue asking "Why?"

Incident ID:     PIR-[YYYY]-[sequential number]
Date of Incident: [date]
Date of Review:   [date]
Severity:        [Critical / Major / Minor / Near-Miss]

1. INCIDENT SUMMARY
[What happened, in 2-3 sentences.]

[Chronological sequence of events from first symptom to resolution.

3. IMPACT

[Five Whys analysis, written as a narrative chain:]

[What went right during the incident and recovery.

[How will the institution verify that the recommended

4.4 Integration of Findings

Findings from the post-incident review must be integrated into the institution's documentation:

  1. Update the Failure Taxonomy (D12-004): If the incident revealed a new failure mode or invalidated a probability estimate, update the taxonomy.
  2. Update the affected recovery procedure: If the recovery procedure was inadequate, update it with the lessons learned.
  3. Update the drill scenarios (D12-002): If the incident represents a scenario not covered by existing drills, add a new scenario or modify an existing one.
  4. Update the cascade analysis (D12-012): If the incident involved cascade propagation, update the dependency map and isolation boundaries.
  5. Record in the operational log: The incident and its review are part of the institution's operational history.
  6. Archive the PIR: Post-incident reports are Tier 1 documents per D6-001, stored permanently as part of the institution's learning record.

4.5 Periodic Incident Review Summary

During the annual operations cycle, the operator should review all post-incident reports from the past year and produce a summary analysis:

  • How many incidents occurred? What severities?
  • Are there patterns? (Same component failing repeatedly, same procedure proving inadequate, same type of error recurring?)
  • Were all findings from previous PIRs addressed within their deadlines?
  • Has the institution's overall resilience posture improved, declined, or remained stable?

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D12-13-01: A post-incident review must be conducted for every real incident, regardless of severity, within the timeline defined in Section 4.1. Near-misses are included.
  • R-D12-13-02: The post-incident review must follow the methodology in Section 4.2 and produce a report per Section 4.3. Abbreviated reports are acceptable for Minor and Near-Miss incidents but must still include sections 1, 4, 6, and 7.
  • R-D12-13-03: Findings with Critical priority must have corrective actions initiated within 7 days. Major findings within 30 days. Minor findings within 90 days.
  • R-D12-13-04: Post-incident reports are Tier 1 documents. They must be backed up and retained permanently.
  • R-D12-13-05: The post-incident review must be honest. ETH-001 Principle 6 (Honest Accounting of Limitations) applies. If the operator made a mistake, the review must acknowledge it without minimization or rationalization. The purpose is learning, not self-protection.
  • R-D12-13-06: Integration of findings into affected documents (Section 4.4) must be completed within the timelines specified for each priority level. Completion must be verified and documented.

Failure Modes

  • Review avoidance. The operator, emotionally exhausted from the incident, postpones the review indefinitely. Lessons are lost. Mitigation: R-D12-13-01 mandates timelines. The operational schedule includes post-incident review as a non-deferrable task.
  • Superficial analysis. The review identifies the immediate cause but not the root cause. "The drive failed" is not a root cause analysis. Mitigation: the Five Whys methodology forces deeper investigation. A review that stops at the immediate cause is incomplete.
  • Findings without follow-through. The review produces excellent findings that are never implemented. The same vulnerability persists. Mitigation: R-D12-13-03 imposes deadlines. R-D12-13-06 requires verification. The annual summary (Section 4.5) tracks open findings.
  • Self-blame spiral. The operator, reviewing their own mistakes, falls into unproductive self-criticism that discourages honest analysis. Mitigation: the blameless principle stated in the Purpose section. The review structure focuses on systems and procedures, not personal shortcomings.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If a post-incident review has been missed: Conduct it now, even if the timeline has elapsed. Late is better than never. Note the delay in the report. If the incident was more than 90 days ago, acknowledge that details may be lost and document what can be reconstructed from logs and memory.
  2. If findings have accumulated without implementation: Conduct a finding audit. List all open findings. Prioritize by severity. Address Critical findings immediately. Schedule the rest. If the backlog is large, escalate to a governance decision: the institution's improvement process has failed and needs restructuring.
  3. If the same type of incident recurs: The previous post-incident review's recommendations were either inadequate or not implemented. Conduct a new review that explicitly examines why the previous recommendations failed to prevent recurrence. The root cause may be in the recommendation process itself, not just in the systems.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The post-incident review process is being established. Early incidents provide the first real test of the methodology. Expect the review template to be refined based on what information proves most useful.
  • Years 5-15: A library of post-incident reports accumulates. The annual summary analysis reveals patterns and trends. The institution's resilience posture is measurably improving because failures are systematically analyzed and addressed.
  • Years 15-30: The PIR library becomes one of the most valuable documents in the corpus. It records not just what failed but how the institution responded, what it learned, and how it changed. New operators can study the PIR library to understand the institution's failure history without experiencing it firsthand.
  • Years 30-50+: Decades of post-incident reviews constitute a comprehensive institutional learning record. The patterns visible in this record -- which components fail most often, which procedures are most fragile, which types of incidents recur despite remediation -- inform the institution's long-term evolution strategy.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The post-incident review is the article in Domain 12 that I most hope will be heavily used. That sounds counterintuitive -- who wants more incidents? But each incident that is properly reviewed makes the institution stronger. The institution that has experienced and learned from ten incidents is more resilient than the institution that has experienced zero, because the first institution's procedures have been tested by reality and refined by experience.

The blameless principle matters even for a single operator. It is easy to look at a mistake and think "I should have known better." Perhaps. But self-recrimination does not prevent the next mistake. Understanding the system conditions that made the mistake possible -- and changing those conditions -- does. The review asks: what about the system, the procedure, the tools, the documentation made this mistake likely? Fix those things. The operator's fallibility is a constant. The system's resilience is the variable.

I have included near-misses in the review scope because near-misses are gifts. They reveal vulnerabilities without extracting a cost. A near-miss is a free lesson. The institution that ignores near-misses is declining free education and waiting for the paid version.
