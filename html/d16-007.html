D16-007 — Typography, Layout & Visual Structure Principles

Document ID
D16-007
Domain
16 -- Interface & Navigation
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D16-001 (Interface Philosophy), D16-006 (Accessibility Standards)
Depended Upon By
D16-010 (Multi-Generational Continuity), D16-014 (Component Catalog)

Purpose

This article establishes the platform-agnostic principles governing typography, layout, and visual structure across every interface the institution presents to its users. It does not prescribe specific typefaces, pixel dimensions, or colour values. It prescribes the rules by which those choices are made, evaluated, and replaced over a fifty-year horizon.

Typography and layout are the primary vehicles of clarity. An institution that holds vast quantities of structured knowledge and fails to render that knowledge legibly has built a library with no lighting. The interface philosophy articulated in D16-001 demands clarity over cleverness and accessibility as non-negotiable. This article translates those demands into concrete structural principles for the visual rendering of content.

This article is addressed to every person who will select a typeface, define a margin, establish a heading hierarchy, set a contrast ratio, or determine how information density is managed on screen or on paper. It is also addressed to the person who, thirty years from now, must decide whether to replace the visual system entirely. That person needs to know not just what was chosen, but why, and what constraints any replacement must satisfy.

Scope

This article covers:

  • Principles for typeface selection and typographic hierarchy that survive platform changes.
  • Rules governing line length, line height, spacing, and information density.
  • Heading hierarchy, visual weight distribution, and structural rhythm.
  • Contrast requirements for legibility across age, ability, and environmental conditions.
  • Layout grid principles that adapt to different screen sizes, orientations, and output media.
  • The relationship between visual structure and semantic structure.
  • Printability requirements for paper-based fallback rendering.
  • Rules for when and how the visual system may be changed.

This article does not cover:

  • Specific typeface names or digital font files (those are implementation details that will change).
  • Colour palettes for branding purposes (the institution does not brand; it communicates).
  • Animation, transition effects, or motion design (these are interaction-layer concerns addressed in D16-008 and D16-009).
  • Content writing style or editorial guidelines (those belong to content domains).
  • Accessibility testing procedures (covered by D16-006 and Domain 19).

Background

The Problem of Visual Ephemerality

Visual design is the most ephemeral layer of any interface. Typographic conventions shift with each decade. What felt modern in 2005 felt dated by 2015 and archaic by 2025. The institution cannot avoid this reality, but it can manage it. The strategy is to separate the permanent from the transient: the principles that make text legible are permanent; the specific typeface that embodies those principles is transient.

The history of long-lived documents offers guidance. The most readable books from five hundred years ago share characteristics with the most readable documents today: adequate margins, consistent heading hierarchies, sufficient contrast between text and background, and line lengths that do not exceed the comfortable range for sustained reading. These characteristics are not aesthetic fashions. They are rooted in the biomechanics of human vision and the cognitive architecture of reading. They will not change in fifty years because they have not changed in five hundred.

Why Platform-Agnostic Principles Matter

The institution's interfaces will be rendered on hardware that does not yet exist. Screen resolutions, pixel densities, display technologies, and rendering engines will change repeatedly over the institution's lifespan. Prescribing specific pixel values or device-dependent measurements would produce rules that become meaningless the moment the target hardware changes. Instead, this article prescribes proportional relationships, minimum ratios, and structural hierarchies that can be mapped onto any rendering technology.

The Air-Gap Constraint on Typography

In a networked environment, interfaces routinely load typefaces from remote servers. The institution cannot do this. Every typeface used must be stored locally. This is not merely a technical constraint; it is a curation responsibility. The institution must select, store, and maintain typeface files as carefully as it maintains any other data asset. Typeface licences must be perpetual and unrestricted. Typeface formats must be documented. Fallback chains must be defined for every scenario in which a preferred typeface cannot be rendered.

System Model

The visual structure system operates through three interdependent subsystems:

Subsystem 1: Typographic Scale

All text within the institution is rendered according to a typographic scale -- a defined set of size relationships between different levels of textual hierarchy. The scale is expressed as ratios, not absolute sizes.

TYPOGRAPHIC SCALE (ratio-based)
================================
Level          Ratio to Body    Usage
------         --------------   -----
Display        2.0 - 2.5x      Page titles, article headings
Heading 1      1.6 - 1.8x      Major section headings
Heading 2      1.25 - 1.4x     Subsection headings
Heading 3      1.1 - 1.15x     Minor headings, labels
Body           1.0x (base)      Running text, paragraphs
Small          0.85 - 0.9x     Metadata, captions, footnotes
Micro          0.75 - 0.8x     Legal text, version stamps

CONSTRAINTS:
- Body text MUST be no smaller than 16px equivalent at standard viewing distance
- Line height MUST be 1.4 - 1.8x the body font size
- Line length MUST be 45 - 80 characters for body text
- Heading weight MUST differ visibly from body weight

Subsystem 2: Spatial Grid

All layouts are governed by a spatial grid that establishes consistent relationships between elements. The grid is not a rigid pixel lattice but a proportional framework.

SPATIAL GRID PRINCIPLES
========================
Element              Rule
-------              ----
Margins              Minimum 5% of viewport width on each side
Content width        Maximum 52em for body text (enforces line length)
Vertical rhythm      All vertical spacing is a multiple of the base line-height
Column gutters       Minimum 1.5em between adjacent content columns
Section spacing      Minimum 2x base line-height between major sections
Paragraph spacing    0.5 - 1.0x base line-height between paragraphs

ADAPTATION:
- Single-column layout is the default and the fallback
- Multi-column layouts MUST collapse to single-column below 50em viewport
- Print layout uses the same proportional rules mapped to paper dimensions

Subsystem 3: Visual Hierarchy

Visual hierarchy ensures that the reader's eye is guided through content in the correct order. Hierarchy is established through four mechanisms, used in combination: size (larger elements are read first), weight (bolder elements are read first), contrast (higher-contrast elements are read first), and position (elements nearer the top-left in left-to-right scripts are read first). Every page, screen, or view must have a clear primary element, a clear secondary level, and a clear tertiary level. If everything is emphasised, nothing is.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D16-07-01: Contrast Minimum. All text must achieve a contrast ratio of at least 4.5:1 against its background for body text and 3:1 for large text (headings at Heading 1 level or above). This is a hard floor, not a target. The target is 7:1 or higher wherever possible. These ratios are drawn from WCAG standards and from the biomechanics of vision in varying light conditions, which is particularly relevant for an institution that may operate in environments without controlled lighting.

  • R-D16-07-02: Typeface Redundancy. The institution MUST maintain at least three complete typeface families in local storage: a primary serif, a primary sans-serif, and a monospace. Each family must include regular, bold, and italic variants at minimum. If the primary typeface becomes unrenderable on a future platform, the fallback chain must produce acceptable results without manual intervention.

  • R-D16-07-03: Semantic-Visual Alignment. Visual structure MUST correspond to semantic structure. A heading that is rendered as Heading Level 2 visually MUST be Heading Level 2 semantically. A list that appears as a list visually MUST be marked up as a list in the underlying data. This alignment is not optional because assistive technologies depend on semantic structure, and any divergence between what sighted users see and what screen readers announce creates a two-tier experience that violates D16-006.

  • R-D16-07-04: No Decoration Without Function. Every visual element must serve a communicative purpose. Decorative borders, ornamental dividers, background patterns, and non-functional colour must be justified by a specific readability or navigational benefit. If a visual element can be removed without reducing comprehension, it SHOULD be removed.

  • R-D16-07-05: Print Fidelity. All content must be renderable on standard paper (A4 or US Letter) in black ink on white stock at 300 DPI or above. The print rendering must preserve the heading hierarchy, list structure, and table formatting of the screen rendering. Images and diagrams must have text-based alternatives that print legibly.

  • R-D16-07-06: Information Density Controls. The interface MUST provide at least two information density modes: a default mode optimised for comfortable reading and a compact mode optimised for scanning and reference. The compact mode MUST NOT violate any contrast or minimum-size requirements. The mode selection MUST be persistent across sessions and MUST NOT require more than one user action to toggle.

  • R-D16-07-07: No External Resource Dependencies. No typeface, icon set, image asset, or visual resource may be loaded from an external network location. All visual resources MUST be stored within the institution's local file system and MUST be included in standard backup and replication procedures.

  • R-D16-07-08: Proportional, Not Absolute. All typographic and layout specifications MUST be expressed in proportional units (em, rem, percentages, ratios) rather than absolute units (px, pt, cm) wherever the rendering technology supports proportional units. Absolute units are acceptable only in print stylesheets where physical dimensions are known and fixed.

Failure Modes

  • Typeface loss. A platform change renders the primary typeface unrenderable. Likelihood: moderate over 50 years. Severity: medium (content remains accessible but visual hierarchy may degrade). Detection: automated rendering tests that compare output against reference screenshots at each platform upgrade.

  • Contrast erosion. Successive interface modifications introduce low-contrast elements that individually seem acceptable but collectively reduce readability, particularly for older users or users in suboptimal lighting. Likelihood: high without periodic auditing. Severity: high (directly affects accessibility). Detection: automated contrast-ratio checking against all text-background pairs; manual review during quarterly accessibility audits.

  • Hierarchy collapse. Over time, the distinction between heading levels erodes as designers add intermediate levels or modify sizes to solve local layout problems. The result is a visual system where headings are barely distinguishable from body text or from each other. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: high (undermines document navigability). Detection: periodic audit of the actual typographic scale in use against the defined scale.

  • Density creep. Information density increases gradually as more content is added to each view. Margins shrink. Spacing tightens. The reading experience becomes oppressive. Likelihood: high without enforcement. Severity: medium (reduces reading comfort and increases error rates). Detection: automated measurement of white-space ratios against defined minimums.

  • Visual-semantic divergence. A heading is styled to look like body text for aesthetic reasons, or body text is enlarged to look like a heading for emphasis. The visual and semantic structures diverge. Sighted users see one structure; screen reader users hear another. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: critical (creates an accessibility violation). Detection: automated comparison of semantic markup against rendered visual properties.

  • Print rendering failure. Changes to the screen-optimised visual system break the print stylesheet. Content that renders correctly on screen produces garbled, truncated, or illegible output on paper. Likelihood: high (print is often untested). Severity: high (paper is the ultimate fallback). Detection: print-rendering test as part of every visual system change.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If the primary typeface is lost: Activate the fallback chain defined in the typeface configuration. Verify that all content renders legibly with the fallback typeface. If the fallback is acceptable, continue operation while sourcing a permanent replacement that meets all requirements in this document. If the fallback is unacceptable, escalate to a system-wide visual review. Document the typeface loss, its cause, and the resolution in the Commentary Section of this article and in the D20 decision log.

  2. If contrast violations are detected: Immediately audit all text-background pairs across the interface. Fix all violations that fall below the 4.5:1 hard floor. For violations between the floor and the 7:1 target, schedule fixes in priority order based on the amount of content affected. Do not deploy any new visual changes until all hard-floor violations are resolved.

  3. If the typographic hierarchy has collapsed: Compare the current rendered output against the typographic scale defined in this article. Identify all deviations. Restore the defined ratios. If the defined ratios no longer serve their purpose (because the content structure has evolved), revise this article through the standard amendment process rather than continuing with an undocumented deviation.

  4. If visual-semantic divergence is found: Treat as a critical accessibility defect. Audit all semantic markup against visual rendering. For every divergence, determine whether the visual rendering or the semantic markup is correct, and fix the other. This is not a cosmetic issue; it is a structural failure that affects assistive technology users.

  5. If print rendering is broken: Disable any changes that broke print rendering. Restore the last known working print configuration. Test print output for all major content types (articles, tables, lists, code blocks, metadata). Fix the print stylesheet before re-deploying screen-side changes.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Establish the initial typographic scale, spatial grid, and typeface inventory. Expect adjustments as real content reveals where the initial choices do not serve readability. Build automated contrast and hierarchy checking early. Establish the print-rendering test pipeline. The decisions made in this period will constrain the next decade, so document every choice and its rationale thoroughly.

  • Years 5-15: The typographic system should be stable. Minor refinements based on accumulated user feedback and accessibility audits. A typeface refresh may occur if a clearly superior option becomes available, but only through the formal amendment process. The proportional rules should not need to change.

  • Years 15-30: A visual refresh is likely and healthy. The presentation layer (Layer 4 per D16-001) is expected to evolve. The refresh must respect all rules in this article. It must not collapse the heading hierarchy, break print rendering, or violate contrast minimums. A full visual refresh is a major institutional decision that must be logged per D20-002.

  • Years 30-50: Rendering technologies may be fundamentally different. The proportional rules in this article should still apply because they are based on human vision, not on any particular display technology. If display technology changes so radically that proportional typographic rules are no longer meaningful, this article must be rewritten from first principles, but the principles in D16-001 will still govern the rewrite.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The hardest part of writing typographic principles for a fifty-year institution is resisting the urge to specify exactly what the text should look like. I know what looks right to me, today, on my screen. But "what looks right to me" is the most perishable knowledge I have. The biomechanics of reading are not perishable. The need for contrast is not perishable. The relationship between line length and reading comfort is not perishable. Those are what this article tries to capture.

I have deliberately avoided naming any typeface. The moment a specific typeface is enshrined in a governance document, it becomes politically difficult to replace, long after it should have been replaced. The institution must be free to choose the best available typeface at any point in time, subject to the constraints documented here. The constraints are the governance. The typeface is the implementation.

One thing I am uncertain about is the information density controls. Two modes may not be enough. A researcher scanning hundreds of entries has different needs than a casual reader browsing an article. Future maintainers should consider whether a continuous density slider or additional discrete modes would serve users better. Record your findings here.
