D9-011 — New Member Onboarding Sequence

Document ID
D9-011
Domain
9 -- Education & Training
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D9-001, D9-002, D9-003, D9-004, D9-008, D10, GOV-001, OPS-001
Depended Upon By
All domains (new members enter through onboarding). GOV-001 (succession protocol references onboarding). D9-008 (cross-training matrix updated after onboarding).

Purpose

This article defines the complete sequence for integrating a new person into the holm.chat Documentation Institution, from first contact through role-specific training to certified independent operation. It is distinct from D9-002, which defines the specific competency model and detailed curriculum for a full successor or sole maintainer. This article addresses the broader case: any new member joining the institution in any capacity, whether as a full successor, a specialist contributor, or an additional operator alongside existing personnel.

The onboarding sequence serves three purposes. First, it ensures that every person who operates within the institution understands its foundational principles, governance model, and security requirements before they take any action. Comprehension before authority is the governing principle. Second, it provides a structured pathway from unfamiliarity to competence, with clear milestones that both the new member and the existing institution can use to track progress. Third, it generates data: every onboarding experience reveals documentation gaps, training material weaknesses, and institutional assumptions that need to be examined.

The sequence is designed to work under two conditions: planned onboarding (where existing operators are available to guide the new member) and documentation-only onboarding (where no guide is available and the documentation must serve as the sole teacher). The planned case is better, faster, and more thorough. The documentation-only case must also work, because the institution cannot guarantee that a guide will always be available. This is the self-teaching requirement (D9-001, R-D9-01) applied to the onboarding process itself.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The onboarding trigger conditions: when and how onboarding is initiated.
  • The universal onboarding core: what every new member learns regardless of their intended role.
  • Role-specific onboarding tracks: how the universal core branches into specialized training.
  • The onboarding timeline: expected duration for each phase.
  • Milestone assessments: how progress is verified at each stage.
  • The onboarding debrief: how the new member's experience is captured to improve the process.
  • The cross-training matrix update: how the new member's competencies are recorded.

This article does not cover:

  • The detailed competency model for full succession (see D9-002).
  • Role-specific curriculum content (see D9-003).
  • Assessment methods and certification standards (see D9-004).
  • The mentorship relationship during onboarding (see D9-005).
  • Degraded-capacity onboarding when no existing operator is available and the inheritor is the sole person (see D9-010).

Background

3.1 Onboarding as Institutional Reproduction

Every time a new person joins the institution, the institution is reproducing itself: transferring its accumulated knowledge, values, and practices to a new mind. This reproduction is fragile. If the transfer is incomplete, the new member will operate with gaps -- gaps in understanding that may not be visible until a crisis reveals them. If the transfer is too narrow, the new member will understand their specific role but not the institution's larger purpose, leading to decisions that serve local optimization at the expense of institutional coherence.

The onboarding sequence addresses this by requiring a universal core before any specialization. Every new member reads the same foundational documents, completes the same orientation exercises, and passes the same comprehension assessments. Only after this common foundation is established does the sequence branch into role-specific training. This ensures that every member shares a common understanding of what the institution is, what it values, and how it governs itself.

3.2 The Two Onboarding Modes

Onboarding operates in one of two modes:

  • Guided Mode: An existing operator serves as the onboarding mentor. They guide the new member through the sequence, answer questions, provide context that the documentation may not fully convey, observe the new member's progress, and conduct assessments. This is the preferred mode.
  • Self-Directed Mode: No mentor is available. The new member works through the sequence using only the documentation, training materials, and self-assessment tools. This mode is slower, harder, and more prone to gaps. It is the mode that must work because it is the mode that will be needed when it is needed most -- during unplanned succession or when the institution's capacity is degraded.

The sequence is written for the self-directed case. In guided mode, the mentor supplements the sequence with direct instruction, but the sequence itself remains the backbone. The mentor does not replace the documentation; they augment it.

3.3 Relationship to Other Onboarding Articles

D9-002 provides the detailed curriculum and assessment framework for a full successor who will assume sole responsibility for the institution. It is the most comprehensive onboarding pathway. This article (D9-011) provides the structural framework that governs all onboarding, including but not limited to the full-succession case. A new member who joins as a specialist (e.g., responsible only for data management) follows the universal core defined here, then follows the role-specific track for their domain, and does not need the full D9-002 succession curriculum unless they are being prepared for eventual full succession.

System Model

4.1 Onboarding Trigger Conditions

Onboarding is initiated whenever:

  • A new person is granted any level of access to the institution's systems.
  • A person is designated as a successor per GOV-001.
  • A person is assigned a new role that they have not previously been onboarded for (role-specific track only; universal core is not repeated).
  • A person returns to the institution after an extended absence (more than twelve months) and needs to re-familiarize with changes that occurred during their absence (abbreviated onboarding, focused on updates).

4.2 The Universal Onboarding Core

Every new member completes the universal core before any role-specific training. The core consists of three stages:

Stage 1: Orientation (Days 1-3).

Reading: CON-001 (Founding Mandate), ETH-001 (Ethical Foundations), GOV-001 (Authority Model), SEC-001 (Threat Model), OPS-001 (Operations Philosophy). These five documents define what the institution is, what it values, how it governs itself, how it protects itself, and how it operates.

Exercises: Written summaries of each document demonstrating comprehension. The summaries are reviewed by the onboarding mentor (guided mode) or self-assessed against the self-check questions in each document (self-directed mode).

Assessment: The new member must be able to articulate the institutional mission, the six ethical principles, the four decision tiers, the air-gap mandate, and the daily operational cycle. This assessment is pass/fail. It must be passed before proceeding.

Stage 2: Institutional Landscape (Days 4-14).

Reading: All domain framework documents (DOMAIN-1 through DOMAIN-20 or current maximum). The goal is not deep understanding of every domain but awareness of the institution's complete scope. For each domain, the new member should understand: what it governs, how it relates to the five root documents, and where its documentation lives.

Exercises: For each domain, a one-paragraph summary answering: what does this domain control, and why does it matter? A domain relationship map showing the connections between domains.

Assessment: The new member must be able to identify which domain governs a given institutional function (e.g., "Where would I find the rules about data backup formats?" Answer: Domain 6). Assessment is ten questions, seven correct to pass.

Stage 3: Systems Familiarization (Days 15-21).

Activity: A guided or self-directed walkthrough of the institution's physical and logical infrastructure. Using Domain 4 (Infrastructure) and Domain 5 (Platform) documentation, the new member creates a physical map (hardware locations, connections, isolation boundaries) and a logical map (services, data locations, backup topology).

Exercises: Create both maps. In guided mode, the mentor verifies the maps against reality. In self-directed mode, the maps are verified against the infrastructure documentation and any discrepancies are noted as potential documentation gaps.

Assessment: The new member must be able to locate any piece of hardware, identify its function, and explain its relationship to the air-gap architecture.

4.3 Role-Specific Onboarding Tracks

After completing the universal core, the new member enters a role-specific track determined by their assigned responsibilities. Each track follows the curriculum defined in D9-003 for its domain(s) and uses the assessment methods defined in D9-004. The tracks share a common structure:

  1. Domain Deep Dive (1-2 weeks): Reading all articles in the assigned domain(s). Completing domain-specific exercises. Achieving Stage 2 (Guided Execution) competence for all Tier A functions in the domain.
  2. Supervised Practice (2-4 weeks): Performing the domain's operational functions under mentor observation (guided mode) or with documentation reference (self-directed mode). Each task is verified against expected outcomes.
  3. Independent Operation (2-4 weeks): Performing the domain's operational functions without guidance. Demonstrating Stage 3 (Independent Execution) competence for Tier A functions and Stage 2 for Tier B functions.
  4. Certification Assessment: Formal assessment per D9-004. The new member demonstrates competence through practical demonstration, oral examination, or portfolio review as appropriate to the domain.

4.4 The Onboarding Timeline

Phase Duration Mode
Universal Core: Stage 1 (Orientation) 3 days Both
Universal Core: Stage 2 (Institutional Landscape) 11 days Both
Universal Core: Stage 3 (Systems Familiarization) 7 days Both
Role-Specific: Domain Deep Dive 7-14 days Both
Role-Specific: Supervised Practice 14-28 days Guided preferred
Role-Specific: Independent Operation 14-28 days Both
Certification Assessment 1-3 days Guided preferred
Onboarding Debrief 1 day Both
Total 57-94 days

These durations are targets, not hard constraints. The governing principle is competency, not speed. A new member who needs more time to achieve competence should receive it. A new member who demonstrates competence quickly should not be artificially delayed -- but should not skip assessments either.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D9-11-01: Every person who is granted access to institutional systems must complete the universal onboarding core (Stage 1 through Stage 3) before operating any system independently. No exceptions. The core ensures that every person who touches the institution understands its purpose, values, and security requirements.
  • R-D9-11-02: The universal core must be completed before role-specific training begins. Comprehension of the institutional foundation precedes operational training. This ordering is non-negotiable.
  • R-D9-11-03: Each milestone assessment must be passed before proceeding to the next stage. Failed assessments require re-study and re-assessment after a waiting period (five days minimum for Stage 1, seven days for subsequent stages). Two consecutive failures in the same assessment trigger a review of the training materials rather than solely a review of the new member's effort.
  • R-D9-11-04: The onboarding debrief is mandatory. Within seven days of onboarding completion, the new member produces a written debrief documenting: what was most helpful, what was confusing, what documentation gaps were discovered, what they would change about the process, and how long each phase actually took compared to the target. This debrief is archived as institutional memory and feeds into D9-012 (training effectiveness evaluation).
  • R-D9-11-05: Upon onboarding completion, the cross-training matrix (D9-008) is updated to reflect the new member's assessed competency levels for all functions covered by their onboarding track. The new member's competency is recorded at the level verified by the certification assessment, not at the level they self-report.
  • R-D9-11-06: Onboarding completion is recorded in the decision log as a Tier 2 governance event. The entry includes: the new member's name or identifier, their assigned role, the onboarding mode (guided or self-directed), the date of completion, and the certifying authority (the mentor in guided mode, or self-certification in self-directed mode with a note that external verification was unavailable).
  • R-D9-11-07: During the supervised practice phase in guided mode, the mentor must not perform tasks on behalf of the new member. The mentor observes, answers questions, and provides feedback. The new member performs all tasks themselves. The goal is competence, not efficiency.
  • R-D9-11-08: The onboarding sequence must be revisable. After each completed onboarding, the sequence is reviewed for improvements based on the debrief data (R-D9-11-04). Revisions are implemented before the next onboarding begins. The sequence is a living document, not a fixed procedure.

Failure Modes

  • The rushed onboarding. Operational pressure leads to compressing or skipping phases. The new member is pushed into independent operation before they are ready, leading to errors that may be costly. Mitigation: R-D9-11-01 and R-D9-11-02 make the ordering and assessment gates non-negotiable. The governing principle is competency, not speed. The institution must accept reduced operational capacity during onboarding rather than risk under-trained operators.

  • The formality trap. Onboarding becomes a bureaucratic checklist rather than a genuine learning experience. The new member passes assessments through memorization rather than understanding. Mitigation: D9-004 assessment methods emphasize practical demonstration over written testing. The onboarding debrief (R-D9-11-04) provides a mechanism for the new member to flag when the process felt perfunctory.

  • The mentor bottleneck. Only one person is qualified to serve as onboarding mentor, and they are too busy to devote adequate time. Onboarding stalls. Mitigation: self-directed mode exists as a fallback. In guided mode, the mentor role should be the mentor's primary responsibility during onboarding, not a secondary task. Per D9-008, the mentorship capability itself should be cross-trained -- more than one person should be able to serve as onboarding mentor.

  • The documentation gap cascade. The new member discovers significant documentation gaps during onboarding that prevent them from progressing. Each gap requires remediation, which slows onboarding and consumes existing operators' time. Mitigation: documentation gaps discovered during onboarding are recorded but need not be fully remediated before onboarding continues. The mentor provides the missing information verbally, the gap is flagged for remediation, and the new member continues. The gap becomes a high-priority maintenance item after onboarding completes.

  • Role mismatch. During onboarding, it becomes clear that the new member's assigned role does not match their aptitudes or the institution's actual needs. Mitigation: the universal core provides enough institutional context for both the new member and the existing operators to assess role fit before the role-specific track begins. Role reassignment during onboarding is acceptable; it is far better than discovering the mismatch after onboarding completes.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If a new member fails an assessment twice: Pause the assessment cycle. Review the training materials and the assessment itself with the new member to determine whether the failure reflects a learning gap, a documentation gap, or an assessment design flaw. If the training materials are inadequate, remediate them and resume. If the assessment is poorly calibrated, revise it. If the learning gap is genuine, provide targeted instruction (in guided mode) or additional study time with specific guidance (in self-directed mode). A third failure triggers a governance review per GOV-001.

  2. If onboarding takes significantly longer than the target timeline: Investigate the cause. Common causes: the new member's background requires more foundational work than expected; the documentation has more gaps than anticipated; operational demands on the mentor are reducing available guidance time. Address the specific cause rather than applying general pressure to speed up.

  3. If the onboarding mentor becomes unavailable mid-onboarding: Transition to self-directed mode for the new member's current phase. If another qualified person can assume the mentor role, transfer mentorship with a handoff that includes the new member's current progress, assessment results, and any notes from the departing mentor. If no replacement is available, the new member continues in self-directed mode with the understanding that their final certification may be delayed until a certifying authority is available.

  4. If a new member leaves during onboarding: Record the departure in the decision log. Archive any debrief feedback the departing member can provide (even partial). Review whether the departure was related to the onboarding experience (overwhelming documentation, role mismatch, institutional culture) and address any systemic issues.

  5. If onboarding reveals that the institution's systems have drifted from their documentation: This is a documentation integrity issue. Flag it for immediate remediation per D19-002 (documentation quality audit). The new member's onboarding may proceed using corrected information provided by the mentor, but the documentation must be updated before the next onboarding or the same problem will recur.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The onboarding sequence is theoretical. No new member has been onboarded. The founding operator should test the sequence by self-onboarding: following the reading lists, completing the exercises, and attempting the assessments as if encountering the institution for the first time. This test will be imperfect (the operator cannot truly simulate naivety) but will catch structural problems in the sequence.

  • Years 5-15: The first real onboarding will expose every weakness in the sequence. The onboarding debrief from this first experience is the most valuable data the education system will ever receive. Expect significant revisions. Expect the timeline targets to be wrong. Expect the assessments to need recalibration. This is normal and healthy -- the sequence is designed to be improved by use.

  • Years 15-30: The sequence should have been used multiple times and refined by experience. Common patterns should have emerged: which stages take longer than expected, which assessments are too easy or too hard, which documentation gaps recur despite remediation. The sequence should be stable in structure but continuously refined in detail.

  • Years 30-50+: The sequence is a mature institutional process. It has survived generational transitions. The universal core may have evolved as the institution's foundational documents have been revised, but the principle of comprehension before authority should remain unchanged. The role-specific tracks will have evolved with the institution's functions. The onboarding debrief archive is a rich source of institutional history, showing how the institution's self-reproduction has changed over decades.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: This article and D9-002 overlap in their concerns -- both address how a new person joins the institution. The distinction matters: D9-002 is the detailed curriculum for a sole successor inheriting full responsibility. This article is the structural framework for any new member in any capacity. D9-002 is a specific, intense instance of this article's general pattern.

The universal core is the most important part of this article. It ensures that every person who touches the institution understands its ethical foundations before they touch its systems. In a purely technical institution, this might seem like overhead. In this institution, it is essential. The ethical foundations are not decorative -- they are load-bearing. A person who modifies the air-gap architecture without understanding why it exists is not just making a technical change. They are making an ethical one. The universal core ensures they know that.

I have set the orientation phase at three days, which may feel long for someone eager to start working. It is not long enough. Three days to absorb five foundational documents is a brisk pace. But the alternative -- letting people start operating before they understand the institution's purpose -- is worse than any delay. Comprehension before authority is not just a governance principle. It is a respect principle: respecting the new member enough to ensure they understand what they are taking responsibility for before they are asked to exercise that responsibility.
