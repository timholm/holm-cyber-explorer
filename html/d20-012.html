D20-012 — Forgetting & Sunset Policies

Document ID
D20-012
Domain
20 — Institutional Memory
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D20-001 (Philosophy), D20-008 (Memory Integrity)
Depended Upon By
D20-011 (Memory Taxonomy, retention tiers)

Purpose

This article defines when and how the institution may deliberately choose not to remember. It establishes the policies for retiring, archiving, and deleting institutional memory records -- the controlled forgetting that prevents the institution from drowning in its own history.

D20-001 established the principle that "forgetting is deliberate" (R-D20-04). This article operationalizes that principle. It specifies the criteria for determining when a record has outlived its usefulness, the process for reviewing and approving forgetting decisions, the safeguards that prevent inappropriate forgetting, and the meta-requirement that the institution always remembers what it chose to forget, even when it no longer remembers the content of the forgotten record.

Forgetting is not the opposite of remembering. It is a component of good memory. A mind that cannot forget is as dysfunctional as a mind that cannot remember. An institution that retains every record with equal weight will find its important records buried under an ever-growing sediment of routine data. Controlled forgetting is curation: it maintains the signal-to-noise ratio of institutional memory.

Scope

This article covers:

  • Sunset criteria: when a record becomes a candidate for retirement.
  • The forgetting decision process: who decides, how, and what documentation is required.
  • Types of forgetting: archival (moved to less accessible storage), retirement (removed from active index but preserved), and deletion (permanent removal).
  • Privacy-motivated forgetting: when personal information should be removed.
  • The forgetting record: the permanent documentation of what was forgotten and why.
  • Safeguards against inappropriate forgetting.
  • Review and appeal procedures for forgetting decisions.

This article does not cover:

  • The philosophy of memory and forgetting (see D20-001, Section 3.4).
  • The classification system that determines retention tiers (see D20-011).
  • The integrity mechanisms that protect records from unauthorized deletion (see D20-008).
  • Governance authority for forgetting decisions (covered by Governance Domains).

Background

3.1 The Growth Problem

Institutional memory grows monotonically. Every decision, every event, every oral history, every audit result, every operational log adds to the total volume. Over a 50-year horizon, the institution will generate an enormous quantity of memory records. Storage is finite, even on an expanding system. More importantly, attention is finite: the more records exist, the harder it becomes to find any specific record, and the more maintenance effort is required to keep the system organized.

Controlled forgetting is the institution's response to the growth problem. By retiring records that have outlived their usefulness, the institution maintains a manageable, navigable memory that remains useful rather than overwhelming.

3.2 The Tension Between Integrity and Forgetting

D20-008 establishes that permanent records are inviolable: they cannot be modified or deleted. This creates an apparent contradiction with the concept of forgetting. The resolution is that forgetting applies only to Tier 2 (Active) and Tier 3 (Transient) records. Tier 1 (Permanent) records are, by definition, never forgotten. The decision about which records are Tier 1 is the decision about what the institution will remember forever.

Additionally, the forgetting decision itself is a Tier 1 record. The institution always remembers that it chose to forget, even when the forgotten content is gone. This creates a memory trail: a future researcher can see that a record existed, what it was classified as, when it was forgotten, why, and who authorized the forgetting. They cannot see the content, but they can see the shape of the gap.

3.3 Privacy and the Right to Be Forgotten

Some forgetting is motivated not by operational concerns but by privacy. A contributor may request that personal information -- information about their life outside the institution, personal circumstances, health, or other private matters -- be removed from institutional records. The institution must balance its need for memory against the legitimate privacy interests of its members. This article establishes the boundary: institutional acts (decisions, contributions, roles) are always remembered; personal information that is not relevant to institutional function may be removed at the subject's request.

System Model

4.1 The Forgetting Spectrum

Forgetting is not binary. It exists on a spectrum from "less accessible" to "permanently removed":

Forgetting TypeDescriptionReversibilityUse Case
ArchivalRecord is moved from active storage to archival storage. It remains in the index but is not immediately accessible.Fully reversibleRecords that are no longer operationally relevant but may have historical value.
RetirementRecord is removed from the active index but preserved in sealed storage. Accessible only through a specific retrieval request.Reversible with effortRecords whose usefulness has expired but whose content is not sensitive.
RedactionSpecific personal information within a record is replaced with a [REDACTED] marker. The rest of the record is preserved.Irreversible for the redacted contentPrivacy-motivated removal of personal information from otherwise institutional records.
DeletionRecord is permanently removed from all storage. Only the forgetting record remains.IrreversibleRecords that pose a genuine risk if retained (compromised security credentials, content found to violate ethics policies).

4.2 Sunset Criteria

A record becomes a candidate for forgetting when it meets one or more of the following criteria:

  • Temporal expiration: The record's retention period (defined by its tier and classification per D20-011) has elapsed.
  • Supersession: The record has been superseded by a newer record that contains all of its information. The newer record references the older one.
  • Obsolescence: The record pertains to a system, process, or configuration that no longer exists and has no historical significance.
  • Privacy request: A contributor has requested removal of personal information per Section 3.3.
  • Security concern: The record contains information whose continued retention poses a security risk (e.g., compromised credentials).
  • Ethical concern: The record contains content that violates the institution's ethical standards (e.g., content admitted through an import error that should have been rejected).

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D20-12-01: Tier 1 Records Are Never Forgotten. No record classified as Tier 1 (Permanent) may be archived, retired, redacted, or deleted under any circumstances. If a Tier 1 record is believed to be inappropriately classified, the classification may be challenged through governance, but the record may not be forgotten while the challenge is pending.

  • R-D20-12-02: Every Forgetting Is Documented. The forgetting record is a Tier 1 permanent record. It contains: the original record's ID, type, classification, and creation date; a summary of the record's content; the reason for forgetting; the forgetting type selected; the authorization chain; and the date of execution. The institution always remembers what it chose to forget.

  • R-D20-12-03: Least Destructive Method. The least destructive forgetting type that addresses the need must be selected. If archival is sufficient, do not retire. If retirement is sufficient, do not delete. Deletion is reserved for genuine security or ethical concerns where even sealed storage poses a risk.

  • R-D20-12-04: Deletion Requires Governance Authorization. Permanent deletion of any institutional record requires authorization from the governance authority defined in GOV-001. No individual maintainer may unilaterally delete a record. This rule applies even to Tier 3 (Transient) records, though the governance review for Tier 3 deletions may be expedited.

  • R-D20-12-05: Privacy Redaction Is Scoped. When personal information is redacted from a record, only the personal information is removed. Institutional acts, decisions, and contributions attributed to the person are preserved. The redaction marker [REDACTED] replaces the removed text, making the removal visible to future readers.

  • R-D20-12-06: Cross-References Are Updated. When a record is forgotten, every record that references it must be annotated to note that the reference target has been archived, retired, or deleted. Broken references are a form of memory corruption and must be prevented.

  • R-D20-12-07: Forgetting Decisions Are Appealable. A forgetting decision may be appealed by any institutional member within 90 days of the decision. During the appeal period, the forgetting is paused (the record is not executed). The appeal is reviewed by a governance authority who was not involved in the original decision.

Failure Modes

  • Premature forgetting. A record is forgotten before its value has expired. Information is lost that later proves to be needed. Severity: variable (depends on what was lost). Likelihood: moderate. Detection: a context recovery attempt (D20-007) fails because the needed record was forgotten. Mitigation: the impact assessment step in the forgetting process; the 90-day appeal period; the least-destructive-method rule.

  • Forgetting paralysis. The documentation burden of the forgetting process is so heavy that no records are ever retired. Memory grows without bound. Severity: moderate (degrades navigability). Likelihood: moderate. Detection: the memory system grows faster than expected; routine records accumulate past their retention period. Mitigation: streamline the forgetting process for low-significance records; accept that the documentation for a Tier 3 routine record retirement can be brief.

  • Unauthorized deletion. A record is deleted without following the forgetting process, either by accident or by someone circumventing the process. Severity: high. Likelihood: low (file permissions and integrity checks should prevent this). Detection: integrity verification (D20-008) detects missing records. Mitigation: treat as a security incident; restore from backup; investigate.

  • Forgetting record omission. A record is forgotten but the forgetting record is not created. The institution does not know what it forgot. Severity: high (violates the fundamental principle that forgetting is documented). Likelihood: low. Detection: the forgetting execution step should be gated on the forgetting record creation. Mitigation: the forgetting process requires the forgetting record to be created before the execution step.

  • Privacy overreach. A privacy request is used to remove institutional decision records, not just personal information. Severity: high (institutional memory is compromised). Likelihood: low. Detection: the scoped redaction rule (R-D20-12-05) limits what can be removed. Mitigation: the privacy review step verifies that only personal information is being redacted.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If a prematurely forgotten record is needed: Check the forgetting type. If the record was archived or retired, it can be retrieved from archival or sealed storage. If it was deleted, consult the forgetting record for a summary of its content. If the summary is sufficient, proceed based on that. If not, conduct context recovery (D20-007) using other sources. Record the incident as a lesson learned (D20-004) and review the sunset criteria that led to the premature forgetting.

  2. If forgetting paralysis has set in: Conduct a bulk sunset review. Identify all records that have passed their retention period. Apply the forgetting process in batches, starting with the lowest-significance records. Accept that the forgetting records for batch-processed routine records may be less detailed than for individual significant records.

  3. If an unauthorized deletion is detected: Restore from backup. Create a forgetting record documenting the unauthorized deletion and the restoration. Investigate the cause. If the deletion was accidental, improve file permissions and training. If it was intentional, escalate as a security and governance incident.

  4. If a forgetting record was omitted: Create a retrospective forgetting record based on whatever information is available about the forgotten record: its ID (if known), its approximate content (if anyone remembers), when it was forgotten, and who did it. Mark the retrospective record as "RECONSTRUCTED" and note that the original forgetting process was not followed.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Very little forgetting should be necessary. The memory system is young and small. Use this period to establish the forgetting process and test it on a few Tier 3 records that have reached their expiration. Build the muscle memory of documented forgetting before the volume of candidate records becomes large.

  • Years 5-15: The first significant sunset reviews occur. Tier 2 records from the early years reach their review dates. The forgetting process is tested at scale. Refine the process based on experience: is the documentation burden proportionate? Are the authorization requirements appropriate? Is the appeal process working?

  • Years 15-30: Forgetting becomes a routine institutional function. The balance between remembering and forgetting should feel natural, not forced. The institution should be comfortable saying "we no longer need this record" while knowing that the forgetting itself is documented. Privacy-motivated forgetting may become more common as the institution's contributor base grows.

  • Years 30-50: The forgetting records themselves become a valuable part of institutional memory. They tell the story of what the institution chose not to keep, and why. A future historian reading the forgetting records gains insight into the institution's values, priorities, and resource constraints over decades. The shape of the gaps is itself informative.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: This article was the hardest to write in Domain 20, because it requires the Historian to advocate for forgetting. Every instinct says "keep everything." But every practical consideration says "you cannot." Storage is finite. Attention is more finite. A memory system that keeps everything with equal weight is a memory system that keeps nothing with effective weight, because the important records are buried under the trivial ones.

The key insight is that the forgetting record is itself a form of remembering. When the institution forgets a routine backup log from 2029, it creates a permanent record that says "on this date, we determined that this log was no longer needed, for these reasons." Twenty years later, a researcher may find the forgetting record and think "I wish they had kept that log." But they will also know that the decision was made deliberately, by specific people, for specific reasons. That is better than finding a gap with no explanation at all.

I want to be very clear about one thing: the anti-forgetting safeguard for Tier 1 records (R-D20-12-01) is absolute. There is no governance override, no emergency exception, no "extraordinary circumstances" clause. If a record is Tier 1, it is permanent. If someone believes a Tier 1 record should be forgotten, they must first successfully argue that the record should be reclassified. That reclassification decision is itself a Tier 1 record. The system is designed so that the path to forgetting a permanent record is so long and so well-documented that it is effectively impossible to do quietly.
