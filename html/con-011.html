CON-011 — The Amendment Process for Foundational Principles

Document ID
CON-011
Domain
1 -- Constitution & Philosophy
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Draft

Purpose

This article defines how the institution's foundational documents -- its constitution, principles, refusals, and core philosophical commitments -- can be changed. It establishes the distinction between legitimate amendment and institutional betrayal, defines the safeguards against erosion, and provides the procedural framework that protects foundational principles from impulsive, uninformed, or self-serving modification.

A constitution that cannot be changed is a constitution that will eventually be ignored. When the gap between documented principles and operational reality becomes too wide, operators stop consulting the documents and start improvising. The amendment process exists to prevent this by providing a legitimate, documented pathway for change that is rigorous enough to prevent casual erosion but flexible enough to accommodate genuine evolution.

This article is also a safeguard against a specific threat: the operator who is tempted to change the principles to fit their preferences rather than adapting their preferences to fit the principles. The amendment process is deliberately slow, deliberate, and documented. It creates friction between the desire to change and the act of changing. That friction is not a bug; it is the primary feature.

Scope

This article governs amendments to all Domain 1 documents (CON-001 through CON-013) and to ETH-001. It also governs amendments to any document in any domain that has been designated as "foundational" -- a designation that means the document is subject to this article's amendment process rather than the standard document revision process.

This article does not govern amendments to non-foundational documents. Those follow the standard revision process defined in the Meta-Documentation domain. The distinction matters: operational procedures can and should change frequently. Foundational principles should change rarely and only with full deliberation.

Background

Constitutional amendment processes in political systems are instructive but imperfect analogies. Political constitutions require supermajorities -- broad consensus among many people. This institution is operated by one person. The concept of a supermajority does not apply. Instead, the amendment process must create internal safeguards that a single person can apply to their own decision-making.

The primary safeguard is time. Impulsive decisions are the greatest threat to foundational principles. A decision that seems urgent today may seem foolish next month. The amendment process therefore imposes mandatory waiting periods between the stages of amendment. These waiting periods create space for reflection, for second thoughts, and for the discovery of consequences that were not initially apparent.

The secondary safeguard is documentation. Every step of the amendment process must be documented in writing. The act of writing forces clarity of thought. A proposal that cannot be clearly articulated in writing is a proposal that has not been sufficiently thought through. The documentation also creates an audit trail -- future operators can see not just what was changed but why, and they can evaluate whether the reasoning still holds.

The tertiary safeguard is the adversarial review -- a formal process in which the operator must argue against their own proposal, identifying every reason the amendment should not be made. This is difficult and uncomfortable, which is exactly the point. It forces the operator to confront the costs of the change, not just the benefits.

System Model

Amendment Tiers

The amendment process has three tiers, corresponding to the principle tiers defined in CON-002:

Tier 3 Amendment (Operational Principles):

  • Minimum deliberation period: 90 days from proposal to ratification.
  • Required documentation: amendment proposal, reasoning document, impact assessment, adversarial review.
  • Approval: operator self-approval after completing all documentation and waiting the deliberation period.

Tier 2 Amendment (Architectural Principles):

  • Minimum deliberation period: 180 days from proposal to ratification.
  • Required documentation: amendment proposal, reasoning document, impact assessment, adversarial review, cross-domain impact analysis (how does this change affect every other domain?).
  • Additional requirement: the amendment must be re-read and re-affirmed at the 90-day mark. If the operator no longer supports the amendment at the 90-day mark, it is withdrawn.
  • Approval: operator self-approval after completing all documentation, passing the 90-day re-affirmation, and waiting the full deliberation period.

Tier 1 Amendment (Existential Principles):

  • Per CON-002, Tier 1 principles may not be amended. They may only be reinterpreted through documented commentary.
  • If the operator determines that a Tier 1 principle must be changed, this constitutes a refounding of the institution, not an amendment. The process for refounding is defined below.

The Amendment Process: Step by Step

Step 1: Proposal. The operator writes a formal amendment proposal that includes: the specific text to be changed, the proposed new text, and a one-paragraph summary of why the change is needed. The proposal is dated and entered into the institutional log. The deliberation period begins on this date.

Step 2: Reasoning Document. Within 14 days of the proposal, the operator writes a detailed reasoning document that explains: what problem the amendment solves, what alternatives to amendment were considered and why they were rejected, what the long-term consequences of the amendment are expected to be, and how the amendment relates to the institution's foundational principles. This document must be at least 500 words. Brevity is a sign that the reasoning has not been fully developed.

Step 3: Impact Assessment. Within 30 days of the proposal, the operator completes an impact assessment that identifies every document, system, and procedure that would be affected by the amendment. For Tier 2 amendments, this must include a cross-domain analysis covering every domain in the institution.

Step 4: Adversarial Review. Within 45 days of the proposal, the operator writes an adversarial review -- a document that argues against the amendment. The adversarial review must identify at least three reasons the amendment should not be made and must honestly evaluate the strength of each objection. If the operator cannot identify three genuine objections, the amendment is probably unnecessary (because the change is trivial) or the operator is not thinking hard enough.

Step 5: Waiting Period. The operator waits out the remainder of the deliberation period. During this time, they should periodically re-read the proposal, the reasoning document, the impact assessment, and the adversarial review. They should note any changes in their thinking.

Step 6: Re-affirmation (Tier 2 only). At the 90-day mark, the operator formally re-reads all amendment documents and writes a re-affirmation statement confirming that they still support the amendment, or a withdrawal statement explaining why they no longer do.

Step 7: Ratification. After the full deliberation period, if the operator still supports the amendment, they ratify it by: updating the affected document(s), incrementing the version number, adding a commentary entry describing the amendment and its reasoning, and updating all cross-references in affected documents.

Step 8: Post-Amendment Review. Six months after ratification, the operator reviews the amendment's effects. Were the expected consequences accurate? Were there unexpected consequences? Is the amendment achieving its purpose? The results of this review are recorded in the amended document's Commentary Section.

The Refounding Process

If the operator determines that a Tier 1 principle must be changed, the institution is being refounded, not amended. Refounding requires:

  1. A comprehensive justification document explaining why the existing Tier 1 principle is no longer tenable.
  2. A new foundational document that replaces the old one, clearly marked as a new version with a new major version number.
  3. A 365-day deliberation period.
  4. A complete identity continuity assessment per CON-004, acknowledging that refounding may constitute an identity break.
  5. Ratification after the deliberation period, with a Commentary entry in the new document acknowledging the refounding and providing the full reasoning.

Refounding is expected to be extraordinarily rare. If it happens more than once in a generation, the institution's foundational principles were probably not sufficiently considered at founding.

Rules and Constraints

  • R-CON-011-01: No foundational document may be changed except through the process defined in this article. Informal, undocumented changes to foundational documents are treated as governance violations.
  • R-CON-011-02: The deliberation periods are minimums, not targets. There is no maximum deliberation period. An amendment that has been in deliberation for years is more trustworthy, not less.
  • R-CON-011-03: An amendment may be withdrawn at any point before ratification. Withdrawal is not a failure; it is the process working as designed.
  • R-CON-011-04: The amendment process itself may only be amended through a Tier 2 amendment. This prevents the amendment process from being weakened to facilitate a specific amendment.
  • R-CON-011-05: All amendment documentation (proposals, reasoning documents, impact assessments, adversarial reviews, re-affirmations, and ratification records) must be preserved permanently as part of the institutional archive. They are never deleted, even if the amendment is withdrawn.
  • R-CON-011-06: If multiple amendments to foundational documents are in process simultaneously, each must be evaluated independently and also in combination. Amendments that are individually benign but collectively problematic must be identified.

Failure Modes

Process circumvention. The operator changes a foundational document without following the amendment process, perhaps rationalizing that "it's just a small change" or "no one will notice." Mitigation: the institutional log and version control system should make unauthorized changes detectable. The post-amendment review process catches undocumented changes retroactively.

Process abuse. The operator follows the letter of the amendment process but not its spirit -- writing perfunctory reasoning documents, phoning in the adversarial review, and counting down the deliberation days impatiently. Mitigation: the 500-word minimum for reasoning documents, the three-objection requirement for adversarial reviews, and the six-month post-amendment review all create checkpoints that are difficult to satisfy superficially.

Amendment paralysis. The amendment process is so intimidating that the operator never proposes amendments, even when they are clearly needed. Foundational documents drift from reality because the process for updating them is too burdensome. Mitigation: the process is designed to be thorough, not impossible. A Tier 3 amendment can be completed in 90 days with perhaps 10-15 hours of total documentation effort. This is proportionate to the importance of foundational documents.

Cascade failure. An amendment to one foundational document has unexpected consequences for other foundational documents, creating inconsistencies that were not identified in the impact assessment. Mitigation: the cross-domain impact analysis (required for Tier 2) and the six-month post-amendment review.

Amendment fatigue. Multiple amendments in rapid succession exhaust the operator's deliberative capacity. Each individual amendment is well-considered, but the cumulative effect of constant foundational change destabilizes the institution. Mitigation: R-CON-011-06 requires evaluation of concurrent amendments in combination. If the operator finds themselves proposing multiple foundational amendments simultaneously, this is a signal that something larger may need to be addressed.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If a foundational document has been changed without following the amendment process: The unauthorized change must be reverted or retroactively processed through the amendment process. If reverted: the document is restored to its pre-change state and the unauthorized change is documented as an incident. If retroactively processed: the amendment enters the process at Step 1, and the current state is treated as a proposal, not a ratified change. The deliberation period still applies in full.
  2. If an amendment has had unexpected negative consequences: Assess the consequences. If they are severe, consider reverting the amendment through a new amendment process (which will be faster because the reasoning is already developed). If they are manageable, document them in the Commentary Section and monitor. The six-month review is designed to catch this scenario.
  3. If the amendment process itself is failing: The amendment process is subject to Tier 2 amendment. If the process is found to be too burdensome or not burdensome enough, it can be adjusted -- through the process itself, which ensures that any relaxation of safeguards is itself deliberate and documented.

Evolution Path

Years 0-5: The amendment process is established but rarely used. The foundational documents are new and the institution is in its building phase. The process should be tested at least once during this period, ideally with a low-stakes Tier 3 amendment, to verify that it works in practice.

Years 5-15: Amendments become more likely as operational experience reveals gaps or friction in the foundational documents. The process is used in earnest. The adversarial review and impact assessment components are validated against real amendments.

Years 15-30: Succession may trigger the need for amendments as a new operator brings new perspectives. The amendment process must prove that it can accommodate fresh thinking while maintaining institutional stability.

Years 30-50+: The amendment record -- the accumulated proposals, reasoning documents, adversarial reviews, and post-amendment reviews -- becomes a valuable historical record of the institution's philosophical evolution. This record is itself a form of institutional knowledge, preserved per CON-005.
