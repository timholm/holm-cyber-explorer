D4-011 — Maintenance Schedules and Procedures

Document ID
D4-011
Domain
4 -- Infrastructure & Power
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D4-001, D4-002, D4-003, D4-005, D4-006, D4-008
Depended Upon By
None (operational article; referenced by all infrastructure articles for maintenance context)

Purpose

This article defines the complete maintenance schedule for all physical infrastructure in the holm.chat Documentation Institution. It consolidates the maintenance tasks referenced across all Domain 4 articles into a single, authoritative schedule that the operator follows. It specifies what is inspected, tested, cleaned, and replaced; how often each task is performed; how each task is documented; and what to do when a maintenance task reveals a problem.

Preventive maintenance is the practice of performing routine tasks on infrastructure before it fails, rather than waiting for failure and reacting. The cost of preventive maintenance is known and predictable: a few hours per week, a day per quarter. The cost of reactive maintenance is unknown and often catastrophic: days of downtime, lost data, emergency procurement of replacement hardware, and the compounding effects of one failure triggering another.

This article is the operator's maintenance manual. It is designed to be opened at the start of every maintenance session and followed step by step. It does not explain why each task matters -- that explanation belongs in the respective system articles. This article explains what to do, when to do it, and how to record that it was done.

Scope

This article covers:

  • Daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, and annual maintenance tasks for all physical infrastructure systems.
  • Maintenance procedures for power systems, environmental controls, network infrastructure, computing hardware, and physical security.
  • Documentation requirements for all maintenance activities.
  • The maintenance log format and retention requirements.
  • Procedures for handling discoveries made during maintenance (anomalies, early-warning signs, failures found during inspection).

This article does not cover:

  • Software maintenance (updates, patches, configuration changes) -- see Domain 5.
  • Data backup procedures -- see Domain 12.
  • The design or specification of the systems being maintained -- see the respective D4 articles for each system.

Background

D4-001 introduces the maintenance philosophy and provides an overview of the maintenance schedule by frequency. This article expands that overview into a complete, task-level maintenance manual. Where D4-001 says "monthly: check all cable connections for physical integrity," this article specifies exactly which cables to check, what to look for, and how to record the results.

The maintenance schedule follows a hierarchical structure: daily tasks are subsets of weekly tasks, weekly tasks are subsets of monthly tasks, and so on. This means that if the operator is behind on daily checks but completes the monthly check, the monthly check implicitly covers the daily items. However, daily checks are not merely subsets of larger checks -- they serve a distinct purpose of early detection. A problem caught during a daily check is a minor inconvenience. The same problem discovered during a quarterly check may have been silently degrading systems for weeks.

OPS-001 establishes the operational tempo: the institution operates at a sustainable pace, with maintenance built into the regular operational rhythm rather than treated as an interruption. This article implements that principle by integrating maintenance into the operator's regular schedule rather than creating a separate, burdensome maintenance calendar.

System Model

11.1 Daily Maintenance (5-10 minutes)

Performed during or immediately after each operational session:

TaskSystemProcedureRecord
Check environmental readingsEnvironmentalRead the temperature and humidity display in the equipment area. Verify both are within the acceptable range (18-27C, 40-60% RH).Log the readings. Flag any out-of-range values.
Visual equipment inspectionAll hardwareWalk through the equipment area. Look for: unusual indicator lights, unusual sounds (clicking drives, grinding fans), unusual smells (ozone, burning), water on the floor.Note any anomalies. If none, record "visual inspection: nominal."
UPS status checkPowerCheck each UPS status display. Verify: online mode, battery health indicator green, no alarm indicators.Log status. Flag any warnings or alarms.
Physical security checkSecurityVerify equipment room door is locked when departing. Check that the access log has been signed for this entry.Access log entry (date, time, purpose).

11.2 Weekly Maintenance (15-30 minutes)

Performed once per week, on a consistent day:

TaskSystemProcedureRecord
Review environmental logsEnvironmentalReview the past week's temperature and humidity logs. Check for trends (gradual increase, unusual spikes, patterns correlated with time of day or weather).Summary note. Flag trends.
Check drive healthStorageReview S.M.A.R.T. data for all drives. Check for: reallocated sectors, pending sectors, uncorrectable errors, temperature warnings.Log any drives with new warnings. Compare to last week's readings.
Review system logsComputingReview hardware-level logs (kernel messages, IPMI/BMC logs if available) for hardware errors, thermal warnings, or ECC corrections.Note any hardware-related log entries.

11.3 Monthly Maintenance (1-2 hours)

Performed once per month, on a consistent date:

TaskSystemProcedureRecord
Cable inspectionNetworkVisually inspect all cable runs. Check for: loose connectors, kinked cables, cables under strain, damaged insulation, cables in contact with heat sources or water pipes.Note condition of each run. Flag any requiring attention.
Clean air filtersEnvironmentalIf equipment or the room has air filters, inspect and clean or replace as needed. Vacuum any dust accumulation around equipment intakes.Record filter condition and action taken.
Test water sensorEnvironmentalApply a small amount of water to the water detection sensor. Verify the alarm triggers. Dry immediately.Record pass/fail.
UPS battery checkPowerAccess the UPS management interface. Record: battery voltage, charge percentage, estimated runtime, battery temperature, number of discharge events since last check.Log all values. Compare to previous month.
Solar system checkPowerIf solar is installed: check inverter status and error logs. Record daily production totals for the past month. Compare to expected production for the season.Log production data and inverter status.
Security hardware checkSecurityTest all locks (equipment room door, equipment chassis locks, storage cabinets). Check door hinges and frames for wear. Inspect window security if applicable.Record condition of each lock and entry point.
Spare parts inventory checkInventoryVerify that all items on the spare parts list (D4-012) are present and in serviceable condition. Check battery-containing spares for charge level.Record any missing or degraded items.

11.4 Quarterly Maintenance (half day)

Performed once per quarter:

TaskSystemProcedureRecord
Internal equipment cleaningComputingPower down each machine in sequence. Open chassis. Use compressed air to remove dust from heat sinks, fans, circuit boards, and power supplies. Inspect for signs of corrosion, bulging capacitors, or discoloration.Record condition of each machine's internals. Flag any concerns.
Secondary power testPowerSimulate primary power failure (disconnect primary power at the distribution panel). Verify secondary power activates. Run on secondary power for at least 15 minutes. Monitor all systems for stability. Restore primary power. Verify clean switchback.Record: switchover time, system stability, duration, any issues.
Network auditNetworkPhysically trace every cable from machine to switch port. Compare physical connections to the network diagram. Verify that no unauthorized cables or devices are connected. Verify unused switch ports are disabled.Record any discrepancies between diagram and reality. Update diagram.
Wireless radio verificationSecurityFor each machine, verify that wireless hardware remains physically removed or disabled. Check BIOS/UEFI settings for any wireless-related changes.Record verification status for each machine.
Battery bank testPowerIf the institution uses battery banks beyond UPS: perform a controlled partial discharge test. Measure capacity against rated capacity. Record cell voltages if accessible.Log capacity measurements. Flag batteries below 80% of rated capacity.
Environmental sensor calibration checkEnvironmentalCompare institutional sensors against a known-good reference thermometer and hygrometer. Record any deviation. Recalibrate or replace sensors deviating more than 2C or 5% RH.Record readings from both sensors and deviation.

Performed once per year:

TaskSystemProcedureRecord
Full hardware health assessmentAll hardwareFor each piece of hardware: assess remaining useful life based on age, condition, S.M.A.R.T. data (for drives), and operational history. Create a replacement timeline for any hardware approaching end-of-life.Hardware health report for each unit. Replacement timeline.
Emergency power testPowerSimulate failure of both primary and secondary power. Verify emergency power activates. Perform a controlled shutdown within the emergency power window. Restart from shutdown state. Verify all systems and data integrity.Full test report: timing, procedure followed, system behavior, data integrity verification.
Power capacity assessmentPowerCalculate current total power draw. Compare against power generation capacity and storage capacity. Assess whether current margins are adequate. Project power needs for the coming year.Power budget spreadsheet. Recommendations.
Site assessmentPhysical siteInspect the building and site for: structural changes, new water risks, changes in the surrounding area (new construction, changes in drainage), condition of the roof, condition of electrical service entrance.Site assessment report. Flag any new risks.
Solar system inspectionPowerIf solar installed: physically inspect panels for damage, soiling, shading from new growth. Check mounting hardware. Review annual production against expected output. Clean panels. Inspect wiring connections.Solar inspection report. Production comparison.
Cable testingNetworkTest all permanent cable runs with a cable tester. Verify each meets its rated specification (Cat6 for new, Cat5e minimum for legacy). Replace any failing cables.Test results for each cable run. Replacement actions.
Documentation reviewAllReview all infrastructure documentation (network diagram, hardware register, power diagram) for accuracy and completeness. Update any outdated entries.Documentation update log.
Maintenance schedule reviewAllReview this maintenance schedule itself. Are all tasks still relevant? Are frequencies appropriate? Should new tasks be added based on the past year's experience? Update this article if changes are needed.Proposed changes to the maintenance schedule.

11.6 The Maintenance Log

All maintenance activities are recorded in the maintenance log. The log is a chronological record with the following fields for each entry: date, maintenance type (daily/weekly/monthly/quarterly/annual), tasks performed, findings (nominal or specific observations), actions taken (if any issues found), and the operator's signature. The maintenance log is a Tier 2 institutional record: actively maintained while the site is in use, retained for the institution's lifetime. The log may be a physical notebook, a digital log file on the institutional system, or both. If digital, the log must be included in the institution's backup procedures.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D4-50: Maintenance Is Not Optional. The maintenance schedule is mandatory, not advisory. Skipping or deferring maintenance tasks requires a documented justification in the maintenance log. Chronically deferred tasks must be reviewed to determine if the schedule is unsustainable and needs adjustment.
  • R-D4-51: Anomalies Trigger Investigation. Any anomaly discovered during maintenance -- an out-of-range reading, a new noise, a failing cable, a S.M.A.R.T. warning -- is investigated within 48 hours. The investigation and its outcome are recorded in the maintenance log.
  • R-D4-52: Maintenance Before Modification. Before any infrastructure modification (adding hardware, changing cables, reconfiguring power), the relevant maintenance tasks for the affected systems are performed first. Modifications are not performed on systems whose maintenance is overdue.
  • R-D4-53: No Simultaneous Tier 1 Maintenance. Maintenance that takes Tier 1 systems offline (as defined in D4-009) is never performed on more than one Tier 1 component simultaneously. If the primary storage server must be powered down for cleaning, the power system must be verified as healthy first, and vice versa.
  • R-D4-54: Maintenance Records Are Reviewed. During each quarterly maintenance session, the operator reviews the maintenance log for the past quarter. Patterns of recurring issues, gradually degrading readings, or frequently deferred tasks are identified and addressed.

Failure Modes

  • Maintenance fatigue. The operator, after years of finding nothing wrong during daily checks, begins to skip or rush through them. A problem that would have been caught early is missed for weeks. Mitigation: the maintenance log requires a recorded entry for each session, even when everything is nominal. The act of recording forces engagement. The quarterly log review catches gaps.
  • Maintenance-induced failure. A maintenance activity itself causes a problem: a cable is accidentally disconnected during inspection, a component is damaged during cleaning, a power test reveals (and triggers) a latent power system defect. Mitigation: maintenance is performed carefully, one system at a time. Before performing any maintenance that involves disconnecting or powering down equipment, the operator verifies that no other systems depend on that equipment being available.
  • Schedule creep. Over time, the maintenance schedule drifts as tasks take longer than expected or the operator's availability changes. Monthly tasks become bimonthly. Quarterly tasks become semiannual. Mitigation: R-D4-50 makes the schedule mandatory. If the schedule proves unsustainable, the correct response is to adjust the schedule formally (through the annual maintenance schedule review), not to silently skip tasks.
  • Incomplete documentation. Maintenance is performed but not recorded. The maintenance log has gaps that prevent pattern detection and undermine the institution's operational memory. Mitigation: the maintenance log is treated as part of the maintenance task. The task is not complete until it is recorded. The quarterly review checks for completeness.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If a maintenance backlog is discovered: Do not attempt to perform all overdue maintenance at once. Prioritize by system criticality (Tier 1 systems first, per D4-009). Perform the most overdue quarterly and annual tasks first, as these have the highest risk of uncovering hidden problems. Once the backlog is cleared, resume the regular schedule and investigate why the backlog accumulated.
  2. If maintenance reveals an imminent failure: Assess whether the component can continue to operate safely until a replacement is available. If yes, reduce the load on the component (per D4-009 degradation procedures) and procure a replacement. If no (risk of data loss or cascade failure), shut down the component immediately and activate the relevant spare or degradation procedure.
  3. If a maintenance activity causes a failure: Stop. Assess the scope of the failure. If it is limited to the component being maintained, restore the component from its pre-maintenance state (reconnect the cable, reseat the component, restore power). If the failure has spread to other systems, follow the relevant recovery procedures in D4-009 and D4-001. Document the incident and update the maintenance procedure to prevent recurrence.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-2: The maintenance schedule is calibrated against reality. Estimated task durations are compared to actual durations. Tasks that consistently find nothing useful are candidates for reduced frequency. Tasks that frequently find issues are candidates for increased frequency. The first annual review produces a significantly refined schedule.
  • Years 2-10: The schedule stabilizes as the operator develops a deep familiarity with the institution's infrastructure. Maintenance becomes a rhythm rather than a checklist. The log becomes a valuable historical record that reveals long-term trends invisible in any single session.
  • Years 10-25: As hardware is replaced, new maintenance procedures may be needed for new equipment types. The quarterly schedule may grow as the infrastructure expands. The principle of preventive maintenance remains constant; the specific tasks evolve with the hardware.
  • Years 25-50: The maintenance schedule has been rewritten multiple times as the hardware estate has turned over. The original specific tasks in this article are historical. The framework -- daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, annual, with documented results and quarterly review -- endures because it is a human-compatible rhythm that scales to any infrastructure of this size.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry:

Maintenance is the least glamorous part of running an institution and the most important. I can write a beautiful article on power architecture and feel proud. I can design an elegant network topology and feel clever. But if I do not check the UPS battery every month, the beautiful architecture fails on the day it is needed. If I do not inspect the cables every quarter, the elegant topology degrades silently into an unreliable mess.

I have designed this schedule to be sustainable for a single operator with other responsibilities. The daily check takes five to ten minutes. The weekly check takes fifteen to thirty minutes. The monthly check takes one to two hours. The quarterly check takes half a day. The annual check takes a full day. If this institution cannot afford one day per year and a few hours per month for maintenance, it cannot afford to exist. The infrastructure does not maintain itself. The operator maintains it, or it degrades.
