D11-010 — Resource Conflict Resolution

Document ID
D11-010
Domain
11 — Administration
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Draft

Purpose

This article defines the framework for resolving conflicts that arise when two or more institutional functions, domains, or projects compete for the same scarce resource. Resources in this context include monetary funds, hardware assets, consumables, the operator's labor-hours, energy capacity, storage capacity, and scheduling time. In a resource-constrained, self-sovereign institution, conflict is not exceptional — it is structural. There will always be more needs than resources, more maintenance than time, more plans than budget.

Without a defined resolution framework, resource conflicts are resolved by whichever need shouts loudest or arrives most recently. The urgent displaces the important. The visible displaces the hidden. The short-term displaces the long-term. This article establishes a principled prioritization framework that resolves conflicts based on institutional survival, operational necessity, and strategic value, in that order.

Scope

In scope:

  • Definition of resource conflict and the conditions that trigger the resolution process.
  • Prioritization framework: how competing needs are ranked.
  • Decision authority: who resolves conflicts at each level of significance.
  • Documentation requirements for conflict resolution decisions.
  • Appeal and review process for contested decisions.
  • Common conflict patterns and pre-resolved categories.

Out of scope:

  • Interpersonal conflict between operators (governance matter, GOV-001).
  • Resource allocation within a single domain (domain-internal matter).
  • Budget creation and resource forecasting (D11-003).
  • Emergency resource allocation during disaster recovery (Domain 12 takes precedence).

Background

Scarcity as a Permanent Condition

This institution operates under permanent scarcity. It is off-grid, so energy is limited. It is air-gapped, so procurement is expensive and infrequent. It is operated by a small number of people (currently one), so labor-hours are the scarcest resource of all. Scarcity is not a temporary condition to be overcome; it is a fundamental design constraint that shapes every operational decision.

In organizations with abundant resources, conflict resolution is often unnecessary because competing needs can simply be funded simultaneously. In this institution, funding one need often means explicitly defunding another. The choice to replace a failing drive this quarter means the choice to defer a planned storage expansion. The choice to spend a weekend on documentation review means the choice not to spend it on hardware maintenance. Every allocation is a trade-off, and trade-offs require a framework to be resolved consistently and transparently.

The Danger of Implicit Prioritization

When no explicit prioritization framework exists, priorities are set implicitly by habit, anxiety, and recency bias. The operator maintains the systems they personally find most interesting. They replace the component whose failure was most recently stressful. They allocate time to whichever domain is most on their mind. These implicit priorities are not necessarily wrong, but they are invisible, inconsistent, and unjustifiable to a successor. Explicit prioritization makes the reasoning visible, consistent, and transferable.

Conflict as Information

Resource conflicts are not merely problems to be solved; they are information about the institution's state. Frequent conflicts in the same resource category suggest that the budget is undersized, the infrastructure is undercapacity, or the operational scope exceeds available resources. The conflict resolution process includes a documentation requirement that captures this information, enabling the institution to detect and address systemic imbalances rather than merely resolving individual conflicts.

System Model

Conflict Identification

A resource conflict exists when:

  • Two or more identified needs require the same specific resource (a particular hardware component, a specific budget allocation, a particular time window).
  • The total need exceeds the total available resource (total maintenance hours needed exceed available labor-hours for the month; total procurement costs exceed the quarterly budget).
  • A proposed allocation would reduce an existing resource below its defined minimum threshold (D11-003 reserve minimums, D11-004 critical spare requirements).

Conflicts may be identified during budget planning, procurement batching, scheduling, or ad hoc when an unexpected need arises against an already-committed resource.

Prioritization Framework

Competing needs are evaluated against a five-tier priority hierarchy. Higher tiers always take precedence over lower tiers. Within a tier, needs are resolved by secondary criteria defined below.

  1. Tier 1 — Institutional Survival: Resources required to prevent the permanent loss of institutional data, the destruction of irreplaceable assets, or a threat to the physical safety of operators. This tier is absolute. Examples: replacing a failing drive that holds the only copy of the documentation corpus; repairing a physical security breach; procuring medication for an operator's health emergency. Tier 1 conflicts should be extremely rare. If they are common, the institution is in crisis.
  2. Tier 2 — Operational Continuity: Resources required to maintain the institution's core operations at minimum acceptable levels. This includes keeping primary systems online, maintaining environmental controls, and ensuring that the daily operational routine can be executed. Examples: replacing a failed UPS that protects the NAS; restocking essential consumables below the three-month threshold; repairing the primary power system.
  3. Tier 3 — Scheduled Obligations: Resources committed to planned maintenance, scheduled reviews, and calendar-driven activities. These represent commitments the institution has made to itself through the master calendar (D11-005). They are important because deferral creates debt. Examples: quarterly documentation review; annual budget reconciliation; scheduled hardware maintenance window.
  4. Tier 4 — Strategic Investment: Resources directed toward future capability, expansion, or improvement that is not immediately required for current operations. Examples: procuring spare components for future need; upgrading storage capacity beyond current requirements; beginning a technology migration that will not be needed for two years.
  5. Tier 5 — Enhancement: Resources directed toward quality-of-life improvements, aesthetic upgrades, experimental features, or nice-to-have capabilities. These are valuable but not critical. Examples: upgrading to quieter fans; reorganizing the workspace; experimenting with a new backup verification tool.

Within-Tier Resolution

When two competing needs fall within the same priority tier, secondary criteria are applied in order:

  1. Reversibility: Prefer the allocation that is more easily reversed. If deferring Need A creates irreversible damage but deferring Need B can be recovered from later, fund Need A.
  2. Time sensitivity: Prefer the allocation with the tighter deadline. A procurement need that must be met before a vendor discontinues a product takes precedence over a need that can wait until the next procurement window.
  3. Cross-domain impact: Prefer the allocation that benefits more domains. Replacing a shared infrastructure component serves the entire institution; upgrading a single-purpose tool serves one domain.
  4. Cost of deferral: Prefer the allocation where the cost of deferral is higher. Deferring maintenance on a component with accelerating degradation is more expensive than deferring maintenance on a stable component.

Decision Authority

The authority to resolve resource conflicts depends on the magnitude and scope of the conflict:

  • Single-domain, within-budget conflicts: Resolved by the domain role holder (or the single operator acting in that role). No formal documentation required beyond the standard record-keeping.
  • Cross-domain or budget-exceeding conflicts: Resolved by the Chief Operator using the prioritization framework. Documented on the Resource Conflict Resolution Record (F-CON-01).
  • Conflicts involving Tier 1 or Tier 2 resources: Resolved by the Chief Operator immediately, with governance review after the fact. Documented on F-CON-01 with an escalation note.
  • Recurring conflicts in the same resource category: Escalated to governance (GOV-001) as a systemic issue requiring a policy or budget adjustment, not just a one-time resolution.

Rules and Constraints

  • R-CON-01: All cross-domain resource conflicts must be resolved using the prioritization framework defined in this article. Resolving conflicts by personal preference, habit, or recency bias without reference to the framework is an administrative process violation.
  • R-CON-02: All cross-domain conflict resolutions must be documented on the Resource Conflict Resolution Record (F-CON-01). The record must include: the competing needs, their assigned priority tiers, the secondary criteria evaluation (if within the same tier), the decision, and the reasoning.
  • R-CON-03: No conflict resolution may deplete a resource below its defined minimum threshold (D11-003, D11-004) unless the conflict involves a Tier 1 (Institutional Survival) need. Reserve thresholds exist precisely to prevent lower-priority conflicts from consuming resources that may be needed for survival.
  • R-CON-04: Recurring conflicts — defined as conflicts in the same resource category occurring more than twice per year — must be escalated to governance as a systemic issue. The conflict resolution process resolves individual instances; governance resolves the underlying imbalance.
  • R-CON-05: Deferred needs resulting from conflict resolution must be rescheduled. A conflict resolution is not complete until the losing need has a new target date. Unscheduled deferrals become forgotten deferrals.
  • R-CON-06: The conflict resolution record must be reviewed as part of the quarterly administrative review (D11-008). Patterns in conflicts — which resources are most contested, which domains are most affected, which tiers are most common — provide valuable data for budget and planning adjustments.

Failure Modes

  • Priority inflation. Every need is classified as Tier 1 or Tier 2 to ensure it receives resources. The priority system collapses because everything is equally urgent. Mitigation: the tier definitions are concrete and specific. Tier 1 requires a threat of permanent data loss, irreplaceable asset destruction, or physical safety risk. If these conditions are genuinely met, the classification is correct. If they are not met, the classification is wrong, and this must be challenged during review.
  • Invisible deferral debt. Deferred needs accumulate without tracking. The operator resolves a conflict by deferring Need B, but B is never rescheduled. Months later, B has been forgotten and the system it was meant to maintain has degraded. Mitigation: R-CON-05 requires rescheduling of deferred needs. The quarterly review (R-CON-06) catches any deferrals that have not been resolved.
  • Single-domain capture. One domain consistently wins resource conflicts because its needs are more visible, more dramatic, or more aligned with the operator's interests. Other domains are systematically starved. Mitigation: the cross-domain impact criterion in within-tier resolution helps counterbalance single-domain capture. The quarterly conflict review should specifically look for patterns of domain-specific winning or losing.
  • Analysis paralysis. The prioritization framework is so thorough that every conflict requires extensive deliberation. Simple allocation decisions take hours instead of minutes. Mitigation: the framework is designed for cross-domain conflicts that genuinely require evaluation. Single-domain, within-budget conflicts are explicitly excluded from formal resolution. The framework provides a structured decision path, not a debate forum.
  • Reserve erosion through cascading conflicts. A series of individually justified Tier 2 allocations gradually depletes reserves below minimum thresholds. Each decision was correct in isolation, but the cumulative effect creates a resource crisis. Mitigation: R-CON-03 protects reserve thresholds. When a Tier 2 allocation would breach a threshold, the conflict must be re-evaluated: is the need truly Tier 2, or is it Tier 3 dressed in urgency?

Recovery Procedures

  1. If the prioritization framework has been bypassed: Review recent resource allocation decisions. For each decision, retroactively apply the prioritization framework and determine whether the outcome would have been different. If allocations were made that would not have been justified under the framework, assess whether they can be reversed or whether the damage is done. Recommit to framework adherence going forward.
  2. If deferral debt has accumulated: Compile a complete list of all deferred needs from conflict resolution records and from any informal notes or memory. Prioritize the list using the standard framework. Schedule the top items for immediate attention. If the backlog is too large to clear within one quarter, escalate to governance as a systemic resource imbalance.
  3. If reserves have been depleted by cascading conflicts: Declare an austerity period per D11-003 recovery procedures. Suspend all Tier 4 and Tier 5 allocations until reserves are restored. Review the conflict resolution records to understand how the depletion occurred. Adjust budget forecasts if the reserve minimum was set too low for the institution's actual conflict rate.
  4. If priority inflation has corrupted the tier system: Re-ground the tier definitions by reviewing actual recent conflicts and honestly classifying them. Tier 1 requires demonstrated risk of permanent loss or safety threat — not inconvenience, not degraded performance, not delayed plans. Re-classify inflated priorities and adjust allocations accordingly.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: Resource conflicts are relatively simple because the institution is small. The prioritization framework may feel over-engineered. Use it anyway — it builds the habit of principled resource allocation and creates a record of early trade-off decisions that will inform future planning.
  • Years 3-10: The institution's complexity has grown. Multiple systems compete for maintenance time, energy, and budget. The conflict resolution records reveal patterns: which resources are chronically scarce, which domains are chronically underfunded. These patterns feed into budget and planning adjustments.
  • Years 10-25: Major technology transitions create intense but temporary resource conflicts. The framework is tested under conditions of genuine scarcity and competing high-priority needs. The within-tier criteria (reversibility, time sensitivity, cross-domain impact, cost of deferral) prove their value in distinguishing between legitimately competing Tier 2 needs.
  • Years 25-50+: The conflict resolution archive tells the story of the institution's resource allocation choices over decades. It reveals what was consistently valued, what was consistently deferred, and what was fought over most intensely. For successor operators and institutional historians, this is a record of priorities in action, not just priorities in theory.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 — Founding Entry: I already face resource conflicts daily, even with an institution this young. Should I spend Sunday afternoon on documentation or on hardware maintenance? Should I buy the spare drives now or save the money for the solar panel upgrade? Should I use the energy surplus for a full system check or save it for tomorrow's backup run? These decisions feel trivial individually, but they compound. A year of systematically favoring hardware over documentation leaves the documentation corpus stale. A year of deferring procurement leaves me without spares when something fails. The prioritization framework is my tool for making these decisions consistently rather than whimsically. I expect to argue with my own framework regularly — and that argument, the process of justifying my intuition against a structured priority system, is exactly the point.
