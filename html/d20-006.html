D20-006 — Legacy Preservation & Attribution

Document ID
D20-006
Domain
20 — Institutional Memory
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D20-001 (Philosophy), D20-008 (Memory Integrity)
Depended Upon By
D20-007 (Context Recovery), D20-009 (Memory Accessibility)

Purpose

This article defines how the holm.chat Documentation Institution records, attributes, and honors the contributions of its past, present, and future participants across generations. It governs the mechanisms by which a person's work within the institution is made visible, traceable, and durable -- not as vanity, but as an essential component of institutional memory and a prerequisite for context recovery.

Attribution serves three functions. First, it is a matter of justice: people who build things deserve to have their work recognized. Second, it is a matter of utility: when a future maintainer encounters a decision, a document, or a system, knowing who created it provides a crucial link in the context recovery chain. If you know who made a decision, you can find their other decisions, their oral histories, their commentary entries, and their patterns of reasoning. Attribution is an index into the rest of institutional memory. Third, it is a matter of motivation: people who know their contributions will be remembered are more likely to document their work carefully and participate in oral history capture willingly.

Legacy preservation goes beyond attribution. It addresses the question of how the institution remembers people as whole contributors, not just as names attached to individual artifacts. Over a 50-year horizon, the institution will be shaped by dozens or hundreds of people. Some will serve for decades; others for months. All will leave something behind. This article ensures that what they leave behind is findable, understandable, and honored.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The attribution model: how contributions are tagged with contributor identity.
  • The contributor registry: a permanent record of every person who has contributed to the institution.
  • Legacy profiles: structured records of a contributor's tenure, roles, major contributions, and departure.
  • Attribution in documentation: how articles, decision log entries, commentary, and other artifacts are attributed.
  • Posthumous and post-departure attribution: how attribution is maintained after a contributor is no longer present.
  • The relationship between attribution and privacy.
  • Dispute resolution for attribution conflicts.

This article does not cover:

  • The decision log format itself (see D20-002), though attribution is a required field in every entry.
  • Oral history capture (see D20-005), though oral histories are a key source for legacy profiles.
  • The interface for browsing contributor records (covered by Domain 16).
  • Contributor identity authentication (covered by Security Domains).
  • Governance authority over contributor records (covered by Governance Domains).

Background

3.1 The Attribution Problem in Long-Lived Institutions

In the early years of an institution, attribution is trivial. Everyone knows who did what. The founder's fingerprints are on everything. But as years pass, as people come and go, as documents are revised and systems rebuilt, attribution erodes. The original author's name is lost in a chain of revisions. The person who designed the architecture is forgotten when the architecture is replaced. The quiet contributor who maintained the backups for a decade is invisible because backup maintenance does not produce visible artifacts.

This erasure is not malicious. It is entropic. Without active effort to preserve attribution, it decays naturally. This article defines the active effort required to resist that decay.

3.2 Attribution as Context Recovery Tool

When a future maintainer encounters a puzzling decision in the decision log, the first question is often "who made this decision?" Not to assign blame, but to find more context. If Decision X was made by Person A, and Person A also made Decisions Y and Z, and Person A gave an oral history in 2035, then the future maintainer has a rich web of context to draw from. Without attribution, each decision stands alone, disconnected from the reasoning patterns of the person who made it.

Attribution transforms the decision log from a flat list of isolated entries into a graph of connected knowledge, navigable by person, by time, by role, and by topic.

3.3 The Ethics of Remembering People

Remembering people within an institution raises ethical considerations. Some contributors may prefer to be forgotten. Some contributions may be associated with decisions that later proved harmful. Some people may have left the institution under difficult circumstances. The institution must balance its need for complete memory against the legitimate interests of individuals. This article establishes that balance: institutional decisions and operational knowledge are always attributed (because the institution's memory depends on it), but personal information beyond what is relevant to institutional function may be redacted at the subject's request.

System Model

4.1 The Contributor Registry

The contributor registry is a permanent, append-only record of every person who has contributed to the institution. Each entry contains:

FieldDescriptionRequired
Contributor IDA unique, permanent identifier assigned at first contributionYes
Display NameThe name by which the contributor is known within the institutionYes
Roles HeldList of roles with start and end datesYes
First Contribution DateDate of first recorded contributionYes
Departure DateDate of departure, if applicableWhen applicable
Departure TypePlanned, unplanned, or role transitionWhen applicable
Oral History SessionsList of oral history session IDs involving this contributorWhen applicable
Legacy Profile ReferenceReference to the contributor's legacy profile documentWhen created
Privacy PreferencesAny redaction or privacy requests from the contributorWhen applicable

The contributor registry is classified as Tier 1 (permanent record). Entries are never deleted. If a contributor requests privacy modifications, a new entry is appended noting the modification, but the original entry is preserved in sealed storage accessible only for integrity verification.

4.2 Legacy Profiles

A legacy profile is a structured narrative document created for contributors who have served the institution in a significant capacity. It is not created for every contributor (a person who made a single minor edit does not need a legacy profile), but it is created for anyone who held a named role, served for more than 12 months, or made contributions that materially shaped the institution.

A legacy profile contains:

  • A narrative summary of the contributor's tenure: when they joined, what they did, what they built, what decisions they shaped.
  • A list of their major contributions, cross-referenced to specific articles, decision log entries, and systems.
  • Key excerpts from their oral history sessions, if any.
  • A note on their departure: when, why (to the extent appropriate), and what knowledge was captured.
  • References to their Commentary Section entries across the documentation.

4.3 Attribution in Artifacts

Every institutional artifact must carry attribution metadata:

  • Articles: Author name, reviewer names, and dates in the metadata aside. Revision authors tracked in the article's version history.
  • Decision log entries: The "Decided By" field is mandatory per D20-002.
  • Commentary sections: Each entry is dated and attributed per the article template.
  • Oral history sessions: Interviewer and subject names recorded at session start per D20-005.
  • System configurations: The person who made each significant configuration change is recorded in the operational log.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D20-06-01: Every Artifact Has an Author. No article, decision log entry, commentary entry, or other institutional record may be created without attribution to a specific contributor. Anonymous contributions are not permitted. If the original author is unknown (as with inherited or imported content), the attribution field must state "Author unknown -- imported by [name] on [date]."

  • R-D20-06-02: Contributor IDs Are Permanent. Once a contributor ID is assigned, it is never reused, even if the contributor departs. The ID permanently links all of the contributor's work across the institution.

  • R-D20-06-03: Legacy Profiles for Significant Contributors. A legacy profile must be created within 90 days of the departure of any contributor who held a named role or served for more than 12 months. The profile is created by the departing contributor's closest institutional colleague, reviewed by at least one other member, and integrated into the archive.

  • R-D20-06-04: Attribution Survives Revision. When an article is revised, the original author's attribution is preserved. The revision adds the reviser's name but does not remove the original author's name. Attribution is cumulative, not replaceable.

  • R-D20-06-05: Privacy Requests Are Honored Within Limits. A contributor may request that personal information beyond their institutional role and contributions be redacted from public-facing records. However, they may not request removal of attribution for institutional decisions, operational actions, or documentation they authored. The institution's need for memory integrity (D20-008) takes precedence over individual preference for anonymity regarding institutional acts.

  • R-D20-06-06: No Posthumous Erasure. After a contributor's departure, their attribution may not be removed or diminished, even if subsequent events cast their contributions in a negative light. If a decision they made proves harmful, the decision log records the correction with a new entry, but the original attribution stands. History is not improved by erasure.

Failure Modes

  • Attribution erosion. Over successive revisions, original attribution is lost. Articles list only the most recent editor. Severity: moderate (degrades context recovery capability). Likelihood: high without active prevention. Detection: periodic audit comparing article metadata to the contributor registry. Mitigation: R-D20-06-04 mandates cumulative attribution.

  • Missing legacy profiles. Contributors depart without legacy profiles being created. Their institutional knowledge is not summarized or cross-referenced. Severity: moderate. Likelihood: moderate (the 90-day deadline may be missed under operational pressure). Detection: departure checklist audit. Mitigation: the departure protocol in GOV-001 must include a legacy profile creation step.

  • Attribution disputes. Two or more contributors disagree about who deserves credit for a particular contribution. Severity: low (institutional function is not impaired, but morale may be). Likelihood: low. Detection: dispute report. Mitigation: attribute to all claimants. When in doubt, over-attribute rather than under-attribute.

  • Privacy conflict. A contributor requests erasure that would compromise institutional memory. Severity: moderate. Likelihood: low. Detection: privacy request review. Mitigation: R-D20-06-05 establishes the boundary: personal information may be redacted, institutional acts may not.

  • Contributor registry corruption. The registry is damaged, and contributor IDs can no longer be resolved. Severity: high (all attribution across the institution becomes unreliable). Likelihood: low (the registry is a Tier 1 permanent record with redundant storage). Detection: integrity verification per D20-008. Mitigation: restore from verified backup; treat as security incident per SEC-001.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If attribution has eroded: Conduct an attribution audit. Compare each article's metadata to the contributor registry and to the article's version history (if maintained). Restore original author attribution where it can be determined. Where it cannot, add an annotation: "Original author not recoverable as of [date]." This itself becomes part of the record.

  2. If a legacy profile is missing: Create a retrospective profile using available sources: the contributor's decision log entries, commentary sections, oral history sessions (if any), and the recollections of current members who worked with them. Mark the profile as "Retrospective -- created [date], [years] after departure" to distinguish it from contemporaneous profiles.

  3. If the contributor registry is corrupted: Restore from the most recent verified backup. Cross-reference the restored registry against article metadata, decision log entries, and other attributed artifacts to verify completeness. Any contributor found in artifacts but missing from the restored registry must be re-entered with a note explaining the gap.

  4. If a privacy dispute cannot be resolved: Escalate to the governance authority defined in GOV-001. The governance body reviews the request against the principles in R-D20-06-05 and makes a determination. The determination itself is recorded in the decision log. If the contributor disagrees with the determination, their disagreement is preserved in the decision log per D20-001's dissent preservation principle.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The contributor registry is small. Legacy profiles are few (ideally zero, because nobody has departed yet). The attribution system is being established. The primary task is ensuring that attribution habits are formed: every artifact has an author, every decision has a name attached. If these habits are not established now, they will be impossible to retrofit later.

  • Years 5-15: The first departures occur. The first legacy profiles are created. The attribution graph begins to have depth: some contributors have dozens of attributed artifacts across multiple domains. The contributor registry becomes a useful research tool for context recovery.

  • Years 15-30: The institution has had multiple generations of contributors. The legacy profiles form a narrative of the institution's human history alongside its operational history. Attribution becomes essential for understanding why the institution evolved as it did: "This design decision was made by Person A, who was influenced by their experience with Problem X, as documented in their oral history from 2032."

  • Years 30-50: Contributors from the founding era are likely no longer active. Their legacy profiles, attribution records, and oral histories are the primary means by which current members understand the founding context. The quality of legacy preservation from the early years determines how well the institution understands its own origins.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: Attribution matters more than most people think. In the short term, everyone knows who did what, and attribution feels like bureaucratic overhead. In the long term, attribution is the connective tissue that holds institutional memory together. Without it, the decision log is a list of anonymous pronouncements. With it, the decision log is a conversation among identifiable people whose reasoning can be traced, understood, and built upon.

I want to be explicit about the anti-erasure principle (R-D20-06-06). The temptation to remove a person's name from the record when their legacy becomes inconvenient is one of the most common forms of institutional revisionism. It is always wrong. The historical record must reflect what actually happened, including the contributions of people who later proved fallible, controversial, or harmful. To erase them is to lie about the institution's history, and an institution that lies about its history cannot learn from it.

Future maintainers: if you are reading a legacy profile and thinking "I wish I had known this person," then the profile has succeeded. If you are reading it and thinking "I do not understand why this person made the decisions they made," then the profile has failed and should be supplemented through context recovery (D20-007).
