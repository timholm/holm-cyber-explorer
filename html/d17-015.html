D17-015 — Federation Health Monitoring

Document ID
D17-015
Domain
17 -- Scaling & Federation
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D17-009 (Topology Models), D17-004 (Conflict Resolution), D17-010 (Resource Allocation)
Depended Upon By
Referenced by D19-ART-010 (Federation-Level QA), D17-008 (governance health triggers), Domain 20 (health event recording).

Purpose

This article defines how the federation assesses its own health -- whether it is functioning as intended, degrading slowly, or approaching failure. Health monitoring for a conventional networked system is automated: sensors report metrics in real time, dashboards display status, alerts fire when thresholds are breached. None of this applies to an air-gapped federation. Nodes cannot ping each other. There is no central dashboard. Status information travels at the speed of sneakernet. The federation's health picture is always delayed, always incomplete, and always assembled from fragmentary reports carried on physical media.

Despite these constraints, the federation must monitor its health. Without health monitoring, degradation is invisible until it becomes crisis. A transport link that has been dormant for months is not noticed until someone needs it. A node that is approaching resource exhaustion does not signal distress because there is no signal channel. A governance process that has stalled does not trigger an alert because there is no alert system. This article provides the framework for assembling a health picture from the data that is available, even when that data is sparse and delayed.

This article is addressed to whoever holds the federation coordination role -- the person or node responsible for compiling the health assessment from reports submitted by individual nodes. It is also addressed to every node operator, who must understand what data they need to report and why their report matters to the federation's self-awareness.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The federation health model: what dimensions of health are measured and how.
  • Health indicators: the specific metrics that compose the federation health picture.
  • The health assessment cycle: when and how the health picture is assembled.
  • Degradation indicators: early warning signs of declining federation health.
  • Health thresholds and response levels: when the health picture triggers action.
  • The health report format: how health information is documented and distributed.

This article does not cover:

  • Individual node health (covered by node operational procedures and OPS-001).
  • Resource allocation decisions in response to health findings (see D17-010).
  • Governance actions triggered by health findings (see D17-008).
  • Quality assurance of the health monitoring process itself (see D19).
  • Historical analysis of health trends (see Domain 20).

Background

3.1 The Challenge of Distributed Health Monitoring Without a Network

In a networked system, health monitoring is continuous and centralized. A monitoring server polls each node, collects metrics, and computes aggregate health. In an air-gapped federation, this model is impossible. There is no monitoring server. There is no polling. Metrics arrive via sync packages, which means they are already old when they arrive. The health picture is assembled from stale data by a human, not computed in real time by a machine.

This constraint shapes everything about the health monitoring approach. The metrics must be meaningful even when they are weeks or months old. The indicators must detect trends, not just instantaneous states. The health assessment must be useful to operators who cannot verify it against current conditions at remote nodes. The entire system must be robust against incomplete data -- a node that has not reported in several cycles is itself an indicator, even though the specific reason for the silence is unknown.

3.2 Health as a Composite Measure

Federation health is not a single number. It is a composite of multiple dimensions, each of which can be healthy or degraded independently. The five health dimensions defined in this article are: connectivity (are nodes syncing?), consistency (are nodes aligned on shared standards?), resource adequacy (do nodes have sufficient resources?), governance vitality (is the governance process functioning?), and human capacity (are operators present, capable, and engaged?). A federation can be healthy on four dimensions and degraded on one. The health assessment must capture this nuance, not collapse it into a single "red/yellow/green" indicator.

3.3 The Observer Problem

In an air-gapped federation, no single node has a complete picture of federation health. Each node knows its own state and the state of its direct sync partners (as reported in the most recent sync package). A hub node may have a broader view than a spoke node, but even the hub's view is delayed and incomplete. The health assessment is therefore always an estimate, always incomplete, and always subject to revision when new data arrives. This uncertainty must be accepted, not hidden. A health report that claims certainty it cannot have is worse than one that acknowledges its limitations.

System Model

4.1 The Five Health Dimensions

Dimension 1: Connectivity. Are nodes exchanging sync packages at the expected frequency? Connectivity is measured by the sync regularity metric: for each transport link in the topology, the ratio of actual sync exchanges to expected sync exchanges over the assessment period. A link with a sync regularity of 1.0 is operating perfectly. A link below 0.5 is significantly degraded. A link at 0.0 is dormant.

Dimension 2: Consistency. Are nodes operating under the same constitutional baseline? Consistency is measured by comparing the baseline version numbers reported by each node in its governance sync. A federation where all nodes report the same baseline version is fully consistent. A federation with multiple baseline versions has consistency gaps that need reconciliation.

Dimension 3: Resource Adequacy. Do nodes have sufficient resources to sustain their institutional and federation functions? Resource adequacy is measured by the resource budget reports from D17-010. Nodes reporting AVAILABLE below 10% of CAPACITY in any resource category are in a pre-failure state. The aggregate resource picture shows whether the federation is well-provisioned or strained.

Dimension 4: Governance Vitality. Is the governance process functioning? Governance vitality is measured by: the number of pending proposals and their age (a growing backlog indicates paralysis), the participation rate in recent votes (declining participation indicates fatigue), and the time since the last ratified change (a very long period may indicate stagnation or stability -- context determines which).

Dimension 5: Human Capacity. Are operators present, capable, and engaged? Human capacity is the most difficult dimension to measure quantitatively. Indicators include: whether each node has submitted its quarterly reports on time, whether the operator has participated in governance, whether succession plans are documented and current, and whether the operator's self-reported wellbeing (in the resource report) indicates sustainability.

4.3 The Health Assessment Cycle

Federation health is assessed quarterly, aligned with the OPS-001 quarterly review cycle. The assessment process is:

  1. Data collection (ongoing). Each node includes its health data in every governance sync package: resource budget, sync log summary, governance participation record, and operator self-assessment. This data accumulates at each node as sync packages arrive.
  2. Compilation (quarterly). The federation coordinator (a rotating role, assigned per D17-008) compiles the health data from all received sync packages. The compilation includes: the date range covered, which nodes have reported, which nodes have not reported (and why, if known), and the current value of each health indicator.
  3. Assessment (quarterly). The coordinator classifies each indicator as healthy, warning, or critical using the thresholds in Section 4.2. The coordinator writes a narrative assessment that contextualizes the numbers: a warning-level sync regularity might be explained by a known seasonal transport disruption, for example.
  4. Distribution (quarterly). The health report is included in the next governance sync package to all nodes. Each node reviews the report, adds its own commentary if needed, and stores it in institutional memory.
  5. Response (as needed). Critical indicators trigger specific response actions, detailed in Section 4.4.

4.4 Response Levels

Health indicators in the warning or critical range trigger graduated responses:

Warning level. The indicator is noted in the health report. The coordinator includes a recommendation for corrective action. The affected node (or the federation as a whole, for aggregate indicators) is expected to address the issue by the next quarterly assessment. No governance action is required at the warning level.

Critical level. The indicator is flagged in the health report as requiring immediate attention. The coordinator issues a HEALTH-ALERT document (included in the next governance sync) that describes the critical indicator, the potential consequences if it is not addressed, and the recommended response. If the critical indicator persists for two consecutive quarters, the coordinator may initiate a governance proposal for structural remediation (topology change, resource redistribution, governance reform) per D17-008.

Multiple critical indicators. If three or more indicators are simultaneously in the critical range, the coordinator issues a FEDERATION-HEALTH-EMERGENCY notice. This triggers a mandatory review at every node: each operator assesses their node's contribution to the emergency and proposes remedial actions. The federation enters a remediation period during which new governance proposals (other than health-related ones) are suspended.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D17-015-01: Health Reporting Is Mandatory. Every federated node MUST include health data in its quarterly governance sync. The data must include at minimum: the node's resource budget summary, sync log for the quarter, governance participation record, and operator self-assessment. Nodes that fail to report for two consecutive quarters are flagged as a critical human-capacity indicator.

  • R-D17-015-02: Health Assessment Must Acknowledge Uncertainty. Every health report MUST explicitly state what data is missing, what data is stale, and what nodes have not reported. A health report that presents an incomplete picture as complete is misleading and dangerous. The health picture is always partial. The report must say so.

  • R-D17-015-03: Coordinator Role Rotates. The federation health coordinator role SHOULD rotate among nodes on an annual basis to prevent any single node from monopolizing the federation's self-perception. The rotation schedule is determined by governance (D17-008). If no node volunteers for the coordinator role, the current coordinator continues, but this itself is a critical human-capacity indicator.

  • R-D17-015-04: Health Data Is Not Punitive. Health reports MUST NOT be used to punish, blame, or shame individual nodes. A node reporting resource scarcity or operator fatigue is being transparent, and transparency must be rewarded, not penalized. The health report identifies problems for the federation to solve collectively, not individuals to discipline.

  • R-D17-015-05: Trend Matters More Than Snapshot. The health assessment SHOULD prioritize trends over individual data points. A single quarter with a warning-level indicator is less concerning than three consecutive quarters of declining metrics. The health report MUST include trend analysis comparing the current quarter to at least the two previous quarters.

  • R-D17-015-06: Health Reports Are Permanent Records. Every health report is stored in institutional memory (Domain 20) as a permanent record. The accumulation of health reports across years and decades provides the longitudinal data needed to detect slow degradation that is invisible in any single report.

Failure Modes

  • Health monitoring theater. The health reports are produced on schedule, but no one reads them or acts on their findings. Warning indicators persist for quarters or years without response. The reports exist as documentation but not as a functional feedback loop. Likelihood: medium over time, especially as the federation matures and health monitoring becomes routine. Severity: high, because it negates the entire purpose of health monitoring. Detection: the trend analysis should reveal persistent warning or critical indicators that are never addressed.

  • Optimistic reporting bias. Node operators underreport problems because they do not want to appear weak, burdened, or uncommitted. Resource budgets show comfortable margins when the reality is tight. Operator self-assessments report "fine" when the operator is struggling. The health picture is systematically rosier than reality. Likelihood: medium to high, as this is a natural human tendency. Severity: medium, because it delays response until problems become visible through other means. Detection: comparison of self-reported data against objective indicators (sync regularity, report timeliness, governance participation) that are harder to misrepresent.

  • Health data staleness. The health assessment is based on data that is weeks or months old by the time it is compiled and distributed. A node that was healthy when it submitted its report may have deteriorated since. The health picture describes the past, not the present. Likelihood: certain -- this is an inherent property of air-gapped health monitoring. Severity: medium, mitigated by designing indicators that detect trends rather than instantaneous states. Detection: cannot be fully detected; acknowledged by R-D17-015-02.

  • Coordinator burnout. The health coordinator role requires compiling data from all nodes, analyzing trends, writing narrative assessments, and distributing reports. For a large federation, this is a significant workload. The coordinator becomes overwhelmed, and health reports become late, superficial, or absent. Likelihood: medium. Severity: high, as it removes the federation's self-awareness. Detection: declining quality or timeliness of health reports; coordinator self-report in their node's operator assessment.

  • Metric fixation. The federation focuses on the measured indicators and ignores everything else. A dimension of health that is not captured by the indicators degrades without notice. The indicators become targets (Goodhart's Law), and operators optimize for the metrics rather than for actual health. Likelihood: medium over decades. Severity: medium. Detection: periodic review of whether the indicators still capture what matters; the five-year health framework review.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If health monitoring theater is detected: Review the last four quarterly health reports. Identify all warning and critical indicators that have persisted without action. For each, assign a responsible node and a deadline for response. Escalate any indicator that has been critical for more than two quarters to a governance proposal for structural remedy. Consider whether the health report format needs to include more prominent "action required" sections.

  2. If optimistic reporting bias is suspected: Introduce cross-validation: compare self-reported resource budgets against objective consumption data (storage usage, sync package sizes, governance participation rates). Where self-reports diverge significantly from objective measures, flag the discrepancy in the health report (without blaming the reporter) and request clarification. Create a culture where reporting problems is valued more than reporting success.

  3. If the coordinator becomes overloaded: Redistribute the coordinator workload. In a large federation, the compilation task can be split by dimension: one node compiles connectivity data, another compiles resource data, and so on. The coordinator then assembles the dimension reports into the final health report. If the workload remains unsustainable, increase the coordinator rotation frequency or reduce the report scope temporarily.

  4. If metric fixation is detected: Conduct a health framework review (scheduled every five years per the Evolution Path). Revisit the five health dimensions: are they still the right dimensions? Are there health concerns that the current indicators miss? Retire or modify indicators that have become targets rather than measures. Add qualitative sections to the health report that invite narrative observations beyond what the metrics capture.

  5. If a federation health emergency is declared: Every node enters remediation mode. All non-essential federation activities (content sharing, discovery enrichment) are suspended. Each node focuses on its critical indicators: addressing resource scarcity, resuming dormant transport links, clearing governance backlogs, or supporting struggling operators. The emergency continues until the majority of critical indicators return to warning level or below. The emergency and its resolution are recorded in institutional memory as a major federation event.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Health monitoring for a small federation is informal. Two or three nodes can assess their health through direct conversation during sync exchanges. Use this period to establish the health reporting habit and the indicator framework, even when the federation is too small for the formal process to be strictly necessary. The habit will be needed when the federation grows.

  • Years 5-15: The federation grows to the point where informal health assessment is insufficient. The formal health report becomes operationally valuable. The first trend analyses become possible, revealing patterns that were not visible in individual quarters. The health indicators may need refinement as the federation discovers which metrics are useful and which are noise.

  • Years 15-30: The health report archive contains years of longitudinal data. Slow degradation trends -- the kind that are invisible in any single report -- become visible in the long-term trend analysis. The five-year framework review cycle produces meaningful refinements to the indicator set. The health monitoring system itself becomes a subject of institutional memory: what health crises has the federation survived, and what did the health reports reveal (or miss) in each case?

  • Years 30-50+: The health monitoring framework is one of the institution's most important self-awareness tools. Future operators inherit not just the framework but decades of health data. They can see the federation's entire health history and detect patterns that span generations of operators. The health report is not just an operational tool -- it is part of the federation's institutional memory, documenting not just whether the federation was healthy but what health meant to the people who built and maintained it.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: Health monitoring for an air-gapped federation is an exercise in humility. You cannot know the current state of the federation. You can only know the state of the federation as it was when each node last sent you a sync package. By the time you compile the health report, every data point in it is already history. This is uncomfortable for anyone accustomed to real-time dashboards and instant alerts.

But delayed health data is not useless health data. A doctor who sees a patient once a year can still detect trends: weight gain, blood pressure changes, declining mobility. A quarterly health report that shows sync regularity declining over three consecutive quarters is a clear signal, even if the most recent data point is six weeks old. The health monitoring framework is designed for trend detection, not real-time alerting. It accepts its limitations and works within them.

The human capacity dimension is the one I am most concerned about. Technical metrics -- sync regularity, baseline versions, resource budgets -- can be measured objectively. Human wellbeing cannot. An operator who reports "fine" may be exhausted, isolated, or losing motivation. The health framework must create space for honest self-report, and the federation must create a culture where honest self-report is safe. This is a social challenge, not a technical one, and no metric can fully solve it.

I debated whether to include a single "overall health score" -- a number from 1 to 10 that summarizes the entire federation's health. I decided against it. A single number invites gaming, obscures nuance, and creates false precision. Health is a story told by multiple indicators, and the narrative assessment in the health report is more valuable than any aggregate number could be.
