CON-005 — The Theory of Knowledge Preservation

Document ID
CON-005
Domain
1 -- Constitution & Philosophy
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Draft

Purpose

This article defines the epistemological framework of the holm.chat Documentation Institution -- the theory of what counts as institutional knowledge, how knowledge is captured, how it is validated, how it decays, and how it is renewed. It answers the question that precedes every documentation effort: why do we write things down, what exactly are we preserving, and how do we know that what we have preserved is still true?

The institution's very name declares that documentation is its core activity. This article provides the philosophical justification for that declaration. Without a theory of knowledge preservation, documentation becomes a mechanical activity -- words are written, files are stored, but the knowledge they were meant to carry may be lost, corrupted, or rendered meaningless without anyone noticing.

This article is addressed to everyone who will ever write, review, maintain, or read institutional documentation. It asks you to think about documentation not as a task but as an act of preservation -- the transfer of understanding across time, from the person who knows something now to the person who will need to know it later, including the possibility that these are the same person, years apart, with the earlier knowledge forgotten.

Scope

This article governs the philosophical and epistemological foundations of all documentation activities across every domain. It does not define specific documentation formats, templates, or procedures -- those are defined in the Meta-Documentation domain and in individual domain articles. It defines the theory that those formats, templates, and procedures must serve.

Specifically, this article covers:

  • The definition of institutional knowledge and its distinction from data, information, and opinion.
  • The mechanisms by which knowledge is captured in documentation.
  • The mechanisms by which documented knowledge decays and becomes unreliable.
  • The mechanisms by which knowledge is validated and renewed.
  • The responsibilities of documentation authors and reviewers.
  • The relationship between documented knowledge and tacit knowledge.

Background

Knowledge is not data. Data is a sequence of symbols. Information is data with context. Knowledge is information with understanding -- the ability to use information correctly, to know its limits, and to recognize when it applies and when it does not.

Documentation, at its best, transfers knowledge. At its worst, it transfers data and calls it knowledge. The difference matters enormously. A configuration file is data. A description of what the configuration does is information. An explanation of why the configuration was chosen, what alternatives were considered, what trade-offs were accepted, and under what circumstances the configuration should be changed -- that is knowledge.

Most documentation efforts fail not because they are incomplete but because they preserve the wrong layer. They record what was done without recording why. They record the current state without recording the reasoning that produced it. They record procedures without recording the mental model that makes the procedures comprehensible. When the circumstances change -- and on a fifty-year horizon, they will -- documentation that recorded only the what becomes useless, because the what was a consequence of a why that is no longer documented.

This institution's documentation philosophy is therefore organized around the preservation of why. Every document in the corpus must capture not just what exists or what is done, but why it exists, why it is done that way, what alternatives were considered, and what would need to change for the current approach to be reconsidered. This is more work than conventional documentation. It is also the only kind of documentation that survives.

System Model

The Knowledge Hierarchy

The institution recognizes four layers of knowledge, each building on the one below:

Layer 1: Procedural Knowledge. How to do things. Step-by-step procedures, commands, sequences. This is the most fragile layer because it is the most dependent on context. A procedure that works today may not work tomorrow if the software is updated, the hardware changes, or the environment shifts. Procedural knowledge must always be accompanied by Layer 2 knowledge to have any long-term value.

Layer 2: Structural Knowledge. How things are organized and connected. System architecture, data models, dependency maps, network topologies. This layer changes less frequently than procedures but is more complex. Structural knowledge allows a reader to understand where a procedure fits in the larger system and to adapt the procedure when the context changes.

Layer 3: Reasoning Knowledge. Why things are the way they are. Design decisions, trade-off analyses, rejected alternatives, risk assessments. This is the most valuable layer for long-term preservation because it survives context changes. Even when a procedure is obsolete and a structure has been redesigned, the reasoning behind the original decisions provides insight into the institution's values, constraints, and priorities. Reasoning knowledge is what allows future operators to make new decisions that are consistent with the institution's identity.

Layer 4: Meta-Knowledge. Knowledge about knowledge. What the institution knows, what it does not know, what it once knew and has forgotten, what it needs to learn. Meta-knowledge includes the documentation about documentation -- the frameworks, the templates, the review processes, and the maintenance plans that govern how knowledge is captured and maintained. This article is itself an example of meta-knowledge.

Knowledge Decay

All documented knowledge decays. The rate of decay depends on the layer:

  • Procedural knowledge decays fastest. A procedure may become inaccurate within weeks of being written if the underlying system changes. Procedures must be validated regularly -- ideally by execution, not just by reading.
  • Structural knowledge decays moderately. Architectures change less frequently than procedures but do change, especially during major system migrations. Structural documentation must be reviewed after any significant system change.
  • Reasoning knowledge decays slowly. The reasoning behind a decision remains valid as long as the constraints and values that informed it remain valid. Reasoning knowledge may become irrelevant (if the system it refers to no longer exists) but rarely becomes false. It is, however, vulnerable to context loss -- the reader may lack the background to understand the reasoning without additional explanation.
  • Meta-knowledge decays at variable rates. Frameworks and philosophies change slowly. Specific process documentation changes at the pace of process evolution.

The Tacit Knowledge Problem

Not all institutional knowledge is documented. Some knowledge exists only in the operator's mind -- habits, intuitions, pattern recognition, contextual awareness. This tacit knowledge is valuable but extremely fragile. It cannot survive succession. It cannot survive memory loss. It cannot even survive a long interruption in active operation.

The institution's documentation philosophy addresses tacit knowledge through two mechanisms: first, by systematically converting tacit knowledge into documented knowledge through the Commentary Sections and decision logs; second, by acknowledging that some knowledge will always remain tacit and designing operational procedures that do not depend on it. The goal is not to eliminate tacit knowledge -- that is impossible -- but to reduce the institution's dependence on it.

Rules and Constraints

  • R-CON-005-01: Every article in every domain must include reasoning knowledge -- not just what the system does but why it was designed that way, what alternatives were considered, and under what circumstances the design should be reconsidered.
  • R-CON-005-02: Procedural documentation must be validated through execution at least annually. Procedures that have not been validated within 18 months must be flagged as potentially unreliable.
  • R-CON-005-03: Structural documentation must be reviewed and verified after any system change that affects the documented structure. A "system change" is defined as any modification to hardware, software, network topology, or data organization.
  • R-CON-005-04: Commentary Sections in all articles are append-only. Previous entries may be annotated or corrected by subsequent entries but may not be deleted or retroactively edited. This preserves the temporal record of institutional understanding.
  • R-CON-005-05: When an operator discovers that they possess tacit knowledge that is not documented, they must either document it or create a documentation task to capture it. The task must be completed within 30 days.
  • R-CON-005-06: Documentation must be written for the reader who does not share the author's context. This means defining terms at point of use, explaining assumptions explicitly, and never relying on oral tradition or institutional folklore to fill gaps in the written record.
  • R-CON-005-07: The institution must maintain an honest accounting of what it does not know. Gaps in documentation must be acknowledged, not hidden. An unknown documented is safer than an unknown concealed.

Failure Modes

Documentation without understanding. Documents are written that contain words but do not contain knowledge. They describe what exists without explaining why. They record procedures without recording the mental model. A reader can follow the steps but cannot adapt when circumstances change. This is the most common documentation failure and the most insidious because it looks like success. Mitigation: the review process must evaluate whether articles contain reasoning knowledge, not just procedural and structural knowledge.

Knowledge rot. Documentation was accurate when written but has decayed without anyone noticing. Systems have changed, but the documentation has not been updated. Readers trust the documentation and make decisions based on information that is no longer true. Mitigation: the validation schedule defined in the maintenance plan. Procedures that have not been validated must be flagged, not trusted.

Tacit knowledge dependence. Critical institutional knowledge exists only in the operator's mind. The operator does not realize this because they can operate the system from memory. When succession occurs, or when the operator returns to a system after a long absence, the undocumented knowledge is lost. Mitigation: the tacit knowledge capture process defined in R-CON-005-05 and the succession procedures in CON-008.

Meta-knowledge collapse. The institution loses its understanding of its own documentation system. The frameworks, templates, and processes for capturing knowledge are no longer understood or followed. Documentation becomes ad hoc, inconsistent, and unreliable. Mitigation: this article and the Meta-Documentation domain provide a stable reference for the documentation philosophy and its implementation.

Volume paralysis. The documentation corpus grows so large that finding relevant knowledge becomes impractical. Knowledge exists in the system but is effectively inaccessible. Mitigation: the indexing, navigation, and search systems defined in Domain 16. The documentation system must be searchable and navigable, not just comprehensive.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If documentation is found to be inaccurate: Correct it immediately. Add a dated annotation explaining what was incorrect, when the inaccuracy was discovered, and what caused it. If the inaccuracy may have affected decisions, trace those decisions and assess whether they need to be revisited.
  2. If a knowledge gap is discovered: Document the gap itself -- what is missing, why it matters, and what is needed to fill it. Create a documentation task with a priority based on the gap's impact on institutional operation. Fill the gap within the timeframe appropriate to its priority.
  3. If tacit knowledge has been lost: Determine what was lost and what impact the loss has. If the knowledge can be reconstructed through experimentation, observation, or external research, do so and document the reconstruction. If it cannot be reconstructed, document the loss honestly and assess whether the systems that depended on that knowledge need to be redesigned.
  4. If the documentation system itself has degraded: Return to this article and the Meta-Documentation domain. Re-establish the frameworks, templates, and processes for knowledge capture. Conduct a documentation audit to assess the current state of the corpus. Prioritize restoration based on operational impact.

Evolution Path

Years 0-5: The knowledge preservation framework is being established. The emphasis is on capturing as much reasoning knowledge as possible while the founder's understanding is fresh. Commentary Sections accumulate rapidly. The documentation validation schedule is tested and refined.

Years 5-15: The corpus matures. The emphasis shifts from initial capture to ongoing maintenance. The validation process becomes routine. Knowledge decay is detected and addressed through regular review cycles. The balance between thoroughness and sustainability is calibrated.

Years 15-30: Succession approaches. The corpus is tested for transferability -- can a new operator understand the institution through its documentation alone? Gaps between documented knowledge and operational reality are identified and addressed. The tacit knowledge of the founding operator is systematically captured before succession.

Years 30-50+: The corpus has survived at least one succession. It has been tested against the ultimate challenge: the departure of the person who wrote most of it. If the knowledge preservation framework has worked, the new operator can understand, maintain, and extend the institution. If it has not worked, the failure modes will be visible in the gaps between what the documentation says and what the operator needs to know.
