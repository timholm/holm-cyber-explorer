D18-012 — Integrity Verification Methods

Document ID
D18-012
Domain
18 -- Import & Quarantine
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D18-001 (Import Philosophy), SEC-001 (Threat Model)
Depended Upon By
D18-003 (Dangerous Content Handling), D18-011 (Inter-Node Import), D18-006 (Quarantine Architecture), D18-013 (Import Logging)

Purpose

This article defines the methods by which the institution verifies the integrity of incoming content -- confirming that content has not been altered, corrupted, or tampered with between its origin and its admission to institutional systems. Integrity verification is one of the most fundamental validation checks in the import pipeline. Without it, the institution cannot distinguish between content that arrived as intended and content that was damaged or deliberately modified in transit.

In an air-gapped system where content travels on physical media through human carriers, integrity risks are significant. Media can degrade, be dropped, be exposed to magnetic fields, or be substituted. The gap between content creation and content import may span hours, days, or years. During that gap, the institution has no visibility into what happened to the content. Integrity verification at import time is the institution's only opportunity to detect these problems before they become part of the institutional record.

This article is addressed to import operators who perform integrity checks, to the infrastructure team that maintains the verification tools, and to future maintainers who must keep integrity verification current as cryptographic standards evolve over the institution's multi-decade lifespan.

Scope

This article covers:

  • Checksum and hash algorithms: which to use, when, and why.
  • Digital signature verification: when signatures are available and how to validate them.
  • Integrity verification at different pipeline stages: at reception, in quarantine, and at admission.
  • Integrity chain maintenance: ensuring that integrity is preserved from quarantine through admission to long-term storage.
  • Algorithm agility: planning for cryptographic algorithm obsolescence and migration.
  • Integrity verification for different media types: optical, magnetic, solid-state, and future media.

This article does not cover:

  • The broader cryptographic infrastructure of the institution (see Security Domain articles).
  • Content validation beyond bit-level integrity (format validity, content quality; see D18-002, D18-007).
  • The provenance chain, which tracks who handled the content (see D18-005; this article tracks whether the content was altered).
  • Long-term integrity of content already admitted to the institution (covered by storage and archival domains).

Background

What Integrity Means

Integrity, in this context, means that the content received is bit-for-bit identical to the content that was sent or created. A single flipped bit means integrity has been lost. This is a binary assessment: content has integrity or it does not. There is no "partial integrity." Even a small corruption may render a file unusable (a corrupted header makes the file unreadable) or subtly dangerous (a single-byte change in a text file alters its meaning without causing an obvious error).

The Three Integrity Questions

Integrity verification answers three questions:

  1. Was it corrupted? Did physical damage to the media alter the content? (Accidental integrity loss.)
  2. Was it modified? Did someone change the content between its origin and its arrival? (Intentional but possibly benign modification.)
  3. Was it substituted? Is this content actually what it claims to be, or was it replaced with different content? (Deliberate deception.)

Checksums answer question 1. Cryptographic hashes answer questions 1 and 2. Digital signatures answer all three. The institution's integrity verification should use the strongest method available for each import scenario.

The Algorithm Obsolescence Problem

Cryptographic algorithms have finite lifespans. MD5, once considered secure, is now trivially broken. SHA-1 is deprecated for security purposes. SHA-256 is currently considered strong, but over a 50-year horizon, it too may be weakened or broken. The institution must plan for algorithm transitions: storing content with today's best hashes while being prepared to re-hash the entire archive when a stronger algorithm is adopted.

Dual-Hash Strategy

For content with a projected retention period exceeding 20 years, the institution SHOULD compute and store hashes using two different algorithm families (e.g., SHA-256 and SHA3-256). If either algorithm is later found to be compromised, the other provides a fallback verification path. The cost of computing and storing a second hash is negligible compared to the cost of losing integrity verification capability for the entire archive.

Integrity Chain

The integrity chain tracks content integrity through every stage of the import process:

  1. Origin Hash: The hash provided by the source (if any). This is the reference against which transit integrity is verified.
  2. Reception Hash: The hash computed when content is first read from the incoming media. If an origin hash is available, compare immediately.
  3. Quarantine Hash: The hash computed after any quarantine processing (format conversion, metadata extraction). If the content was modified during quarantine processing, the hash will differ from the reception hash, and this difference must be documented.
  4. Admission Hash: The final hash computed at the moment of transfer from quarantine to production. This becomes the permanent reference hash stored in the institutional integrity register.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D18-I01: Every Imported File Must Be Hashed. A cryptographic hash MUST be computed for every file that enters the import pipeline. The hash MUST be computed at reception (when the file is first read from incoming media) and at admission (when the file is transferred to production). These two hashes are compared: if they differ and no authorized modification occurred in quarantine, the file is compromised.

  • R-D18-I02: SHA-256 Is the Minimum Standard. The primary hash algorithm MUST be SHA-256 or stronger. Weaker algorithms (MD5, SHA-1) MUST NOT be used as the sole integrity verification for any import. They may be computed for compatibility with external reference hashes but are supplementary, not primary.

  • R-D18-I03: Hash Results Are Permanently Recorded. All hash values computed during the import process MUST be recorded in the import log. The admission hash MUST be recorded in the institutional integrity register. These records are append-only and integrity-protected.

  • R-D18-I04: Algorithm Migration Plan Required. The institution MUST maintain a documented plan for migrating to a new hash algorithm if the current primary algorithm is compromised or deprecated. The plan MUST include: the criteria for triggering migration, the process for re-hashing admitted content, and the transition period during which both old and new hashes are maintained.

  • R-D18-I05: Integrity Failure Is a Hard Stop. If a file's reception hash does not match its provided origin hash (when available), or if a file's admission hash does not match its reception hash (absent authorized quarantine modifications), the file MUST NOT be admitted. Integrity failure is not a soft warning; it is a mandatory rejection. The operator MAY request re-submission from the source.

  • R-D18-I06: Signature Verification When Available. If incoming content includes digital signatures, the signatures MUST be verified if the institution possesses the necessary public keys. Signature verification failure does not automatically reject the content (the signature may be from an unknown key), but it MUST be recorded and factored into the trust assessment.

  • R-D18-I07: Quarantine Modifications Are Hash-Tracked. Any authorized modification of content during quarantine (format conversion, metadata stripping, sanitization) MUST produce a new hash. The modification, the reason, and both the pre-modification and post-modification hashes MUST be recorded. The chain from original content to admitted content must be fully traceable.

Failure Modes

  • Silent corruption. Content is corrupted in a way that does not produce read errors (the media is readable, but some bits have flipped). Without hash verification, the corruption is undetected. The institution admits corrupted content as if it were intact. Likelihood: Moderate, especially for older media types or media stored in adverse conditions. Severity: Variable -- from cosmetic damage to complete content loss. Detection: Hash verification at reception catches this if an origin hash is available. If no origin hash is available, silent corruption cannot be detected at import time; it can only be detected later if the content is compared against another copy.

  • Hash algorithm compromise. The primary hash algorithm is found to have collisions or other weaknesses. The institution's integrity register, built on that algorithm, can no longer be fully trusted. Likelihood: Low for SHA-256 in the near term; moderate over a 50-year horizon. Severity: High -- the integrity of the entire archive is in question. Detection: Monitoring cryptographic research (to the extent possible in an air-gapped system). Mitigation: Dual-hash strategy and algorithm migration plan.

  • Integrity verification tooling failure. The hash utility itself is compromised or buggy, producing incorrect hashes. Content that should fail verification passes, or vice versa. Likelihood: Very low for well-established tools. Non-zero over decades as tools are updated. Severity: Critical if the tool passes corrupted content. Detection: Periodic verification of the hash tool against known test vectors. Use of multiple independent hash tools for cross-verification on high-value content.

  • Origin hash unavailable. Content arrives without any pre-existing integrity data. The institution can hash it at reception but has nothing to compare against. Transit corruption or modification cannot be detected. Likelihood: High for many types of external content. Severity: Medium -- the institution can verify that content does not change after reception, but cannot verify that it arrived intact. Detection: Not directly detectable. Mitigation: Record the absence of origin hash in the provenance record. Factor into trust scoring (carrier trust is reduced when integrity protection is absent).

  • Re-hashing burden during algorithm migration. When the institution transitions to a new hash algorithm, every file in the institutional archive must be re-hashed. For large archives, this is a significant computational undertaking. If the migration is triggered by an urgent algorithm compromise, the institution faces a crisis: the old hashes are untrustworthy, and the new hashes have not yet been computed. Likelihood: Low probability but high impact. Severity: High during the transition window. Mitigation: Dual-hash strategy reduces urgency; begin transition before the old algorithm is critically compromised.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If content with integrity failure has been admitted: Identify the content. Remove it from the production catalog (quarantine in place). Attempt to obtain an uncorrupted copy from the original source. If no uncorrupted copy is available, assess the damage: is the corruption localized (a few bytes) or pervasive? Can the content still be used with a corruption annotation? If the content is irreparably damaged, reject it from the institutional record and document the loss.

  2. If the primary hash algorithm is compromised: Activate the algorithm migration plan. Begin computing new hashes for all institutional content using the replacement algorithm. During the transition, maintain both old and new hashes. Old hashes are marked as "deprecated -- algorithm compromised" but not deleted (they may still be useful for identifying content). Prioritize re-hashing of high-value and recently-imported content. Document the transition in the institutional memory (Domain 20).

  3. If the hash utility is found to be producing incorrect results: Stop all import operations. Verify the hash utility against known test vectors. If the tool is confirmed faulty, identify all content processed by the faulty tool. Re-hash using a verified tool. Compare results. If discrepancies are found, investigate each discrepancy individually (was the original hash wrong, or is the content actually corrupted?). Replace the faulty tool. Document the incident.

  4. If a large algorithm migration is needed: Plan the migration as a project with defined phases, timeline, and resource allocation. Phase 1: Compute new hashes for all content admitted in the last 12 months (most likely to be referenced and verified). Phase 2: Compute new hashes for all remaining content in priority order (most-accessed first). Phase 3: Verify that all institutional integrity register entries have new-algorithm hashes. Phase 4: Deprecate old-algorithm hashes. Maintain old hashes indefinitely for historical reference.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: SHA-256 is the primary algorithm. The integrity register is established. Operational patterns are developed: how long does it take to hash a typical import? What percentage of imports arrive with origin hashes? The dual-hash strategy is implemented for all content with expected retention exceeding 20 years.

  • Years 5-15: The integrity register grows. The institution begins to see the value of its integrity chain: re-verification of admitted content catches storage-level degradation. The algorithm migration plan is tested with a tabletop exercise: "If SHA-256 were compromised tomorrow, what would we do?" The exercise reveals gaps in the plan, which are addressed.

  • Years 15-30: A hash algorithm migration may become necessary. If the institution has maintained its dual-hash strategy, the migration is orderly. If it has not, the migration is urgent and expensive. This period is the most likely window for the first real algorithm transition, and the institution's preparedness determines whether it is a manageable project or a crisis.

  • Years 30-50+: The institution may have undergone multiple algorithm migrations. The integrity register contains hashes in multiple algorithms spanning decades. The institution's ability to verify the integrity of its oldest content depends on having maintained a continuous chain of algorithm transitions, each bridged by a period of dual-hashing. The integrity chain -- from original import to current verification -- is one of the institution's most valuable assets.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The decision to make integrity failure a hard stop (R-D18-I05) rather than a warning was deliberate. In many systems, integrity failures produce warnings that operators learn to dismiss. "Checksum mismatch" becomes noise rather than signal. By making it a hard stop -- the file is not admitted, period -- we ensure that integrity failures cannot be normalized. The operator must either obtain an uncorrupted copy or explicitly document why the content should be admitted despite the integrity failure (which requires escalation, not a click-through dialog).

The dual-hash strategy adds approximately 50% to hash computation time (computing two hashes instead of one). This is a real cost. For batch imports of thousands of files, it may add hours to processing time. The alternative -- depending on a single algorithm that may be compromised during the institution's lifetime -- is worse. The computation cost is paid once; the integrity assurance lasts decades.

Open question: should the institution invest in hardware-accelerated hashing? Current commodity hardware can hash a gigabyte per second with SHA-256. For most imports, this is adequate. For major acquisitions (terabytes of content), hashing time becomes significant. Hardware acceleration is available but adds cost and complexity. This decision should be revisited when the first major acquisition stresses the current capability.
