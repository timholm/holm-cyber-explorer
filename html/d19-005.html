D19-005 — Quality Standards Taxonomy

Document ID
D19-005
Domain
19 -- Quality Assurance
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D19-001 (Quality Philosophy)
Depended Upon By
D19-006, D19-007, D19-008, D19-009, D19-010, D19-011, D19-013, D19-014, D19-015. Referenced by all domains that define quality standards for their outputs.

Purpose

This article establishes the taxonomy of quality standards used across the entire institution. It defines the categories by which quality is organized, the levels at which standards operate, and the lifecycle through which standards are adopted, maintained, and retired. Without a taxonomy, the institution's quality standards become a disorganized accumulation of ad hoc requirements -- some overlapping, some contradictory, many unmaintained.

A taxonomy does not define individual standards. It defines the structure into which individual standards are placed. It answers: What kinds of quality exist? How are standards classified so they can be found, applied, and reviewed? What distinguishes a hard requirement from a soft guideline? How does a new standard get adopted, and how does an obsolete one get retired?

This article is addressed to everyone who creates, applies, or evaluates quality standards. It is addressed with particular emphasis to domain leads, who are responsible for proposing standards specific to their domain's outputs, and to the QA Architect, who is responsible for maintaining coherence across all standards.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The categorical structure of quality standards across all institutional outputs.
  • The classification of standards by type (hard rule, soft guideline, aspiration).
  • The classification of standards by scope (universal, domain-specific, artifact-specific).
  • The lifecycle of a standard from proposal through adoption, review, revision, and retirement.
  • The naming and identification system for standards.
  • The relationship between this taxonomy and the five quality dimensions defined in D19-001.

This article does not cover:

  • The content of any individual quality standard (each standard is defined in its relevant domain or in a subsequent D19 article).
  • Specific metrics for documentation quality (see D19-006).
  • Testing procedures for verifying compliance with standards (see D19-003).
  • Audit procedures for assessing institutional compliance (see D19-002).

Background

3.1 Why a Taxonomy Is Necessary

An institution that operates over decades will accumulate hundreds or thousands of quality requirements. Without a taxonomy, these requirements become an impenetrable thicket. New participants cannot find the standards that apply to their work. Overlapping standards create confusion about which takes precedence. Obsolete standards remain in force because no one knows they exist, let alone that they are outdated. The institution eventually has two quality regimes: the documented one and the one people actually follow, and the gap between them grows wider each year.

A taxonomy prevents this by imposing structure. It forces every standard to be classified, to declare its scope, to identify its authority, and to have a defined lifecycle. This does not eliminate the complexity of quality management. But it makes the complexity navigable.

3.2 Prior Art and Alternatives Considered

Several approaches to organizing quality standards were considered. ISO 9001 provides a process-oriented quality framework, but its assumptions about organizational structure and external auditing make it inappropriate for a small, air-gapped institution. The Capability Maturity Model (CMM/CMMI) provides maturity levels, but its focus on organizational process capability is oriented toward large software teams. Internal custom taxonomies risk insularity. The approach adopted here draws from these frameworks selectively: it uses ISO's emphasis on documented procedures, CMM's concept of progressive maturity, and a custom categorical structure fitted to the institution's specific nature as a documentation-first, air-gapped, multi-generational operation.

3.3 Design Decisions

Two key decisions shaped this taxonomy. First, standards are classified by what they measure (the quality dimension they address) and by how mandatory they are (their enforcement level). This two-axis classification allows a single standard to be both located conceptually and applied operationally. Second, standards have an explicit lifecycle. A standard that has no mechanism for retirement will never die, even after it becomes harmful. The lifecycle ensures that every standard has a defined path to obsolescence.

System Model

4.1 Quality Dimensions (from D19-001)

All standards map to one or more of the five quality dimensions established in D19-001:

  • Correctness -- factual accuracy, procedural validity, data integrity.
  • Completeness -- coverage of required scope, no unaddressed gaps.
  • Clarity -- comprehensibility, navigability, appropriate language level.
  • Currency -- up-to-dateness, alignment with current institutional reality.
  • Durability -- resistance to time, context loss, format obsolescence.

A standard may address multiple dimensions. For example, a standard requiring that every article include a "Last Reviewed" date addresses both Currency (it enables staleness detection) and Completeness (it requires a specific metadata field).

4.2 Standard Types by Enforcement Level

Every standard is classified into exactly one of the following enforcement levels:

  • MUST (Hard Rule). Compliance is mandatory. Failure to comply blocks publication or progression through a review gate. Hard rules are reserved for requirements where non-compliance creates genuine risk to institutional integrity, safety, or mission. Every hard rule must cite the specific harm it prevents.
  • SHOULD (Soft Guideline). Compliance is expected under normal circumstances. Deviation is permitted with documented justification. Soft guidelines represent best practices that admit of reasonable exceptions. The justification for deviation is recorded but does not require escalation.
  • MAY (Aspiration). Compliance is encouraged but not expected. Aspirations represent the institution's vision of ideal quality. They guide improvement efforts but do not constrain current work. An aspiration that consistently proves achievable should be promoted to a SHOULD. An aspiration that consistently proves impractical should be retired.

4.3 Standard Scopes

Every standard is classified by its scope of applicability:

  • Universal. Applies to all institutional outputs across all domains. Universal standards are few and fundamental. They are adopted by governance-level authority and require governance-level authority to change.
  • Domain-Specific. Applies to all outputs within a single domain. Domain-specific standards are proposed by the domain lead, reviewed by QA, and adopted by the domain lead with QA concurrence.
  • Artifact-Specific. Applies to a specific type of output within a domain (for example, standards for import validation reports, or standards for interface component specifications). Artifact-specific standards are the most numerous and the most likely to change.

4.4 Standard Identification

Each standard receives a unique identifier following this pattern:

QS-[SCOPE]-[DIMENSION]-[SEQUENCE]

Where:
  SCOPE     = U (Universal), D##  (Domain-Specific), A (Artifact-Specific)
  DIMENSION = COR (Correctness), COM (Completeness), CLA (Clarity),
              CUR (Currency), DUR (Durability), MUL (Multi-dimensional)
  SEQUENCE  = Three-digit number, assigned sequentially within category

Examples:
  QS-U-CLA-001    = Universal clarity standard #1
  QS-D19-COR-003  = Domain 19 correctness standard #3
  QS-A-DUR-012    = Artifact-specific durability standard #12

4.5 Standard Lifecycle

Every standard moves through a defined lifecycle:

  1. Proposed. The standard is drafted and submitted for review. The proposal must include: the standard text, the quality dimension(s) addressed, the enforcement level, the scope, the specific harm prevented or benefit provided, and the proposed review schedule.
  2. Under Review. The standard is evaluated by QA and by representatives of the domains or outputs it will affect. Review assesses testability, proportionality, and feasibility.
  3. Adopted. The standard is accepted and enters force. It is added to the Standards Registry. All affected parties are notified.
  4. Active. The standard is in force and subject to periodic review per its defined schedule.
  5. Under Revision. The standard is being updated. The existing version remains in force until the revision is adopted.
  6. Deprecated. The standard is marked for retirement. It remains in force for a transition period during which affected works may be updated. New work must not rely on a deprecated standard.
  7. Retired. The standard is no longer in force. It remains in the Standards Registry for historical reference, marked with its retirement date and the rationale for retirement. Retired standards are never deleted.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D19-05-01: Every Standard Must Be Classified. No quality standard may be adopted without being assigned a quality dimension, an enforcement level, and a scope. Unclassified requirements are not enforceable.

  • R-D19-05-02: Hard Rules Must Cite Harm. Every standard classified as MUST must include an explicit statement of the specific harm that non-compliance would cause. "Because it is best practice" is not a valid harm statement. "Because unreviewed changes to root documents risk contradicting the founding mandate" is valid.

  • R-D19-05-03: Standards Must Be Testable. As established in D19-001, R-D19-02, every standard must be expressed in terms that allow objective assessment. If a standard cannot be tested, it cannot be enforced and should be classified as MAY (Aspiration) until a testable formulation is found.

  • R-D19-05-04: Review Frequency Must Be Defined. Every adopted standard must have a defined review frequency. No standard may go more than 24 months without review. Standards that have not been reviewed within their defined period are flagged for immediate review.

  • R-D19-05-05: Retirement Requires Rationale. A standard may not be retired without a documented rationale. The rationale must explain why the standard is no longer needed, what (if anything) replaces it, and what happens to works that were created under the retired standard.

  • R-D19-05-06: Universal Standards Require Governance Authority. Universal standards (scope U) may only be adopted or retired by governance-level authority as defined in GOV-001. Domain-specific and artifact-specific standards may be adopted by the relevant domain lead with QA concurrence.

  • R-D19-05-07: No Retroactive Enforcement. As established in D19-001, R-D19-06, new standards apply to new work and to revisions of existing work. Existing work is evaluated against the standards that were active at the time of its creation or most recent revision.

Failure Modes

  • Taxonomy bloat. Too many categories, too many enforcement levels, too many subcategories. The taxonomy becomes as complex as the standards it organizes. People stop using it. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: high. Detection: measure the time required for a new participant to locate the standard relevant to their work. If it exceeds 15 minutes, the taxonomy is too complex.

  • Classification gaming. Standards are strategically classified to be easier or harder to enforce. A standard that should be MUST is classified as SHOULD to avoid the overhead of enforcement. A standard that should be SHOULD is classified as MUST to give QA more authority. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: moderate. Detection: periodic audit comparing classification to actual enforcement behavior.

  • Orphaned standards. Standards that are adopted but never reviewed, never enforced, and never retired. They clutter the registry and create confusion. Likelihood: high over long timeframes. Severity: moderate. Detection: the Standards Registry must track last-review-date and flag overdue standards.

  • Scope creep in universal standards. Requirements that should be domain-specific are adopted as universal, imposing irrelevant burdens on unrelated domains. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: high. Detection: when a domain consistently seeks exemptions from a universal standard, the standard's scope may be wrong.

  • Taxonomy rigidity. The taxonomy itself becomes a constraint that prevents needed standards from being expressed. Real quality needs do not fit the predefined categories. Likelihood: low initially, increasing over decades. Severity: high. Detection: when proposed standards consistently require category workarounds, the taxonomy needs revision.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If the taxonomy has become bloated: Conduct a simplification review. Count the total number of categories and subcategories. Interview users about which categories they actually use. Collapse unused or rarely-used categories. The goal is the minimum number of categories that preserves meaningful distinctions. Do not add categories speculatively; add them when a real standard does not fit existing categories.

  2. If classification gaming is detected: Audit a sample of standards. For each, independently assess what the enforcement level should be based on the harm prevented. Compare to actual classification. Where discrepancies exist, reclassify the standard. If the pattern is systematic, investigate whether the enforcement overhead for MUST standards is disproportionate, creating an incentive to avoid the MUST classification. Fix the overhead problem rather than tolerating the gaming.

  3. If orphaned standards accumulate: Run a registry audit. Flag all standards not reviewed within their defined period. For each, determine whether the standard is still needed. If yes, review and re-adopt. If no, retire with documented rationale. Implement automated flagging so orphaned standards are detected before they accumulate.

  4. If universal scope is being abused: Review all universal standards. For each, determine whether it genuinely applies to all domains. If not, reclassify to domain-specific. Establish a higher bar for future universal standard proposals, requiring evidence that the standard is needed across at least three domains.

  5. If the taxonomy is too rigid: Convene a taxonomy revision. Identify the specific ways in which real standards do not fit the current structure. Propose structural changes. Ensure that the revision preserves backward compatibility: existing standard identifiers remain valid, even if the taxonomy structure around them changes.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: The taxonomy starts simple. The five quality dimensions and three enforcement levels are sufficient for the initial body of standards. Resist the urge to add subcategories before operational experience reveals whether they are needed. The Standards Registry will be small and manageable.

  • Years 3-10: As the institution's standards body grows, the taxonomy may need refinement. New artifact types may emerge that require new artifact-specific scopes. The identification scheme may need extension. This is normal and expected. Revise the taxonomy when the pressure to do so becomes clear, not before.

  • Years 10-25: The taxonomy will have been tested by succession events. Ensure that incoming participants can understand and use the taxonomy without oral instruction. If the taxonomy requires a human guide to navigate, it is too complex or its documentation is insufficient.

  • Years 25-50+: The taxonomy itself may need fundamental redesign if the institution's nature has changed significantly. The five quality dimensions from D19-001 may need revision. The enforcement levels may need adjustment. The identification scheme may have exhausted its namespace. Plan for this by ensuring the taxonomy is documented as a versioned artifact with a clear migration path from each version to the next.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The temptation with a taxonomy is to make it comprehensive from the start. Every possible category, every edge case, every special scope. Resist this. A taxonomy that covers everything explains nothing. Start with the coarsest useful distinctions and refine only when operational experience proves the coarse categories insufficient. The three enforcement levels (MUST, SHOULD, MAY) were chosen because they map to RFC 2119 language that is widely understood and because the institution can meaningfully distinguish between them. Adding a fourth level (such as "MUST UNLESS" or "SHOULD STRONGLY") would create ambiguity where none needs to exist. If the three levels prove insufficient, a future revision should add levels, but only with documented justification.
