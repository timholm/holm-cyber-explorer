D19-006 — Documentation Quality Metrics

Document ID
D19-006
Domain
19 -- Quality Assurance
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D19-001 (Quality Philosophy), D19-005 (Quality Standards Taxonomy)
Depended Upon By
D19-013 (Quality Degradation Detection). Referenced by all domains whose primary output is documentation.

Purpose

This article defines measurable criteria for evaluating the quality of documentation within the institution. Documentation is the institution's primary output and its primary mechanism for surviving across generations. If documentation quality cannot be measured, it cannot be managed, it cannot be compared over time, and its degradation cannot be detected until the damage is severe.

Quality metrics for documentation must balance rigor with practicality. An overly precise metric that requires hours to assess a single article is useless in practice. An overly vague metric that anyone can pass without effort is useless in principle. The metrics defined here are designed to be assessable within a reasonable time by a competent reviewer, to be consistent across reviewers, and to be meaningful as trend indicators when tracked over time.

This article is addressed to every person who writes, reviews, or maintains documentation within the institution. It is particularly important for reviewers, who need concrete criteria against which to evaluate the work of others, and for the QA function, which must track these metrics across the institution's entire documentation corpus.

Scope

This article covers:

  • Measurable criteria for each of the five quality dimensions (Correctness, Completeness, Clarity, Currency, Durability) as applied to documentation specifically.
  • Scoring methodology for each dimension.
  • Composite scoring and minimum threshold definitions.
  • Assessment procedures -- how to apply the metrics during review.
  • Trend tracking -- how metrics are recorded and compared over time.

This article does not cover:

  • Quality metrics for non-documentation outputs (interface quality, infrastructure quality, process quality). Those are defined within their respective domains.
  • The review process itself (see D19-002 for audit procedures).
  • The testing framework for automated or semi-automated quality checks (see D19-003).
  • The taxonomy within which these metrics are classified (see D19-005).

Background

3.1 The Problem of Measuring Documentation Quality

Documentation quality is notoriously difficult to measure. Unlike software, documentation has no compiler that rejects syntactically invalid input. Unlike physical artifacts, documentation has no material failure mode. A document can be utterly useless and still exist as a valid file on disk. The metrics defined here attempt to operationalize quality by breaking it into observable, assessable components.

The approach is deliberate in its imprecision. Documentation quality metrics cannot achieve the exactness of engineering tolerances. They can achieve sufficient consistency to detect trends, to compare articles, and to identify the specific dimension in which a given article falls short. That is enough to be useful.

3.2 Why Five Dimensions

The five dimensions (Correctness, Completeness, Clarity, Currency, Durability) were established in D19-001 after considering both simpler and more complex models. A single-axis model ("good vs. bad") provides no diagnostic information -- it tells you an article is poor quality but not why. A ten-dimension model provides too many axes for practical assessment. Five dimensions were found to be the minimum necessary to distinguish meaningfully between different kinds of quality failure while remaining assessable within a reasonable time.

3.3 The Self-Referential Test

Per D19-001 R-D19-03, this article must pass its own metrics. This is not merely a philosophical requirement. If this article is unclear about how to assess clarity, or incomplete in its coverage of completeness metrics, its own deficiency demonstrates the unreliability of its standards. This article has been written with that constraint in mind, and reviewers should hold it to its own criteria.

System Model

4.1 Metric Structure

Each quality dimension is assessed through a set of specific indicators. Each indicator is scored on a three-point scale:

  • Pass (2). The indicator is fully satisfied. No material deficiency.
  • Partial (1). The indicator is partially satisfied. Some deficiency exists but does not render the article unusable for its stated purpose.
  • Fail (0). The indicator is not satisfied. Material deficiency that impairs the article's usefulness.

4.2 Correctness Indicators

IndicatorAssessment Question
Factual accuracyAre all factual claims verifiable against authoritative sources or institutional records?
Procedural validityIf the article describes a procedure, does following the procedure produce the claimed result?
Internal consistencyDoes the article contradict itself anywhere?
Cross-reference accuracyDo all references to other articles cite correct document IDs and are quotations or paraphrases accurate?

4.3 Completeness Indicators

IndicatorAssessment Question
Template complianceAre all required sections present per the article template?
Scope coverageDoes the article cover everything declared in its Scope section?
Edge case treatmentAre boundary conditions, exceptions, and edge cases addressed?
Failure mode coverageAre failure modes identified with likelihood, severity, and detection methods?
Metadata completenessAre all metadata fields (ID, version, date, dependencies) populated and current?

4.4 Clarity Indicators

IndicatorAssessment Question
Audience appropriatenessIs the language accessible to the stated audience without unnecessary jargon?
Structural navigabilityCan a reader find a specific piece of information without reading the entire article?
Definition of termsAre specialized terms defined on first use or referenced in a glossary?
Logical flowDoes the article progress logically from context to rules to procedures?

4.5 Currency Indicators

IndicatorAssessment Question
Last review dateHas the article been reviewed within its defined review period?
Factual currentnessDo described systems, processes, and configurations match current reality?
Reference currentnessDo referenced documents still exist, and are the cited versions still current?

4.6 Durability Indicators

IndicatorAssessment Question
Context embeddingDoes the article explain the "why" behind decisions, not just the "what"?
Assumption explicitnessAre assumptions that might become invalid over time stated explicitly?
Format resilienceIs the article in a format that will remain readable without proprietary tools?
Successor comprehensibilityCould a competent reader with no prior context understand this article?

4.7 Composite Scoring

Each dimension receives a dimension score: the sum of its indicator scores divided by the maximum possible score for that dimension, expressed as a percentage. The composite score is the unweighted average of all five dimension scores. Minimum thresholds:

  • Publication threshold: No dimension below 50%. Composite score at least 70%.
  • Quality target: No dimension below 75%. Composite score at least 85%.
  • Exemplary: All dimensions at 90% or above.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D19-06-01: Every Article Must Be Scored. Every article that passes through a review gate must receive a documented quality score using the metrics defined in this article. Scores are recorded and retained for trend analysis.

  • R-D19-06-02: Scores Must Be Justified. A reviewer must provide a brief justification for any Partial (1) or Fail (0) score. The justification identifies the specific deficiency so the author can address it.

  • R-D19-06-03: Minimum Thresholds Are Hard Rules. An article that does not meet the publication threshold MUST NOT be published until the deficiencies are corrected. An article may be published below the quality target, but the shortfall must be recorded as a known deficiency to be addressed in the next revision.

  • R-D19-06-04: Metrics Apply Forward. Consistent with D19-001 R-D19-06, existing articles are not retroactively scored against new metrics unless they are undergoing revision. When an existing article is revised, it is scored against the metrics current at the time of revision.

  • R-D19-06-05: Reviewer Calibration. At least once per year, multiple reviewers must independently score the same article and compare results. If scores diverge by more than 20 percentage points on any dimension, the scoring criteria for that dimension must be clarified or the reviewers must be recalibrated.

Failure Modes

  • Metric gaming. Authors optimize articles for high metric scores without improving actual quality. Sections are present but empty of substance. Cross-references are correct but irrelevant. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: high. Detection: metrics must be assessed by a human reviewer evaluating substance, not just form. Automated checks alone are insufficient.

  • Reviewer inconsistency. Different reviewers assign significantly different scores to the same article. Metrics lose credibility. Likelihood: high without calibration. Severity: moderate. Detection: the calibration exercise required by R-D19-06-05.

  • Metric obsolescence. The indicators no longer measure what matters as the institution evolves. Likelihood: certain over long timeframes. Severity: moderate if detected and addressed. Detection: periodic review of whether the indicators still correspond to actual quality failures experienced in practice.

  • Assessment burden. Scoring every article on every indicator becomes so time-consuming that reviews are delayed or skipped. Likelihood: moderate for large document corpora. Severity: high. Detection: track time spent on quality assessment versus time spent on production. If the ratio exceeds 1:3, the assessment process needs streamlining.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If metric gaming is detected: Review the articles in question for substantive quality, ignoring metric scores. Identify the specific indicators being gamed. Revise those indicators to require substantive assessment. Consider whether the scoring rubric provides perverse incentives. Address the incentive rather than blaming the author.

  2. If reviewer inconsistency is persistent: Conduct a calibration workshop using three or four articles of varying quality. Have all active reviewers score independently, then compare and discuss. Identify which indicators produce the most divergence. Rewrite those indicators to be more specific. Create scoring examples for each level (Pass, Partial, Fail) for each indicator.

  3. If metrics have become obsolete: Convene a metrics revision. Identify which indicators no longer correspond to experienced quality failures. Replace them with indicators that do. Preserve the five-dimension structure unless a fundamental change in D19-001 warrants restructuring. Version the metric set so that historical scores remain comparable within their metric version.

  4. If assessment burden is excessive: Introduce tiered assessment. Low-risk articles (commentary updates, minor revisions) receive a streamlined assessment covering only the dimensions affected by the change. Full assessment is reserved for new articles, major revisions, and high-risk documents. This reduces burden while maintaining rigor where it matters most.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: Establish baseline scores across the initial document corpus. Expect low scores initially; this is diagnostic, not punitive. Use baseline data to identify the most common quality deficiencies and prioritize improvements.

  • Years 3-10: Accumulate trend data. The most valuable use of metrics is not the absolute score of any single article but the trajectory of scores over time. Are articles getting better or worse? Are certain dimensions consistently weak across all domains? Trend data informs targeted improvement efforts.

  • Years 10-25: Consider whether the indicator set needs revision based on accumulated experience. Some indicators may prove consistently easy to pass (and therefore uninformative). Others may prove consistently problematic (suggesting the standard is too high or the indicator is poorly calibrated). Revise accordingly.

  • Years 25-50+: The metrics should be on their second or third major revision by this point. The five-dimension framework from D19-001 may itself have evolved. Ensure that metric revisions maintain comparability with historical data where possible, and document breaks in comparability where they occur.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The three-point scoring scale (Pass/Partial/Fail) was chosen deliberately over a five-point or ten-point scale. Finer-grained scales create an illusion of precision that documentation quality assessment cannot support. The difference between a 6 and a 7 on a ten-point scale is not reliably distinguishable by human reviewers. The difference between Pass and Fail is. The Partial score exists to acknowledge that quality is not always binary, but it should not be the default -- most indicators should be clearly Pass or Fail. If a reviewer finds themselves assigning Partial scores to more than a third of indicators, either the article has widespread moderate deficiencies or the reviewer is avoiding the discomfort of assigning Fail scores. The latter is more common and more dangerous.
