D7-008 — Intelligence Product Standards

Document ID
D7-008
Domain
7 -- Intelligence & Analysis
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D7-001, D7-002, D7-004, D7-011, D6-001, D6-003, ETH-001, GOV-001, OPS-001
Depended Upon By
D7-006, D7-007, D7-010, D7-012, D10-002, D10-003. All articles that produce or consume intelligence products.

Purpose

This article defines the formats, templates, quality standards, and classification system for all intelligence products produced within the holm.chat Documentation Institution. An intelligence product is any documented output of the intelligence cycle -- the artifact that converts raw information into actionable understanding. Without standardized products, intelligence work produces ad hoc notes, informal observations, and undocumented conclusions that cannot be reviewed, cannot be referenced by future analysts, and cannot be audited when decisions go wrong.

The intelligence cycle defined in D7-002 generates information that must be packaged into forms that are useful to the decision-maker (who may be the same person as the analyst, but whose role is distinct per D7-001 R-D7-04). The analytical frameworks in D7-004 produce structured conclusions that must be recorded in a way that preserves the reasoning, the evidence, the assumptions, and the confidence level. The estimative language defined in D7-011 must be applied consistently. This article provides the container for all of this: the standard product types, their required elements, their quality criteria, and the archival requirements that ensure products remain available for future reference, calibration, and institutional learning.

A well-designed intelligence product serves three audiences across three time horizons. It serves the current decision-maker immediately, providing the understanding they need to act. It serves the calibration function within months to years, enabling the institution to assess whether past analyses were accurate. And it serves future analysts across decades, providing precedent, methodology, and institutional memory that inform future intelligence work. The standards in this article are designed to serve all three audiences.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The product taxonomy: the types of intelligence products the institution produces and when each is appropriate.
  • Required elements for every intelligence product, regardless of type.
  • Product-specific templates and format standards.
  • Quality criteria: what makes a product good enough to publish and use for decision-making.
  • Product classification: sensitivity levels and access considerations.
  • Archival requirements: how products are stored, indexed, and retrieved.
  • The product review and feedback cycle.

This article does not cover:

  • The intelligence cycle that generates the content of products (see D7-002).
  • The analytical methods used in producing assessments (see D7-004).
  • The estimative language used to express confidence (see D7-011, though it is referenced extensively).
  • The decision-support interface that determines how products reach decision-makers (see D7-010).
  • The data storage infrastructure for archiving products (see D6-001, D6-003).

Background

3.1 Why Standards Matter

Intelligence analysis without standardized output is indistinguishable from thinking out loud. The analyst processes information, reaches a conclusion, and acts on it -- but no artifact exists to record the reasoning, challenge the assumptions, or learn from the outcome. In a single-analyst institution, this is especially dangerous because there is no second pair of eyes to catch errors in real time. The product itself must serve as the external check: by requiring the analyst to write down their reasoning in a structured format, the product forces clarity that internal deliberation alone does not.

Product standards also enable institutional memory. An analyst's informal notes are intelligible to the analyst who wrote them, perhaps for a few months. A standardized product with explicit structure, dating, and indexing remains intelligible to a successor decades later. Given the institution's 50-year design horizon, this is not an academic concern. The intelligence archive will be one of the institution's most valuable assets, but only if its contents are findable, readable, and interpretable by people who were not present when they were written.

3.2 Product Types in Context

Intelligence organizations of all sizes produce a range of products, from brief alerts to comprehensive assessments. The institution's small scale does not eliminate the need for variety. Different situations call for different product types: an imminent threat demands a brief, action-oriented flash; a strategic question demands a thorough assessment; a routine monitoring observation demands a concise update. Forcing all intelligence into a single format either buries urgent findings in lengthy analysis or strips strategic thinking of necessary depth.

3.3 The Tension Between Thoroughness and Utility

A product that is comprehensive but unread serves no one. A product that is brief but incomplete may misinform. The product standards in this article attempt to balance thoroughness with utility by defining different product types for different purposes, each with appropriate depth requirements. The universal requirement across all products is that they must state their confidence level and key assumptions -- the minimum information a decision-maker needs to assess whether the analysis is trustworthy.

System Model

4.1 Product Taxonomy

The institution produces six types of intelligence products, ordered from briefest to most comprehensive:

Type 1: Intelligence Flash (IF). Purpose: immediate notification of a time-sensitive development. Length: one page or less. Trigger: an event or observation that requires immediate awareness and may require rapid decision-making. Required elements: what happened, what it means, what the analyst recommends, confidence level. The Flash is not a full assessment. It is an alert with preliminary analysis. Full assessment follows if warranted. Product ID format: IF-[YYYY]-[NNN].

Type 2: Intelligence Update (IU). Purpose: routine reporting on monitored indicators and standing requirements. Length: one to three pages. Trigger: scheduled reporting cycle (weekly, monthly, or as defined in D7-009). Required elements: status of monitored indicators, changes since last update, any new information relevant to standing requirements, areas of note. The Update is the workhorse product -- most intelligence output will be in this format. Product ID format: IU-[YYYY]-[NNN].

Type 3: Intelligence Assessment (IA). Purpose: a structured analytical conclusion on a specific question or topic. Length: three to ten pages. Trigger: a standing or ad hoc intelligence requirement that demands focused analysis. Required elements: full analytical narrative using structured techniques per D7-004, evidence inventory with source evaluation, confidence level using the epistemic hierarchy per D7-001, key assumptions, alternative analyses considered, devil's advocate where required. The Assessment is the institution's primary analytical product. Product ID format: IA-[YYYY]-[NNN].

Type 4: Threat Assessment (TA). Purpose: formal threat evaluation per D7-006. Length: three to eight pages per threat, plus the threat register summary. Trigger: annual threat review or ad hoc threat identification. Required elements: all five components of the threat assessment framework per D7-006 Section 4.1. Product ID format: TA-[YYYY]-[NNN].

Type 5: Opportunity Assessment (OA). Purpose: formal opportunity evaluation per D7-007. Length: two to six pages per opportunity, plus the opportunity register summary. Trigger: annual opportunity review or ad hoc opportunity identification. Required elements: all five components of the opportunity evaluation framework per D7-007 Section 4.2. Product ID format: OA-[YYYY]-[NNN].

Type 6: Strategic Estimate (SE). Purpose: a comprehensive, long-range analytical product that synthesizes multiple threads of analysis into a strategic picture. Length: ten to thirty pages. Trigger: annual cycle, major environmental change, or strategic decision requiring broad context. Required elements: full synthesis across threat, opportunity, and environmental domains; scenario analysis; long-range trend assessment; strategic recommendations; comprehensive assumption list; extensive devil's advocate analysis. The Strategic Estimate is the institution's most ambitious product. It should be produced at least annually. Product ID format: SE-[YYYY]-[NNN].

4.2 Universal Required Elements

Every intelligence product, regardless of type, must include the following elements:

  • Product ID: Unique identifier in the format specified for each product type.
  • Date: Date of production.
  • Analyst: Identifier of the analyst who produced the product.
  • Classification: Sensitivity level per Section 4.4.
  • Subject: Clear, concise statement of what the product addresses.
  • Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF): The key finding or conclusion, stated in one to three sentences at the beginning of the product. The decision-maker who reads nothing else must understand the core message from the BLUF alone.
  • Confidence Level: Using the epistemic hierarchy from D7-001 Section 4.1 and the estimative language from D7-011. Every claim in the product must be tagged with an appropriate confidence indicator.
  • Key Assumptions: Per D7-001 R-D7-03. What must remain true for the analysis to hold?
  • Evidence Date: The date of the most recent evidence incorporated. This allows readers to assess staleness.
  • Review Date: When this product should be revisited.

4.3 Quality Criteria

An intelligence product is considered ready for publication when it meets all of the following criteria:

  1. Completeness: All required elements for the product type are present. No section is marked "TBD" or left blank.
  2. Analytical Soundness: The conclusions follow logically from the evidence. The analytical method is stated. Alternative interpretations have been considered.
  3. Honest Uncertainty: Confidence levels are stated. Uncertainty is acknowledged, not hidden. The product does not claim more certainty than the evidence supports.
  4. Assumption Transparency: Key assumptions are explicitly listed. The reader can identify what, if changed, would invalidate the analysis.
  5. Actionability: The product provides enough information for the decision-maker to act. If recommendations are included, they are specific and feasible.
  6. Clarity: The product is written in plain language. Jargon is defined or avoided. The structure is logical and navigable.
  7. Self-Contained: The product can be understood without reference to other products, though it should cite them. A reader who encounters this product in the archive 30 years from now should be able to understand its content and context.

4.4 Product Classification

Intelligence products are classified by sensitivity into three levels:

LevelLabelDescriptionHandling
1ROUTINEGeneral analytical products with no special sensitivity. Standard intelligence updates, routine monitoring reports.Standard archival per D6-001. No special access restrictions beyond the institution's normal access controls per SEC-001.
2SENSITIVEProducts containing vulnerability information, specific threat details, or analysis that could be exploited if accessed by an adversary.Encrypted storage per SEC-003. Access limited to the operator and designated successors. Not included in general knowledge exports.
3RESTRICTEDProducts containing the institution's most sensitive assessments: critical vulnerabilities, specific defense gaps, personal security information, or analysis whose disclosure could directly enable harm to the institution.Encrypted storage with separate key per SEC-003. Access limited to the active operator only. Successor access granted only upon confirmed succession per GOV-001. Destruction schedule per D6-005 if the product's sensitivity expires.

4.5 Archival Requirements

All intelligence products are institutional records and must be archived per D6-001 and D6-003. Specific requirements:

  • Format: Products must be stored in plain text or a long-term archival format approved per D6-003. Proprietary formats are not acceptable.
  • Indexing: Each product must be indexed by product ID, date, type, subject, and classification level. The intelligence archive index must be searchable and must be maintained as a separate document updated with each new product.
  • Retention: All intelligence products are retained permanently unless a specific destruction schedule applies per the classification level. Intelligence products gain value over time as they enable calibration tracking and provide institutional memory.
  • Backup: Products are included in the institution's standard backup procedures per D6-002. SENSITIVE and RESTRICTED products are included in encrypted backups only.
  • Cross-Reference: Products must reference other products they depend on or supersede. When a new assessment supersedes an older one, the older product is not deleted but is marked as superseded with a pointer to the replacement.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D7-08-01: Every intelligence product must include all universal required elements defined in Section 4.2. Products missing required elements must not be used for Tier 1-3 decisions per GOV-001.
  • R-D7-08-02: Every product must include a Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF). The BLUF must be comprehensible on its own without reading the full product.
  • R-D7-08-03: Confidence levels must use the epistemic hierarchy from D7-001 and the estimative language from D7-011. Informal confidence expressions ("I think," "probably," "maybe") are not acceptable in finished products.
  • R-D7-08-04: All products must be archived per Section 4.5. A product that is not archived does not exist for institutional purposes.
  • R-D7-08-05: Products must be classified upon creation per Section 4.4. Classification may be downgraded when sensitivity decreases but may not be removed entirely -- all products retain at least ROUTINE classification.
  • R-D7-08-06: The intelligence archive index must be updated within 48 hours of product creation. An unindexed product is effectively lost.
  • R-D7-08-07: Product quality must meet all criteria in Section 4.3 before the product is considered finished. The analyst may publish preliminary products (marked "DRAFT -- SUBJECT TO REVISION") when time pressure demands it, but must replace drafts with finished products within 14 days.
  • R-D7-08-08: At least one Strategic Estimate (Type 6) must be produced annually as part of the annual intelligence cycle. This is the capstone product that synthesizes the year's analytical work.

Failure Modes

  • Template compliance without analytical substance. The analyst fills in all required fields but the content is superficial. The product looks correct but the analysis is shallow -- assumptions are listed but not genuinely examined, confidence levels are stated but not calibrated against evidence, alternatives are mentioned but not seriously considered. Mitigation: the quality criteria in Section 4.3 require analytical soundness, not just completeness. Periodic self-review should assess whether products are genuinely analytical or merely formally compliant.

  • Product proliferation without utility. The analyst produces many products that are never consulted. The intelligence archive grows but does not inform decisions. Mitigation: D7-010 (Decision Support Doctrine) defines how products connect to decisions. The annual review should assess which products were actually used and adjust the production schedule accordingly.

  • Classification creep. Over time, more products are classified as SENSITIVE or RESTRICTED than warranted, making the archive increasingly inaccessible. Mitigation: classification must be justified at creation time. Annual review should include a classification audit to identify products that can be downgraded.

  • Archive degradation. Products are created but the archive index is not maintained, making retrieval impossible. Mitigation: R-D7-08-06 requires indexing within 48 hours. The quarterly review per D7-001 R-D7-05 should include an index integrity check.

  • Format obsolescence. Products are stored in formats that become unreadable as technology changes. Mitigation: products must use plain text or approved long-term formats per D6-003. Format migration is governed by D6 procedures.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If products are found to lack analytical substance on review: Conduct a product quality audit. Select five recent products at random and assess each against the quality criteria in Section 4.3. If three or more fail on analytical soundness, the analyst must redesign their production process -- possibly by allocating more time per product and producing fewer. Quality always takes precedence over quantity. Document findings and corrective action.

  2. If the intelligence archive index has fallen out of date: Conduct an immediate reconciliation. Compare the archive contents against the index. Add missing entries. Verify existing entries for accuracy. Establish a weekly index update habit rather than relying on the 48-hour rule alone.

  3. If classification creep is detected: Review all SENSITIVE and RESTRICTED products from the past three years. For each, assess whether the original classification justification still applies. Downgrade where appropriate. Document the review.

  4. If products are not being used for decision-making: Diagnose the disconnect. Possibilities: the products do not answer questions the decision-maker is actually asking (misaligned requirements); the products are produced too late to be useful (timing failure); the products are too long or complex for the decision context (format mismatch). Address the root cause through the decision-support interface per D7-010.

  5. If format obsolescence threatens archived products: Invoke the format migration procedures per D6-003. Prioritize products by classification and recency -- RESTRICTED and recent products first. Document the migration process and verify product integrity after migration.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The initial product portfolio will be dominated by Intelligence Updates (Type 2) and Intelligence Assessments (Type 3) as the institution establishes its analytical baseline. The templates will feel constraining at first -- the analyst may resist the structure. Follow the templates anyway. The discipline of structured output is more important than the comfort of informal notes. The first Strategic Estimate will be the hardest to produce; subsequent ones benefit from having the first as a reference.

  • Years 5-15: The archive should be substantial enough to enable calibration tracking -- comparing past assessments against outcomes. The product templates may need refinement based on experience: some required elements may prove less useful than expected, while unstated elements may prove essential. Revise the templates thoughtfully, preserving backward compatibility so that older products remain comprehensible.

  • Years 15-30: The intelligence archive becomes a major institutional asset. A successor analyst will study past products to understand the institution's analytical history, learn from past successes and failures, and calibrate their own judgment against their predecessor's track record. The self-contained quality criterion (Section 4.3, criterion 7) pays off here: products written for the current decision-maker also serve the future student.

  • Years 30-50+: The archive now spans decades. Pattern recognition across such a time span is uniquely valuable -- long-term trends in the threat landscape, recurring opportunity categories, evolving analytical confidence. The product standards should remain stable to preserve this longitudinal coherence, even as the content and concerns change completely.

  • Signpost for revision: If the analyst consistently produces products that do not follow the templates, or if the templates consistently fail to capture the information that matters, the templates need revision. However, revision must preserve the universal required elements and the quality criteria -- those are the minimum standard for institutional intelligence work and must not be relaxed.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The hardest part of this article was resisting the urge to over-specify. I could have defined templates for every conceivable intelligence product. I chose six types because six covers the range from "urgent alert" to "strategic synthesis" with useful granularity. If a seventh type proves necessary, it can be added -- the framework is extensible.

The Bottom Line Up Front (BLUF) requirement will feel unnatural to anyone trained to build arguments sequentially. Intelligence products are not academic papers. The reader needs to know the conclusion first so they can decide whether to read the supporting analysis. If the BLUF says "critical threat identified," the reader will read the full assessment. If the BLUF says "routine monitoring, no changes," the reader files it. This is not a dumbing down -- it is respect for the reader's time and attention, which in a single-operator institution are the scarcest resources available.

The classification system is deliberately simple. Three levels. An institution of this size does not need a complex classification hierarchy. The key distinction is between products that are safe to include in general knowledge exports (ROUTINE), products that should stay within the institution (SENSITIVE), and products that should be restricted even within the institution during succession (RESTRICTED). If this proves insufficient, the system can be extended, but I suspect it will prove more than sufficient for decades.
