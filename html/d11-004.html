D11-004 — Procurement Procedures and Supply Chain Management

Document ID
D11-004
Domain
11 — Administration
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Draft

Purpose

This article defines how the institution identifies, sources, acquires, verifies, and receives goods, hardware, consumables, and services from outside the air-gap boundary. Procurement in a self-sovereign, off-grid, air-gapped institution is fundamentally different from procurement in a connected environment. Every acquisition event requires crossing the air gap, which means exposure to external networks, physical travel, and disruption to the operational routine. There is no overnight shipping, no cloud marketplace, no automated reordering. Every procurement event is a planned expedition.

This document establishes the procedures that transform procurement from a reactive scramble into a disciplined, predictable process. It defines how needs are identified, how sources are evaluated, how acquisitions are timed and batched, how received goods are verified and integrated into the asset registry, and how the institution maintains supply chain awareness even from behind an air gap. It also addresses degraded supply chain scenarios — situations where normal commercial channels are disrupted and alternative sourcing becomes necessary.

Scope

In scope:

  • Procurement lifecycle: need identification, specification, sourcing, acquisition, verification, receipt, and integration.
  • Air-gap crossing protocols for procurement events.
  • Vendor evaluation and relationship management.
  • Batch procurement strategy and procurement calendar.
  • Quality verification and acceptance testing for received goods.
  • Degraded supply chain procedures (supply disruption, vendor loss, regional unavailability).
  • Ethical constraints on procurement (sourcing ethics, labor practices, environmental impact).
  • Salvage and secondary-market procurement.
  • Integration with the budget framework (D11-003) and asset registry (D11-002).

Out of scope:

  • Budget creation and resource allocation (D11-003).
  • Asset lifecycle management post-receipt (D11-002).
  • Security protocols for air-gap crossing (Domain 3, SEC articles).
  • Specific product recommendations or vendor names (ephemeral data).

Background

The Cost of Crossing the Air Gap

In a conventional connected environment, procurement is nearly frictionless. A component fails, an order is placed online, and a replacement arrives within days. The cognitive cost is minimal, the time cost is low, and the security exposure is bounded and well-understood. None of this applies to this institution.

Every procurement event here requires the operator to physically leave the institutional site, travel to a location with commercial access, interact with external systems, transport goods back to the site, verify their integrity, and integrate them into the institutional inventory. The time cost of a single procurement event is measured in hours to days, not minutes. The security exposure includes physical transit, interaction with commercial networks, and the introduction of new hardware that may contain firmware-level compromises.

This structural cost means that the institution cannot afford reactive procurement. Every unplanned procurement event is an emergency that consumes disproportionate resources. The procurement system must therefore be overwhelmingly proactive: needs anticipated, purchases batched, and timelines planned well in advance.

Supply Chain Fragility

The institution's 50-year horizon means it will almost certainly experience periods of supply chain disruption. Component shortages, vendor bankruptcy, trade disruptions, shipping delays, and regional economic instability are not hypothetical risks — they are statistical certainties over decades. The procurement framework must account for these realities by maintaining alternative sources, stockpiling critical components, and developing salvage capabilities.

The institution also faces a specific vulnerability: technology obsolescence. Components available today may not be manufactured in ten years. Standards change, connectors evolve, and backward compatibility is never guaranteed. Procurement must therefore include a forward-looking component that identifies components at risk of obsolescence and acquires spares before they become unavailable.

System Model

The Procurement Lifecycle

Every procurement event follows a seven-phase lifecycle:

  1. Need Identification: A need is identified through one of three channels: the asset lifecycle calendar (a component approaching end-of-life), the consumable inventory (stock falling below the three-month threshold per R-BUD-04), or an operational requirement (a new capability or replacement for a failed component). Needs are documented in the procurement queue with a priority level, a justification, and a reference to the relevant budget line item.
  2. Specification: The need is translated into a specific procurement specification: exact model numbers where possible, acceptable alternatives, minimum quality standards, quantity needed, and any compatibility requirements with existing systems. The specification is reviewed against the asset registry (D11-002) to ensure compatibility.
  3. Sourcing: Potential sources are identified and evaluated. For routine items, the institution maintains a source registry of previously used vendors with quality and reliability notes. For new or unusual items, research is required. Sourcing must consider price, quality, availability, shipping logistics, and ethical constraints. At least two sources should be identified for any critical component.
  4. Batching and Scheduling: Individual procurement needs are batched into procurement events. The goal is to minimize the number of air-gap crossings by combining multiple purchases into single trips. The procurement calendar defines regular procurement windows (recommended quarterly, aligned with the budget review cycle). Urgent needs that cannot wait for the next procurement window require a documented justification.
  5. Acquisition: The actual purchase and transport of goods. This phase requires crossing the air gap and follows the security protocols defined in Domain 3. The operator carries a procurement list derived from the batched needs. All purchases are documented at time of acquisition with receipts, serial numbers, and source information.
  6. Verification: Received goods are inspected and tested before integration into the institution. Hardware is visually inspected for damage, powered on for basic functionality testing, and (where applicable) checked for firmware integrity. Consumables are checked for quantity, quality, and shelf life. Any item that fails verification is quarantined and a return or replacement is initiated at the next procurement window.
  7. Integration: Verified goods are entered into the asset registry (D11-002) or consumable inventory. Budget records (D11-003) are updated with actual costs. The procurement queue entry is closed with a completion date and any notes on the procurement experience (vendor reliability, product quality, lessons learned).

The Procurement Calendar

The institution maintains a procurement calendar that defines planned procurement windows throughout the year. The recommended cadence is quarterly, timed to follow the quarterly budget review. Each procurement window consists of three phases: preparation (1-2 weeks before the window, during which the procurement list is finalized), execution (the procurement event itself), and integration (1-2 weeks after, during which received goods are verified and registered).

The procurement calendar also includes annual strategic procurement events: purchases of items with long lead times, bulk acquisitions of consumables at favorable prices, and pre-emptive acquisition of components approaching obsolescence.

Degraded Supply Chain Operations

When normal commercial channels are disrupted, the institution activates degraded supply chain procedures:

  • Level 1 — Vendor Disruption: A specific vendor is unavailable. Switch to alternative sources from the source registry. If no alternative is listed, research and qualify a new source.
  • Level 2 — Product Unavailability: A specific product is discontinued or unavailable. Evaluate compatible alternatives per the specification's acceptable-alternatives list. If none exist, consult Domain 13 (Evolution) for technology migration options.
  • Level 3 — Regional Disruption: Commercial infrastructure in the region is significantly disrupted. Activate stockpiled reserves. Extend component lifespans through conservation measures. Explore salvage and secondary-market sources. Begin planning for extended self-sufficiency.
  • Level 4 — Sustained Isolation: Commercial procurement is impossible for an extended period. The institution operates entirely on existing inventory and reserves. All non-essential resource consumption is suspended. Salvage, repair, and repurposing become primary supply strategies. This scenario is the ultimate test of the institution's reserve and stockpile policies.

Rules and Constraints

  • R-PRO-01: No procurement event may occur without a corresponding entry in the procurement queue that includes: item specification, justification, budget line item reference, priority level, and estimated cost.
  • R-PRO-02: Procurement events must be batched into planned procurement windows whenever possible. Unplanned procurement (outside scheduled windows) requires written justification documenting why the need cannot wait for the next window.
  • R-PRO-03: For any component classified as critical (failure would cause system shutdown), the institution must maintain at least one spare or have a verified alternative source that can deliver within the institution's maximum acceptable downtime.
  • R-PRO-04: All hardware received must undergo verification testing before being entered into the asset registry as operational. No component is considered available for deployment until it has passed verification.
  • R-PRO-05: The source registry must be reviewed annually. Sources that have not been used in three years must be re-verified for continued availability and reliability. Sources that have delivered substandard goods must be flagged and, after two failures, removed from the primary source list.
  • R-PRO-06: Procurement decisions must comply with the ethical constraints defined by Domain 15. When ethical compliance and cost optimization conflict, ethical compliance takes precedence, and the additional cost is documented as the cost of ethical operation.
  • R-PRO-07: Salvaged and secondary-market components must undergo enhanced verification before integration, including extended burn-in testing where applicable. The provenance uncertainty of salvaged components justifies additional caution.
  • R-PRO-08: The procurement queue must include a forward-looking section that tracks components at risk of obsolescence. For any component with an estimated remaining market availability of less than five years, a stockpile decision must be made: acquire spares now, or plan a migration to a successor technology.

Failure Modes

  • Reactive procurement spiral. The operator stops planning procurement and instead responds to each failure or shortage with an emergency trip. Each emergency trip disrupts the operational routine, which causes other maintenance to be deferred, which creates more failures, which creates more emergency trips. Mitigation: the procurement calendar (quarterly windows) and R-PRO-02's requirement to batch. Even one quarter of planned procurement breaks the spiral.
  • Single-source dependency. The institution relies on a single vendor for a critical component. When that vendor fails — bankruptcy, discontinuation, quality decline — there is no fallback. Mitigation: R-PRO-03 requires either a spare on hand or a verified alternative source. The source registry must include at least two sources for every critical component.
  • Verification bypass. Under time pressure, the operator skips verification and deploys new hardware directly. A compromised or defective component enters the production environment. Mitigation: R-PRO-04 is absolute. The air-gap nature of the institution means that a compromised component introduced past the boundary is catastrophically dangerous. No shortcut is acceptable.
  • Obsolescence surprise. A critical component becomes unavailable with no warning. The institution discovers that spares cannot be procured at any price. Mitigation: R-PRO-08 requires tracking components at risk of obsolescence. The forward-looking section of the procurement queue is specifically designed to prevent this failure mode.
  • Procurement hoarding. The operator over-procures from anxiety about supply disruption, tying up financial resources in excess inventory and creating storage burdens. Mitigation: procurement quantities must be justified against consumption rates and storage capacity. The budget framework (D11-003) provides the check: if procurement spending consistently exceeds budget, hoarding may be the cause.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If procurement has become reactive: Halt all non-emergency procurement. Conduct a comprehensive needs assessment across all systems. Prioritize needs by urgency and criticality. Create a single, batched procurement list. Execute one planned procurement event. Use the results to re-establish the quarterly calendar.
  2. If a critical component is unavailable: Check the asset registry (D11-002) for any existing spare, even if not ideal. Consult the source registry for alternative vendors. Search secondary markets. If the component is truly unobtainable, escalate to Domain 13 (Evolution) for a technology migration assessment. Document the event as a lesson learned for the forward-looking procurement queue.
  3. If a compromised component was deployed without verification: Isolate the component immediately. Conduct a security assessment (Domain 3 protocols). If compromise is confirmed, remove the component and initiate disaster recovery procedures (Domain 12) as needed. Conduct a root-cause analysis of why verification was bypassed and add a procedural safeguard.
  4. If supply chain is disrupted: Activate the appropriate degraded supply chain level (Section: System Model). Inventory all reserves. Calculate burn rate for critical consumables. Identify the most constrained resource and prioritize conservation efforts there. Document the disruption for future planning.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: The procurement system is being established. The source registry is sparse. Lead times and vendor reliability are unknown. The procurement calendar may feel over-structured for the institution's current simplicity. Maintain it anyway — the structure will prove its value when complexity increases.
  • Years 3-10: The source registry is populated with real experience data. Vendor reliability patterns emerge. The procurement calendar is refined based on actual consumption patterns. Obsolescence tracking becomes increasingly important as the institution's original hardware approaches end-of-life.
  • Years 10-25: The institution has survived at least one major technology transition and probably at least one supply chain disruption. The procurement procedures have been tested under stress. The degraded supply chain procedures may have been activated. The source registry reflects multiple generations of vendor relationships. Alternative sourcing strategies (salvage, secondary market, community exchange) are well-developed.
  • Years 25-50+: Procurement patterns have changed significantly. Technologies used at founding may be entirely obsolete. The procurement system must have evolved to handle whatever the current technology landscape looks like while maintaining the core discipline: plan ahead, batch purchases, verify everything, track everything. The procurement archive — every purchase, every vendor interaction, every lesson learned — is a valuable institutional record.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 — Founding Entry: The single most stressful aspect of running an air-gapped, off-grid system is not the technical complexity. It is the procurement. Connected systems can fail and recover quickly because replacement parts are a credit card transaction and two days away. Here, a failed drive means a trip to town, which means a day lost, which means deferred maintenance on other systems, which means cascading delays. I have already experienced this cycle, and the institution is barely started. The procurement calendar is my defense against this cycle. If I can buy what I need before I need it, I eliminate the emergency. The challenge is psychological: it feels wasteful to buy spare drives that might sit unused for years. But the cost of a spare drive gathering dust is far less than the cost of an emergency procurement trip. I must train myself to see unused inventory not as waste but as insurance.
