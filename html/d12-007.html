D12-007 — Hardware Failure Recovery

Document ID
D12-007
Domain
12 -- Disaster Recovery
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D12-001, D12-004, D12-005, D4-001, D5-002, D11-002, SEC-002
Depended Upon By
D12-012 (cascade failure analysis), D12-002 (drill scenarios), D13-002 (hardware sunset triggers).

Purpose

This article provides step-by-step recovery procedures for every category of hardware failure identified in D12-004 Section 4.1. It is the practical, hands-on guide that the operator follows when a physical component fails: storage drives, power supplies, RAM, motherboards, UPS batteries, and peripheral devices.

Hardware failure is the most frequent failure category the institution will face over its fifty-year horizon. D12-004 rates single storage drive failure as Near-Certain over fifty years and Likely over ten. Power supply failure and peripheral failure are similarly probable. This article must therefore be the most well-rehearsed and immediately accessible of all recovery procedure articles.

Each procedure in this article follows a consistent structure: symptom identification (how do you know this component has failed), impact assessment (what is affected), immediate response (what to do right now to prevent further damage), replacement procedure (how to swap the component), data recovery (if applicable), and verification (how to confirm the system is restored). The procedures are written for a stressed operator who may be discovering the failure at an inconvenient time and needs clear, sequential instructions.

Scope

In scope:

  • Recovery procedures for: storage drives (HDD and SSD), power supplies, RAM modules, motherboards/CPUs, UPS batteries, and peripheral devices.
  • Spare parts inventory requirements for each component class.
  • Diagnostic procedures for identifying which component has failed.
  • Preventive monitoring to detect impending failures.
  • Procedures for operating in degraded mode when replacement parts are not immediately available.

Out of scope:

  • Complete system rebuild from bare metal (D12-003).
  • Data corruption recovery that is not caused by hardware failure (D12-008).
  • Facility-level failures such as power grid loss (D12-009, D12-011).
  • Hardware procurement and budgeting (D11-001, D11-002).

Background

3.1 The Replace-Not-Repair Principle

This institution does not repair failed hardware components. It replaces them. A failed hard drive is replaced with a spare, not sent for data recovery. A failed power supply is swapped, not soldered. This principle exists for three reasons: repair requires specialized skills and equipment that may not be available; repair introduces uncertainty about whether the repaired component will fail again; and replacement from a spare can be done immediately, while repair takes days or weeks.

The replace-not-repair principle requires maintaining an inventory of spare parts. D11-002 defines the asset registry that tracks these spares. This article defines the minimum spare parts inventory for disaster recovery purposes.

3.2 Diagnostic Discipline

Before replacing a component, the operator must diagnose which component has actually failed. Symptoms can be misleading: a system that will not boot may have a failed drive, a failed power supply, a failed RAM module, or a failed motherboard. Replacing the wrong component wastes a spare and leaves the actual problem unresolved. This article includes diagnostic procedures for each failure class to minimize misdiagnosis.

3.3 The Spare Parts Dilemma

Spare parts have a shelf life. Components purchased today may not be compatible with hardware purchased ten years from now. Spare parts themselves can degrade in storage. The spare parts inventory must balance readiness (having the right part available immediately) against waste (stockpiling parts that become obsolete). The evolution path of this article addresses this dilemma over the fifty-year horizon.

System Model

4.1 Storage Drive Failure Recovery

Symptoms: System fails to boot (if OS drive). Read/write errors in system logs. SMART warnings (reallocated sectors, pending sectors, uncorrectable errors). Unusual sounds (clicking, grinding for HDDs). Sudden filesystem corruption.

Diagnostic procedure:

  1. Check SMART status: smartctl -a /dev/[device]
  2. Look for: Reallocated_Sector_Ct > 0, Current_Pending_Sector > 0, Offline_Uncorrectable > 0.
  3. Check kernel logs for I/O errors: dmesg | grep -i "error\|fault\|fail" | grep -i sd
  4. If the drive is not detected at all, check physical connections (SATA cable, power cable).

Immediate response:

  1. If the drive is still partially readable, immediately create an emergency backup of any data not yet backed up: dd if=/dev/[failing-device] of=/path/to/image.img bs=4096 conv=noerror,sync status=progress
  2. Do not write to the failing drive. Every write operation risks further degradation.
  3. If the OS is on a separate drive and still functional, the system can continue operating without the failed data drive (degraded mode per D12-011).

Replacement procedure:

  1. Power down the system cleanly.
  2. Replace the failed drive with a spare of equal or greater capacity.
  3. If the failed drive was the OS drive, follow D12-003 Phase 2 and Phase 3 to install the OS and configure accounts on the new drive.
  4. If the failed drive was a data drive, create the partition and encryption per D12-003 Phase 2 Step 3.
  5. Restore data from the most recent verified backup per D6-002 Section 4.4.
  6. Verify the restored data per D12-006.

Verification: System boots normally. All data is accessible. SMART reports healthy status on the new drive. Backup system resumes normal operation.

4.2 Power Supply Failure Recovery

Symptoms: System will not power on. System powers off unexpectedly and will not restart. Burning smell or visible damage to the power supply unit. Fan noise changes (stops or becomes erratic).

Diagnostic procedure:

  1. Verify that the power source is functional (test the outlet with another device).
  2. Check the UPS: is it providing power? Are its indicator lights normal?
  3. If the system is a desktop/tower, attempt to start with minimal components (motherboard, one RAM stick, CPU, no drives) to isolate the PSU.
  4. Listen for any signs of life when the power button is pressed (fans spinning briefly, LED flickers).

Immediate response:

  1. If the system powered down suddenly, the drives may contain uncommitted writes. Do not panic. Modern journaling filesystems (ext4) handle this gracefully in most cases.
  2. Assess whether the power supply failure caused damage to other components (check for burning smell, visible scorch marks).

Replacement procedure:

  1. Disconnect all power cables from the failed PSU.
  2. Remove the failed PSU and install the spare. Ensure the replacement PSU meets or exceeds the wattage rating of the original.
  3. Reconnect all power cables to drives, motherboard, and peripherals.
  4. Power on and verify that all components are recognized during POST.
  5. Check the filesystem for inconsistencies: fsck -n /dev/[partitions] (read-only check first).

Verification: System boots and runs stable under load. All drives recognized and healthy. Filesystem clean.

4.3 RAM Failure Recovery

Symptoms: System crashes (kernel panic, blue screen equivalent). Applications crash randomly. Memory errors in system logs. System fails to boot or behaves erratically during boot.

Diagnostic procedure:

  1. Run memory diagnostics: boot from a Linux installation USB and run memtest86+ for at least two passes.
  2. If multiple RAM modules are installed, test each module individually by removing all others.
  3. Test each RAM slot individually to rule out a defective slot (which indicates motherboard failure).

Replacement procedure:

  1. Power down. Remove the failed RAM module.
  2. Install the spare module in the same slot (or a known-good slot if the slot is suspect).
  3. Run memtest86+ for at least one pass to confirm the new module is healthy.
  4. Boot the system and verify normal operation.

Verification: memtest86+ passes. System stable under normal workload for 24 hours. No memory errors in system logs.

4.4 Motherboard and CPU Failure Recovery

Motherboard and CPU failures are the most disruptive hardware failures because they require the most extensive rebuild. A motherboard swap may change the hardware platform entirely, requiring driver updates or OS reinstallation.

Symptoms: System completely dead (no response to power button, no fans, no LED). POST fails (beep codes, no video output). Intermittent crashes that are not attributable to RAM or storage.

Replacement procedure:

  1. This is effectively a partial rebuild. Follow D12-003 procedures, starting at Phase 2 if the OS must be reinstalled on the new platform, or testing the existing drives in the new system first.
  2. Install the replacement motherboard/CPU. Connect all existing drives.
  3. Attempt to boot from the existing OS drive. If the OS boots successfully with the new motherboard, verify all hardware is recognized and reconfigure as needed.
  4. If the OS will not boot (driver incompatibility), reinstall the OS per D12-003 Phase 2, preserving the data partitions.
  5. Restore any system configurations that were specific to the old hardware.

Verification: All hardware recognized. OS stable. All data accessible. All services operational. SMART healthy on all drives.

4.5 UPS Battery Recovery

Symptoms: UPS reports battery degradation or battery replacement needed. UPS fails to provide backup power during a power interruption. UPS runtime is significantly shorter than rated.

Replacement procedure:

  1. Procure a compatible replacement battery.
  2. Perform a clean shutdown of the system (the UPS may not provide reliable protection during the swap).
  3. Replace the battery per the UPS manufacturer's instructions.
  4. Power on the UPS and allow the battery to charge fully before resuming operations.
  5. Test the UPS by disconnecting it from mains power and verifying that it provides the rated runtime.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D12-07-01: The minimum spare parts inventory defined in Section 4.6 must be maintained at all times. When a spare is used, a replacement must be procured within 30 days.
  • R-D12-07-02: SMART monitoring must be enabled on all storage drives and checked at least weekly as part of the operational checklist (D10-002).
  • R-D12-07-03: Any drive reporting SMART warnings (reallocated sectors, pending sectors) must be proactively replaced within 30 days, even if the drive is still functional.
  • R-D12-07-04: After any hardware replacement, the system must pass the verification procedure defined for that component class before being returned to production service.
  • R-D12-07-05: Hardware replacement events must be documented in the operational log and the asset registry (D11-002) updated within 7 days.
  • R-D12-07-06: Spare parts inventory must be audited annually for compatibility with current hardware. Incompatible spares must be replaced.

Failure Modes

  • Misdiagnosis. The operator replaces the wrong component, wasting a spare and leaving the real problem unresolved. Mitigation: the diagnostic procedures in each subsection. When uncertain, test components in isolation before committing to replacement.
  • Spare parts depletion. The spare is used for a replacement and not replenished. When the next failure occurs, no spare is available. Mitigation: R-D12-07-01 requires replenishment within 30 days. The quarterly operations review checks spare parts inventory.
  • Spare parts incompatibility. The spare was purchased for the previous hardware generation and does not fit the current system. Mitigation: R-D12-07-06 requires annual compatibility audit.
  • Secondary damage from failure. A power supply failure sends a voltage spike that damages the motherboard and drives. A single component failure cascades into a multi-component failure. Mitigation: UPS protection. Surge protection. D12-012 (Cascade Failure Analysis) addresses this scenario specifically.
  • Data loss during emergency imaging. The operator's attempt to create an emergency image from a failing drive accelerates the drive's failure. Mitigation: use conv=noerror,sync to continue past read errors. Accept that some data may be unrecoverable from the failing drive and rely on backups for the remainder.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If no spare part is available for the failed component: Enter degraded mode per D12-011 at the appropriate tier. Procure a replacement using emergency procurement procedures. If the failed component renders the system entirely inoperable and no spare exists, the rebuild procedure (D12-003) may be required on alternative hardware.
  2. If the replacement part is incompatible: Assess whether an adapter, firmware update, or configuration change can make it work. If not, procure the correct part. Document the incompatibility in the asset registry.
  3. If a hardware failure causes data loss: Restore from backup per D6-002. If the data lost was not yet backed up (changes since the last backup), document the loss per ETH-001 Principle 6. Assess whether the backup frequency should be increased to reduce future exposure.
  4. If multiple components fail simultaneously: Prioritize diagnosis. Address the component that blocks all other recovery first (typically the power supply or motherboard). If the system is beyond component-level repair, escalate to D12-003 (System Rebuild).

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Hardware is new and failures are rare. Use this period to build the spare parts inventory and establish monitoring. The first hardware failure (which will come) tests these procedures for the first time.
  • Years 5-15: The original hardware reaches mid-life. Storage drive failures become more frequent. UPS batteries require replacement. The spare parts inventory must be refreshed to match any hardware upgrades performed during this period.
  • Years 15-30: Major hardware platform transitions are likely. The specific diagnostic commands, part numbers, and procedures in this article will need significant revision. The structure -- diagnose, respond, replace, verify -- remains constant.
  • Years 30-50+: The founding hardware is museum-grade. This article has been rewritten multiple times for successive hardware generations. Each rewrite benefits from the accumulated history of actual hardware failures recorded in the operational log.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: Hardware failure is the disaster recovery scenario I find most psychologically manageable. A failed drive is a tangible problem with a tangible solution: replace the drive, restore from backup. There is something satisfying about swapping a part and watching the system come back to life.

The harder challenge is the spare parts dilemma. I am stockpiling parts for hardware that will be obsolete in a decade. This feels wasteful but the alternative -- having no spare when a drive fails on a Friday evening -- is worse. I have resolved to accept the waste as the cost of readiness, and to audit the inventory annually for relevance.

The scenario that worries me is the cascade: a power supply fails and takes a drive with it. Suddenly I need two spares instead of one, and the data recovery is more complex because the drive may have suffered electrical damage rather than simple mechanical wear. The UPS is the first line of defense against this scenario, and I intend to test it regularly.
