D19-007 — Review Gate Definitions

Document ID
D19-007
Domain
19 -- Quality Assurance
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D19-005 (Quality Standards Taxonomy), D19-003 (Testing Framework)
Depended Upon By
All domains whose workflows include quality checkpoints. D18-003 (Import Validation Pipeline).

Purpose

This article defines the review gates -- formal checkpoints at which work must pass quality criteria before advancing to the next stage. Review gates are the operational mechanism through which quality standards become enforceable. Without gates, standards are aspirations. With gates, standards are requirements.

Gates must be placed carefully. Too many gates slow the institution to a crawl. Too few gates allow defects to propagate unchecked. Gates in the wrong places catch the wrong things. This article defines where gates are placed, what each gate checks, who has authority at each gate, and how gates can be bypassed when circumstances demand it.

This article is addressed to every person whose work passes through a review gate and to every person who operates a gate. It is particularly important for domain leads, who must understand where gates apply within their domain's workflows, and for the QA function, which monitors gate effectiveness.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The definition and classification of review gates.
  • Gate placement principles -- where in a workflow a gate belongs.
  • Pass/fail criteria at each gate type.
  • Gate authority -- who decides pass or fail.
  • Bypass procedures for exceptional circumstances.
  • Gate effectiveness monitoring.

This article does not cover:

  • The specific quality standards checked at gates (see D19-005 for taxonomy, D19-006 for documentation metrics).
  • The audit procedures for evaluating institutional compliance with gate processes (see D19-002).
  • Defect tracking for issues found at gates (see D19-008).

Background

3.1 The Gate Concept

A review gate is a defined point in a workflow at which work must be evaluated against specific criteria before proceeding. The concept originates in manufacturing (where quality gates prevent defective parts from advancing down an assembly line) and has been adopted in software development (where code reviews and staging environments serve as gates before production deployment). In a documentation institution, gates serve the same function: they prevent work that does not meet standards from reaching the corpus where it might be relied upon by others.

3.2 Gate Placement Philosophy

The fundamental principle of gate placement is: place the gate where the cost of catching a defect is lowest, relative to the cost of letting it pass. Catching an error in a draft before publication is cheap. Catching the same error after the document has been relied upon by other documents, by operators, or by downstream processes is expensive -- not just to fix the document, but to identify and correct everything that was built on the faulty foundation.

There is a secondary principle: gates should be placed at natural transition points in a workflow. A document transitions from draft to review, from review to publication, from active to under-revision. These transitions are natural gate locations because the work is already changing state. Adding a gate at a transition point adds less overhead than inserting a gate in the middle of an uninterrupted workflow.

3.3 The Single-Operator Challenge

In a traditional organization, a gate implies that someone other than the creator evaluates the work. In a single-operator institution, this is impossible for most work. The single operator must serve as both creator and gate operator. This creates a genuine risk of self-review blindness -- the inability to see one's own errors. The mitigation strategies include: temporal separation (review your own work after a gap of at least 24 hours), checklist-driven review (follow a structured checklist rather than relying on free-form self-assessment), and selective external review (identify the highest-risk work and seek external review for that subset even if it means delay).

System Model

4.1 Gate Types

The institution defines four types of review gates, each appropriate for different risk levels:

Gate Type 1: Self-Review Gate. The creator reviews their own work against a defined checklist. Appropriate for low-risk changes (typo corrections, minor updates, commentary additions). Time requirement: 15-30 minutes. Authority: the creator. Record: a dated note in the article's Commentary Section or revision log confirming self-review was conducted.

Gate Type 2: Peer Review Gate. A person other than the creator reviews the work. Appropriate for standard-risk work (new articles, significant revisions, procedure changes). Time requirement: 1-4 hours depending on article length and complexity. Authority: the reviewer, who must be a domain participant or the QA Architect. Record: a documented review with scores per D19-006 and any findings.

Gate Type 3: Cross-Domain Review Gate. Reviewers from multiple domains evaluate the work. Appropriate for work that affects multiple domains (cross-domain procedures, universal standards, framework changes). Time requirement: 2-8 hours aggregate across reviewers. Authority: all participating domain leads must concur. Record: a documented review from each participating domain.

Gate Type 4: Governance Review Gate. The governance body reviews and approves the work. Appropriate for the highest-risk changes (changes to root documents, changes to governance structure, changes to ethical foundations). Time requirement: variable, potentially requiring a governance meeting. Authority: governance body as defined in GOV-001. Record: a governance decision log entry per D20-002.

4.3 Gate Authority and Escalation

Each gate has a designated authority -- the person or body empowered to declare pass or fail. If the gate authority and the work creator disagree, escalation follows this path:

  1. Discussion between creator and gate authority to resolve specific findings.
  2. If unresolved, escalation to the relevant domain lead (if the gate authority is not the domain lead).
  3. If still unresolved, escalation to the QA Architect for interpretation of the applicable standard.
  4. If the QA Architect's interpretation is disputed, escalation to governance per GOV-001.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D19-07-01: Every Published Article Must Pass a Gate. No article may be added to the institution's active corpus without passing at least a Type 1 (Self-Review) gate. New articles and major revisions must pass at least a Type 2 (Peer Review) gate.

  • R-D19-07-02: Gate Results Must Be Recorded. Every gate passage (pass or fail) must be documented with the date, the gate type, the reviewer identity, and the outcome. For Type 2 and above, the quality scores per D19-006 must be included.

  • R-D19-07-03: Bypass Requires Documentation. A gate may be bypassed in exceptional circumstances (urgent operational need, unavailability of qualified reviewer). A bypass must be documented with: the reason for bypass, the authority who approved the bypass, and a scheduled date by which the bypassed gate will be retroactively conducted. A bypass is not a waiver -- the gate must still be passed, just on a delayed schedule.

  • R-D19-07-04: Gate Criteria Must Be Known in Advance. The pass/fail criteria for a gate must be known to the work creator before they submit work to the gate. A gate that applies secret or unstated criteria is not a quality gate; it is an arbitrary obstacle.

  • R-D19-07-05: Proportional Gating. Per D19-001 R-D19-04, the depth of review at each gate must be proportional to the risk of the work. Low-risk changes receive lighter review. High-risk changes receive heavier review. The gate type classification in this article operationalizes this principle.

  • R-D19-07-06: Gate Effectiveness Must Be Monitored. The QA function must periodically assess whether gates are catching defects (if they catch nothing, the gate may be unnecessary) and whether defects are escaping gates (if defects regularly escape, the gate criteria may be insufficient). This data feeds into D19-011 (Continuous Improvement).

Failure Modes

  • Gate proliferation. Too many gates. Every change requires multiple reviews. The institution's throughput drops. People start bypassing gates routinely, undermining the entire system. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: high. Detection: track the ratio of bypasses to normal gate passages. If bypasses exceed 20% for any gate, that gate is either in the wrong place or checking the wrong things.

  • Rubber-stamp gates. Gates exist on paper but reviews are cursory. Reviewers check boxes without examining the work. The institution has the appearance of quality control without the substance. Likelihood: high over time, especially under workload pressure. Severity: high. Detection: spot-check gate records for substantive review comments. If a gate consistently produces zero findings, either the submitted work is consistently perfect (unlikely) or the review is not thorough.

  • Gate bottleneck. A single reviewer becomes the bottleneck for multiple gates. Work queues up waiting for review. The institution's pace is dictated by one person's availability. Likelihood: high in a small team. Severity: moderate. Detection: track gate waiting time. If work consistently waits more than 5 business days for gate review, the bottleneck must be addressed.

  • Adversarial gates. The gate becomes a site of conflict between QA and the domain. Reviewers use gates to impose preferences rather than enforce standards. Creators view gates as obstacles to resist rather than quality tools to embrace. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: high. Detection: recurring escalations from the same gate. If a gate consistently produces disputes, the criteria may be subjective or the gate authority may be overstepping.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If gates have proliferated: Inventory all active gates. For each, determine: How many defects has this gate caught in the last 12 months? What would happen if this gate were removed? Eliminate gates that catch few or no defects and whose absence would not create unacceptable risk. Consolidate gates that check overlapping criteria.

  2. If gates are being rubber-stamped: Interview reviewers to understand why. Common causes include: excessive workload, unclear criteria, belief that the work does not warrant review. Address the root cause. If workload is the issue, reduce the number of gates or the scope of review at each gate. If criteria are unclear, clarify them per R-D19-07-04. If the gate is genuinely unnecessary, remove it.

  3. If a gate is bottlenecked: Cross-train additional reviewers for that gate type. If the bottleneck is a Type 3 or Type 4 gate that requires specific authority, consider delegated authority for lower-risk variants of the gated workflow.

  4. If a gate has become adversarial: Revert to the documented criteria. Both the reviewer and the creator should independently assess the work against the published criteria, then compare. Where they agree, proceed. Where they disagree, the specific criterion and the specific finding must be documented and escalated per the escalation path in Section 4.3. Personal preference is not a gate criterion.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: Start with minimal gates. A Type 1 gate for all work and a Type 2 gate for new articles is sufficient. As the institution's corpus grows and cross-domain dependencies develop, add Type 3 gates selectively. Track gate effectiveness data from the start -- it will inform decisions about gate placement later.

  • Years 3-10: Gate placement should stabilize. The institution should have enough data to know which gates are effective and which are overhead. Expect to remove some initial gates and add others based on experience.

  • Years 10-25: As the institution grows and potentially federates, gate authority becomes distributed. Ensure that gate criteria remain consistent even as the people operating gates change. The gate map in Section 4.2 should be reviewed and updated to reflect new workflow types that have emerged.

  • Years 25-50+: The gate system should be well-established. The risk at this stage is ossification -- gates that made sense in year 5 may be pointless in year 35. The gate effectiveness monitoring required by R-D19-07-06 must remain active to prevent gates from becoming empty rituals.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The single most important thing about review gates is that they must be fast enough to use. A gate that adds three weeks to every change will be bypassed. A gate that adds three hours to a significant change will be used. Speed is not the enemy of quality; delay is the enemy of compliance. Design gates to be thorough enough to catch genuine defects and fast enough that people do not route around them. If you find yourself routinely approving gate bypasses, the problem is not with the people requesting bypasses -- it is with the gates.
