D14-011 — Research Ethics and Permissible Experimentation

Document ID
D14-011
Domain
14 -- Research & Theory
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
ETH-001, CON-001, GOV-001, SEC-001, D14-001, D14-002, D15-002, D15-003
Depended Upon By
D14-007 (research prioritization constrained by ethics). All Domain 14 articles that propose or execute experiments. Referenced by Domain 15 (Ethics) for research-specific ethical oversight.

Purpose

This article defines the ethical boundaries for research activities within the institution. It answers the question: what experiments are permissible, what safeguards are required, and what oversight mechanisms exist to prevent the research function from causing harm to the institution itself?

In a conventional research institution, ethics review exists primarily to protect human subjects, animal subjects, and the environment. This institution has none of those concerns in the traditional sense -- there are no human subjects, no animal testing, and no environmental impact beyond the institution's own footprint. But the absence of traditional ethical concerns does not mean the absence of all ethical concerns.

The institution's ethical framework (ETH-001) establishes principles that constrain research activity. Principle 2 (Integrity Over Convenience) prohibits cutting corners in research methodology even when thoroughness is burdensome. Principle 6 (Honest Accounting of Limitations) prohibits overstating research confidence. And the institution's air-gap and sovereignty commitments (CON-001) create ethical obligations around security: research that compromises the air gap, even temporarily, violates a founding principle regardless of the scientific value of the research.

This article operationalizes these ethical principles into specific, actionable constraints on research activity. It also establishes the self-governance mechanism by which the operator evaluates the ethical permissibility of their own research -- a task that is inherently conflicted and must be managed with structural safeguards.

Scope

In scope: - Ethical principles specific to research activity in this institution. - The permissibility framework: criteria for evaluating whether an experiment is ethically permissible. - Risk categories: what kinds of research risk are acceptable and what kinds are not. - The Ethics Pre-Check: a mandatory assessment before experiments that involve institutional risk. - Self-governance mechanisms: how the operator manages the conflict of interest inherent in self-regulated research ethics. - The relationship between research ethics and the broader ethical framework (Domain 15).

Out of scope: - The broad institutional ethical framework (ETH-001, Domain 15). - Specific experimental methodology (D14-002). - Security policy (SEC-001, Domain 3, though security constraints inform research ethics). - Data privacy and sovereignty (Domain 6, though sovereignty principles constrain research data handling).

Background

3.1 Ethics Without an Ethics Board

In institutional research, ethical oversight is typically provided by an ethics board or institutional review board (IRB) -- a committee of people who are not the researchers and who evaluate proposed research for ethical compliance. This institution has no such board. The researcher, the research director, and the ethics committee are all the same person.

This structural conflict of interest is the central challenge of this article. The person proposing an experiment is also the person evaluating its ethical permissibility. The incentives are misaligned: the researcher wants to conduct the experiment, and any ethical restriction is an obstacle to their goal. Without external oversight, the temptation to rationalize ethically borderline experiments is strong.

The mitigation is the same strategy used throughout Domain 14: structured self-governance with mandatory documentation and adversarial self-review. The operator cannot appoint an independent ethics board, but they can create a formal ethics pre-check process that forces explicit ethical reasoning before experimentation begins, that documents the reasoning for future review, and that establishes bright-line rules that cannot be rationalized away.

3.2 The Institutional Harm Principle

The primary ethical concern in this institution's research is harm to the institution itself. Experiments that risk data loss, system damage, security compromise, or operational disruption are ethically significant because they threaten the institution's survival and mission. This is not a trivial concern. An experiment that crashes the primary storage system and causes data loss has harmed the institution as surely as a medical experiment that harms a patient has harmed a person. The scale is different; the principle is the same.

The institutional harm principle states: no experiment may impose a risk of irreversible harm to the institution that exceeds the expected value of the knowledge to be gained. This is a proportionality test, not an absolute prohibition. Experiments that carry risk are permitted -- indeed, some of the most valuable experiments (testing disaster recovery procedures, evaluating hardware failure modes) inherently involve risk. The ethical requirement is that the risk is proportionate, that it is identified and managed, and that irreversible harm is specifically guarded against.

3.3 The Intellectual Honesty Obligation

ETH-001, Principle 6 creates an ethical obligation that is specific to research: the obligation to be honest about the limitations of the institution's research. This means not overstating confidence in results, not suppressing negative findings, not presenting provisional knowledge as verified, and not allowing the desire for a particular outcome to distort the research process.

Intellectual dishonesty in research is not just a methodological failure -- it is an ethical failure. When the operator overstates the reliability of a backup strategy based on insufficient testing, they are making a promise to their future self (or successor) that the backup strategy works. If it does not work when needed, the failure traces back to the dishonest assessment. The ethical obligation is to be honest, even when honesty is uncomfortable, even when it means admitting that the institution does not know something it wishes it knew.

System Model

4.1 The Permissibility Framework

Research activities are classified into three permissibility categories based on their risk profile:

Category A: Unrestricted. Research that poses no risk of harm to the institution. Examples: analyzing existing data, reviewing documentation, conducting software tests in a fully isolated environment, literature review from the reference library. Category A research requires no ethics pre-check and may proceed under the standard experimental methodology (D14-002).

Category B: Restricted. Research that poses a manageable risk of harm if safeguards are in place. Examples: testing on non-production hardware, running software experiments that could affect system stability, evaluating technologies that require temporary configuration changes, conducting performance tests that may stress hardware. Category B research requires an Ethics Pre-Check (Section 4.2) and documented safeguards before proceeding.

Category C: Prohibited without explicit approval. Research that poses a risk of irreversible harm to the institution. Examples: experiments on production systems with live data, tests that require temporarily weakening security controls, experiments that involve connecting air-gapped systems to external networks (even briefly), and experiments whose failure mode includes data loss. Category C research requires a full Ethics Pre-Check, documented approval through GOV-001 Tier 2 decision process, and specific risk mitigation measures including verified backups of all data at risk.

4.2 The Ethics Pre-Check

The Ethics Pre-Check is a structured assessment performed before any Category B or Category C experiment begins. It is documented and filed with the experiment's pre-registration (D14-002). The pre-check contains:

  • Risk identification: What could go wrong? Be specific and exhaustive. Consider hardware damage, data loss, security compromise, operational disruption, and any other form of institutional harm.
  • Severity assessment: For each identified risk, how severe would the harm be? Is it reversible or irreversible? What is the worst-case scenario?
  • Probability assessment: How likely is each identified risk? Base the assessment on evidence, not hope. If the probability is unknown, state that honestly -- unknown probability is not low probability.
  • Proportionality assessment: Is the expected value of the knowledge to be gained proportionate to the risk? Would a reasonable future operator, reviewing this decision, consider the risk justified?
  • Safeguards: What specific measures will be taken to prevent or mitigate each identified risk? Safeguards must be concrete and verifiable (e.g., "verified backup of all data on the test system completed within 24 hours before experiment begins"), not aspirational (e.g., "will be careful").
  • Rollback plan: If the experiment goes wrong, how will the institution be restored to its pre-experiment state? The rollback plan must be tested or verifiable before the experiment begins.
  • Bright-line check: Does this experiment violate any of the bright-line rules (Section 4.3)? If so, the experiment is not permissible regardless of its expected value.

4.3 Bright-Line Rules

Certain research activities are prohibited absolutely, regardless of their potential value. These bright-line rules are not subject to proportionality analysis or operator discretion:

  • BL-01: No experiment may breach the air gap. Connecting an air-gapped system to any external network, even briefly, for research purposes is prohibited. If a research question requires network access, the question must be answered through the air-gap crossing process (Domain 18) using external reference materials, not through direct connection.
  • BL-02: No experiment may be conducted on production data without a verified, complete backup of that data made within 24 hours before the experiment begins. "I am pretty sure I have a recent backup" is not verified.
  • BL-03: No experiment may intentionally disable or weaken security controls on production systems. Security controls may be tested (to verify they work), but they may not be disabled to see what happens without them.
  • BL-04: No experiment may be conducted that could cause irreversible loss of institutional knowledge. If an experiment's failure mode includes the possibility of destroying data that cannot be recovered from backups, the experiment must be redesigned to eliminate that failure mode.
  • BL-05: No experiment may be designed to produce a predetermined outcome. Research that is structured to confirm a desired conclusion rather than to test a hypothesis honestly is a violation of ETH-001, Principle 2, regardless of the methodology used.

4.4 Self-Governance Mechanisms

The operator manages the conflict of interest in self-regulated research ethics through four mechanisms:

Mechanism 1: Mandatory documentation. Every Ethics Pre-Check is documented and filed permanently. The documentation creates accountability to future operators and to the operator's future self. If a decision was ethically questionable, the documentation will show it.

Mechanism 2: Adversarial self-review. For Category C experiments, the operator must conduct an adversarial review of their own ethics pre-check: "If I were reviewing someone else's proposal to conduct this experiment, would I approve it? What concerns would I raise?" This review is documented.

Mechanism 3: Cooling period. Category C experiments require a 14-day waiting period between the Ethics Pre-Check and the experiment's start. This waiting period serves the same function as the temporal review in D14-009: it allows the operator's enthusiasm to fade and their judgment to be exercised with greater objectivity.

Mechanism 4: Bright-line rules. The bright-line rules (Section 4.3) are not subject to judgment, rationalization, or proportionality analysis. They are absolute prohibitions that the operator cannot override, no matter how compelling the research question seems. Their absolute nature is their value -- they remove the most dangerous ethical decisions from the operator's discretion entirely.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-RET-01: All experiments must be classified into Category A, B, or C before they begin. Category B and C experiments must have a completed Ethics Pre-Check filed with the pre-registration document (D14-002).
  • R-RET-02: The bright-line rules (Section 4.3) are absolute and may not be overridden by any governance tier, including Tier 1. They may only be changed through the amendment process for foundational documents per GOV-001.
  • R-RET-03: Category C experiments require approval through the GOV-001 Tier 2 decision process, which includes the 30-day waiting period. The Ethics Pre-Check and adversarial self-review must be completed before the Tier 2 decision is filed.
  • R-RET-04: If an experiment produces unexpected harm to the institution, the incident must be documented with the same rigor as a disaster recovery event (Domain 12). The experiment must be halted, the damage assessed, and a post-incident review conducted that evaluates whether the Ethics Pre-Check was adequate.
  • R-RET-05: The intellectual honesty obligation (Section 3.3) is enforceable: any research output that demonstrably overstates confidence, suppresses negative results, or presents predetermined conclusions as discoveries is in violation of this article and ETH-001. Such outputs must be corrected or deprecated in the knowledge registry.
  • R-RET-06: Ethics Pre-Checks must be preserved permanently in the research archive. They are part of the institution's ethical record and serve as evidence that ethical considerations were taken seriously -- or, if they were not, as a basis for process improvement.
  • R-RET-07: Research that has been explicitly prohibited by the ethics framework may not be reclassified as "operational testing" or "routine maintenance" to bypass the prohibition. The substance of the activity, not its label, determines its ethical classification.

Failure Modes

  • Ethics rationalization. The operator wants to conduct an experiment that falls in Category C and rationalizes it into Category B to avoid the stricter requirements. "It is not really on production data because I will only read, not write" or "the air gap is not really breached because the connection is only for thirty seconds." Mitigation: the bright-line rules are binary, not scalar. They either apply or they do not. R-RET-07 prohibits relabeling activities to avoid ethical scrutiny.
  • Ethics fatigue. The Ethics Pre-Check becomes a bureaucratic chore, completed mechanically without genuine ethical reflection. The operator fills in the forms but does not actually think about the risks. Mitigation: the adversarial self-review for Category C experiments forces engagement. The annual ethics review (part of the research continuity audit per D14-006) should assess whether ethics pre-checks show evidence of genuine reflection.
  • Risk underestimation. The operator consistently underestimates the probability and severity of experimental risks because they are optimistic, experienced, or impatient. The safeguards they design are adequate for the risks they estimated, not the risks that actually exist. Mitigation: the proportionality assessment must be based on evidence, not intuition. The cooling period for Category C experiments provides time for the initial assessment to be reconsidered. Post-incident reviews after experimental harm provide calibration data for future risk estimates.
  • Bright-line erosion. Over time, the operator begins to treat the bright-line rules as guidelines rather than absolutes. "Just this once" exceptions accumulate. Mitigation: R-RET-02 states that bright-line rules may only be changed through the foundational document amendment process. Individual exceptions are not possible by design.
  • Intellectual dishonesty normalization. Small acts of intellectual dishonesty -- rounding a number, omitting a contradictory data point, overstating confidence by one level -- become normalized because each individual act seems trivial. Collectively, they erode the integrity of the research archive. Mitigation: the validation process (D14-009) provides a check against dishonesty. The negative results registry (D14-005) creates an institutional expectation that failures are documented. R-RET-05 makes intellectual dishonesty a formal violation.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If ethics pre-checks have been neglected: Conduct retroactive ethics reviews for all Category B and C experiments that proceeded without pre-checks. Document the risk assessment that should have been done. If any experiment proceeded in violation of the bright-line rules, document this as an ethics incident, assess whether harm occurred, and strengthen the pre-check enforcement process.
  2. If an experiment has caused institutional harm: Follow the Domain 12 disaster recovery procedures for the immediate damage. Then conduct a post-incident ethics review: was the ethics pre-check adequate? Were the safeguards sufficient? Were the risks underestimated? What would have prevented this harm? Document the findings and update the Ethics Pre-Check process to incorporate the lessons learned.
  3. If bright-line rules have been violated: Document the violation, the circumstances, and the outcome. Assess whether harm occurred. If the violation was deliberate, conduct a serious self-assessment: why was the rule violated? Was it rationalized? Was the rule itself inappropriate? If the rule is still considered sound, recommit to it in the Commentary Section with a detailed explanation of why the violation occurred and what will prevent recurrence. If the rule is considered inappropriate, initiate the foundational document amendment process to change it properly.
  4. If intellectual dishonesty is discovered: Identify all affected research outputs. Correct the record: update experiment logs, knowledge registry entries, and any operational decisions that were based on the dishonest research. File negative results entries for any findings that were overstated. Document the discovery in the Commentary Section as a lesson for future operators.
  5. If the ethics process has become purely ceremonial: Re-read ETH-001. Conduct a genuine Ethics Pre-Check for the next planned experiment, spending at least one hour on the risk assessment alone. If the process feels onerous, that is evidence that it is working -- genuine ethical reflection is supposed to be uncomfortable. If it feels easy, the reflection is probably insufficient.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The ethics framework is being established. Most experiments will be Category A or B. The bright-line rules will likely not be tested because the early research program is conservative. The value of establishing the framework now is that it creates norms before they are needed urgently.
  • Years 5-15: The research program becomes more ambitious. Category B and potentially Category C experiments become more common. The ethics pre-check process is tested against real ethical dilemmas. Some pre-checks will reveal that a desired experiment is not permissible, which is the process working as intended.
  • Years 15-30: If succession occurs, the ethics archive provides the successor with a record of how the predecessor navigated ethical decisions. The successor may have different ethical intuitions and may revise the permissibility framework -- which is acceptable as long as the revision follows the proper amendment process. The bright-line rules, if they have remained unchanged, demonstrate institutional commitment to ethical stability.
  • Years 30-50+: The ethics archive is a record of the institution's ethical character. It shows what experiments were considered, what was permitted, what was prohibited, and how ethical dilemmas were resolved. This record is as valuable as the research findings themselves because it demonstrates that the institution's intellectual achievements were earned honestly.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: I am writing an ethics policy for research conducted by one person on their own computers. The temptation to dismiss this as unnecessary is real. Who am I protecting? Who is the oversight for? The answer is: myself, from myself.

I know how I behave when I am excited about an experiment. I cut corners. I tell myself the risk is smaller than it is. I convince myself that "just a quick test" does not need the full safety protocol. I have done this before in other contexts, and I have gotten away with it often enough that the habit feels safe. It is not safe. It is a debt that compounds until the one time I do not get away with it, and then the cost is catastrophic.

The bright-line rules are my gift to my future impulsive self. I know that future-me will encounter a research question that seems incredibly important and that requires bending a rule. The bright-line rules take that decision out of future-me's hands. The air gap does not get breached. Production data does not get touched without a verified backup. Security controls do not get disabled. These are not negotiable, not because I trust the rules more than I trust myself, but precisely because I do not trust myself enough to make the right decision in the moment.
