D19-009 — Internal Compliance Framework

Document ID
D19-009
Domain
19 -- Quality Assurance
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D19-002 (Audit Procedures), D19-005 (Quality Standards Taxonomy)
Depended Upon By
D19-004 (Cross-Domain Audit Protocols). Referenced by governance domains for institutional self-assessment.

Purpose

This article defines how the institution verifies that it follows its own rules. Internal compliance is the practice of systematically checking whether the institution's actual behavior matches its documented policies, procedures, and standards. This is not the same as quality assurance in general; quality assurance asks "is the output good?" while compliance asks "did we follow the process we said we would follow?"

An institution that does not practice internal compliance has no way of knowing whether its documented procedures are being followed, whether its standards are being applied, or whether its policies are being respected. The documentation becomes a fiction -- a description of how things should work, increasingly divorced from how they actually work. Internal compliance closes this gap by making the discrepancy visible and actionable.

This article is addressed to all institutional participants, but particularly to domain leads (who are responsible for compliance within their domain), the QA Architect (who coordinates compliance activities across domains), and the governance body (which sets the policies that compliance checks).

Scope

This article covers:

  • The definition of internal compliance and its distinction from quality assurance.
  • The compliance assessment methodology.
  • Compliance domains -- what categories of institutional rules are subject to compliance checking.
  • Self-assessment procedures for domain leads.
  • Compliance evidence -- what constitutes proof of compliance.
  • Non-compliance handling -- what happens when a rule is not being followed.

This article does not cover:

  • Compliance with external legal or regulatory obligations (the institution is air-gapped and its obligations are internal).
  • Cross-domain audit procedures (see D19-004).
  • Quality standards themselves (see D19-005).
  • The audit methodology in detail (see D19-002).

Background

3.1 Compliance vs. Quality

Quality and compliance are related but distinct. An article might be high-quality (clear, correct, complete) while being non-compliant (it was published without passing through the required review gate). Conversely, an article might be compliant (all procedures were followed) while being low-quality (the review gate failed to catch a significant error). Quality asks "is it good?". Compliance asks "did we follow the rules?"

Both are necessary. Quality without compliance means good outcomes achieved through unreliable processes -- processes that might not produce good outcomes next time. Compliance without quality means reliable processes that produce mediocre outcomes -- which means the processes themselves are flawed or the standards they enforce are too low.

3.2 The Air-Gapped Compliance Context

In a traditional organization, compliance is often driven by external auditors -- regulators, certification bodies, customers. An air-gapped institution has no external auditors. All compliance is self-imposed. This is both a strength and a risk. The strength is that compliance can be designed to serve the institution's actual needs rather than an external framework. The risk is that without external pressure, compliance can atrophy. There is no regulator to catch the institution failing its own standards. The institution must be its own regulator, which requires unusual discipline and honesty.

3.3 Why Self-Assessment

The primary compliance mechanism is self-assessment by domain leads. This is not because self-assessment is the most rigorous approach -- it is not. It is because the institution cannot sustain a full-time compliance function separate from its operational functions. Self-assessment is supplemented by periodic cross-domain audits (D19-004) and by the QA Architect's oversight. The combination provides reasonable coverage without creating a separate compliance bureaucracy.

System Model

4.1 Compliance Domains

Internal compliance is assessed across four categories of institutional rules:

  • Process Compliance. Are documented procedures being followed? Are review gates being used? Are maintenance schedules being maintained? Are defects being tracked?
  • Documentation Compliance. Do published artifacts conform to the article template? Are all required sections present? Are metadata fields populated? Are cross-references accurate?
  • Policy Compliance. Are governance policies being observed? Are authority boundaries being respected? Are ethical principles being upheld in practice?
  • Technical Compliance. Do systems conform to their documented configurations? Are security practices being followed? Are backup procedures being executed?

4.2 Self-Assessment Process

Each domain lead conducts a self-assessment on a defined schedule (see Rules & Constraints). The self-assessment follows this procedure:

  1. Identify applicable rules. For each compliance domain (process, documentation, policy, technical), list the specific rules that apply to the domain. This list comes from the domain's own articles, from universal standards (D19-005), and from governance policies.
  2. Gather evidence. For each rule, identify the evidence that compliance exists. Evidence may include: review gate records, defect registry entries, maintenance log entries, published articles with correct metadata, system configuration records.
  3. Assess compliance. For each rule, determine: Compliant (evidence of compliance exists), Partially Compliant (compliance is inconsistent or incomplete), Non-Compliant (no evidence of compliance or evidence of non-compliance), or Not Applicable (the rule does not apply to this domain with documented justification).
  4. Document findings. Record the assessment in a Compliance Assessment Report. Include: the date, the assessor, the rules assessed, the evidence examined, the findings, and any corrective actions identified.
  5. Act on findings. For each Non-Compliant or Partially Compliant finding, determine the appropriate response: immediate correction, defect report (per D19-008), process revision, or escalation to governance.

4.4 Non-Compliance Response

Non-compliance is not a disciplinary event. It is a diagnostic signal. The response follows this escalation path:

  1. If the non-compliance is easily correctable: Correct it. Log the correction. No further action needed.
  2. If the non-compliance is systemic (multiple instances of the same non-compliance): Determine the root cause. Is the rule impractical? Is there insufficient training? Is there insufficient time? Address the root cause. If the rule is impractical, propose a revision rather than tolerating persistent non-compliance.
  3. If the non-compliance involves a governance policy: Report the finding to the governance body. The governance body determines the appropriate response, which may include policy revision, process improvement, or (in rare cases) remedial action.
  4. If a rule is consistently non-compliant across the institution: The rule itself should be examined. A rule that no one follows is either the wrong rule or is not supported by the institution's resources and culture. It is better to have a weaker rule that is followed than a strong rule that is ignored.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D19-09-01: Annual Self-Assessment. Every domain lead must conduct a compliance self-assessment at least once per year. The assessment covers all four compliance domains (process, documentation, policy, technical) as they apply to the domain.

  • R-D19-09-02: Assessment Reports Must Be Retained. Compliance Assessment Reports are retained as institutional records per Domain 20 requirements. They are available for review by the QA Architect and by governance.

  • R-D19-09-03: Non-Compliance Must Be Addressed. A finding of Non-Compliant must result in either: corrective action, a defect report with a resolution plan, a proposal to revise the rule, or an escalation to governance. A finding of Non-Compliant may not be ignored or indefinitely deferred.

  • R-D19-09-04: Honest Assessment. A self-assessment that finds 100% compliance across all rules should be treated with skepticism and reviewed by the QA Architect. No domain is perfectly compliant. An assessment that claims perfection is likely insufficiently rigorous.

  • R-D19-09-05: Compliance Does Not Equal Quality. Compliance assessment does not replace quality assessment. A domain may be fully compliant and still produce low-quality outputs if the standards themselves are insufficient. Compliance assessment and quality assessment are complementary, not substitutable.

Failure Modes

  • Compliance theater. Self-assessments are conducted as a formality. Findings are superficial. The reports declare compliance without substantive examination. Likelihood: high over time. Severity: high (the institution believes it is compliant when it is not). Detection: QA Architect reviews a sample of self-assessments for depth and rigor. If assessments consistently find no non-compliance, they are likely theatrical.

  • Rule proliferation without compliance capacity. The institution creates more rules than it can realistically assess compliance against. Self-assessments become impossibly long. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: moderate. Detection: measure the time required to conduct a self-assessment. If it exceeds two days for a single domain, the rule set may be too large for meaningful compliance checking.

  • Compliance as punishment. Non-compliance findings are treated as failures rather than as diagnostic information. People become defensive about compliance assessments. Self-assessments become less honest. Likelihood: moderate, especially after leadership changes. Severity: high. Detection: declining honesty in self-assessments (fewer findings, more defensive language, more "Not Applicable" classifications).

  • Divergence between documented and actual practice. The compliance framework checks against documented rules, but actual practice has evolved away from the documentation. The assessment finds compliance because people follow the documentation during assessment periods and revert to actual practice afterward. Likelihood: moderate over long timeframes. Severity: high. Detection: compare assessment-period behavior with normal-period behavior. Random spot-checks outside assessment periods.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If compliance theater has taken hold: Replace self-assessment for one cycle with a cross-domain audit (per D19-004) conducted by a person from a different domain. The external perspective often reveals non-compliance that self-assessment missed. Use the contrast between the two assessments to recalibrate self-assessment rigor. Address the root cause -- often compliance theater develops because the assessment is viewed as busywork rather than useful self-diagnosis.

  2. If rules have proliferated beyond assessment capacity: Prioritize. Not all rules carry equal risk. Identify the rules whose violation would cause the most harm. Ensure compliance assessment covers those rules thoroughly. Assess lower-risk rules on a rotating basis (a subset each year) rather than attempting to assess everything annually.

  3. If compliance has become punitive: Restate the purpose of compliance assessment in a formal communication. Reference D19-001 R-D19-08 (Document Quality Failures Honestly). Implement a "compliance improvement" framing: non-compliance findings are opportunities for improvement, not evidence of failure. If the punitive culture has become entrenched, governance intervention may be needed.

  4. If documented and actual practice have diverged: This is a serious finding. Determine which is correct: the documentation or the practice. If the practice is better, update the documentation. If the documentation is correct, retrain on the documented procedure. In either case, the divergence itself is a failure of the maintenance process and should be recorded.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: Compliance assessment is primarily a self-education exercise. Domain leads learn what rules apply to their domain and how to check compliance. Expect many findings of non-compliance during initial assessments -- this is healthy, not alarming.

  • Years 3-10: Compliance maturity improves. The number of non-compliance findings should decrease as processes are corrected and rules are refined. The focus shifts from catching non-compliance to maintaining compliance and updating rules that have proven impractical.

  • Years 10-25: The compliance framework should be well-established. Succession events will test whether the framework survives personnel changes. Ensure that new domain leads can conduct self-assessments without oral guidance from predecessors.

  • Years 25-50+: The compliance framework itself will need revision. Rules that were appropriate in year 5 may be irrelevant in year 35. The framework must evolve with the institution. Ensure that the revision process for the compliance framework is itself defined and followed.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The most honest thing a self-assessment can say is: "We have this rule. We do not follow it. Here is why." That statement is more valuable than a hundred green checkmarks, because it forces a decision: fix the behavior, fix the rule, or explicitly accept the gap. The institution that tolerates undocumented gaps between its rules and its behavior is an institution that does not know itself. Compliance assessment is, at its core, an exercise in institutional self-knowledge.
