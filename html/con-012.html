CON-012 — Glossary of Foundational Terms

Document ID
CON-012
Domain
1 -- Constitution & Philosophy
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Draft

Purpose

This article provides precise definitions of every foundational term used across Domain 1 and, by extension, across the entire documentation corpus. It exists because language drifts. Words that seem clear today may carry different connotations in a decade and different meanings in fifty years. A reader in 2076 encountering the word "sovereignty" in CON-002 must understand what the institution means by that word, which may or may not align with what the word means in common usage at that time.

This glossary is not a dictionary. It does not provide general definitions. It provides institutional definitions -- what these words mean within the specific context of the holm.chat Documentation Institution. These definitions are authoritative. When a word is used in any institutional document and that word appears in this glossary, the glossary definition governs.

This is a living document. New terms may be added at any time through a simple documentation update. Existing definitions may only be changed through the amendment process defined in CON-011, because changing the definition of a foundational term retroactively changes the meaning of every document that uses it.

Scope

This glossary covers terms that are used across Domain 1 documents and that have specific institutional meanings. It does not cover general technical terms (those should be defined at point of use in the relevant domain articles) or common English words used in their ordinary sense.

A term belongs in this glossary if it meets any of the following criteria:

  • It appears in multiple Domain 1 documents.
  • Its institutional meaning differs from its common English meaning.
  • Its meaning is critical to understanding a foundational principle or policy.
  • A future reader might reasonably misinterpret it without a specific definition.

Background

Every institution that endures beyond its founding generation faces the problem of semantic drift. The United States Constitution uses the phrase "well regulated Militia," which meant something quite different in 1789 than it does today. Legal disputes over the meaning of centuries-old documents are fundamentally disputes over language.

This institution cannot prevent semantic drift in the broader culture. But it can establish a clear, documented baseline of what its own words mean, so that future readers have a reference point. When the culture's meaning of a word diverges from the institution's meaning, the glossary provides the authoritative interpretation.

The glossary also serves an educational function. New operators inheriting the institution may not be familiar with the institution's conceptual vocabulary. The glossary provides a single point of reference for learning the language of the institution, reducing the learning curve and preventing misunderstandings during the critical succession period.

System Model

Terms are organized alphabetically. Each entry includes: the term, the institutional definition, any common misinterpretations to be avoided, and cross-references to the documents that use the term most significantly.

Air Gap. A physical and logical separation between the institution's systems and all external networks, including the public internet. The air gap is not a software firewall or a network policy. It is the absence of any physical connection (wired or wireless) capable of carrying data between the institution's systems and any external system. Limited, controlled, temporary exceptions for data import are governed by Domain 18. See CON-006, SEC-001.

Amendment. A formal, deliberate change to a foundational document, executed through the process defined in CON-011. Distinguished from a "revision" (a change to a non-foundational document through the standard process) and from an "update" (a change to operational content like logs or commentary). An amendment requires a deliberation period, documentation, and formal ratification. See CON-011.

Autonomy. The institution's ability to operate indefinitely without any external service, supply chain, or support structure. Autonomy does not mean the institution never uses external resources -- it means it is always capable of operating without them. Autonomy is a capability, not a practice. See CON-002 (Principle 4).

Black Box. Any system, component, or process whose internal operation is not fully inspectable by the operator. This includes proprietary software without available source code, hardware with undisclosed firmware, systems with insufficient documentation, and processes that produce outputs without explainable reasoning. Black boxes are prohibited in production systems. See CON-010, REF-D03.

Commentary Section. An append-only section in every institutional document reserved for dated observations, interpretations, and reflections by current and future operators. Commentary Sections are never edited retroactively -- only new entries are added. They serve as the institution's living interpretive record. See CON-005.

Deliberation Period. A mandatory waiting period between the proposal and ratification of a foundational amendment. The length varies by amendment tier: 90 days for Tier 3, 180 days for Tier 2, 365 days for refounding. The deliberation period cannot be shortened. See CON-011.

Durability. The institution's design target of operating for a minimum of fifty years. Durability is achieved not through stasis but through replaceability -- every component is designed to be replaced without loss of data, capability, or institutional knowledge. See CON-002 (Principle 2).

Foundational Document. Any document subject to the amendment process defined in CON-011. All Domain 1 documents and ETH-001 are foundational by default. Other documents may be designated as foundational through the governance process. See CON-011.

Identity (Institutional). The continuity of purpose, principles, knowledge, and practice that makes the institution recognizably itself over time. Institutional identity is defined by four elements, detailed in CON-004. Identity can be intact, strained, or broken. See CON-004.

Institution. The holm.chat Documentation Institution in its totality: its hardware, software, data, documentation, principles, procedures, and the relationships between them. The institution is more than any individual component. It is the organized whole. See CON-001.

Integrity. The property of being truthful and consistent. Applied to data: the data is what it claims to be, has not been corrupted, and accurately represents what it was intended to represent. Applied to documentation: the documentation describes reality as it actually is, not as it was planned or as it once was. Applied to the institution: the institution's practices match its documented principles. See CON-002 (Principle 3), ETH-001.

Isolation. The state of operating without connection to external networks or services. Distinguished from air gap (which is the physical implementation) by its emphasis on the operational and philosophical aspects. Isolation is the policy; the air gap is the mechanism. See CON-006.

Knowledge. Information combined with understanding -- the ability to use information correctly, to know its limits, and to recognize when it applies and when it does not. Distinguished from data (raw symbols) and information (data with context). See CON-005.

Migration. The process of moving from one technology, format, or system to another. All technologies in the institution must have a documented migration plan. Migration is expected and normal, not exceptional. See CON-010.

Operator. The person currently responsible for the institution's operation and maintenance. The operator has both rights and obligations as defined in CON-007. At any given time, there is one operator. See CON-007.

Principle. A foundational commitment that constrains institutional decisions. Principles are organized into three tiers (Existential, Architectural, Operational) and may only be changed through the amendment process. Distinguished from a "rule" (a specific binding requirement derived from a principle) and from a "guideline" (a recommendation that does not carry binding force). See CON-002.

Refounding. The process of changing a Tier 1 (Existential) principle. Refounding is not an amendment; it is a reconstitution of the institution's fundamental identity. It requires a 365-day deliberation period and an identity continuity assessment. See CON-011.

Refusal. A documented commitment not to do something, maintained in the Refusal Registry (CON-003). Refusals carry a default answer of "no" and can only be overridden through the process defined in CON-003 and CON-011. See CON-003.

Sovereignty. The operator's absolute right and practical ability to control their own digital life: to decide what data exists, where it resides, who can access it, and when it is destroyed. Sovereignty is the institution's highest principle and the reason for its existence. It is not merely privacy or security; it encompasses total authority over all institutional systems and data. See CON-002 (Principle 1).

Stewardship. The relationship between the operator and the institution, characterized by care, responsibility, and non-possessive commitment. The operator is a steward, not an owner. See CON-007.

Succession. The transfer of operational responsibility from one operator to another. Succession may be planned (with a multi-year timeline) or unplanned (due to incapacitation or death). See CON-008.

Tacit Knowledge. Knowledge that exists in the operator's mind but is not documented. Tacit knowledge is valuable but fragile and does not survive succession. The institution systematically converts tacit knowledge to documented knowledge through Commentary Sections, decision logs, and the tacit knowledge capture process. See CON-005.

Transparency. The property of being fully understandable by the operator. Every system must be documented thoroughly enough, and designed clearly enough, that the operator can understand what it does and why. Transparency is internal -- the institution is transparent to its operator and opaque to outsiders. See CON-002 (Principle 6).

Rules and Constraints

  • R-CON-012-01: When a term defined in this glossary is used in any institutional document, the glossary definition governs. If an author intends a different meaning, they must define the term explicitly at point of use and explain the departure from the glossary definition.
  • R-CON-012-02: New terms may be added to the glossary through the standard documentation revision process. No deliberation period is required for additions.
  • R-CON-012-03: Existing definitions may only be changed through the amendment process defined in CON-011, at the tier appropriate to the term's importance. Definitions of terms that appear in Tier 1 principles require Tier 2 amendment. Definitions of terms that appear only in Tier 3 principles or operational documents require Tier 3 amendment.
  • R-CON-012-04: Every new Domain 1 document must be reviewed against this glossary before ratification to ensure that all foundational terms are used consistently with their glossary definitions.
  • R-CON-012-05: The glossary must be reviewed annually for completeness. If new terms have entered institutional usage that are not yet defined in the glossary, they should be added.

Failure Modes

Semantic drift despite the glossary. The glossary definitions exist but are not consulted. Authors write using their own understanding of terms, which may differ from the institutional definition. Over time, the same word means different things in different documents. Mitigation: the review process (R-CON-012-04) should catch inconsistencies. The annual glossary review (R-CON-012-05) should identify terms whose usage has drifted from their definitions.

Glossary staleness. New concepts enter institutional discourse but are never defined in the glossary. The glossary covers founding-era terms but not the vocabulary of later generations. Mitigation: the annual review and the low barrier for adding new terms.

Over-definition. The glossary becomes so comprehensive that it includes common words used in their ordinary sense. This dilutes the glossary's authority and makes it harder to find genuinely institutional definitions. Mitigation: the inclusion criteria in the Scope section. Terms belong in the glossary only if they meet at least one of the specified criteria.

Definition ossification. A definition that was clear at founding becomes opaque to future readers because the explanatory context has shifted. The words in the definition are correct but no longer communicative. Mitigation: definitions should be written in the plainest possible language, avoiding idioms and culturally specific references. The Commentary Section can be used to add contemporary explanations without changing the formal definition.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If a term has been used inconsistently across documents: Determine which usage matches the glossary definition. Flag documents that deviate. Revise the deviating documents to match the glossary definition, or, if the deviating usage is actually better, propose an amendment to the glossary definition through the appropriate process.
  2. If a term is missing from the glossary: Add it. This is a simple addition that does not require the amendment process. Draft a clear, institution-specific definition. Review existing documents to ensure the new definition is consistent with how the term has been used.
  3. If a definition has become unclear to a successor: The successor should add a Commentary entry to this article explaining their difficulty and requesting clarification. They should also propose a revised definition through the amendment process if they believe the current definition is genuinely unclear.

Evolution Path

Years 0-5: The glossary is established with the founding vocabulary. New terms are added frequently as the institutional vocabulary develops. The annual review identifies terms that were missed in the initial drafting.

Years 5-15: The core vocabulary stabilizes. New terms are added less frequently but the existing definitions may need refinement based on operational experience. The glossary becomes a reference tool for cross-domain consistency.

Years 15-30: The glossary becomes critical during succession. A new operator's first reading of the glossary may be their primary introduction to the institutional vocabulary. The definitions must be clear enough to serve this educational function.

Years 30-50+: Some founding-era terms may have become archaic. The glossary must bridge the gap between the language of the founding documents and the language of current operators. This bridging function -- helping modern readers understand historical documents -- becomes increasingly important with time.
