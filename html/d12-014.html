D12-014 — Communication and Coordination During Disasters

Document ID
D12-014
Domain
12 -- Disaster Recovery
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D12-001, D12-004, D12-011, GOV-001, GOV-004, SEC-001, ETH-001
Depended Upon By
D12-002 (all drill scenarios benefit from communication protocols), D12-009 (facility loss coordination), D12-010 (successor coordination during personnel loss).

Purpose

This article defines how the institution communicates and coordinates during a disaster, when normal channels and tools may be unavailable. In an air-gapped, typically single-operator institution, "communication" may seem like a non-issue -- who is there to communicate with? But the reality is more nuanced. Even a sole operator must communicate: with their own future self (through logging and documentation), with potential successors (through the Succession Readiness Package), with external parties (if assistance is needed for hardware procurement or facility matters), and with the institution's own documentation (which must be accessible during a crisis when the normal access methods may be impaired).

This article also addresses the multi-person scenarios that arise during succession events, when a departing operator must coordinate with an incoming successor, or when the operator enlists trusted assistance for complex recovery tasks. And it addresses the self-communication discipline that is essential for effective solo incident response: the practice of narrating the recovery to oneself through real-time logging, which prevents decision drift, provides an audit trail, and forces the operator to think before acting.

The air-gapped nature of the institution means that during a disaster, the operator cannot search the internet for solutions, email a colleague for advice, or call a vendor for support. All coordination must use channels and resources that are available without network connectivity. This article defines what those channels are and how to use them.

Scope

In scope:

  • Real-time incident logging: the practice of documenting the crisis as it unfolds.
  • Decision documentation during crisis: how to record decisions made under pressure.
  • Communication with external parties during disasters (hardware procurement, facility assistance).
  • Coordination between operator and successor during transition crises.
  • Access to institutional documentation when normal access methods are unavailable.
  • Communication security during disasters (maintaining the air gap even under pressure).

Out of scope:

  • Normal operational communication (routine logging per D10-002).
  • Governance authority during crises (GOV-001 decision tiers apply).
  • Succession planning details (GOV-004).

Background

3.1 The Cognitive Load Problem

During a crisis, the operator's cognitive load is at its peak. They are simultaneously diagnosing the problem, executing recovery procedures, managing their emotional state, and making decisions under uncertainty. Adding communication tasks to this load may seem like an unwelcome burden. But real-time logging -- the primary communication activity during a crisis -- actually reduces cognitive load rather than increasing it. The act of writing down what is happening, what has been tried, and what will be attempted next externalizes the operator's working memory. It prevents the operator from losing track of where they are in a complex procedure. It creates a record that the operator can consult if they need to backtrack. And it produces the raw material for the post-incident review (D12-013).

3.2 The Paper Fallback

During a severe disaster, the institution's computer systems may be offline. The documentation corpus may be inaccessible. The operational logging system may be unavailable. In these conditions, communication reverts to its most basic form: pen and paper. The operator writes by hand in a physical notebook. This is not elegant, but it is resilient. Paper does not require electricity, does not crash, and does not depend on a functioning storage system.

The rebuild kit (D12-003) includes printed copies of the most critical procedures. This article extends that principle: the operator should maintain a physical incident log notebook at the primary facility, pre-labeled and ready for use, so that if the digital logging system is unavailable, real-time logging can continue without interruption.

3.3 Communication Security During Disasters

The pressure of a disaster creates a temptation to break the air gap. The operator encounters a problem they cannot solve from the institution's documentation. The answer is almost certainly available on the internet. Connecting a device to the network "just for a moment" to look up a solution seems harmless. It is not. SEC-001 R-SEC-01 establishes the air gap as absolute. A disaster does not suspend the security model. If the air gap is breached during a crisis, the operator must follow the quarantine procedures in Domain 18 for any device that was connected, adding complexity to an already complex situation.

This article provides alternatives to breaking the air gap: maintaining a local reference library, including troubleshooting resources in the rebuild kit, and designing recovery procedures that do not require external information.

System Model

4.1 Real-Time Incident Logging

When an incident occurs, the operator should begin logging immediately. The log captures the evolving state of the crisis and the operator's response. The format is deliberately simple to minimize overhead:

INCIDENT LOG

Date: [date]
Incident Type: [brief description]
Start Time: [time incident was detected]

[HH:MM] [entry]
[HH:MM] [entry]
...

Each log entry should include:

  • What was observed or discovered.
  • What action was taken (or decided not to take, and why).
  • What the result of the action was.
  • What the next planned action is.

Example entries:

14:32  System failed to boot after power cycle. No POST beep.
       Suspect: PSU or motherboard. Will test PSU first.
14:45  Swapped PSU with spare. System still does not POST.
       PSU is not the problem. Suspect motherboard.
14:52  Removed all peripherals and non-essential cards.
       Tested with single RAM stick in slot 1.
       System POSTs with single RAM. Suspect bad RAM in slot 2.
15:05  Confirmed: system stable with RAM module from slot 2 removed.
       Module in slot 2 has failed.
       Installing spare RAM module.
15:15  System boots normally with spare RAM.
       Running memtest to verify. Will monitor for 1 hour.

If the digital logging system is unavailable, use the physical incident log notebook.

4.2 Crisis Decision Documentation

Decisions made during a crisis must be recorded even more carefully than routine decisions because they are made under stress with incomplete information. For each significant decision during an incident:

  1. State the decision. What are you choosing to do?
  2. State the alternatives. What else could you do?
  3. State the reasoning. Why this option over the alternatives?
  4. State the risk. What could go wrong with this decision?
  5. Record the outcome (after the decision is executed).

This level of documentation may seem excessive during a crisis. It is not. It takes sixty seconds to write and prevents hours of confusion later if the decision needs to be revisited. It also provides essential material for the post-incident review (D12-013).

4.3 Documentation Access During Disasters

The institution's documentation must be accessible during a disaster, even if the primary system is offline. The access hierarchy is:

  1. Primary access: Normal system is operational. Documentation is accessed through the standard interface.
  2. Secondary access: Primary system is down, but a secondary device (spare machine, laptop) is available. The Location A or Location B backup can be mounted on the secondary device to access documentation.
  3. Tertiary access: No computing device is available. Printed documentation in the rebuild kit (D12-003) provides the critical recovery procedures.

The rebuild kit must include printed copies of at minimum: D12-003 (System Rebuild), D12-007 (Hardware Failure Recovery), D12-009 (Facility Loss), and this article (D12-014). These printed copies ensure that the most critical disaster recovery procedures are available even in the worst scenario.

4.4 Communication with External Parties

During a disaster, the operator may need to communicate with external parties for:

  • Hardware procurement: Purchasing replacement parts. This communication occurs outside the air gap using personal devices and does not compromise institutional security. The operator should not disclose the nature or contents of the institution to vendors -- the purchase is simply for computer hardware.
  • Facility matters: If the facility is damaged, the operator may need to coordinate with contractors, insurance providers, or building management. Maintain the principle of minimal disclosure: the institution is a personal computing setup, not something that requires detailed explanation.
  • Succession contacts: If the operator is incapacitated or anticipates incapacitation, the designated successor (GOV-004) must be contacted. The Succession Readiness Package should include contact information for reaching the successor through non-digital means (physical mail, trusted intermediary) if necessary.

4.5 Coordination During Succession Crises

When a succession event occurs during or as a result of a disaster, clear coordination between the departing operator (if available) and the incoming successor is critical:

  • If the departing operator is available: Prioritize knowledge transfer for the immediate crisis. What is broken? What has been tried? Where are the backups? Where are the keys? The operator should provide this information in writing (not just verbally) to create a permanent record.
  • If the departing operator is not available: The successor follows GOV-004 Stage 3 procedures, using the Succession Readiness Package as their primary guide. The real-time incident log (if the operator was able to maintain one before becoming unavailable) provides critical context.

4.6 Self-Communication Discipline

For a solo operator, the most important communication partner during a crisis is their own future self -- the version of them that will exist in two hours, tomorrow, or next week, and will need to understand what happened and why. Self-communication discipline means:

  • Narrate before acting. Before executing a recovery step, write down what you are about to do and why. If the step goes wrong, the log explains the intent.
  • Document deviations. If you deviate from a documented procedure, explain why in the log. The procedure may be wrong, or the situation may be unusual. Either way, the deviation and its rationale should be recorded.
  • Pause at checkpoints. At major recovery milestones, stop and write a status summary: where am I in the recovery, what is the current state of the system, what remains to be done. This summary helps if the recovery is interrupted (operator needs to sleep, another problem arises) and resumed later.
  • Be honest about uncertainty. If you are not sure whether an action worked, say so in the log. "I think the restore completed successfully, but I have not verified the checksums yet" is more useful than "Restore complete" without qualification.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D12-14-01: Real-time incident logging must begin as soon as an incident is recognized and continue until the incident is resolved. If digital logging is unavailable, use the physical incident log notebook.
  • R-D12-14-02: The air gap must not be breached during a disaster. SEC-001 R-SEC-01 is not suspended during crises. If external information is needed, use the local reference library or the printed documentation in the rebuild kit.
  • R-D12-14-03: Printed copies of the critical disaster recovery articles (D12-003, D12-007, D12-009, D12-014) must be maintained in the rebuild kit and at the primary facility. These copies must be updated when the articles are revised.
  • R-D12-14-04: A physical incident log notebook (blank, pre-labeled, with a pen) must be maintained at the primary facility in a known location, accessible without the primary system being operational.
  • R-D12-14-05: Communication with external parties during disasters must follow the minimal disclosure principle: reveal no more about the institution than necessary for the specific task at hand.

Failure Modes

  • Logging abandoned under pressure. The operator stops logging because the crisis demands all their attention. The post-incident review is impoverished. Mitigation: the logging format is deliberately minimal (timestamp + one line). Even abbreviated entries are valuable. If logging is truly impossible in the moment, resume as soon as possible and reconstruct the gap from memory.
  • Air-gap breach under pressure. The operator connects to the internet "just to look up one thing." The security model is compromised. Mitigation: R-D12-14-02 is absolute. The rebuild kit and local reference library provide alternatives. If a problem truly cannot be solved from local resources, the operator should complete the immediate crisis, then separately address the knowledge gap through the quarantined import process (Domain 18).
  • Printed documentation outdated. The printed recovery procedures in the rebuild kit reference an old system configuration. The operator follows outdated steps, causing confusion or errors. Mitigation: R-D12-14-03 requires updating printed copies when articles are revised. The annual rebuild kit verification (D12-003 R-D12-03-02) includes checking printed documentation currency.
  • No physical notebook available. The incident log notebook was used up, lost, or never established. Mitigation: R-D12-14-04. Any paper will do in an emergency -- the notebook is a convenience, not a requirement. The discipline of logging matters more than the medium.
  • Successor cannot be reached. The operator needs to invoke succession but cannot reach the designated successor. Mitigation: GOV-004 should include multiple contact methods and a secondary successor designation.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If the incident log was not maintained during a crisis: Immediately after the crisis is resolved, reconstruct the log from memory, system logs, and file timestamps. Write down everything you remember, in order, with approximate timestamps. This reconstructed log is less accurate than a real-time log but far better than no log at all. It will serve as the basis for the post-incident review (D12-013).
  2. If printed documentation is unavailable during a crisis: If the digital documentation is also unavailable, the operator must work from memory and first principles. After the crisis, document what was done from memory and update the printed documentation cache. Assess why the printed documentation was unavailable and correct the gap.
  3. If the air gap was breached during a crisis: After the crisis is resolved, immediately quarantine the device that was connected per Domain 18 procedures. Assess whether any institutional data was exposed. Document the breach and the reasons for it. Update the local reference library or rebuild kit to include the information that was missing, so the breach need not be repeated.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Establish the incident logging practice and the physical notebook. Build the local reference library with troubleshooting resources for the institution's specific hardware and software. The first real incident will test whether the communication protocols work under actual stress.
  • Years 5-15: The incident log history accumulates. The reference library expands based on actual information needs encountered during incidents and drills. If a succession event occurs, the communication protocols between operator and successor are tested for real.
  • Years 15-30: Communication protocols may need to adapt to new personnel configurations (multiple operators, remote successors, etc.). The principles -- real-time logging, paper fallback, air-gap discipline, minimal disclosure -- remain constant.
  • Years 30-50+: The accumulated incident logs from decades of operation are a unique historical record of how the institution navigated crises. They inform not just technical improvements but the institutional culture of crisis response.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The self-communication discipline described in Section 4.6 comes from my professional experience with incident response. The most effective incident responders I have observed share one habit: they narrate what they are doing, either aloud or in writing. They say "I am going to check the backup integrity before restoring" before they do it. This narration serves as a circuit breaker against hasty action -- the act of stating the plan creates a moment of reflection before execution.

In a solo-operator institution, there is no one to narrate to. The incident log serves as the listener. Writing "I am about to overwrite the primary volume with the backup" before doing it forces the operator to confront the action explicitly. If the action is wrong -- if they are about to overwrite good data with bad -- the act of writing it down may trigger the recognition that the act of just doing it would not.

The air-gap discipline during crises is the hardest rule in this article to follow. When the system is down and you do not know why, the internet is an almost irresistible temptation. I am building the local reference library specifically to reduce this temptation: if the answer is available locally, the temptation to go online diminishes. The answer will not always be available locally. In those cases, the operator must accept a slower, harder recovery path rather than compromise the security model. The security model protects the institution for fifty years. The internet search saves thirty minutes. The math is clear, even if the emotional calculus in the moment is not.
