D9-009 — Training Material Standards

Document ID
D9-009
Domain
9 -- Education & Training
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D9-001, D9-003, D6-001, D6-003
Depended Upon By
D9-006 (self-directed learning requires well-structured materials), D9-013 (material standards extend to documentation design), D6 (training materials stored per D6 policy)

Purpose

This article defines the format, structure, and quality requirements for all training materials produced within or for the holm.chat Documentation Institution. Training materials are a distinct category of institutional artifact: they exist not merely to record information (the function of documentation) but to transfer competence (the function of education). This distinction matters because a document that records a procedure accurately may still fail to teach someone how to perform that procedure. Recording and teaching are different acts, and they require different standards.

The institution's educational capability depends on the quality and durability of its training materials. D9-001 establishes that the documentation system is the primary educational vehicle. This article specifies what that means in practice: what formats are permitted, what structural elements every training material must contain, how materials are reviewed for pedagogical effectiveness, and how they are maintained over the institution's fifty-year horizon.

A training material that cannot be opened is useless. A training material in a proprietary format that requires specific software is a ticking time bomb -- it will become inaccessible when that software is no longer available. A training material without learning objectives is a collection of information rather than a pathway to competence. This article prevents these failures by establishing clear, enforceable standards that align with the institution's archival requirements (D6-003) and educational philosophy (D9-001).

Scope

This article covers:

  • The classification of training material types (written guides, worked examples, video/audio recordings, practice exercises, assessment instruments).
  • Format requirements for each material type, aligned with D6-003 format longevity doctrine.
  • Structural templates for written training materials.
  • Quality criteria for all training materials.
  • The review process for training materials.
  • Maintenance and version control of training materials.
  • Storage and archival requirements, aligned with D6-001 data tier classification.

This article does not cover:

  • The content of specific training curricula (see D9-003).
  • Assessment design methodology (see D9-004).
  • Tacit knowledge capture methods, though the artifacts produced by those methods are subject to the format standards defined here (see D9-007).
  • The documentation system's general standards (see the Meta-Framework), though training materials must comply with those standards in addition to the ones defined here.

Background

3.1 The Longevity Problem

Training materials must remain accessible and usable for decades. The institution's fifty-year horizon means that a training video recorded today must be viewable in 2076. A written guide created today must be readable in 2076. This is not a speculative concern -- it is the central design constraint. Formats that are popular today may be obsolete in twenty years. Software that plays those formats may not exist. The hardware that runs that software may not exist.

The D6-003 Format Longevity Doctrine addresses this problem for all institutional data. This article applies those principles specifically to training materials, with additional attention to the pedagogical dimensions: a training video that has been transcoded to a future format may lose quality that was essential to its educational function (resolution of text on screen, clarity of audio narration, timing of visual demonstrations).

3.2 The Completeness Standard

A training material is complete when it contains everything a learner needs to achieve the stated learning objective without external assistance. This is the self-teaching requirement (D9-001, R-D9-01) applied at the material level. "Without external assistance" means: without a live instructor, without undocumented prior knowledge, and without reference to materials that are not explicitly listed as prerequisites.

Completeness is difficult to achieve and easy to overestimate. The author of a training material already knows the subject. Steps that seem obvious to the author are invisible in the material. Assumptions about prior knowledge go unstated because the author does not realize they are assumptions. The completeness standard is enforced through the solo-learner review process defined in Section 6 of this article.

3.3 Material Types

The institution recognizes five types of training material, each suited to different educational purposes:

  • Written Guides: Step-by-step textual instructions for performing a procedure or understanding a concept. The most durable and most universally accessible material type. The backbone of the institution's training capability.
  • Worked Examples: Detailed walkthroughs of specific real or realistic scenarios, showing each decision point and the reasoning behind each choice. More contextual than abstract guides. Essential for developing judgment.
  • Video/Audio Recordings: Narrated demonstrations, recorded lectures, structured interviews. Valuable for preserving tacit knowledge and the experiential dimension of procedures (the "feel" of a system operating normally versus abnormally). Less durable than text but higher-bandwidth for certain types of knowledge.
  • Practice Exercises: Structured activities that require the learner to perform a function or make a decision, with defined expected outcomes and feedback mechanisms. The bridge between reading and doing.
  • Assessment Instruments: Tests, practical demonstrations, oral examination frameworks, and portfolio review criteria used to evaluate competence. Defined in detail in D9-004 but subject to the material standards in this article.

System Model

4.1 Format Requirements

All training materials must comply with D6-003 format longevity requirements. The following table specifies approved formats by material type:

Material Type Approved Formats Rationale
Written Guides Plain text (.txt), Markdown (.md), HTML (.html) Open formats, human-readable without specialized software, universally supported.
Worked Examples Plain text, Markdown, HTML Same rationale as written guides. May include inline images in PNG or JPEG format.
Video Recordings MP4 (H.264 video, AAC audio), WebM (VP9 video, Opus audio) Open or widely-supported codecs with broad hardware decoder availability. Two formats for redundancy.
Audio Recordings FLAC (.flac), Opus (.opus), WAV (.wav) Open, lossless or high-quality lossy formats. WAV for maximum compatibility; FLAC for storage efficiency.
Images/Diagrams PNG (.png), SVG (.svg), JPEG (.jpg) PNG for screenshots and diagrams. SVG for scalable vector diagrams. JPEG for photographs where lossless is unnecessary.
Practice Exercises Plain text, Markdown, HTML Exercise descriptions are text. Any associated files (sample data, test scenarios) follow their native format requirements.
Assessment Instruments Plain text, Markdown, HTML Assessment criteria and rubrics are text documents.

No training material may be created in or depend upon a proprietary format. This means no Microsoft Office formats (.docx, .pptx), no Apple-specific formats (.pages, .keynote), no DRM-protected media, and no cloud-only formats that require an internet connection or active account to access.

4.2 Structural Template for Written Training Materials

Every written training material (guide, worked example, or exercise) must contain the following structural elements:

  1. Title and Identifier. A clear, descriptive title and a unique identifier that links the material to its parent curriculum (D9-003) and domain.
  2. Learning Objectives. A numbered list of specific, measurable outcomes the learner should achieve after completing the material. Each objective must begin with an action verb (explain, demonstrate, perform, identify, evaluate). Vague objectives such as "understand the system" are not acceptable.
  3. Prerequisites. An explicit list of what the learner must already know or be able to do before beginning this material. Prerequisites must reference specific documents, prior training materials, or competency levels.
  4. Estimated Duration. A realistic estimate of the time required to complete the material, including any practice time. This helps learners plan and helps the institution schedule training.
  5. Instructional Content. The body of the material. For guides, this is step-by-step instruction. For worked examples, this is the narrative walkthrough. For exercises, this is the task description and expected outcomes.
  6. Key Concepts Summary. A brief recapitulation of the most important points. This serves as both a review aid and a quick reference for returning learners.
  7. Self-Check Questions. Three to five questions that the learner can use to verify their own comprehension. Answers are provided (at the end of the material, separated from the questions) so that self-directed learners can assess themselves.
  8. Next Steps. Guidance on what to study or practice next. Links to related materials, the next material in the curriculum sequence, or assessment opportunities.
  9. Version and Date. The material's version number and date of last revision. This allows learners to verify they are using current materials.
  10. Author and Reviewer. The name or role of the person who created the material and the person who reviewed it for pedagogical quality.

4.3 Quality Criteria

Training materials are evaluated against five quality dimensions:

  • Accuracy: The material correctly describes the current state of the system, procedure, or concept it teaches. Outdated materials are worse than no materials, because they teach the learner something that is wrong.
  • Completeness: The material includes every step, prerequisite, and piece of context necessary for the learner to achieve the stated learning objectives. No step is assumed. No context is omitted because it seems "obvious."
  • Clarity: The material is written in plain language appropriate to the intended audience. Jargon is defined on first use. Sentences are direct. Instructions are unambiguous. A learner following the material should never encounter a step where they do not know what to do.
  • Pedagogy: The material employs sound educational techniques: it moves from simple to complex, from concrete to abstract. It provides examples. It explains not just what to do but why. It includes opportunities for the learner to check their understanding.
  • Durability: The material is in an approved format, avoids references to ephemeral technologies or tools, and is structured so that updates to specific sections do not require rewriting the entire document.

4.4 The Training Material Registry

All training materials are registered in a central inventory that records: the material's identifier, title, type, parent curriculum, current version, date of last review, assigned reviewer, and storage location. The registry is a plain-text or HTML document stored alongside the materials themselves. It serves as a table of contents for the institution's entire training library and is the primary tool for identifying materials that need updating.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D9-09-01: All training materials must be in formats approved by D6-003 and specified in the format table in Section 4.1. No exceptions. If a desired format is not on the approved list, a formal request to add it must be made through the governance process (GOV-001) with a format longevity assessment.
  • R-D9-09-02: All written training materials must conform to the structural template defined in Section 4.2. Materials that omit required elements are incomplete and may not be classified as ready for use.
  • R-D9-09-03: Training materials are Tier 1 data per D6-001 and D9-001 (R-D9-08). They receive the highest level of preservation: multiple backup copies, integrity verification, and migration priority during format transitions.
  • R-D9-09-04: Every training material must undergo a solo-learner review before being classified as ready for use. The solo-learner review is performed by a person who does not already possess the competence the material teaches, working through the material without assistance and documenting every point of confusion, missing step, or ambiguity. This review is the primary quality gate.
  • R-D9-09-05: Training materials must be reviewed for accuracy at least every twenty-four months, or whenever the system, procedure, or concept they teach is modified. A material that has not been reviewed within its review cycle is flagged as potentially outdated in the registry.
  • R-D9-09-06: Video and audio training materials must include a text transcript or text-based companion guide that covers the same content. This serves three purposes: accessibility, searchability, and format resilience (the text survives even if the media format becomes unplayable).
  • R-D9-09-07: Training materials must not depend on external resources (internet links, cloud services, third-party applications). All resources necessary to complete the material must be stored within the institution's archival system.
  • R-D9-09-08: The training material registry must be updated whenever a material is created, revised, retired, or reassigned. The registry is reviewed during the annual cross-training audit (D9-008) and during the biannual documentation quality audit (D19-002).

Failure Modes

  • Format obsolescence. A training video recorded in a format that becomes unplayable. The knowledge it contained is lost unless a text companion exists. Mitigation: R-D9-09-06 requires text companions for all media. D6-003 format migration procedures apply. Dual-format storage (two approved video formats) provides additional resilience.

  • Accuracy decay. A system changes but the training material describing it is not updated. A learner follows the outdated material and either fails to complete the procedure or completes it incorrectly. Mitigation: R-D9-09-05 mandates regular review. Triggered maintenance occurs whenever source systems change. The registry flags materials past their review date.

  • The expert-blindness problem. A training material is written by the most knowledgeable person, who unconsciously omits steps and context that seem obvious to them. The material is complete from the expert's perspective but incomprehensible to a novice. Mitigation: R-D9-09-04 requires solo-learner review by someone who does not already have the competence. This catches expert-blind gaps.

  • Material proliferation without curation. Over time, materials accumulate: old versions, superseded guides, redundant exercises. A learner cannot tell which material is current. Mitigation: the registry (Section 4.4) is the authoritative index. Materials not in the registry are not official. Retired materials are marked as retired, not deleted (they may have historical value), but they are clearly labeled as superseded.

  • The text-only trap. The institution creates abundant written materials but no demonstrations, worked examples, or practice exercises. Written text alone cannot teach every skill -- some skills require seeing a procedure performed or practicing it in a safe environment. Mitigation: D9-003 curriculum design specifies material types for each learning objective. The curriculum review verifies that the appropriate mix of material types exists.

  • Dependency on a single authoring tool. All materials are created with a specific text editor, screen recorder, or diagram tool. When that tool becomes unavailable, creating new materials or editing existing ones becomes difficult. Mitigation: R-D9-09-01 and R-D9-09-07 ensure that all materials are in open formats. The authoring tool does not matter as long as the output is in an approved format.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If a training material is found to be inaccurate: Immediately mark it as "Under Review" in the registry. Do not allow learners to use it until it is corrected. Identify the specific inaccuracies. Revise the material. Conduct a focused review of the revised sections. Update the registry with the new version and review date. If learners have recently completed the inaccurate material, notify them of the corrections and assess whether re-training is necessary.

  2. If a media format becomes unplayable: Activate D6-003 format migration procedures. Transcode to a current approved format. Verify that the transcoded version retains educational quality (visual clarity, audio clarity, timing). Update the registry. If transcoding quality is insufficient, consider recreating the material. The text companion (R-D9-09-06) provides continuity while migration is in progress.

  3. If the solo-learner review reveals fundamental pedagogical problems: The material is not ready for use. Return it to the author with specific feedback from the solo-learner review. The author revises. A second solo-learner review is conducted. This cycle repeats until the material passes. Do not deploy materials that have not passed solo-learner review -- the cost of bad training is higher than the cost of delayed training.

  4. If the training material registry is lost or corrupted: Rebuild it from the materials themselves. Each material contains its own identifier, version, and date (per the structural template). The rebuild is labor-intensive but possible because the materials are self-describing. This is one reason the structural template requires metadata in each material rather than relying solely on the registry.

  5. If a required material type is missing for a curriculum topic: Prioritize creation based on the criticality of the associated function. Tier A functions receive immediate attention. Use the structural template to draft the material. Conduct solo-learner review. Register and deploy. Track the gap in the registry as a remediation item until it is closed.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The training material library is being built from scratch. The focus is on creating materials for Tier A and Tier B functions. Written guides are the priority because they are the most durable and the fastest to produce. Video and audio materials are created opportunistically, primarily through the tacit knowledge capture processes of D9-007. The structural template is applied from the beginning, but the solo-learner review may be deferred for materials where no second person is available -- in that case, the founding operator performs a "future learner" review by attempting to follow their own materials as if encountering them for the first time after a long gap.

  • Years 5-15: The first real learners encounter the materials. This is the most valuable period for quality improvement. Every confusion, every missing step, every false assumption discovered by a real learner is a data point for revision. The materials should undergo significant revision based on actual use. The solo-learner review process should now involve actual newcomers, not just the author role-playing as a newcomer.

  • Years 15-30: The material library should be comprehensive and well-tested. The focus shifts from creation to maintenance: keeping materials accurate as systems evolve, migrating formats as needed, and curating the library to prevent bloat. The registry becomes an increasingly important navigation tool as the library grows.

  • Years 30-50+: Some of the original materials may be historical artifacts rather than current training tools. The institution must distinguish between current materials (actively used for training), archival materials (preserved for historical context and institutional memory), and retired materials (superseded but retained). Format migration may have occurred multiple times. The structural template and quality criteria defined in this article should remain recognizable, even if specific format choices have evolved with technology.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The hardest part of this article is the solo-learner review requirement (R-D9-09-04). It is the most important quality gate in the entire training material pipeline, and it is also the one I cannot properly execute right now. I cannot review my own materials as a naive learner because I am not naive about these systems. I built them. I know things that are not in the materials, and I cannot reliably identify which things those are.

The workaround I am using is temporal distance: I write a material, wait at least thirty days, then attempt to follow it as written. The gap in time provides a partial simulation of naivety -- I forget details, I lose context, and the material must stand on its own. It is not as good as a genuine naive review, but it is better than nothing.

The format restrictions will feel constraining to anyone accustomed to modern authoring tools. I could produce much more polished training materials using presentation software, interactive web applications, or video editing suites. But "polished" is not the same as "durable." A beautifully formatted Keynote presentation is useless if the only computer that can open it has been recycled. A plain text file with clear structure will be readable on any computer built in the next fifty years. That is the trade-off this article makes, and it is the right one.
