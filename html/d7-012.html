D7-012 — Intelligence Failure: Detection, Response, Learning

Document ID
D7-012
Domain
7 -- Intelligence & Analysis
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D7-001, D7-002, D7-004, D7-005, D7-006, D7-008, D7-010, D7-011, ETH-001, GOV-001, OPS-001
Depended Upon By
D7-013, D10-003. All Domain 7 articles benefit from lessons learned through failure analysis.

Purpose

This article defines how the institution recognizes when its intelligence function has failed, how it responds to that failure, and how it learns from the failure to prevent recurrence. Intelligence failure is not a question of whether -- it is a question of when, and how badly, and what happens next. Any intelligence function that operates over decades will make errors. The measure of the function is not whether errors occur but whether they are detected, honestly examined, and used to improve.

The intelligence philosophy in D7-001 establishes the posture of structured doubt. This article operationalizes that posture for the worst case: the moment when the institution's analysis was wrong, incomplete, or misleading, and the institution suffered for it. The natural human response to this moment is defensive -- to explain away the error, blame external circumstances, or minimize the failure's significance. This article demands the opposite: honest, structured examination of what went wrong, why, and what must change.

Intelligence failure in a single-analyst institution is uniquely painful because the analyst must conduct the post-mortem on their own work. There is no external review board, no inspector general, no colleague who can deliver hard truths. The analyst must find their own errors, diagnose their own biases, and prescribe their own corrections. This article provides the structure that makes this difficult self-examination possible and productive.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The taxonomy of intelligence failure: the types of failures that can occur in the intelligence function.
  • Detection mechanisms: how the institution recognizes that an intelligence failure has occurred.
  • The post-mortem procedure: a structured method for examining the failure and identifying root causes.
  • The failure register: a record of past failures and the lessons derived from them.
  • Corrective action: how findings from the post-mortem translate into improvements to the intelligence function.
  • The distinction between intelligence failure and unavoidable surprise: not every bad outcome represents a failure of analysis.

This article does not cover:

  • Incident response when a threat materializes (see D10-003 -- this article deals with the analytical failure, not the operational response).
  • The cognitive biases that cause many failures (see D7-005 -- this article references biases as root causes but does not catalog them).
  • The calibration tracking that detects systematic analytical errors (see D7-011 -- calibration is a detection mechanism, but this article covers the broader failure response).
  • The decision-making failures that may occur even when intelligence was accurate (see D7-010, GOV-001).

Background

3.1 What Is Intelligence Failure?

Intelligence failure occurs when the intelligence function provides inaccurate, incomplete, or misleading analysis that results in the institution being surprised by a development it should have anticipated, or taking an action it would not have taken with accurate analysis. Not every bad outcome is an intelligence failure. If the analyst correctly assessed a drive failure as "unlikely" (21-39%) and the drive failed, that is not a failure -- unlikely events happen. The assessment was honest about the probability. Intelligence failure occurs when the assessment was wrong in a way that better collection, better analysis, or better attention would have prevented.

This distinction is critical and difficult. The institution must resist two opposite errors: attributing every bad outcome to intelligence failure (which leads to analysis paralysis and risk aversion) and excusing every analytical error as unavoidable surprise (which leads to complacency and prevents learning).

3.2 The Taxonomy of Failure

Intelligence failures fall into five categories, each with different root causes and different corrective actions:

Type 1: Collection Failure. The information needed for accurate analysis existed but was not collected. The analyst did not look in the right places, did not ask the right questions, or did not allocate sufficient collection resources to the relevant area. The raw material for good analysis was available but was not brought into the institution.

Type 2: Processing Failure. The information was collected but was not properly processed into a form the analyst could use. It was lost in the archive, miscategorized, not translated or converted, or not flagged for analytical attention. The raw material existed within the institution but did not reach the analysis function.

Type 3: Analytical Failure. The information was collected and processed, but the analyst drew incorrect conclusions from it. This is the classical intelligence failure -- the evidence was available, but the analyst's reasoning was flawed. Root causes include cognitive biases (D7-005), methodological errors, incorrect assumptions, or failure to apply structured analytical techniques (D7-004).

Type 4: Communication Failure. The analysis was accurate, but it was not communicated to the decision-maker in a timely, clear, or persuasive manner. The intelligence product existed but was not read, was not understood, or arrived too late to inform the decision. The intelligence-decision interface (D7-010) failed.

Type 5: Imagination Failure. The development was not anticipated because it fell outside the analyst's mental model of what was possible. No collection was directed at it because no one thought to look. No analytical framework addressed it because the possibility was not conceived. This is the hardest failure to prevent and the most dangerous -- the threat you cannot imagine is the threat you cannot prepare for.

3.3 The Self-Investigation Challenge

In institutions with multiple analysts, intelligence failures are investigated by teams that did not produce the failed analysis. They bring fresh eyes and different biases. In a single-analyst institution, the person who produced the failed analysis must investigate it themselves. This creates obvious problems: the same biases that caused the failure may prevent the analyst from seeing it clearly. The same blind spots that allowed the surprise may still be blind spots.

The post-mortem procedure in this article is designed to mitigate this problem through structure. By requiring the analyst to examine each component of the intelligence cycle separately, applying the failure taxonomy systematically, and documenting findings in a format that a future analyst could review, the procedure creates external scaffolding for an inherently internal process. It is imperfect -- self-investigation will always be harder than external investigation. But it is far better than no investigation at all.

System Model

4.1 Detection Mechanisms

Intelligence failures are detected through five mechanisms:

Mechanism 1: Surprise Trigger. The institution is surprised by a development -- a threat materializes that was not on the threat register, an opportunity is missed that should have been identified, a condition changes unexpectedly. Any surprise triggers a preliminary assessment: was this a genuine surprise (an event outside the range of reasonable prediction) or an intelligence failure (an event that should have been anticipated)?

Mechanism 2: Calibration Trigger. The semiannual calibration exercise per D7-011 reveals systematic analytical errors. Assessments in a particular domain or at a particular confidence level are consistently wrong. This indicates a chronic intelligence failure rather than a single event.

Mechanism 3: Decision Outcome Review. The annual decision review per D7-010 reveals that decisions based on intelligence products produced worse outcomes than decisions made without them, or that the intelligence basis for a decision was materially wrong. This indicates a failure in the intelligence-decision pipeline.

Mechanism 4: Self-Discovery. The analyst, in the course of normal work, realizes that a past assessment was wrong. New information reveals that the evidence was misinterpreted, the assumptions were incorrect, or the conclusion was flawed. Self-discovery is the most common detection mechanism in a single-analyst institution and should be encouraged -- the analyst who catches their own errors demonstrates the epistemic honesty that D7-001 demands.

Mechanism 5: Successor Discovery. A new analyst, reviewing the predecessor's work, identifies errors that the predecessor did not detect. This is one of the most important functions of succession: fresh eyes on old analysis. Findings from successor discovery should be documented as intelligence failures and processed through the post-mortem procedure.

4.2 The Post-Mortem Procedure

When an intelligence failure is detected, the following procedure is invoked. The post-mortem must be completed within 30 days of detection, while the details are fresh.

Step 1: Scope the Failure. Define precisely what failed. What was the inaccurate or missing assessment? What was the actual outcome? What was the impact on the institution? Resist the temptation to broaden the scope to "everything we got wrong" -- a focused post-mortem is more productive than a vague one.

Step 2: Classify the Failure. Using the taxonomy in Section 3.2, determine which type(s) of failure occurred. Many failures involve multiple types -- a collection failure may have compounded an analytical failure. Identify all contributing types.

Step 3: Walk the Intelligence Cycle. Trace the failed assessment through every stage of the intelligence cycle per D7-002. At each stage, ask: did this stage function correctly? If not, what specifically went wrong?

  • Requirements: Was the relevant question included in the intelligence requirements? If not, why not?
  • Collection: Was relevant information collected? If not, was it available? Was the collection plan adequate?
  • Processing: Was collected information properly processed and made available for analysis?
  • Analysis: Was the analytical method appropriate? Were the evidence and assumptions correctly evaluated? Were biases at work?
  • Dissemination: Was the product produced and delivered in time? Was it in a usable format?
  • Feedback: Was there a feedback mechanism that should have caught the error earlier?

Step 4: Identify Root Causes. For each failure point identified in Step 3, determine the root cause. Use the "five whys" technique: ask "why did this happen?" and then ask "why?" of each answer until the root cause is reached. Root causes typically fall into: resource constraints (not enough time or information), methodological gaps (no procedure for this type of analysis), cognitive biases (specific bias per D7-005), systemic gaps (no monitoring for this type of development), or skill gaps (analyst lacked the knowledge to interpret the evidence correctly).

Step 5: Assess Preventability. Honestly determine: could this failure have been prevented with the resources and capabilities the institution actually had (not idealized capabilities)? If yes, specify how. If no, acknowledge the limitation. Not every failure is preventable. Some events are genuinely unforeseeable. The post-mortem must distinguish between preventable failures (which demand corrective action) and genuine surprises (which demand humility and, where possible, expansion of the institution's ability to anticipate).

Step 6: Define Corrective Actions. For preventable failures, specify what must change: new collection requirements, revised analytical procedures, additional monitoring indicators per D7-009, updated bias countermeasures per D7-005, improved product formats per D7-008, or revised decision-support procedures per D7-010. Each corrective action must be specific, actionable, and assigned a completion deadline.

Step 7: Document and Archive. Produce the Intelligence Failure Report (IFR) per the template in Section 4.4. Archive it per D6-001. Update the failure register per Section 4.3.

4.3 The Failure Register

The failure register is the institution's record of intelligence failures and the lessons learned from them. It serves two purposes: it prevents the same failure from recurring (because each failure produces corrective actions that are tracked to completion), and it provides a longitudinal view of the institution's analytical weaknesses (because patterns across failures reveal systemic problems that individual post-mortems might miss).

The register is reviewed annually as part of the intelligence function's self-assessment. The review asks: Are corrective actions being implemented? Are the same types of failures recurring despite corrective actions? Are new types of failures emerging? What does the failure pattern say about the institution's systemic analytical weaknesses?

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D7-12-01: Every institutional surprise that results in harm or missed opportunity must be evaluated through the preliminary assessment described in Section 4.1, Mechanism 1. The evaluation must determine whether the surprise constitutes an intelligence failure or a genuine unforeseeable event. The determination must be documented.
  • R-D7-12-02: When an intelligence failure is confirmed, the post-mortem procedure in Section 4.2 must be completed within 30 days. Extensions may be granted for complex failures but must be documented with a revised deadline.
  • R-D7-12-03: Post-mortem reports must be honest. The temptation to minimize the failure, excuse the error, or attribute the failure entirely to external circumstances must be resisted. Per ETH-001 Principle 6 (Honest Accounting of Limitations), the institution's relationship with its own errors must be one of candor, not defensiveness.
  • R-D7-12-04: Corrective actions identified in the post-mortem must be tracked to completion. An intelligence failure that produces lessons but no changes is a waste of the failure. The failure register tracks corrective action status, and incomplete actions must be reported in the quarterly intelligence requirements review per D7-001 R-D7-05.
  • R-D7-12-05: The failure register must be reviewed annually. If the same type of failure recurs despite corrective actions, the corrective actions are insufficient and must be redesigned. Recurring failure is a systemic problem, not a series of isolated incidents.
  • R-D7-12-06: Intelligence failure reports are classified SENSITIVE per D7-008 Section 4.4 because they contain detailed information about the institution's analytical weaknesses and gaps. However, they must not be suppressed or hidden. The institution's ability to learn from failure depends on the failure record being accessible to the analyst and any successor.
  • R-D7-12-07: The post-mortem must distinguish between intelligence failure (the analysis was wrong in a preventable way) and decision failure (the analysis was correct but the decision-maker chose not to follow it). If the decision-maker ignored accurate intelligence, the failure lies in the decision-support interface per D7-010, not in the intelligence function.

Failure Modes

  • Post-mortem avoidance. The analyst avoids conducting post-mortems because examining one's own errors is psychologically uncomfortable. Failures are acknowledged informally but not investigated formally. The failure register is sparse or empty, not because failures have not occurred but because they have not been documented. Mitigation: R-D7-12-01 and R-D7-12-02 make post-mortems mandatory. The quarterly review per D7-001 R-D7-05 should assess whether the failure register reflects reality -- a register with no entries over multiple years is suspect, not laudable.

  • Defensive post-mortems. Post-mortems are conducted but are defensive in nature -- attributing failures to unforeseeable circumstances, external factors, or insufficient resources rather than examining analytical errors honestly. The institution goes through the motions but does not learn. Mitigation: R-D7-12-03 demands honesty. The preventability assessment in Step 5 must be genuine, not perfunctory. The "five whys" technique in Step 4 is designed to push past surface explanations to root causes.

  • Over-attribution of failure. The opposite of avoidance: every bad outcome is treated as an intelligence failure, including genuinely unforeseeable events. This leads to constant self-criticism, erosion of analytical confidence, and increasingly cautious (and less useful) assessments. Mitigation: the distinction between intelligence failure and genuine surprise is fundamental to this article. The preliminary assessment in Section 4.1 must honestly evaluate preventability. Some surprises are not failures.

  • Corrective action without follow-through. Post-mortems produce lists of corrective actions that are never implemented. The failure register shows many failures with corrective actions listed but none marked complete. Mitigation: R-D7-12-04 requires tracking to completion. The annual register review per R-D7-12-05 audits action completion. Incomplete corrective actions must be escalated.

  • Pattern blindness. Individual failures are examined in isolation without recognizing that multiple failures share a common root cause. The same type of collection gap or cognitive bias causes repeated failures, but each post-mortem addresses only the specific instance. Mitigation: the failure register is designed for pattern analysis. The annual review must explicitly look for recurring failure types, common root causes, and persistent analytical weaknesses. Related failures must be cross-referenced per the report template.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If post-mortem backlog has accumulated (multiple failures unexamined): Prioritize by impact. Examine the failure with the greatest institutional impact first. Conduct abbreviated post-mortems (Steps 1, 2, 4, and 6 only) for lower-impact failures. Complete full post-mortems within 90 days. Establish a standing rule: no more than one unexamined failure may exist at any time.

  2. If the failure register reveals a pattern of recurring failure: Convene a special review (even if a self-review for a single analyst). Analyze all failures of the recurring type together. Identify the common root cause that individual post-mortems missed or inadequately addressed. Design a systemic correction -- not a patch for each instance but a change to the process, methodology, or monitoring system that addresses the root cause. Document the systemic review as a separate report and track the systemic corrective action separately.

  3. If the analyst has lost confidence after a significant failure: This is a genuine risk in a single-analyst institution. A major analytical error, especially one with visible consequences, can cause the analyst to second-guess every subsequent assessment, producing products that are hedged to the point of uselessness. Recovery requires perspective: review the calibration register per D7-011 and the overall track record. One failure does not invalidate all previous successes. The post-mortem procedure exists precisely so that the failure can be examined, understood, and addressed -- after which the analyst must move forward with appropriate confidence, informed by but not paralyzed by the experience.

  4. If a successor discovers that the predecessor's failure register is empty or incomplete: The successor should conduct retrospective failure analysis on the predecessor's intelligence archive, identifying assessments that proved materially wrong. These retrospective post-mortems will be less detailed than contemporaneous ones (the context is lost) but still valuable for understanding the predecessor's analytical strengths and weaknesses. Document findings in the failure register with the note "RETROSPECTIVE -- CONDUCTED BY SUCCESSOR."

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The failure register will grow rapidly as the institution's analytical function is immature and the analyst is still learning. This is healthy. Early failures are the tuition for later competence. The post-mortems from this period will establish the analytical self-correction culture that defines the institution's intelligence function for decades.

  • Years 5-15: The frequency of failures should decrease as the analyst matures, collection improves, and analytical methods are refined. But the severity of remaining failures may increase as the institution tackles more complex analytical questions. The failure register should show a shift from Type 1 and Type 3 failures (basic collection and analytical errors) toward Type 4 and Type 5 failures (communication and imagination failures), which are harder to prevent.

  • Years 15-30: The failure archive is a significant analytical resource. Pattern analysis across 15-30 years of failures reveals the institution's persistent analytical blind spots. If succession occurs, the failure register is one of the most important documents the successor should study -- it tells them not what the predecessor got right (which is visible in successful products) but what they got wrong, which is harder to learn from the surface record alone.

  • Years 30-50+: The institution has multiple decades of failure analysis. This archive becomes a tool for anticipating future failures: what types of developments has the institution historically been worst at predicting? What analytical weaknesses persist despite corrective action? These insights inform the intelligence function's priorities and resource allocation. An institution that learns from 30 years of failures is fundamentally more capable than one that has not kept track.

  • Signpost for revision: If the post-mortem procedure consistently fails to produce useful corrective actions -- if the institution keeps having the same types of failures despite following the procedure -- then the procedure itself needs revision. The procedure is a tool. If the tool does not work, replace the tool.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: This is the article I least want to need and most want to exist. Writing about intelligence failure before the intelligence function has produced its first assessment feels premature. But the procedures described here must be in place before the first failure occurs, because the post-mortem conducted immediately after a failure is the most valuable one -- the details are fresh, the emotional impact is raw, and the motivation to learn is strongest.

The hardest part of this article is the self-investigation problem. I am asking a single analyst -- possibly me, for years -- to honestly examine their own errors, identify their own biases, and prescribe their own corrections. This is asking a lot. It may be asking too much. But the alternative is asking nothing: letting failures pass unexamined, letting the same errors recur, letting the institution's analytical function degrade without accountability.

I chose to classify intelligence failure reports as SENSITIVE rather than RESTRICTED because the failure record must be accessible. A failure report locked away where the successor cannot read it serves no one. The institution's weaknesses, documented honestly, are among its most valuable assets -- because they tell the reader where not to trust the analysis, which is information as important as the analysis itself.

The distinction between intelligence failure and genuine surprise (Section 3.1) will be the most contested judgment in every post-mortem. The analyst will always be tempted to classify failures as surprises because surprises are nobody's fault. The procedure demands honesty, but honesty is hard when you are examining your own work. Future operators: be honest. The institution that lies to itself about its failures is an institution that cannot improve.
