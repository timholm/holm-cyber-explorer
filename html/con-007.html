CON-007 — Relationship Between Institution and Individual

Document ID
CON-007
Domain
1 -- Constitution & Philosophy
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Draft

Purpose

This article defines the relationship between the holm.chat Documentation Institution and the individuals who operate, maintain, and inherit it. It establishes what the institution owes to its operators, what operators owe to the institution, and how personal autonomy coexists with institutional discipline.

This is a necessary document because the institution, by design, makes demands on its operator. It requires time, attention, discipline, and sustained effort over years and decades. These demands are not incidental to the institution's design; they are part of it. An institution that does not demand anything is not an institution -- it is a toy.

But demands must be bounded. An institution that consumes its operator -- that requires heroic effort, that leaves no room for rest, that treats the person as a resource to be optimized -- is an institution that will burn through operators and fail. This article establishes the boundaries that protect the operator from the institution and the institution from an exhausted or resentful operator.

This article is written for every person who will ever serve as the institution's operator, and for the institution itself -- as an articulation of the promises it makes to the people who sustain it.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The rights of the operator with respect to the institution.
  • The obligations of the operator to the institution.
  • The obligations of the institution to the operator.
  • The boundaries between institutional duties and personal life.
  • The resolution of conflicts between institutional needs and personal needs.
  • The special considerations that apply to the founding operator versus successor operators.

This article does not define specific governance structures (see Domain 2) or operational procedures (see Domain 10). It defines the philosophy that those structures and procedures must embody.

Background

Most institutions solve the relationship problem through employment -- the institution pays the individual, and the employment contract defines the mutual obligations. This institution has no employment relationship. The operator is not an employee. The institution does not pay anyone. The relationship is voluntary, personal, and -- because it is designed to last a lifetime -- far more intimate than any employment.

The closest analogy is stewardship. A steward cares for something that is larger and longer-lived than themselves. A steward of a historical property maintains the building not because they own it in the sense of property rights, but because they have accepted responsibility for its continuation. The building was there before them and, if they do their work well, will be there after them.

But the analogy is imperfect. Unlike a historical building, this institution was created by its first operator. The founding operator has a relationship to the institution that no successor can replicate -- they are both creator and steward. This dual role creates tensions that must be acknowledged: the temptation to treat the institution as a personal possession rather than a trust, the difficulty of accepting that the institution must eventually be handed to someone who will make different choices, and the emotional attachment that comes from building something with your own hands.

This article does not resolve all of these tensions. Some can only be resolved through experience and reflection. But it establishes the principles that should guide the resolution.

System Model

What the Operator Owes the Institution

Duty of maintenance. The operator must maintain the institution's systems, documentation, and procedures at a level sufficient to ensure continued operation. This does not mean everything must be perfect at all times. It means that degradation must be noticed, documented, and addressed within a reasonable timeframe. The specific maintenance schedule is defined in the operational domains.

Duty of honesty. The operator must be honest with the institution -- which means, in practice, being honest in its documentation. If a system is broken, the documentation must say so. If a procedure was skipped, the log must record it. If a principle was violated, the violation must be acknowledged. The institution's integrity depends on the operator's honesty.

Duty of stewardship. The operator must treat the institution as a trust, not as a possession. This means making decisions that serve the institution's long-term interests, not just the operator's immediate preferences. It means maintaining systems that the operator personally finds boring. It means investing time in succession planning even when succession feels distant.

Duty of succession. The operator must prepare for their own departure. This means documenting tacit knowledge, training potential successors when possible, and maintaining the institution in a state that can be transferred. The succession philosophy is elaborated in CON-008.

What the Institution Owes the Operator

Right to rest. The institution must be designed to operate at a sustainable pace. No procedure may require more time or effort than one person can sustainably provide over years. If the institution's demands exceed what is sustainable, the institution must be simplified, not the operator's limits pushed. The institution must accommodate vacations, sick days, periods of low motivation, and the ordinary fluctuations of human energy. Systems must degrade gracefully during periods of reduced attention, not catastrophically.

Right to understanding. Every system must be documented well enough that the operator can understand it without relying on memory, external resources, or other people. The institution must never require the operator to operate in the dark. If a system exists that the operator does not understand, the institution has failed in its obligation, and the system must be documented or simplified until understanding is achieved.

Right to autonomy. The operator's life outside the institution is not the institution's concern. The institution may define how its systems are used and maintained, but it may not dictate how the operator lives, what they believe, or what they do with their time outside institutional duties. The boundary between operator-as-steward and operator-as-person must be respected.

Right to evolve. The operator may change the institution. The principles and amendment processes exist to govern change, not to prevent it. An operator who is forbidden from evolving the institution is not a steward; they are a custodian of a museum. The institution is a living system, and the operator's judgment is part of what keeps it alive.

Right to departure. The operator may leave. This right is unconditional. The institution imposes obligations, but it cannot impose captivity. If the operator chooses to depart, the institution's succession procedures activate. The departing operator should provide reasonable transition support, but their fundamental right to walk away is absolute.

The Founding Operator: Special Considerations

The founding operator occupies a unique position. They created the institution, defined its principles, and wrote its foundational documents. This gives them a kind of authority that no successor can claim. But it also imposes a unique obligation: the obligation to build the institution in a way that does not depend on them.

The founding operator must resist the temptation to make themselves indispensable. Every system must be documented. Every decision must be recorded with reasoning. Every procedure must be learnable by someone new. The founding operator's ultimate duty is to make themselves replaceable -- not because they are unimportant, but because the institution is more important than any individual, including its creator.

The founding operator's commentary in the documentation carries special weight but not special authority. Future operators should take the founder's reasoning seriously but are not bound by it. The institution belongs to whoever is maintaining it, not to whoever created it.

Rules and Constraints

  • R-CON-007-01: The total operational burden of the institution must not exceed what one person can sustainably manage, defined as no more than 10 hours per week of routine maintenance averaged over any 12-month period. Peak periods may exceed this, but sustained excess is a design failure, not an operator failure.
  • R-CON-007-02: No system may be designed such that its failure during a period of operator absence (up to 30 consecutive days) results in unrecoverable data loss or permanent system damage.
  • R-CON-007-03: Every operator, including the founder, must maintain a current succession document that describes the state of the institution, the location of critical documentation, and the steps a successor would need to take to assume operation.
  • R-CON-007-04: Institutional documentation must never record personal information about the operator beyond what is necessary for institutional operation. The operator's right to privacy within their own institution is absolute.
  • R-CON-007-05: When institutional needs and personal needs conflict, the conflict must be resolved by reference to the principles in CON-002, not by defaulting to institutional priority. The operator is a person first and a steward second. Principles like sustainable operation and honest accounting apply to the treatment of the operator, not just to the treatment of data.

Failure Modes

Operator burnout. The institution demands more than the operator can sustain. Maintenance falls behind. Documentation accumulates debt. The operator begins to resent the institution. Eventually, the operator either abandons the institution or continues operating it at a degraded level that compromises integrity. Mitigation: the sustainability constraint (R-CON-007-01) and the graceful degradation requirement (R-CON-007-02).

Institutional narcissism. The institution's needs are always prioritized over the operator's needs. Every free hour is consumed by maintenance. Personal projects, relationships, and rest are sacrificed. The institution becomes a tyrant. Mitigation: the right to rest and the right to autonomy, defined in this article's System Model section.

Founder indispensability. The founding operator builds systems that only they can maintain, creates documentation that only they can understand, and accumulates tacit knowledge that they never transfer. When succession eventually occurs, the institution effectively dies. Mitigation: the duty of succession and the specific succession requirements in CON-008.

Successor disconnection. A successor operates the institution mechanically, following procedures without understanding or caring about the principles behind them. The institution continues to function but loses its character. Mitigation: the succession procedures in CON-008, which require demonstrated comprehension of the institution's philosophy, not just its procedures.

Role confusion. The operator confuses their role as steward with ownership. They treat the institution as personal property, making decisions based on whim rather than principle. Or conversely, they treat themselves as the institution's servant, allowing institutional demands to override their personal needs and judgment. Mitigation: the balanced framework of rights and obligations defined in this article.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If the operator is burned out: Invoke the graceful degradation protocols. Reduce operational activity to the minimum required to preserve data integrity. Defer all non-critical maintenance. Take the time needed to recover. When returning, review the operational burden and identify what must be simplified to prevent recurrence.
  2. If the institution has become unsustainably complex: Conduct a complexity audit per OPS-001. Identify systems that can be simplified, deprecated, or eliminated. The goal is to bring the institution back within the sustainability constraint, not to preserve every system at the expense of the operator.
  3. If the founder has become indispensable: Immediately begin a systematic tacit knowledge capture effort. Review all systems for single-person dependencies. Create documentation and procedures that would enable a successor to operate without the founder's presence. This is urgent work because indispensability is a time bomb.
  4. If the operator-institution relationship has become dysfunctional: Return to this article. Re-read the rights and obligations. Identify specifically which elements are out of balance. Create a concrete plan to restore balance, with measurable outcomes and a timeline.

Evolution Path

Years 0-5: The relationship is defined primarily by the founding operator's dual role as creator and steward. The operational burden is high as the institution is being built. The sustainability constraint may be strained during this period, but must not be permanently exceeded.

Years 5-15: The institution is built and the relationship shifts from construction to maintenance. The operational burden should decrease. The emphasis shifts to the duty of succession and the systematic capture of tacit knowledge.

Years 15-30: Succession approaches or occurs. The relationship model is tested by its most demanding scenario: the transfer of stewardship from one person to another. The rights and obligations must prove transferable, not just theoretical.

Years 30-50+: Multiple succession events may have occurred. The operator-institution relationship has been tested across different personalities, motivations, and circumstances. The framework in this article should have evolved through commentary to accommodate the diversity of human relationships with the institution.
