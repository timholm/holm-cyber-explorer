GOV-008 — Decision-Making Frameworks

Document ID
GOV-008
Domain
2 -- Governance & Authority
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
ETH-001, CON-001, GOV-001
Depended Upon By
GOV-003, GOV-005, GOV-010, GOV-012, GOV-013

Purpose

This article defines the taxonomy of decisions within the holm.chat Documentation Institution and the process required for each category. GOV-001 established four authority tiers and their requirements. This article goes deeper: it provides the actual frameworks by which decisions are identified, classified, deliberated, and executed.

A framework is not a formula. It does not make decisions for you. It ensures that you approach each decision with the appropriate level of care, that you consider what needs considering, and that you do not skip steps that exist for good reasons. The purpose of a decision-making framework is to prevent the two most common failure modes of single-operator governance: deciding too quickly on matters that deserve reflection, and reflecting too long on matters that require action.

This article exists because knowing who has authority (GOV-001) and what roles exist (GOV-006) is not sufficient for effective governance. You also need to know how to decide. The how is where institutions succeed or fail. An institution with perfect authority structures but no decision methodology will make inconsistent, unreasoned choices. An institution with rigorous decision methodology but unclear authority will deliberate endlessly without action. Both are needed. GOV-001 provides the authority. This article provides the methodology.

Future maintainers of this institution will face decisions that the founder could not have anticipated. The frameworks here are designed to be durable across changing circumstances. They do not prescribe specific outcomes. They prescribe a process for arriving at outcomes that are deliberate, documented, and defensible.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The taxonomy of decisions: routine, significant, critical, and existential, with precise definitions and examples for each category.
  • The decision process for each category, including consensus requirements, quorum rules (relevant when the institution has more than one member), and time limits for deliberation.
  • The relationship between decision categories and the authority tiers in GOV-001.
  • Decision templates: structured formats for recording the reasoning behind each decision category.
  • The escalation protocol: how to recognize when a decision belongs in a higher category than initially assumed.
  • The de-escalation protocol: how to recognize when excessive deliberation is being applied to a simple decision.

This article does not cover emergency decision-making (see GOV-005), dispute resolution when a decision is contested (see GOV-003), or the veto and override mechanisms that can halt or force a decision (see GOV-010). It covers the normal, non-emergency, non-contested process of making a decision and seeing it through to implementation.

Background

Most individuals do not think of their personal decisions as falling into categories. They make choices as they arise, with varying levels of thought, and move on. This works tolerably for a human life, where most decisions are reversible and the consequences of a poor choice are borne by the person who made it.

It does not work for an institution, even a small one. Institutional decisions accumulate. They form layers. A decision made today becomes the foundation for a decision made next year, which becomes the constraint on a decision made in ten years. If the foundational decision was made carelessly, the entire chain inherits that carelessness. If it was made thoughtfully but the reasoning was not recorded, the chain inherits the decision without its justification, which is almost as bad.

The taxonomy in this article is borrowed, with significant adaptation, from organizational decision science. Large organizations distinguish between strategic, tactical, and operational decisions. This institution adapts that framework for a context where the "organization" may be a single person, where "consensus" may mean agreeing with your own documentation rather than with other people, and where the time horizon is measured in decades rather than fiscal quarters.

The key insight behind this framework is that not all decisions deserve the same process. Treating every decision as critical leads to decision paralysis. Treating every decision as routine leads to institutional drift. The framework provides the discriminating intelligence to match the process to the decision. It is, in essence, a decision about how to decide.

System Model

4.1 Decision Taxonomy

Every decision within the institution falls into one of four categories. These categories are orthogonal to the authority tiers in GOV-001 but related to them. A decision's category determines the deliberation process; its tier determines the authority and documentation requirements.

Category 1: Routine Decisions. Decisions that follow established policy and do not create new precedent. They are predictable, reversible, and low-impact. Examples: scheduling a backup verification, choosing the order in which to perform weekly maintenance tasks, deciding which documentation article to review next.

Process: The operator makes the decision, records it in the operational log if it involves a system change, and moves on. No deliberation period. No template required. The decision should be consistent with existing policy; if the operator notices inconsistency, escalate to Significant.

Typical authority tier: Tier 4 (Operational).

Time limit: None. Routine decisions should be made promptly. If a routine decision is taking more than fifteen minutes of deliberation, it is probably not routine.

Category 2: Significant Decisions. Decisions that affect the institution's operations for weeks or months, create modest precedent, or involve meaningful trade-offs. They are generally reversible but at some cost. Examples: changing a backup schedule, adopting a new file naming convention, modifying a monitoring threshold, deciding to upgrade a software component.

Process: The operator identifies the decision, drafts a brief rationale (three to five sentences), considers at least one alternative, and records the decision using the Significant Decision Template (Section 4.5). A 7-day waiting period applies before implementation. During the waiting period, the operator should revisit the decision at least once.

Typical authority tier: Tier 3 (Policy).

Time limit: The decision should be made within 30 days of being identified. If deliberation extends beyond 30 days, the operator should either decide or formally document why the delay is warranted.

Category 3: Critical Decisions. Decisions that affect the institution's architecture, security posture, or long-term direction. They may be difficult or costly to reverse. They create significant precedent. Examples: changing the storage architecture, modifying the security perimeter, altering the backup strategy fundamentally, adding or removing a domain from the documentation framework.

Process: The operator drafts a full decision proposal including rationale, alternatives considered, impact assessment, reversibility analysis, and alignment check against ETH-001 and CON-001. The proposal is recorded using the Critical Decision Template (Section 4.5). A 30-day waiting period applies. The operator must review the proposal at least twice during the waiting period, with each review documented. If a successor has been designated, the successor should be informed.

Typical authority tier: Tier 2 (Architectural).

Time limit: The decision should be made within 90 days of the proposal being drafted. If deliberation extends beyond 90 days, the proposal should be formally withdrawn and re-submitted if still desired.

Category 4: Existential Decisions. Decisions that alter the institution's foundational documents, purpose, ethical framework, or governance structure. They are irreversible in the sense that even if formally reversed, the institution will have been changed by the act of making and then reversing them. Examples: amending ETH-001, modifying GOV-001, changing CON-001, dissolving or fundamentally restructuring the institution.

Process: The operator drafts a comprehensive decision proposal that includes everything required for a Critical Decision, plus: a historical analysis of why the current state exists, a projection of how the change will affect the institution over the next 20 years, and an explicit statement of what would be lost by making this change. The proposal is recorded using the Existential Decision Template (Section 4.5). A 90-day waiting period applies. The operator must review the proposal at least four times during the waiting period, at roughly equal intervals, with each review documented. If a successor has been designated, the successor must be notified and given the opportunity to comment.

Typical authority tier: Tier 1 (Constitutional).

Time limit: No time limit. Existential decisions should not be rushed. However, if a proposal remains under deliberation for more than one year without a decision, it should be formally withdrawn. It may be re-submitted, but the re-submission resets the clock and requires fresh justification.

4.2 Category Assignment

When a decision arises, the operator's first task is to assign it a category. This assignment is itself a judgment call, and it should be made deliberately rather than instinctively. The following questions assist in category assignment:

  1. Does this decision follow existing policy without creating new precedent? If yes, it is Routine.
  2. Does this decision affect operations for more than a week but less than a year? If yes, it is likely Significant.
  3. Does this decision alter the institution's architecture, security model, or long-term trajectory? If yes, it is Critical.
  4. Does this decision change a root document or the institution's fundamental purpose? If yes, it is Existential.

When in doubt, assign the higher category. It is better to over-deliberate on a Significant decision that turns out to be Routine than to under-deliberate on a Critical decision that was treated as Significant. The de-escalation protocol (Section 4.4) allows you to move a decision to a lower category if the initial assignment proves too conservative.

4.3 Escalation Protocol

A decision may need to be escalated to a higher category after deliberation has begun. Escalation is triggered by any of the following:

  • The operator discovers that the decision has broader implications than initially understood.
  • The decision creates precedent that the operator did not anticipate.
  • The decision interacts with a root document in ways not initially recognized.
  • Implementation of the decision reveals dependencies or consequences that increase its scope.

When a decision is escalated, the deliberation resets. The waiting period for the new category begins from the date of escalation, not from the original decision date. All work done under the previous category is preserved and incorporated into the new category's template. An entry is added to the decision log noting the escalation and its reason.

4.4 De-escalation Protocol

A decision may be de-escalated to a lower category if, during deliberation, the operator determines that the initial category was too conservative. De-escalation is permitted only under the following conditions:

  • The operator can articulate a specific reason why the lower category is appropriate.
  • No new information has emerged that supports the higher category.
  • The de-escalation is itself recorded in the decision log.

De-escalation does not reset the waiting period. If a decision has already waited 20 days under a 30-day Critical process and is de-escalated to Significant (7-day process), the waiting period is considered satisfied. The operator may proceed to implementation after completing the lower category's template.

4.5 Decision Templates

Each decision category has a corresponding template. The template is not a bureaucratic form to be filled in mindlessly. It is a structured prompt for thinking. Each field exists because experience has shown that omitting it leads to worse decisions.

Routine Decision Log Entry:

  • Date.
  • Decision summary (one sentence).
  • Relevant policy or precedent that supports the decision.

Significant Decision Template:

  • Decision ID and date.
  • Decision summary (one to three sentences).
  • Rationale (three to five sentences explaining why).
  • Alternative considered (at least one, with reason for rejection).
  • Reversibility assessment (can this be undone? at what cost?).
  • Implementation date (after waiting period).
  • Review date (90 days after implementation).

Critical Decision Template:

  • Decision ID and date.
  • Decision summary.
  • Full rationale (one to two paragraphs).
  • Alternatives considered (at least two, with reasons for rejection).
  • Impact assessment (what systems, processes, or documents are affected).
  • Reversibility analysis (detailed assessment of whether and how this can be undone).
  • Alignment check (explicit statement of how this decision aligns with ETH-001 and CON-001).
  • Waiting period review log (entries for each review during the waiting period).
  • Implementation plan (step-by-step).
  • Rollback plan (step-by-step, in case the decision proves wrong).
  • Post-implementation review date.

Existential Decision Template:

  • All fields from the Critical Decision Template, plus:
  • Historical analysis (why does the current state exist? what was the reasoning behind it?).
  • 20-year projection (how will this change affect the institution two decades from now?).
  • Loss statement (what will be lost by making this change? what value does the current state provide that will be sacrificed?).
  • Successor notification log (if applicable: date notified, any comments received).
  • Quarterly review entries during the waiting period (at least four).

4.6 Consensus and Quorum

In a single-operator institution, consensus means agreement between the operator's current judgment and the institution's documented principles and precedents. If the operator wishes to make a decision that conflicts with a documented precedent, the precedent must be explicitly acknowledged and the departure justified. Consensus with oneself is not trivial; it requires honest engagement with one's own documentation.

When the institution has multiple members, the following quorum and consensus rules apply:

  • Routine: Any authorized member may decide unilaterally within their role's scope.
  • Significant: The deciding member must notify all other members. If no objection is raised within the 7-day waiting period, the decision is approved.
  • Critical: Requires explicit approval from a majority of members. The member who proposed the decision may not count toward the majority unless there are only two members.
  • Existential: Requires unanimous approval from all members. If unanimity cannot be achieved, the decision does not proceed. There is no override for Existential decisions (see GOV-010 for the override framework and its explicit exclusion of Existential matters).

Rules & Constraints

  • R-GOV-08-01: Every non-routine decision must be assigned a category before deliberation begins. The category assignment must be recorded in the decision log.
  • R-GOV-08-02: Waiting periods are mandatory and may only be shortened under emergency conditions as defined in GOV-005. They may not be shortened for convenience, urgency, or impatience.
  • R-GOV-08-03: Escalation resets the waiting period. De-escalation does not. This asymmetry is deliberate: it is designed to discourage premature de-escalation while permitting genuine correction of overly conservative initial assessments.
  • R-GOV-08-04: The decision templates are minimum requirements, not maximum requirements. The operator may add additional analysis to any template. The operator may not omit required fields.
  • R-GOV-08-05: If a Significant or Critical decision is implemented without the required waiting period, the implementation must be halted and the waiting period initiated retroactively. If halting is not possible (the decision has already been executed), the situation must be documented as a governance deviation and reviewed within 14 days.
  • R-GOV-08-06: Existential decisions that do not achieve consensus (unanimity in a multi-member institution, or honest agreement with institutional principles in a single-operator institution) do not proceed. There is no mechanism to force an Existential decision over principled objection.
  • R-GOV-08-07: The decision taxonomy and templates must be reviewed annually as part of the governance health assessment (GOV-007). If the review reveals that the categories are poorly calibrated (too many decisions clustering in one category, or too few), adjustments should be proposed as a Significant decision.

Failure Modes

  • Category compression. All decisions are treated as the same category, either because the operator does not bother with classification or because habit leads them to default to one level. When everything is Routine, critical decisions are under-deliberated. When everything is Critical, the institution grinds to a halt. Mitigation: the annual review in R-GOV-08-07, which checks the distribution of decisions across categories. A healthy institution should have many Routine decisions, some Significant ones, occasional Critical ones, and rare Existential ones.
  • Template fatigue. The operator begins to fill in templates mechanically, treating them as forms rather than thinking tools. The rationale field contains boilerplate. The alternatives field contains straw men. Mitigation: the waiting period reviews require the operator to re-read the template with fresh eyes. If the template reads as mechanical, the review is the moment to notice and correct.
  • Deliberation as avoidance. The operator uses the deliberation process to avoid making difficult decisions. A Critical decision enters its 30-day waiting period and is then withdrawn at day 29, re-submitted, and enters another 30-day period. The decision is never made. Mitigation: the time limits in Section 4.1 and the withdrawal-and-resubmission requirement, which forces the operator to acknowledge that they are restarting rather than continuing.
  • Escalation ratchet. Decisions are escalated but never de-escalated, leading to an ever-increasing burden of deliberation. Mitigation: the de-escalation protocol in Section 4.4, which is available whenever the operator genuinely determines that a lower category is appropriate.
  • Consensus theater. In a multi-member institution, consensus is obtained formally but not substantively. Members approve without engaging. Mitigation: the templates require explicit engagement with rationale and alternatives. The dispute resolution process in GOV-003 provides a mechanism for members who disagree but feel pressure to conform.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If decisions have been made without category assignment: Retroactively classify all unclassified decisions in the decision log. For each, assess whether the process that was actually followed was appropriate for the category. Where the process was insufficient (a Critical decision was made with Routine process), initiate a review of that decision to confirm or revise it through the proper process.
  2. If templates have been filled in mechanically: Select the five most recent non-routine decisions. Re-read each template. For each, ask: "Does this template reflect genuine thought, or does it reflect compliance?" For any that reflect compliance rather than thought, redo the analysis. Record the redo in the Commentary Section as a lesson learned.
  3. If deliberation has been used as avoidance: Identify the decision or decisions that have been in deliberation for longer than their time limits. For each, set a firm deadline: decide within 14 days or formally withdraw. Record the deadline in the decision log. If the deadline passes without a decision, the proposal is automatically withdrawn.
  4. If consensus has broken down in a multi-member institution: Refer to GOV-003 (Dispute Resolution). The decision-making framework does not resolve disputes; it identifies them. When a decision cannot achieve the required consensus, the dispute resolution process takes over.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The decision taxonomy is new and untested against real institutional decisions. The founder should expect to refine the category boundaries and template fields based on experience. The first few Critical and Existential decisions will test whether the waiting periods and review requirements are well-calibrated. Adjustments should be made as Significant decisions.
  • Years 5-15: The decision log should contain enough entries to reveal patterns. Are most decisions Routine, as expected? Are the Significant decisions genuinely significant, or are some of them over-classified? The templates may evolve to include additional fields that experience has shown to be valuable, or to remove fields that have proven unhelpful.
  • Years 15-30: If succession has occurred, the new operator will inherit a rich decision log and a refined set of templates. The new operator should review the templates during their onboarding period and propose any modifications through the normal process. The frameworks should be stable by this point, requiring only minor adjustments.
  • Years 30-50+: The frameworks should be deeply embedded in institutional culture. Multiple operators may have used them. The Commentary Section should contain reflections from different operators about what works and what does not. The taxonomy itself may need re-evaluation: are four categories still the right number? Has the institution's scale changed enough to require a fifth? These are Significant or Critical decisions in their own right.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators. Each entry should include the date, the author's identifier, and the context for the commentary.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The hardest part of designing a decision-making framework is deciding how much process is enough. Too little, and you might as well not have a framework. Too much, and the framework becomes the enemy of action. I have tried to err on the side of simplicity for Routine and Significant decisions, and on the side of rigor for Critical and Existential ones. The reasoning is asymmetric: the cost of under-deliberating on a routine decision is small and recoverable. The cost of under-deliberating on an existential decision may be the institution itself.

I am particularly uncertain about the time limits. Telling yourself you must decide within 90 days feels arbitrary. But I have watched decisions languish for years in other contexts, not because they were genuinely difficult but because deciding felt uncomfortable. The time limits are a commitment device. They force the question: are you still deliberating, or are you avoiding?
