SEC-010 — Cryptographic Principles and Standards

Document ID
SEC-010
Domain
3 -- Security & Integrity
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
SEC-001 (Threat Model), SEC-002 (Access Control)
Depended Upon By
SEC-003 (Key Management), SEC-012 (Data Integrity), SEC-005 (Long-Term Cryptographic Survival), Domain 5 (Platform)

Purpose

This article defines the cryptographic principles and standards that govern all uses of cryptography within the holm.chat Documentation Institution. Cryptography is the mathematical foundation upon which the institution's data confidentiality, integrity, and authenticity guarantees rest. Without well-chosen cryptographic primitives, properly implemented and correctly applied, every other security measure in this institution is built on sand.

The purpose of this document is not to teach cryptography. It is to establish the criteria by which cryptographic algorithms, key lengths, modes of operation, and implementations are selected, evaluated, and retired within this institution. It is to make explicit the reasoning behind every cryptographic choice so that future maintainers can evaluate whether those choices remain sound as the cryptographic landscape evolves.

This document deliberately separates cryptographic principles (which change slowly, if at all) from cryptographic implementations (which must change as algorithms age and new attacks emerge). The principles stated here should remain valid for decades. The specific algorithm choices will need periodic revision, and the evolution path section of this document describes how those revisions should be conducted.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The institution's cryptographic philosophy: why cryptography is used, what it is expected to provide, and what it cannot provide.
  • Algorithm selection criteria: the principles that govern which cryptographic algorithms the institution adopts.
  • Current algorithm selections: the specific algorithms, key lengths, and modes of operation approved for institutional use at the time of writing.
  • The rejection of security through obscurity: why the institution's security must not depend on the secrecy of its algorithms or procedures.
  • Hash function standards for data integrity verification.
  • Symmetric encryption standards for data at rest.
  • Asymmetric encryption and signature standards for authentication and key exchange.
  • Random number generation requirements.
  • The framework for post-quantum migration planning.

This article does not cover key management procedures (those are in SEC-003), long-term cryptographic evolution strategy (that is in SEC-005), or the specific software implementations of cryptographic operations (those are in Domain 5). This article defines what cryptography the institution uses and why. Other articles define how it is managed and when it must change.

Background

3.1 Cryptography as Infrastructure

In this institution, cryptography is not a feature that can be enabled or disabled. It is infrastructure -- as fundamental as the filesystem or the power supply. Every piece of data at rest is encrypted. Every piece of data that crosses a trust boundary is verified for integrity. Every authentication event relies on cryptographic operations. If the cryptographic infrastructure fails, the institution does not degrade gracefully. It stops functioning.

This total dependence on cryptography creates a paradox: the institution's greatest strength (strong cryptographic protection of all assets) is also its greatest vulnerability (loss of cryptographic keys or obsolescence of cryptographic algorithms would be catastrophic). This paradox is managed, not resolved. It is managed through redundant key storage, algorithm agility, and the long-term planning described in SEC-005.

3.2 The Kerckhoffs Principle

Auguste Kerckhoffs articulated in 1883 a principle that remains the foundation of all sound cryptographic practice: a cryptographic system should be secure even if everything about the system, except the key, is public knowledge. This institution adopts this principle without reservation.

The implication is direct: this document, and every other security document in this institution, can be read by anyone without compromising the institution's security. The algorithms are public. The modes of operation are public. The key lengths are public. Only the keys themselves are secret. If any security measure in this institution depends on the secrecy of its design rather than the secrecy of its keys, that measure is defective and must be redesigned.

3.3 The Problem of Longevity

Most cryptographic standards are designed for a deployment horizon of five to fifteen years. This institution is designed for fifty. An algorithm that is considered strong today may be broken in twenty years. A key length that provides adequate security margin today may be insufficient in thirty years. The institution must therefore adopt a posture of cryptographic conservatism: choosing algorithms with the widest safety margins, selecting key lengths that exceed current recommendations, and building systems that can migrate to new algorithms without losing access to data encrypted under old ones.

This conservatism has a cost. Larger key lengths require more storage and more computation. Algorithm agility requires more complex implementations. But the cost of cryptographic failure -- permanent loss of access to encrypted data, or permanent compromise of data confidentiality -- is categorically greater than the cost of conservative choices.

System Model

4.1 Algorithm Selection Criteria

The institution selects cryptographic algorithms based on the following criteria, in order of priority:

Criterion 1: Proven security. The algorithm must have been subjected to extensive public cryptanalysis over a period of at least ten years without the discovery of practical attacks. Algorithms that are new, proprietary, or insufficiently studied are not acceptable, regardless of their theoretical properties. The institution does not adopt cryptographic algorithms based on vendor claims, government endorsements, or theoretical proofs alone. It requires the sustained scrutiny of the open cryptographic research community.

Criterion 2: Conservative parameters. Key lengths and security parameters must exceed the minimum recommendations of recognized standards bodies (NIST, BSI, ANSSI) by at least one tier. If the current recommendation is 128-bit security, the institution uses 256-bit security. If the recommendation is a 2048-bit RSA key, the institution uses 4096-bit. This margin is the insurance premium the institution pays against future cryptanalytic advances.

Criterion 3: Simplicity of implementation. When two algorithms provide equivalent security, the institution prefers the one that is simpler to implement correctly. Cryptographic vulnerabilities are more often the result of implementation errors than mathematical weaknesses. An algorithm that is theoretically perfect but practically difficult to implement correctly is inferior to one that is theoretically adequate and practically robust.

Criterion 4: Algorithm agility. The system architecture must support the replacement of any cryptographic algorithm without requiring re-architecture of the entire system. Data must be tagged with the algorithm used to protect it. Migration procedures must allow old data to be re-encrypted under new algorithms incrementally.

4.2 Current Algorithm Selections

The following algorithms are approved for institutional use as of the date of this document. These selections are subject to revision per the evolution path described in Section 9.

Symmetric Encryption: AES-256 in XTS mode for full-disk encryption. AES-256 in GCM mode for file-level encryption and authenticated encryption of data in transit between zones. The choice of AES-256 provides a 256-bit security level against classical attacks and a 128-bit security level against quantum attacks using Grover's algorithm, which remains well above any foreseeable attack capability.

Hash Functions: SHA-3-256 (Keccak) as the primary hash function for data integrity verification, document fingerprinting, and hash chain construction. SHA-256 (SHA-2 family) as a secondary hash function, permitted where SHA-3 is not available in the implementation. BLAKE3 is approved as a performance-optimized alternative for bulk integrity checking where cryptographic collision resistance is not the primary concern. The institution computes dual hashes (SHA-3-256 and SHA-256) for all archival data, providing defense in depth against the compromise of either hash function family.

Asymmetric Encryption and Signatures: Ed25519 for digital signatures where performance and key size are priorities. RSA-4096 with OAEP padding for encryption and PSS padding for signatures where compatibility with existing tools is required. X25519 for key agreement. These algorithms are considered transitional; the institution anticipates migration to post-quantum algorithms as they mature (see Section 4.4).

Random Number Generation: All cryptographic operations requiring randomness must use the operating system's cryptographically secure random number generator (e.g., /dev/urandom on Linux, or equivalent). No userspace pseudo-random number generators are permitted for cryptographic operations. The operator must verify that the system's entropy pool is adequately seeded before generating any cryptographic keys, using hardware entropy sources where available.

4.3 Modes of Operation and Protocol Choices

The choice of cryptographic mode is as important as the choice of algorithm. The institution mandates authenticated encryption (encryption that simultaneously provides confidentiality and integrity) for all encryption operations. Encryption without authentication is prohibited because it provides confidentiality without integrity, allowing an attacker to modify ciphertext without detection.

Specifically: GCM mode provides authenticated encryption for general use. XTS mode is an exception permitted only for full-disk encryption, where the sector-level operation model makes GCM impractical; XTS does not provide authentication, so full-disk encryption must be supplemented with filesystem-level integrity verification. Nonce/IV management must follow the standards for each mode -- GCM nonces must never be reused with the same key, which means either using a counter-based nonce scheme or using random nonces with a sufficiently large nonce space.

4.4 Post-Quantum Preparedness

Quantum computing poses a specific and quantifiable threat to the asymmetric cryptographic algorithms currently in use. Shor's algorithm, if executed on a sufficiently powerful quantum computer, would break RSA and elliptic curve cryptography. Grover's algorithm would halve the effective security of symmetric algorithms and hash functions, which is why the institution uses 256-bit symmetric keys rather than 128-bit.

The institution's post-quantum preparedness strategy has three components. First, monitor the maturation of NIST post-quantum standards (ML-KEM, ML-DSA, SLH-DSA, and their successors). Second, when these standards have been subjected to at least five years of public cryptanalysis beyond their standardization date, begin the migration of asymmetric operations to post-quantum algorithms. Third, in the interim, use hybrid schemes where feasible -- combining a classical algorithm with a post-quantum algorithm so that the combined scheme is secure if either algorithm remains unbroken.

The detailed migration timeline and procedures are defined in SEC-005. This article establishes the principle: the institution must be prepared to replace its asymmetric cryptography entirely, and the architecture must support that replacement without data loss.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-CP-01: No cryptographic algorithm may be used within the institution unless it appears on the approved list in Section 4.2 of this document or a subsequent amendment. Unapproved algorithms are prohibited regardless of their theoretical properties.
  • R-CP-02: All encryption operations must use authenticated encryption. Encryption without integrity verification is prohibited. The sole exception is full-disk encryption using XTS mode, which must be supplemented with filesystem-level integrity checks.
  • R-CP-03: Key lengths must meet or exceed the conservative parameters defined in this document. No reduction in key length is permitted for performance, compatibility, or convenience reasons.
  • R-CP-04: All archival data must be hashed with at least two independent hash functions from different algorithmic families. This dual-hash requirement protects against the catastrophic failure of any single hash function.
  • R-CP-05: Cryptographic random number generation must use only the operating system's CSPRNG. Deterministic seeding, reduced entropy pools, or userspace PRNGs are prohibited for any cryptographic purpose.
  • R-CP-06: Every encrypted artifact must be tagged with metadata identifying the algorithm, mode, key identifier, and date of encryption. This metadata is stored alongside the ciphertext and is not itself encrypted (it is not secret; recall Kerckhoffs' principle).
  • R-CP-07: The approved algorithm list must be reviewed annually against current cryptographic research. Any algorithm for which a practical attack (as opposed to a theoretical attack with impractical complexity) has been published must be flagged for immediate review and potential removal from the approved list.
  • R-CP-08: No security measure in the institution may depend on the secrecy of an algorithm, a mode of operation, or a system design. Security must derive entirely from the secrecy of cryptographic keys and the correct implementation of public algorithms.

Failure Modes

  • Algorithm compromise. A cryptographic algorithm on the approved list is found to have a practical vulnerability. Depending on the severity, this ranges from an inconvenience (requiring migration to an alternative) to a catastrophe (if data encrypted under the compromised algorithm has been exfiltrated, its confidentiality is lost permanently). Mitigation: the dual-hash requirement for integrity, the conservative parameter selection for encryption, and the algorithm agility requirement all provide layers of defense. The annual review ensures that emerging threats are detected early.
  • Implementation vulnerability. The algorithm is sound, but the specific software implementation contains a bug that leaks key material, introduces timing side channels, or otherwise undermines the cryptographic guarantee. Mitigation: use only well-audited, widely deployed cryptographic libraries. Prefer implementations that have been formally verified where available. Never implement cryptographic primitives from scratch.
  • Nonce reuse. In GCM mode, reusing a nonce with the same key catastrophically compromises both confidentiality and authenticity. Mitigation: use counter-based nonce management for all GCM operations. Document and enforce the nonce scheme. Test for nonce uniqueness as part of regular audits.
  • Entropy starvation. The random number generator does not have sufficient entropy, producing predictable output that weakens key generation. Mitigation: verify entropy pool status before key generation. Use hardware entropy sources. Never generate keys immediately after system boot when the entropy pool may be empty.
  • Cryptographic stagnation. The institution fails to update its cryptographic standards as the field advances. Algorithms that were strong when adopted become weak, but inertia prevents migration. Mitigation: the annual review mandate, the algorithm agility requirement, and the detailed migration planning in SEC-005.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If an approved algorithm is compromised: Immediately assess the scope of exposure. Identify all data protected by the compromised algorithm. If the compromise affects confidentiality (encryption), prioritize re-encrypting the most sensitive data under a non-compromised algorithm. If the compromise affects integrity (hash function), re-hash all affected data using the remaining approved hash function and expedite adoption of a replacement. Update this document to remove the compromised algorithm from the approved list and add a replacement. Document the incident in the Commentary Section.
  2. If an implementation vulnerability is discovered: Identify all systems using the vulnerable implementation. Patch or replace the implementation. Assess whether the vulnerability was exploitable in the institution's specific deployment (air-gapped systems may not be vulnerable to network-based timing attacks, for example). If exploitation is possible, treat as a potential breach per SEC-008.
  3. If nonce reuse is detected: Identify all data encrypted with the reused nonce. Assume that the authentication tag for affected ciphertexts is no longer reliable. Re-encrypt affected data with fresh nonces. Investigate the cause of the reuse and fix the nonce management scheme.
  4. If entropy starvation is suspected: Identify all keys generated during the period of suspected starvation. Regenerate those keys using a verified entropy source. Re-encrypt all data that was protected by the suspect keys. This is a labor-intensive process, which is why prevention (verifying entropy before key generation) is critical.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Establish the cryptographic infrastructure based on the algorithms selected in this document. Implement the dual-hash scheme for all archival data. Verify that all encryption uses authenticated modes. Build and test the algorithm migration framework so that it is ready when needed, not built under pressure.
  • Years 5-10: Conduct the first major cryptographic review. Assess whether the approved algorithms remain strong. Evaluate the maturity of post-quantum standards. If NIST post-quantum algorithms have accumulated sufficient cryptanalytic confidence, begin planning hybrid deployments.
  • Years 10-20: Quantum computing may begin to pose practical threats to asymmetric cryptography. Execute the migration to post-quantum algorithms for key agreement and digital signatures. AES-256 and SHA-3-256 should remain strong against quantum attacks at this horizon due to their 128-bit and 128-bit post-quantum security levels respectively.
  • Years 20-35: The cryptographic landscape may have changed in ways that are difficult to predict from the present vantage point. New mathematical discoveries, new computational paradigms, or new implementation technologies may require a fundamental reassessment of the institution's cryptographic choices. The algorithm agility requirement ensures that this reassessment can be acted upon without architectural rework.
  • Years 35-50+: Multiple algorithm generations will have come and gone. The institution's cryptographic history -- which algorithms were used, when they were replaced, and why -- becomes itself a valuable record. Ensure that this history is preserved in the Commentary Section and that all data remains accessible regardless of which generation of algorithms was used to protect it.

Founder Commentary

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: Choosing cryptographic algorithms feels like choosing which mountain to build your house on. You are making a bet that this particular piece of mathematics will remain solid for decades. You are betting against the collective ingenuity of every mathematician and every computer that will exist in the next fifty years. That is not a bet you can win with certainty. It is a bet you can improve with conservatism.

I have chosen algorithms that are boring. AES is boring. SHA-3 is boring. Ed25519 is boring. They are boring because they have been studied exhaustively, attacked relentlessly, and survived. The exciting new algorithm that promises better performance or smaller keys is exactly what this institution does not need. We need proven algorithms with wide safety margins, implemented in well-audited code, managed with discipline.

The one thing I know with certainty is that some of these choices will eventually be wrong. An algorithm will weaken. A mode will be found to have a subtle flaw under some condition we did not anticipate. When that happens, the institution's survival depends not on having chosen the right algorithm in 2026, but on having built the machinery to replace it. Algorithm agility is not a nice-to-have. It is the cryptographic survival strategy.
