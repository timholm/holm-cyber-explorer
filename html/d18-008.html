D18-008 — Trust Scoring Framework

Document ID
D18-008
Domain
18 -- Import & Quarantine
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D18-005 (Provenance Tracking), D18-001 (Import Philosophy), D18-007 (Format Recognition)
Depended Upon By
D18-009 (Rejection & Appeal), D18-011 (Inter-Node Import), D18-002 (Validation Pipeline -- trust-dependent routing)

Purpose

This article defines the Trust Scoring Framework for the holm.chat Documentation Institution -- the systematic method by which the institution assesses how much confidence to place in incoming content during the import process. D18-001 established that trust is not binary and identified four trust dimensions (source, carrier, format, content). This article operationalizes those dimensions into a concrete scoring system that import operators and automated validation tools can use to make consistent, explainable, and auditable trust decisions.

Without a trust scoring framework, every import decision is ad hoc. One operator might admit content that another would reject. Decisions cannot be compared, reviewed, or improved upon because there is no common language for expressing the basis of trust. The framework provides that language.

Trust scoring does not replace human judgment. It informs it. The score is a structured summary of what is known about a piece of content's trustworthiness. The human operator uses that summary, along with context that no scoring system can capture, to make the final decision. The framework's value is in making the basis of trust visible, consistent, and correctable.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The four trust dimensions and how each is assessed.
  • The scoring scale and its interpretation.
  • How individual dimension scores are combined into an aggregate trust assessment.
  • Trust decay: how scores change over time in the absence of re-assessment.
  • Trust inheritance: how trust transfers between related content items, sources, and carriers.
  • The trust registry: maintaining records of trust assessments for known sources and carriers.
  • How trust scores influence the validation pipeline's behavior.

This article does not cover:

  • The provenance data that feeds trust scoring (see D18-005, Provenance Tracking).
  • The format classification that feeds format trust (see D18-007, Format Recognition).
  • The specific validation checks triggered by trust levels (see D18-002, Validation Pipeline).
  • Inter-node trust relationships for federated imports (see D18-011, though this article defines the trust model that D18-011 extends).
  • The final admission/rejection decision (see D18-009, Rejection & Appeal).

Background

The Problem with Binary Trust

Many systems treat trust as binary: a source is either trusted or untrusted. This creates two failure modes. First, trusted sources are not scrutinized, so their content is admitted without adequate validation -- and trusted sources can be compromised, can make mistakes, or can degrade over time. Second, untrusted sources are treated with maximum suspicion, which discourages importing content from new or unfamiliar sources even when that content might be valuable. Binary trust is too coarse a tool for the nuanced decisions that import requires.

Why Four Dimensions

Trust in imported content is not a single quality. Consider: a well-known author (high source trust) publishes a paper that was downloaded from an anonymous file-sharing site (low carrier trust) in a proprietary format (low format trust) and the content contains internally contradictory data (low content trust). No single trust score captures this situation. The four-dimensional model allows the institution to assess and communicate the specific nature of its trust or distrust, enabling more appropriate responses.

Trust Decay as a Safety Mechanism

Trust scores that never change are dangerous. A source assessed as trustworthy in 2026 may not deserve that assessment in 2036. The source may have changed, the carrier may have been compromised, the format may have become obsolete, or the standards for content quality may have evolved. Trust decay -- the gradual reduction of trust scores in the absence of re-assessment -- ensures that the institution never rests on stale trust judgments. Trust must be periodically re-earned.

System Model

Trust Dimensions in Detail

Dimension 1: Source Trust (ST). Assesses the originator of the content. Factors include: Is the source identified or anonymous? Does the source have a track record with the institution? Has previous content from this source been reliable? Is the source a recognized authority in the content's subject area? Source trust ranges from 0 (completely unknown, no basis for trust) to 5 (established source with extensive positive track record).

Dimension 2: Carrier Trust (CT). Assesses the chain of custody between the source and the institution. Factors include: How many intermediaries handled the content? Is the carrier known and accountable? Was the content integrity-protected during transit (e.g., checksums, signatures)? Is the carrier the source themselves, or a third party? Carrier trust ranges from 0 (unknown chain of custody, no integrity protection) to 5 (direct from source with verified integrity).

Dimension 3: Format Trust (FT). Assesses the file format. This is derived primarily from the Format Registry (D18-007). Factors include: Is the format open or proprietary? Is the format executable or static? Is the format well-documented? Is the format in current use or obsolete? Format trust ranges from 0 (unknown format, executable, or undocumented) to 5 (open, static, well-documented, widely used).

Dimension 4: Content Trust (CoT). Assesses the content itself after examination. Factors include: Is the content internally consistent? Does it match its claimed description? Is the content verifiable against external references? Is the content well-structured and complete? Content trust ranges from 0 (contradictory, unverifiable, or anomalous) to 5 (internally consistent, verifiable, well-structured).

Aggregate Trust Assessment

  Aggregate Trust = min(ST, CT, FT, CoT)

  The aggregate trust level is equal to the LOWEST
  individual dimension score. This is a conservative
  model: a chain is only as strong as its weakest link.

  Example:
    Source Trust:  4 (known, reliable author)
    Carrier Trust: 1 (passed through unknown intermediary)
    Format Trust:  5 (plain text UTF-8)
    Content Trust: 4 (consistent, well-structured)

    Aggregate Trust: 1 (due to low carrier trust)

  This means the content requires full validation
  despite high marks in three dimensions.

Trust Score Interpretation

Aggregate ScoreLabelPipeline Behavior
5HIGHExpedited validation; automated checks sufficient for standard content
4MODERATE-HIGHStandard validation; automated checks plus spot-check by operator
3MODERATEFull validation; all automated checks plus operator review of flagged items
2LOWEnhanced validation; all checks plus mandatory manual review of all content
1VERY LOWMaximum scrutiny; every item individually examined; consider rejection if cost of validation exceeds expected value
0UNTRUSTEDPresumptive rejection; may proceed only with explicit operator justification documented in import log

Trust Decay Model

Trust scores for sources and carriers decay over time if not re-assessed. The decay rate is:

  • Source Trust: decays by 1 point every 24 months without a new positive interaction (successful import from that source with no quality issues).
  • Carrier Trust: decays by 1 point every 12 months without a new verified delivery.
  • Format Trust: does not decay on a time basis but is re-assessed when the Format Registry is reviewed (per D18-007, R-D18-F05).
  • Content Trust: does not decay; it is assessed per-item at import time.

Decay does not reduce scores below 1 for previously-assessed entities. An entity that was once assessed cannot decay to 0 (unknown); it can only decay to 1 (known but stale). Reaching 0 requires an explicit negative event (e.g., discovery that a source provided fabricated content).

Trust Inheritance

When new content arrives from a source that has an existing trust record, the source trust dimension inherits the recorded score (subject to decay). This avoids re-assessing known sources from scratch. However, inheritance is not automatic promotion: a previously trusted source that sends content through an untrusted carrier still receives a low aggregate score.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D18-T01: All Four Dimensions Required. Every import MUST have an assessment for all four trust dimensions before the admission decision is made. If any dimension cannot be assessed (e.g., source is unknown), that dimension MUST be scored 0.

  • R-D18-T02: Aggregate Uses Minimum. The aggregate trust score MUST be the minimum of the four dimension scores. Alternative aggregation methods (average, weighted sum) are explicitly rejected because they can mask low scores in critical dimensions.

  • R-D18-T03: Trust Scores Are Recorded. Every trust score assessment MUST be recorded in the import log alongside the import record. The recording MUST include each dimension score, the aggregate score, the basis for each assessment, and the identity of the assessor (human or automated system).

  • R-D18-T04: Trust Decay Is Automatic. The trust registry MUST apply decay to source and carrier trust scores according to the defined schedule. Decay MUST NOT require manual intervention -- it is a systemic safety mechanism, not a judgment call.

  • R-D18-T05: Trust Cannot Be Assigned by Fiat. No operator, administrator, or governance body may assign a trust score without providing a documented justification. Trust scores SHOULD be derived from observable evidence (track record, provenance data, format analysis, content examination). Trust by assertion alone is forbidden.

  • R-D18-T06: Negative Events Reset Trust. If content from a previously trusted source is found to be harmful, corrupted, or fabricated, that source's trust score MUST be immediately re-assessed. The re-assessment MUST consider the nature and severity of the negative event. A single incident of corruption may reduce trust by 1-2 points; deliberate fabrication may reduce trust to 0.

  • R-D18-T07: Trust Framework Is Auditable. The trust scoring framework itself MUST be auditable by Domain 19 (QA). This includes: Are scores being applied consistently? Are decay rules being followed? Are inheritance rules being applied correctly? Is the aggregate calculation correct? QA may audit any trust assessment.

Failure Modes

  • Trust inflation. Over time, frequently-used sources accumulate high trust scores that are rarely challenged. The institution develops blind spots -- content from high-trust sources receives insufficient scrutiny. A compromise of a high-trust source has outsized impact because the institution's defenses are relaxed. Likelihood: Moderate over long periods. Severity: High. Detection: Periodic audit of trust scores. Random full-validation of content from high-trust sources regardless of score.

  • Trust deflation. Aggressive decay or overcautious scoring drives all trust scores downward. Every import requires maximum scrutiny. Import throughput collapses. Operators lose faith in the scoring system and begin ignoring it. Likelihood: Moderate, especially after a negative trust event. Severity: Medium. Detection: Monitor average trust scores over time. If scores trend uniformly downward, the framework may be over-penalizing.

  • Gaming the trust system. A malicious actor establishes a source with a positive track record (high source trust) through a series of legitimate imports, then exploits the high trust score to import harmful content with reduced scrutiny. Likelihood: Low but catastrophic if successful. Severity: Critical. Detection: Random full-validation regardless of trust score (defense-in-depth). Anomaly detection in content patterns from trusted sources.

  • Dimension masking. The minimum-based aggregation correctly flags low scores, but operators may not investigate which dimension caused the low aggregate. A score of 1 could mean "unknown source" or "dangerous format" -- very different situations requiring different responses. Likelihood: Moderate if operators are not trained. Severity: Medium. Detection: Import log review should confirm that operators noted the specific dimension(s) driving low scores.

  • Trust registry corruption. The trust records for known sources and carriers are corrupted or tampered with. A trusted source's record is inflated, or an untrusted source's record is deleted. Likelihood: Low if the trust registry is integrity-protected. Severity: High. Detection: Checksum verification of the trust registry. Append-only storage for trust records.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If trust inflation is detected: Conduct a full audit of the trust registry. Identify all sources with scores of 4 or 5. For each, verify that the high score is justified by recent positive interactions. Apply decay to any source that has not been re-assessed within the appropriate period. Mandate random full-validation samples from high-trust sources for the next 12 months.

  2. If a high-trust source is compromised: Immediately set the source's trust score to 0. Identify all content previously imported from that source. Re-validate content imported since the suspected compromise date. If the compromise was recent, the scope may be limited. If the compromise is of unknown duration, all content from the source must be re-examined. Document the incident and update the trust registry with a permanent note.

  3. If the trust registry is corrupted: Restore from the most recent verified backup. If no backup is available, reconstruct trust records from the import log (which records trust assessments for every import). All active trust scores should be independently verifiable from import log data. Implement additional integrity protection for the trust registry.

  4. If operators are ignoring trust scores: Investigate why. Common reasons: scores are perceived as inaccurate, the scoring system is too complex, or the framework adds processing time without perceived benefit. Address the root cause. If the framework is too complex, simplify it. If scores are inaccurate, recalibrate. If the framework is sound but poorly understood, invest in training (D18-014). Do not respond by making the framework mandatory without addressing operator concerns -- forced compliance without buy-in produces worse outcomes than voluntary non-compliance.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The trust scoring framework is established with the simple 0-5 scale and four dimensions. Expect calibration issues -- initial scores may be too generous or too strict. Use the first two years of import data to calibrate the scoring guidelines. The trust registry begins with few entries and grows as the institution encounters repeat sources and carriers.

  • Years 5-15: The trust registry is populated with years of operational data. Trust decay should be producing observable effects -- sources not recently used should have reduced scores. The institution can begin analyzing trust patterns: Which sources are consistently reliable? Which carriers have the best track records? Are there format-level trust patterns that the Format Registry should reflect?

  • Years 15-30: Generational change among operators means the trust framework must be self-explanatory. New operators must be able to understand and apply the framework without relying on institutional oral tradition about "which sources to trust." The documented trust registry and import log together provide this institutional knowledge.

  • Years 30-50+: The trust framework's longevity depends on its simplicity. The four-dimension model and the minimum-based aggregation are simple enough to be re-implemented on any platform. If future maintainers inherit the principle (trust is multi-dimensional, aggregation is conservative, trust decays) they can rebuild the specific implementation. If they inherit only the implementation without the principle, the framework will break on the first significant change.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The decision to use minimum-based aggregation rather than weighted averaging was deliberate and will be controversial. Weighted averages allow a high score in one dimension to compensate for a low score in another. This is precisely the behavior we want to prevent. If the carrier trust is 1, it does not matter that the source trust is 5 -- the chain of custody is compromised. A weighted average would produce a reassuring overall score that masks a critical weakness. The minimum function is blunt, but in security-critical systems, blunt is often correct.

The 0-5 scale was chosen for human readability. A more granular scale (0-100) would provide finer discrimination but would be harder for operators to calibrate consistently. Two operators assessing the same content would be unlikely to independently arrive at the same score on a 100-point scale. On a 6-point scale, inter-operator agreement is more achievable. Precision that cannot be reliably reproduced is false precision.

Open question: Should content from federated nodes receive a baseline trust bonus? D18-011 (Inter-Node Import) will address this, but the philosophical question belongs here. The argument for: federated nodes are part of the institution and their content has already passed some validation. The argument against: a federated node may have been compromised, or its standards may have diverged. The current framework treats federated content the same as external content in terms of the trust model, with the node's trust record serving as the source trust dimension. Future operators should evaluate whether this is too strict or too lenient based on federation experience.
