D20-005 — Oral History Capture Methodology

Document ID
D20-005
Domain
20 — Institutional Memory
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D20-001 (Philosophy), D20-011 (Memory Taxonomy)
Depended Upon By
D20-007 (Context Recovery), D20-006 (Legacy Preservation)

Purpose

This article defines the methodology for capturing oral history within the holm.chat Documentation Institution. Oral history is the systematic process of recording knowledge, context, reasoning, and experience that exists in people's heads but has not been written down. It is the institution's primary defense against the most perishable form of knowledge: the knowledge that walks out the door when a person leaves.

Written documentation captures what was decided. Oral history captures what was felt, what was doubted, what was assumed, what was obvious at the time but will not be obvious later. The decision log records that Format X was chosen over Format Y. The oral history records that the team was exhausted, that the deadline was arbitrary, that the person who championed Format Y had reservations about Format X that were never fully addressed, and that the real reason Format X won was that its documentation was better at the time, not that it was technically superior.

This article is addressed to every person who will conduct an oral history interview, every person who will be interviewed, and every future maintainer who will read the resulting transcripts and attempt to reconstruct the context of decisions made by people they never met. The methodology described here is designed to be executable by non-specialists. It does not require training in historiography, journalism, or ethnography. It requires patience, a recording device, and the discipline to ask "why" more than once.

The institution cannot afford to lose the knowledge held in its members' heads. That knowledge is irreplaceable once the person departs. Every day that passes without capture is a day of risk. This article provides the net; the institution must provide the will to cast it.

Scope

This article covers:

  • When oral history capture should be initiated and by whom.
  • How to prepare for an oral history session.
  • Interview techniques appropriate for institutional knowledge extraction.
  • Recording formats, storage, and redundancy requirements.
  • Transcription standards and procedures.
  • Integration of oral history material into the institutional archive.
  • Ethical considerations: consent, privacy, correction rights, and withdrawal.
  • Quality criteria for oral history records.

This article does not cover:

  • The philosophy of why institutional memory matters (see D20-001).
  • The decision log format or procedures (see D20-002).
  • The timeline construction process, though oral histories feed the timeline (see D20-003).
  • The memory taxonomy used to classify oral history records (see D20-011).
  • The interface for accessing oral history records (covered by Domain 16).
  • The quality audit of oral history records (covered by Domain 19).

Background

3.1 The Problem of Tacit Knowledge

Every institution runs on two kinds of knowledge: explicit knowledge (written down, findable, transferable) and tacit knowledge (held in people's heads, invisible until needed, lost when the person leaves). Explicit knowledge is what the documentation says. Tacit knowledge is what the experienced operator knows that the documentation does not say: the workaround for the hardware quirk, the reason the backup schedule is set to that specific time, the personality dynamic that shaped the governance structure, the thing that almost went catastrophically wrong but was caught at the last moment and never documented because everyone was too exhausted afterward.

Tacit knowledge is, paradoxically, the most valuable and the most fragile. It is valuable because it captures the reality of operations, not just the theory. It is fragile because it exists only in memory, and memory is mortal. The purpose of oral history is to convert tacit knowledge into explicit knowledge before the conversion becomes impossible.

3.2 Why Written Documentation Is Not Enough

Written documentation captures the intended state of things. Oral history captures the actual state. Documentation says "the backup runs at 0300." Oral history reveals "the backup was moved to 0300 because at 0200 there was a power fluctuation that corrupted the process twice, and we never updated the documentation because the person who knew how to edit the config file had already left."

Furthermore, written documentation is filtered through the author's judgment about what matters. The author decides what to include and what to omit. Oral history, conducted well, captures things the subject did not think to write down -- things that seemed too obvious, too trivial, or too embarrassing to document. The interviewer's job is to surface these omissions by asking questions the subject would not have thought to ask themselves.

3.3 Prior Art

Oral history as a discipline has been practiced by historians, anthropologists, and journalists for over a century. The methodology described here draws on established practices: open-ended questioning, active listening, the funnel technique (broad to narrow), and the principle that the interviewer's job is to facilitate, not to lead. However, this methodology is adapted for institutional rather than personal history. The goal is not biography. It is operational knowledge extraction. The product is not narrative. It is indexed, searchable, cross-referenced institutional memory.

4.2 Trigger Events

Oral history capture is triggered by any of the following events:

  • Planned departure: A member announces they will leave the institution. Oral history capture must begin within 7 days of the announcement and be completed before departure.
  • Role transition: A member is changing roles within the institution. Their knowledge of the outgoing role must be captured before the transition completes.
  • Milestone completion: A major project, system deployment, or institutional event has concluded. Participants' recollections and reflections should be captured within 30 days while memory is fresh.
  • Scheduled periodic capture: Every 6 months, the institution conducts at least one oral history session with a long-serving member, regardless of whether any trigger event has occurred. This is preventive capture.
  • Context recovery need: A future maintainer identifies a gap in the record and a living witness exists. Oral history is conducted to fill the gap.
  • Unplanned departure: A member has left without warning. If they can be contacted, conduct an emergency remote session. If not, conduct sessions with those who worked most closely with them to capture secondhand knowledge.

4.3 Session Structure

Each oral history session follows a standard structure designed to maximize knowledge extraction while minimizing subject fatigue:

  1. Opening (5 minutes): State the date, the names of the interviewer and subject, the purpose of the session, and the consent confirmation. This creates the metadata header for the recording.
  2. Broad context (15 minutes): Ask the subject to describe their role, how long they have held it, what they consider the most important things they do, and what they worry would be lost if they left tomorrow.
  3. Deep dive (30-45 minutes): Using the interviewer's prepared question list (derived from the pre-session review of documentation and decision logs), explore specific areas of knowledge. Use the funnel technique: start with open-ended questions, then narrow based on what the subject reveals.
  4. Undocumented knowledge sweep (15 minutes): Explicitly ask: "What do you know that is not written down anywhere? What would surprise your successor? What has almost gone wrong that nobody else knows about?"
  5. Closing (5 minutes): Ask if there is anything the subject wants to add that was not covered. Confirm they will review the transcript. Thank them.

Total session length: 70-85 minutes. Sessions longer than 90 minutes suffer diminishing returns and should be split into multiple sessions.

4.4 Recording and Storage

Every oral history session must be recorded in at least two formats simultaneously: an audio recording and the interviewer's written notes. The audio recording is the primary record. The notes are the backup and the basis for the transcription index.

Recordings are stored as Tier 1 permanent records (per D20-001, Section 4.1). They are stored in at least two physically separate locations. The audio format must be uncompressed or losslessly compressed and must use an open, non-proprietary codec. Transcripts are stored alongside the audio, cross-referenced by timestamp.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D20-05-01: Departure Capture Is Mandatory. When a member announces a planned departure, at least one oral history session must be conducted before their departure date. If the departure window is less than 7 days, an abbreviated emergency session must be conducted within 48 hours of the announcement. There are no exceptions to this rule. A departure without oral history capture is an institutional failure.

  • R-D20-05-02: Consent Is Required. No oral history session may be recorded without the explicit, informed consent of the subject. The subject must be told: what will be recorded, how the recording will be stored, who will have access, that they may review and annotate the transcript, and that they may request redaction of specific personal information (but not of institutional decisions or operational knowledge). Consent must be recorded at the beginning of each session.

  • R-D20-05-03: Transcription Within 30 Days. Every recorded oral history session must be transcribed within 30 days of the session. If the institution lacks the capacity for verbatim transcription, a detailed summary transcript with timestamped references to the audio is acceptable. The audio recording itself remains the authoritative record.

  • R-D20-05-04: Subject Review. The subject must be given the opportunity to review the transcript before it is integrated into the archive. They may add annotations, correct factual errors in the transcript (not alter what they said, but note where the transcription is inaccurate), and flag personal information for redaction review. The review period is 14 days from transcript delivery. If no response is received within 14 days, the transcript is integrated as-is.

  • R-D20-05-05: Two Trained Interviewers. At least two members of the institution must be trained in oral history interview technique at all times. If only one trained interviewer remains, training a second interviewer becomes an immediate priority. Training consists of reading this article, conducting a practice interview under supervision, and reviewing at least three previous oral history transcripts.

  • R-D20-05-06: Cross-Reference Required. Every oral history transcript must be indexed and cross-referenced to relevant decision log entries (D20-002), timeline events (D20-003), and domain articles. An unindexed transcript is nearly useless to future researchers. The indexing must be completed within 14 days of transcription.

  • R-D20-05-07: No Leading Questions on Disputed Matters. When the oral history touches on disputed decisions or contested events, the interviewer must not lead the subject toward a particular interpretation. The subject's account is recorded as their account, not as institutional fact. If multiple subjects give conflicting accounts of the same event, all accounts are preserved. Conflicts are noted but not resolved by the interviewer.

Failure Modes

  • Departure without capture. A member leaves and no oral history was conducted. Their tacit knowledge is lost. Severity: high. Likelihood: moderate (especially for unplanned departures). Detection: the operational review cycle (OPS-001) includes a check for uncaptured departures. Mitigation: preventive periodic capture ensures that even if a departure-specific session is missed, some of the member's knowledge has already been recorded.

  • Superficial capture. An oral history session is conducted, but the interviewer fails to probe deeply enough. The transcript contains only information that was already documented. Severity: moderate. Likelihood: moderate (especially when the interviewer is inexperienced). Detection: quality review of transcripts against existing documentation. If the transcript adds no new information, the session was superficial. Mitigation: the pre-session preparation step, which requires the interviewer to identify specific gaps in the documentation to probe.

  • Transcription failure. The recording exists but is never transcribed. It sits in the archive, accessible only to someone who has time to listen to the entire recording. Severity: moderate. Likelihood: high (transcription is labor-intensive and often deprioritized). Detection: the 30-day transcription deadline (R-D20-05-03). Mitigation: accept summary transcripts with timestamps when verbatim transcription is not feasible.

  • Consent violation. An oral history is conducted or distributed without proper consent. Severity: high (undermines trust in the entire oral history program). Likelihood: low. Detection: the consent statement at the beginning of each recording. Mitigation: the consent protocol is non-negotiable and is verified during quality audit.

  • Indexing failure. Transcripts exist but are not cross-referenced. A future researcher cannot find the relevant oral history when they need it. Severity: moderate. Likelihood: high. Detection: periodic audit of oral history index completeness. Mitigation: R-D20-05-06 mandates indexing within 14 days.

  • Single interviewer risk. Only one person in the institution knows how to conduct oral histories. If they leave, the capability is lost. Severity: high. Likelihood: moderate. Detection: the two-interviewer rule (R-D20-05-05). Mitigation: training is documented in this article and requires no external resources.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If a departure occurred without capture: Determine whether the departed member can be contacted. If yes, arrange a remote session (even a phone call with notes is better than nothing). If no, conduct secondhand capture sessions with the people who worked most closely with the departed member. Document what was lost: create a "KNOWLEDGE GAP" entry in the decision log identifying the areas where institutional knowledge was diminished by the uncaptured departure.

  2. If transcription is overdue: Prioritize the oldest unprocessed recordings. If verbatim transcription is impossible, create summary transcripts with timestamps. If even summary transcription is impossible, create a metadata record for the recording that describes its content at a high level (who, when, topics covered) so that a future researcher knows the recording exists and can listen to it when needed.

  3. If the index is incomplete: Conduct an index audit. List all oral history transcripts. For each, verify that it is cross-referenced to relevant decision log entries and timeline events. Prioritize indexing by institutional significance: sessions with founders and long-serving members first, routine periodic captures second.

  4. If interviewer capacity is lost: The remaining trained interviewer (if any) must immediately train a replacement using the procedure in R-D20-05-05. If no trained interviewer remains, any member may conduct an oral history session by following the session structure in Section 4.3 of this article. The session will be less polished but will still capture knowledge that would otherwise be lost. Perfect is the enemy of captured.

  5. If a consent dispute arises: Halt distribution of the disputed transcript. Review the consent recording from the session. If consent was properly obtained, document the dispute and explain the consent scope to the subject. If consent was not properly obtained, quarantine the transcript and conduct a new session with proper consent if the subject is willing. If they are not willing, the transcript is sealed (not destroyed) with a note explaining why it is sealed, pending future resolution.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: The oral history program is being established. The first sessions are practice as much as production. Interviewers are learning the technique. Subjects are learning to trust the process. The archive is small. This is the period to establish the habit. Conduct at least one session every three months, even if no trigger event has occurred. Build the muscle memory of capture.

  • Years 3-10: The archive grows. Patterns emerge. Cross-referencing reveals connections between sessions. The institution begins to rely on oral histories for context recovery. The methodology may need refinement based on experience: are sessions too long? Too short? Are the questions effective? Is the transcription process sustainable? Revise this article based on operational evidence.

  • Years 10-25: Succession events test the oral history archive. New members who never met the founders read transcripts to understand why the institution works the way it works. The quality of the oral history program is measured by how well these new members can reconstruct context. If they can, the program has succeeded. If they cannot, the sessions were too superficial or too poorly indexed.

  • Years 25-50: The oral history archive is one of the institution's most valuable assets. It contains the voices and reasoning of people who are no longer present. It is the closest thing the institution has to a conversation with its past. The maintenance challenge shifts from capture to curation: which sessions are most valuable? How are they surfaced to new members? How is the archive kept navigable as it grows?

Revision triggers: This article should be revised when operational experience reveals gaps in the methodology, when new recording technologies become available, when the institution's size changes significantly (more members means more potential subjects and more interviewers needed), or when a succession event reveals that the oral history archive was insufficient.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: I write this article with some trepidation, because the most important oral histories are the ones that have not been conducted yet. The knowledge in the founders' heads is, right now, the richest and most perishable knowledge the institution possesses. Every day that passes without capturing it is a day of risk.

The methodology here is deliberately simple. I resisted the temptation to create an elaborate protocol with multiple form types, scoring rubrics, and mandatory question banks. That kind of complexity kills programs. The oral history program will succeed if it is easy enough that people actually do it. It will fail if it is so rigorous that people avoid it.

A note on the emotional dimension: oral history sessions can be emotionally significant. When a long-serving member sits down to describe what they know, they are acknowledging their own eventual absence from the institution. The interviewer should be sensitive to this. The session is not an interrogation. It is a gift of knowledge from one generation to the next. Treat it accordingly.

A practical note: the best time to conduct an oral history is before you need it. The second-best time is now.
