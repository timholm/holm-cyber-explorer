D11-012 — Facilities and Physical Plant Management

Document ID
D11-012
Domain
11 — Administration
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Draft

Purpose

This article defines how the institution maintains its physical spaces, utilities, environmental controls, and physical security infrastructure as administrative functions. The physical plant is the material foundation upon which all digital systems rest. Without adequate physical infrastructure, the most carefully designed storage arrays, the most meticulously planned backup schedules, and the most thorough documentation are all vulnerable to environmental threats: water damage, excessive heat, dust, vermin, structural failure, and electrical hazards.

Facilities management in a self-sovereign institution is not delegated to a landlord or a building management company. The operator is responsible for every aspect of the physical environment, from roof integrity to cable routing to pest control. This article establishes the maintenance schedules, inspection procedures, environmental standards, and documentation requirements that keep the physical plant functional over decades.

Scope

In scope:

  • Physical spaces: the rooms, enclosures, and areas where institutional systems are housed.
  • Environmental controls: temperature management, humidity control, dust mitigation, ventilation.
  • Utilities: electrical wiring, water supply/drainage (if applicable), lighting.
  • Physical security: locks, barriers, access controls for physical spaces.
  • Cable management and physical network infrastructure.
  • Pest and vermin control.
  • Structural maintenance: roof, walls, floors, foundations.
  • Fire prevention and suppression.
  • Inspection schedules and maintenance procedures for all physical infrastructure.

Out of scope:

  • Power generation and storage systems (Domain 4, though heavily interdependent).
  • Computing hardware maintenance (Domain 5).
  • Cybersecurity (Domain 3, though physical security overlaps).
  • Construction of new facilities (a governance and planning decision before it becomes an administrative one).

Background

The Physical Environment as Threat Surface

In discussions of institutional resilience, the physical environment is often overlooked in favor of hardware redundancy and data backup strategies. This is a dangerous oversight. A data center with three-way drive redundancy is still destroyed by a flood. A meticulously maintained RAID array is still killed by sustained temperatures above its operating threshold. A backup drive stored in a damp room will develop corrosion and data rot regardless of the filesystem's integrity checks.

The physical environment is the largest and least controllable threat surface the institution faces. Hardware failures are bounded and predictable; environmental failures can be cascading and catastrophic. A roof leak during a storm can damage electrical systems, which can cause power surges, which can corrupt data, which can overwhelm backup capacity. The chain of causation from physical environment to data loss can be startlingly short.

Off-Grid Physical Plant Challenges

An off-grid institution faces physical plant challenges that connected, urban installations do not. There is no municipal water pressure; water management (if applicable) is self-contained. There is no grid power; all electrical infrastructure is self-generated and self-maintained. There may be no fire department response within a meaningful timeframe. There may be no HVAC contractor to call when the cooling system fails.

These constraints mean that the operator must maintain a level of physical plant competence that goes well beyond what most technology professionals possess. Facilities management in this institution requires knowledge of basic construction, electrical wiring, plumbing (if applicable), environmental physics (thermal management, humidity control), and practical pest management. Deficiencies in these skills are personnel vulnerabilities that should be documented in the skills inventory (D11-006).

The Slow Failure of Physical Infrastructure

Physical infrastructure degrades slowly. A roof does not fail suddenly; it develops small leaks that grow over months and years. Wiring insulation degrades over decades, gradually increasing fire risk. Structural settling creates cracks that admit moisture. Rodents chew through cable insulation over weeks, not minutes. Because these failures are slow, they are easy to ignore. The daily operational routine does not encounter them unless the operator deliberately looks for them. Scheduled inspection is therefore not optional; it is the only mechanism for detecting physical infrastructure degradation before it becomes a crisis.

System Model

Physical Plant Components

The institutional physical plant consists of the following component categories, each requiring specific maintenance:

Structural Components

  • Roof and exterior: Prevents water intrusion. Inspection: biannual (spring and autumn). Look for: damaged shingles or panels, clogged gutters, flashing separation, standing water, vegetation intrusion.
  • Walls and insulation: Maintains thermal envelope and prevents moisture intrusion. Inspection: annual. Look for: cracks, moisture staining, mold, insulation displacement, gaps around penetrations (cables, pipes).
  • Floors and foundations: Provides structural support and prevents ground moisture. Inspection: annual. Look for: cracks, settling, moisture wicking, standing water, pest entry points.
  • Doors and access points: Provides physical security and environmental sealing. Inspection: quarterly. Look for: hinge wear, lock function, weatherstripping condition, frame alignment.

Environmental Controls

  • Temperature management: Electronics require operating temperatures typically between 10-35 degrees Celsius, with optimal performance between 18-27 degrees Celsius. The facility must maintain temperatures within this range through passive design (insulation, ventilation, thermal mass) and active systems (fans, heaters) as needed. Temperature monitoring should be continuous where possible, or at minimum daily recorded observations.
  • Humidity control: Electronics are sensitive to both excessive humidity (condensation, corrosion) and insufficient humidity (static electricity). Target range: 30-60% relative humidity. Monitor with a hygrometer. Mitigate with ventilation, dehumidifiers, or humidifiers as needed.
  • Dust management: Dust accumulation reduces cooling efficiency, clogs fans, and can cause electrical shorts. Dust control measures: sealed enclosures where possible, air filtration for ventilated spaces, regular cleaning schedule (monthly for equipment, quarterly for deep cleaning).
  • Ventilation: Adequate airflow prevents heat buildup and moisture accumulation. Ventilation paths must be designed to avoid drawing in dust, insects, or moisture. Filter replacement or cleaning is a scheduled maintenance item.

Electrical Infrastructure

  • Wiring and circuits: All institutional wiring must meet applicable safety standards. Inspection: annual visual inspection, with professional inspection every five years where feasible. Look for: insulation degradation, overloaded circuits, corrosion, loose connections, evidence of overheating (discoloration, melting).
  • Grounding: Proper grounding prevents equipment damage from electrical faults and lightning strikes. Verification: annual ground resistance test where equipment is available.
  • Surge protection: All computing equipment must be protected by surge suppressors or UPS systems. Surge suppressors have a limited lifespan and must be replaced per manufacturer recommendations or every five years, whichever is sooner.

Cable Management

  • Cable routing: All cables (power, data, network) must be routed to avoid physical damage, avoid heat sources, and allow inspection. Cables should not rest on the floor where they can be stepped on, exposed to water, or accessed by rodents. Cable trays or conduit are preferred.
  • Cable labeling: Every cable must be labeled at both ends with its function and destination. Unlabeled cables create maintenance hazards: the operator does not know what they can safely disconnect.
  • Cable inspection: Quarterly visual inspection of all cable runs for physical damage, rodent chewing, connector corrosion, and routing degradation.

Fire Prevention

  • Prevention measures: No flammable materials stored near electrical equipment. Electrical loads within rated capacity for all circuits. No daisy-chained power strips. Lint and dust cleared from equipment regularly.
  • Detection: Smoke detectors in all rooms containing institutional equipment. Battery test: monthly. Battery replacement: annually or per manufacturer specification.
  • Suppression: Fire extinguisher accessible within the primary equipment area. Must be rated for electrical fires (Class C / CO2 or clean agent). Inspection: annual. Replacement: per manufacturer specification.

Pest Control

  • Prevention: Seal all entry points larger than 6mm. Store no food in equipment areas. Remove vegetation in contact with the building exterior. Use mesh or screens on ventilation openings.
  • Detection: Inspect for evidence of pest activity during monthly cleaning: droppings, gnaw marks on cables, nesting material, insect activity.
  • Response: If pest activity is detected, identify the species, seal the entry point, clean the affected area, and deploy appropriate control measures. Inspect all cables in the affected area for damage.

Rules and Constraints

  • R-FAC-01: All physical spaces housing institutional equipment must meet the environmental standards defined in this article (temperature, humidity, dust, ventilation). Spaces that cannot meet these standards must not be used for equipment without documented risk acceptance.
  • R-FAC-02: The facilities maintenance calendar must be followed. Deferred inspections must be rescheduled within the same quarter per D11-005 R-SCH-04. No inspection may be deferred more than once consecutively.
  • R-FAC-03: All cables must be labeled and routed according to the standards in this article. Unlabeled cables found during inspection must be identified and labeled before the inspection is considered complete.
  • R-FAC-04: Fire prevention measures are non-negotiable. Smoke detectors must be operational at all times. Fire extinguisher must be present, accessible, and current. Failure of fire safety equipment is an emergency maintenance priority that overrides all other scheduled activities.
  • R-FAC-05: Pest evidence discovered during any inspection triggers an immediate pest response as defined in this article. Pest control is not deferrable; pest damage to cables and equipment can be rapid and catastrophic.
  • R-FAC-06: All facilities maintenance activities must be recorded in the maintenance ledger (D11-009). The record includes: date, area inspected, findings (including "no issues found"), actions taken, and follow-up items.
  • R-FAC-07: Modifications to the physical plant (new cable runs, new equipment locations, structural changes) must be reflected in the facility map within one week of completion. The facility map is a critical component of the Administrative Continuity Package (D11-011).

Failure Modes

  • Deferred maintenance accumulation. Facilities inspections are consistently rescheduled because they feel less urgent than digital system maintenance. Small issues compound: a minor roof leak becomes water damage; dust accumulation becomes fan failure; a rodent entry point becomes a cable-chewing infestation. Mitigation: R-FAC-02 limits deferral. The physical plant is the foundation; neglecting it is like neglecting the foundation of a building because the rooms above seem fine.
  • Environmental drift. Temperature, humidity, or dust levels gradually shift outside acceptable ranges because environmental monitoring is inconsistent or ignored. Equipment operates under stress, reducing lifespan and reliability. Mitigation: quarterly environmental readings review catches gradual drift. Continuous monitoring (even a simple min/max thermometer) provides early warning.
  • Cable chaos. Over time, cables are added, rerouted, and replaced without updating labels or documentation. The cable plant becomes a tangle of unlabeled wires that no one can safely modify. Mitigation: R-FAC-03 requires labeling. R-FAC-07 requires documentation updates. The cable inspection (quarterly) is the enforcement mechanism.
  • Fire safety complacency. Years pass without a fire event, and fire prevention measures feel unnecessary. Smoke detector batteries are not replaced. The fire extinguisher is expired. Flammable materials accumulate near equipment. Mitigation: R-FAC-04 is absolute. Fire safety measures exist precisely because fires are rare but catastrophic. Monthly smoke detector tests and annual extinguisher inspection are minimal time investments against existential risk.
  • Invisible pest damage. Rodents or insects damage cables in concealed spaces (wall voids, ceiling cavities, under floors). The damage is not discovered until a system fails. Mitigation: prevention (sealed entry points) is more effective than detection. Monthly cleaning inspections include pest evidence checks. Cable runs in concealed spaces should be protected with conduit where feasible.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If facilities maintenance has been neglected: Conduct a comprehensive physical plant inspection covering all component categories in this article. Prioritize findings by risk: fire safety first, water intrusion second, environmental controls third, structural and cable management fourth. Address critical findings immediately. Schedule the remainder across the next two quarters. Re-establish the maintenance calendar and commit to adherence.
  2. If environmental damage has occurred (water, heat, humidity): Remove or isolate affected equipment immediately. Assess damage: is the equipment functional? Is data intact? Dry the affected area and address the environmental cause (fix the leak, repair the ventilation, seal the entry point). Do not return equipment to the space until the cause is resolved and environmental conditions are verified stable for at least 72 hours.
  3. If pest damage is discovered: Isolate the affected area. Inspect all cables and equipment in the area for damage. Replace damaged cables. Identify and seal all pest entry points in the affected area and adjacent areas. Deploy control measures. Monitor the area weekly for one month to confirm the pest issue is resolved. Document the event as an incident (D11-008).
  4. If a fire event occurs: Evacuate. Use the fire extinguisher only if the fire is small, contained, and you have a clear exit path. If the fire is beyond extinguisher capability, evacuate completely and contact emergency services if available. After the fire: do not re-enter until the space is confirmed safe. Assess damage. Invoke Domain 12 disaster recovery procedures if institutional systems are affected. Document everything.
  5. If cable infrastructure has become unmanageable: Conduct a cable audit. Trace and label every cable. Remove any cable that is not in active use. Document the updated cable plant. This is a significant time investment (potentially several days for a complex installation) but it eliminates a persistent source of maintenance confusion and risk.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: The physical plant is new or recently established. Initial conditions are known. The maintenance calendar establishes rhythms. The primary risk is deferred maintenance as the operator focuses on digital system setup. Resist this tendency: establishing physical plant maintenance habits early is far easier than recovering from neglect later.
  • Years 3-10: Wear patterns emerge. The operator learns which components degrade fastest, which areas are most vulnerable to environmental challenges, and what the real maintenance burden looks like. The maintenance calendar is refined based on experience. Some items may need more frequent attention than initially estimated; others may need less.
  • Years 10-25: Significant physical plant components begin reaching end-of-life: roofing materials, wiring insulation, structural sealants. Major maintenance events (reroofing, rewiring) are expensive and disruptive. These should have been anticipated through the horizon forecast (D11-003). Physical plant renovation is a major institutional event that requires careful planning and temporary alternative arrangements for equipment.
  • Years 25-50+: The physical plant may have been through one or more major renovations. The maintenance archive shows the full lifecycle of physical components: installation, degradation, repair, replacement. This data is invaluable for planning future maintenance and for successor operators who need to understand the building's history and condition.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 — Founding Entry: I am a software person being asked to care about roofing, wiring, and pest control. This is not where my expertise or interest lies. But I have already learned, in the short history of this project, that the physical environment is the foundation everything else rests on. A month ago I found mouse droppings near a cable run and realized that a rodent could do more damage to my institution in one night than any software bug could do in a year. I replaced the cables, sealed the entry point, and set traps. The experience convinced me that facilities management is not a low-status afterthought — it is infrastructure work as fundamental as any server configuration. The building is the chassis. If the chassis fails, everything inside it fails.
