D10-008 — Access Control: Operational Procedures

Document ID
D10-008
Domain
10 -- User Operations
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
OPS-001, SEC-001, SEC-002, SEC-003, D10-002, D10-003
Depended Upon By
D10-005 (security incident classification), D10-014 (access during handoffs), all articles referencing operational access control.

Purpose

This article defines the day-to-day operational procedures for managing access to the institution's systems, data, and physical spaces. While SEC-002 defines the access control architecture and SEC-003 defines cryptographic key management, this article addresses the operational reality: the daily, weekly, and periodic procedures that the operator performs to maintain access controls in a healthy state.

The distinction is between design and operation. SEC-002 says "the institution uses role-based access control." This article says "every Monday during the weekly duty period, verify that the active account list matches the authorized account registry, and investigate any discrepancies." SEC-003 says "encryption keys are rotated on a defined schedule." This article says "on the first business day of each quarter, perform the key rotation procedure documented in SEC-003 Section X."

Access control that is designed correctly but operated carelessly is access control in name only. The operational procedures in this article are what transform the security architecture into a living defense. Without them, the architecture is documentation. With them, it is security.

Scope

This article covers:

  • Daily access control verification procedures.
  • Procedures for granting, modifying, and revoking access.
  • Key management operational procedures (the operational layer over SEC-003).
  • Physical security operational routines.
  • Authentication procedures and credential management.
  • Access audit procedures and their frequency.
  • Emergency access procedures (lock-out recovery, emergency override).

This article does not cover:

  • The design of the access control system (see SEC-002).
  • The design of the cryptographic infrastructure (see SEC-003).
  • The threat model that informs security decisions (see SEC-001).
  • Incident response for security breaches (see D10-003 and SEC-002 breach procedures).

Background

3.1 Operational Security vs. Security Architecture

Security architecture is the design of defenses. Operational security is the daily practice that keeps those defenses effective. The strongest lock in the world provides no security if it is left open. The most sophisticated encryption provides no protection if the keys are stored in a predictable location. Security architecture creates capability; operational discipline creates reality.

In a single-operator institution, the temptation is to relax operational security because there is only one person with access. "I do not need to verify the account list because I am the only account." This reasoning is dangerously wrong. The purpose of operational access control in a solo institution is not to protect against unauthorized users (though it does that too). It is to detect compromise, detect configuration drift, and detect the effects of software bugs or automation errors that may create unauthorized accounts or modify permissions without human intent.

3.2 The Account Registry as Ground Truth

The account registry is the authoritative list of every account that should exist on every system in the institution. It records which accounts exist, what access level each has, when each was created, and why. The operational procedure for access control is fundamentally simple: compare reality to the registry. If they match, access control is healthy. If they diverge, something has happened that requires investigation.

3.3 Physical Security Is Operational

Physical security is not a one-time installation. It is a daily practice. Locks must be verified. Physical access logs must be reviewed. Environmental barriers must be inspected. Physical security degrades as silently as digital security: a door that is propped open, a lock that is left unlocked, a camera that has been repositioned. The operational procedures for physical security are as important as the procedures for digital access control.

System Model

4.1 Daily Access Control Procedures

Integrated into the D10-002 daily checklist:

  • Review authentication logs for the past 24 hours. Look for: failed login attempts, successful logins at unusual times, logins from unexpected sources (if the system is networked).
  • Verify that no unexpected accounts, processes, or services have appeared.
  • Verify that physical security measures are intact (locks engaged, access barriers in place).
  • Record findings in the operational log.

Estimated time: 2-3 minutes as part of the daily health check.

4.2 Weekly Access Audit

During the weekly duty period:

  • Compare the active account list on each system against the account registry. Every active account must have a registry entry. Every registry entry for an active account must match reality (permissions, group memberships).
  • Review the past week's authentication logs for patterns: consistent failed attempts, unusual access patterns, timing anomalies.
  • Verify that service accounts (accounts used by automated systems per D8) are functioning within their documented scope.
  • Record the audit results in the operational log with the specific finding: "Account audit: N accounts on system X, all match registry" or "Discrepancy found: [details]."

4.3 Quarterly Key Management Procedures

During the quarterly duty period:

  • Perform key rotation for any keys on a quarterly rotation schedule per SEC-003.
  • Verify the integrity of all key storage locations (encrypted key stores, physical key storage).
  • Verify that backup copies of keys exist and are accessible from their documented locations.
  • Review the key inventory: are all keys documented? Are any documented keys no longer in use?
  • Test at least one key recovery procedure from backup to verify that recovery is possible.
  • Record all key management activities in the operational log and, for key rotations, in the change log.

4.4 Access Grant Procedure

When a new account must be created or an existing account's permissions must be expanded:

  1. Determine the minimum access level required for the purpose. Apply the principle of least privilege per SEC-001.
  2. Record the request in the decision log with rationale. For new human operator accounts, this is a Tier 2 governance decision per GOV-001. For new service accounts, this is a Tier 1 decision.
  3. Create the account or modify permissions per the documented procedure for the specific system.
  4. Update the account registry with: account name, system, access level, creation date, authorizing decision log entry, purpose.
  5. Test the new access to verify it works correctly and does not grant more access than intended.
  6. Record the creation in the change log.

4.5 Access Revocation Procedure

When an account must be removed or an account's permissions must be reduced:

  1. Determine whether immediate revocation is required (security incident) or scheduled revocation is acceptable (role change, departure).
  2. For immediate revocation: disable the account first, then investigate. Do not delete the account until investigation is complete, as deletion destroys evidence.
  3. For scheduled revocation: record the planned revocation in the decision log. Execute on the scheduled date.
  4. Verify that revocation was successful: attempt to authenticate with the revoked credentials. The attempt must fail.
  5. Update the account registry: mark the account as revoked with date and reason.
  6. Record the revocation in the change log.

4.6 Emergency Access Procedures

Emergency access procedures address scenarios where normal access is unavailable:

Lockout recovery: If the operator loses access to their primary credentials (forgotten password, corrupted keystore, lost physical key):

  1. Use the emergency recovery credentials stored per SEC-003's emergency access provisions.
  2. These credentials must be stored in a physically separate, secure location (e.g., sealed envelope in a safe).
  3. Log the use of emergency credentials immediately upon regaining access.
  4. Reset the primary credentials. Update the emergency credentials if the previous ones were exposed.
  5. Classify this as an S3 incident and conduct a post-incident review to understand how the lockout occurred.

Succession access: If a new operator must gain access to systems without the previous operator's assistance (incapacitation, unexpected departure):

  1. The succession access package (credentials, keys, procedures) must exist per SEC-003 and D10-014.
  2. The new operator uses the succession package to gain initial access.
  3. Once access is established, all credentials in the succession package are rotated.
  4. The new operator creates their own credentials and updates the account registry.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D10A-01: The daily access control check defined in Section 4.1 is part of the mandatory daily cycle per D10-002.
  • R-D10A-02: The weekly account audit must reconcile the account registry with reality on every system. Discrepancies are investigated and resolved within 7 days or escalated as incidents per D10-003.
  • R-D10A-03: Every account creation, modification, or revocation must follow the procedures in Sections 4.4 and 4.5 and must be recorded in both the account registry and the change log.
  • R-D10A-04: Emergency access credentials must exist, must be tested at least annually, and must be stored separately from the systems they protect.
  • R-D10A-05: Use of emergency access credentials is an S3 incident minimum and must be logged and reviewed.
  • R-D10A-06: Physical security checks are part of the daily operational cycle. A propped-open door or disengaged lock is treated as a security anomaly per D10-003.
  • R-D10A-07: No account may have broader access than its documented purpose requires. The principle of least privilege is enforced during every access grant and verified during every weekly audit.

Failure Modes

  • Registry drift. The account registry gradually falls out of sync with reality. Accounts are created without updating the registry. Permissions are changed informally. The registry becomes unreliable and the weekly audit becomes meaningless. Mitigation: R-D10A-03 mandates registry updates for every change. The weekly audit detects drift that slipped through.

  • Audit fatigue. The weekly account audit finds no discrepancies for months. The operator begins performing it perfunctorily or skipping it. When a real discrepancy occurs, it goes undetected. Mitigation: the audit takes minutes and is integrated into the weekly cycle. Even months of clean audits are valuable as evidence that access control is healthy.

  • Emergency credential staleness. Emergency access credentials are created and stored, then forgotten. Years pass. The credentials expire, the systems they protect are reconfigured, and when the emergency occurs, the credentials do not work. Mitigation: R-D10A-04 requires annual testing of emergency credentials.

  • Over-privileged accounts. Accounts are created with more access than needed because it is easier. Over time, the institution has multiple accounts with administrative access when only one should. Mitigation: R-D10A-07 enforces least privilege. The weekly audit should flag accounts with access exceeding their documented purpose.

  • Physical security neglect. Physical security checks are the first thing dropped from the daily checklist when time is short. The operator assumes physical security is unchanged because it usually is. Mitigation: physical checks take seconds and are integrated into the daily cycle. They detect slow degradation (weathering, wear) as well as acute changes.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If the account registry is unreliable: Conduct a full reconciliation. Enumerate every account on every system. For each account, determine whether it is authorized, verify its purpose, and document it in the registry. Accounts that cannot be identified should be disabled pending investigation. This is a half-day task that must be completed before the weekly audit cycle can resume meaningfully.

  2. If emergency credentials have not been tested: Test them now. If they fail, create new emergency credentials using your current access. Store them per SEC-003. Schedule the annual test.

  3. If a security breach is detected during access control procedures: Invoke D10-003 incident response. Classify per Section 4.1 of D10-003 (S1 if unauthorized activity is confirmed, S2 if suspected). Follow SEC-002 breach procedures. The access control procedures in this article resume after the incident is resolved.

  4. If physical security measures have degraded: Conduct a full physical security inspection. Replace worn locks, repair barriers, and re-secure any access points that have been compromised. Record the inspection and repairs in the maintenance log (D10-004) and the operational log.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The access control operational procedures are being established alongside the security architecture. The account registry is small and manageable. The weekly audit is quick. The primary challenge is establishing the habits rather than managing complexity.

  • Years 5-15: If the institution has grown, the account registry may include service accounts for automated systems. The weekly audit may take slightly longer. Key rotation procedures should be well-practiced. Emergency credentials should have been tested multiple times.

  • Years 15-30: Succession planning makes access control operational procedures especially critical. The succession access package must be current and tested. The procedures must be clear enough that a new operator can follow them on their first day.

  • Years 30-50+: Multiple generations of operators may have used these procedures. The account registry should contain a history of all accounts that have ever existed, including those long revoked. This history is a security audit trail of institutional value.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: In a single-operator, air-gapped institution, access control procedures may seem like security theater. I am the only person with access. Why do I need to audit my own accounts weekly? The answer is that I am auditing the systems, not myself. I am verifying that no software bug, no misconfigured automation, no forgotten test account, and no degraded configuration has created access that I did not intend. The audit is not about trust. It is about verification.

The emergency access procedures are the ones I hope never to need and the ones I consider most important to have ready. Losing access to your own systems is a uniquely frustrating and dangerous scenario. The sealed envelope in the safe is a low-tech, high-reliability solution. It works when nothing else does. I intend to test it annually, and I encourage future operators to do the same.
