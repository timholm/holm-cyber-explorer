D7-010 — Decision Support Doctrine

Document ID
D7-010
Domain
7 -- Intelligence & Analysis
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D7-001, D7-002, D7-006, D7-007, D7-008, D7-011, ETH-001, GOV-001, OPS-001, D10-002
Depended Upon By
D7-012, D7-013, D10-002, D10-003. All governance and operational articles that consume intelligence products.

Purpose

This article defines how intelligence products connect to institutional decision-making. It specifies the interface between the intelligence function (which produces understanding) and the governance function (which makes decisions). Intelligence without a clear path to decisions is academic exercise. Decisions without intelligence are guesswork. This article ensures that the two functions are connected by explicit, documented, and auditable procedures.

The fundamental principle, established in D7-001 R-D7-04, is that intelligence informs decisions but does not make them. The analyst's role is to present the best available understanding, honestly stated, with uncertainty acknowledged. The decision to act belongs to the operator in their governance capacity per GOV-001. In a single-operator institution, the analyst and the decision-maker are the same person -- but the functions are distinct and must be documented separately. This article provides the structures that maintain that distinction.

The decision-support interface matters most when it is most tempting to skip it. Under time pressure, the operator will want to act without documenting the intelligence basis. When the conclusion seems obvious, the operator will want to skip the formal briefing. When the same person fills both roles, the internal conversation between "what do I know?" and "what should I do?" happens silently and instantly. This article insists that the conversation be externalized and recorded -- not to slow things down, but to create the audit trail that enables learning, calibration, and accountability.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The decision-support interface: how intelligence products reach the decision function.
  • Briefing protocols: how intelligence is presented for decision-making, including the self-briefing protocol for single-operator institutions.
  • Decision documentation requirements: what intelligence must be recorded when a decision is made.
  • The feedback loop: how decision outcomes feed back into the intelligence cycle.
  • Decision tiering and intelligence requirements: which decisions require what level of intelligence support.
  • The analyst's limits: what the intelligence function may and may not do in the decision process.

This article does not cover:

  • The governance framework that defines decision authority (see GOV-001).
  • The intelligence cycle that produces the products (see D7-002).
  • The product standards and formats (see D7-008).
  • The daily operational procedures that implement decisions (see D10-002, OPS-001).
  • The estimative language used in products (see D7-011, though it is referenced).

Background

3.1 The Intelligence-Decision Gap

In organizations of all sizes, the most common intelligence failure is not analytical error -- it is the failure of good analysis to reach the decision-maker at the right time in a usable form. The analysis is produced, filed, and forgotten. The decision-maker acts on instinct, habit, or incomplete information. The intelligence function and the decision function operate in parallel without connecting.

In a single-operator institution, this gap manifests differently but no less dangerously. Instead of organizational silos, the gap is cognitive. The operator thinks about a problem, reaches a conclusion, and acts -- all in their head, in seconds. No product is produced, no confidence level is stated, no assumptions are listed, and no record is created. When the decision turns out well, no one learns why. When it turns out badly, no one learns why either, because there is nothing to audit.

3.2 The Self-Briefing Problem

Intelligence briefings exist to present analytical findings to someone who has not been doing the analysis. In a single-operator institution, the briefing is redundant in the traditional sense -- the analyst already knows what they found. But the self-briefing serves a different and essential purpose: it forces the analyst to articulate their findings in a structured way, committing them to a documented form that can be reviewed, challenged, and compared against outcomes. The self-briefing is not about informing oneself; it is about disciplining one's own thinking by requiring it to survive the transition from thought to text.

3.3 The Role Separation Principle

GOV-001 establishes that the operator fills multiple roles: owner, administrator, analyst, and operator. These roles have different responsibilities and different obligations. The analyst must present the best available understanding regardless of what the decision-maker might want to hear. The decision-maker must consider the intelligence but is not bound by it -- they may weigh factors the analyst did not consider, accept risks the analyst would not recommend, or act contrary to the analytical recommendation if they judge it necessary.

In a single person, these roles are psychologically difficult to separate. The temptation is to produce analysis that supports the decision one has already made. The decision-support procedures in this article create structural barriers against this by requiring that the intelligence product exist before the decision is documented, and that the decision record explicitly acknowledge the intelligence basis and any points of departure from the analytical recommendation.

System Model

4.1 Decision Tiers and Intelligence Requirements

GOV-001 defines four decision tiers. This article specifies the intelligence support required for each:

4.2 The Self-Briefing Protocol

For a single-operator institution, the self-briefing replaces the traditional intelligence briefing. The protocol is as follows:

  1. Produce the intelligence product first. The product must exist in finished form before the decision is made. This prevents the analysis from being shaped to support a predetermined conclusion.
  2. Read the product as a decision-maker, not as the analyst who wrote it. Focus on the BLUF, the confidence level, the key assumptions, and the recommendations. Ask: "If someone else had written this, would I trust it? What would I question?"
  3. Document the decision separately from the product. The decision record (per GOV-001) is a distinct document that references the intelligence product but records the decision-maker's reasoning, including any factors beyond the intelligence that influenced the decision.
  4. Note any departures from the intelligence recommendation. If the decision-maker (the operator in their governance role) decides to act contrary to the analyst's recommendation (the same operator in their intelligence role), the departure must be documented with the reasoning. This is not a failure -- the intelligence function does not have veto power over decisions. But the departure must be conscious and recorded.
  5. Close the loop. After the decision is implemented and its outcomes are known, the decision record must be updated with the result. The intelligence product that supported the decision must be flagged for calibration tracking per D7-011.

4.3 The Decision-Intelligence Feedback Loop

Intelligence products are hypotheses about the world. Decisions are experiments that test those hypotheses. The feedback loop connects the outcome of the experiment back to the hypothesis, enabling the intelligence function to learn and improve.

The feedback loop operates at three timescales:

Immediate feedback (days to weeks): Did the decision produce the expected immediate result? If the intelligence said "this drive will fail within 30 days" and the institution replaced it, did the old drive in fact show signs of imminent failure? If the intelligence said "this import source is reliable" and the imported data was checked, was it in fact reliable? Immediate feedback is the most direct calibration tool.

Medium-term feedback (months to a year): Did the strategic assessment that informed a resource allocation decision prove accurate? Did the threat assessment correctly predict the threat landscape for the year? Medium-term feedback feeds into the annual calibration exercise per D7-011.

Long-term feedback (years to decades): Did the institution's strategic estimates correctly identify the trends that mattered? Did the opportunity framework capture the developments that actually advanced the institution's mission? Long-term feedback is the province of the Strategic Estimate (SE) per D7-008 and the institutional memory function per D20.

4.4 The Analyst's Limits

The intelligence function provides understanding, not directives. The analyst's role is bounded:

  • The analyst may: Present findings and assessments. State confidence levels. List assumptions. Identify risks and opportunities. Recommend actions. Argue for or against specific courses of action within the product. Request additional collection to fill identified gaps.
  • The analyst may not: Make decisions that bind the institution. Withhold analysis because the conclusion is inconvenient. Shade analysis to support a preferred outcome. Refuse to present findings because they are uncertain -- uncertainty must be presented honestly, not concealed. Override a governance decision because it departs from the intelligence recommendation.

These limits apply even when the analyst and the decision-maker are the same person. The discipline is about maintaining two distinct modes of thinking: the analytical mode ("what is true?") and the decision mode ("what should we do?"). The intelligence product answers the first question. The decision record answers the second. The self-briefing protocol is the bridge between them.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D7-10-01: Tier 1 and Tier 2 decisions per GOV-001 must be supported by a corresponding intelligence product per Section 4.1. Decisions made without the required intelligence support are procedurally deficient and must be documented as such, with remediation (producing the intelligence product post-hoc) required within 30 days.
  • R-D7-10-02: The self-briefing protocol per Section 4.2 is mandatory for all Tier 1 and Tier 2 decisions. The intelligence product must exist before the decision is documented. Back-dating or simultaneous creation of the product and the decision record is not acceptable.
  • R-D7-10-03: Decision records for Tier 1-3 decisions must cite the intelligence products consulted. If the decision departs from the intelligence recommendation, the departure must be documented with reasoning per Section 4.2 Step 4.
  • R-D7-10-04: The feedback loop per Section 4.3 must be maintained. Decision outcomes must be recorded in the decision record and flagged for calibration tracking. At least annually, the intelligence function must review the past year's decisions to assess how well the supporting intelligence predicted outcomes.
  • R-D7-10-05: The intelligence function must not be subordinated to the decision function. The analyst must present findings honestly regardless of what the decision-maker might prefer. Per ETH-001, Principle 3 (Transparency of Operation), analysis must not be shaped to support predetermined conclusions.
  • R-D7-10-06: Intelligence products must be presented in a format that serves the decision-maker per D7-008. If the decision-maker (even when the same person as the analyst) finds the products unusable for decision-making, the product format must be revised -- not abandoned.
  • R-D7-10-07: When time pressure prevents full adherence to the self-briefing protocol, the operator must produce a minimum viable intelligence record: a one-paragraph summary stating the analytical basis for the decision, the confidence level, and the key assumptions. This record must be expanded to a full intelligence product within 14 days per D7-008 R-D7-08-07.

Failure Modes

  • Decision-intelligence collapse. The operator stops distinguishing between their analyst role and their decision-maker role. Decisions are made without intelligence products, or products are created retroactively to justify decisions already taken. The self-briefing protocol is abandoned as unnecessary formality. Mitigation: the annual calibration review per R-D7-10-04 will reveal this pattern -- decisions without corresponding intelligence products, or products whose dates suspiciously match decision dates. Recovery requires recommitting to role separation as described in Section 4.2.

  • Intelligence as rubber stamp. Products are produced but shaped to confirm decisions already made. The analysis always supports the preferred action. Confidence levels are inflated to remove doubt. Assumptions are not stated because they would reveal the analysis is circular. Mitigation: calibration tracking will reveal this over time -- if intelligence consistently supports decisions but outcomes are worse than predicted, the intelligence was probably shaped. The devil's advocate requirement per D7-001 R-D7-02 provides a structural check against this tendency.

  • Intelligence ignored. Products are produced to standard, but the decision-maker ignores them. Decisions are made on instinct, convenience, or habit. The intelligence archive grows but has no effect on institutional behavior. Mitigation: R-D7-10-01 requires Tier 1-2 decisions to cite intelligence products. R-D7-10-03 requires departures from recommendations to be documented. These requirements create a structural incentive to engage with the intelligence product, even if only to explain why it is being disregarded.

  • Feedback loop failure. Decision outcomes are not recorded. The intelligence function produces assessments but never learns whether they were right. Calibration is impossible. Systematic errors persist uncorrected. Mitigation: R-D7-10-04 mandates feedback loop maintenance. The annual calibration exercise is designed to detect this gap.

  • Time pressure override. In practice, the operator regularly invokes R-D7-10-07 (time pressure exception) and produces one-paragraph summaries instead of full products. Over time, this becomes the default rather than the exception. Mitigation: the quarterly review per D7-001 R-D7-05 should assess the ratio of full products to time-pressure summaries. If more than 25% of products are time-pressure summaries, the operator is either under-resourced or avoiding the full protocol. Diagnose and address the root cause.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If decision-intelligence collapse is detected: Audit the last 20 Tier 1-3 decisions. For each, verify that a corresponding intelligence product exists, that it pre-dates the decision, and that the decision record cites it. Where gaps exist, produce retroactive intelligence products (marked "POST-HOC -- NOT USED FOR ORIGINAL DECISION") to establish the analytical record. Recommit to the self-briefing protocol. Consider implementing a mandatory waiting period between product production and decision documentation to enforce temporal separation.

  2. If intelligence-as-rubber-stamp is suspected: Review the last 10 intelligence products that supported decisions. For each, assess whether the devil's advocate analysis was genuine or perfunctory, whether the confidence level was warranted by the evidence, and whether the assumptions were honestly stated. If three or more products show signs of confirmation bias, re-analyze the outstanding decisions using fresh analytical techniques per D7-004. Document findings in the institutional log.

  3. If intelligence is being ignored: Interview the decision function (that is, engage in honest self-reflection about why intelligence is not being used). Common reasons: the products do not answer the questions actually being asked (requirements misalignment); the products arrive too late (timing failure); the products are too long or technical (format mismatch); the analyst's track record is poor and the decision-maker has lost confidence (credibility failure). Address the specific root cause. Requirements misalignment is fixed through the intelligence cycle. Timing is fixed through production scheduling. Format is fixed through D7-008. Credibility requires calibration tracking and honest assessment of analytical quality.

  4. If feedback loop has broken: Conduct an immediate retrospective. Review the last year of decisions and their outcomes. For each decision that had an intelligence basis, compare the predicted outcome with the actual outcome. Record findings. Re-establish the feedback loop with a specific operational trigger -- for example, adding "record decision outcome" to the monthly operations checklist per OPS-001.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The self-briefing protocol will feel artificial and burdensome. The operator will resist the overhead of producing an intelligence product before making a decision, especially when the decision seems straightforward. Follow the protocol anyway. The habits formed now determine whether the intelligence-decision interface becomes natural or perpetually resisted. Early products will be imperfect -- that is expected. The discipline of producing them is more valuable than their initial quality.

  • Years 5-15: The feedback loop should be producing useful calibration data. The operator should be able to assess: "When my intelligence said X, how often was X actually true? When I departed from intelligence recommendations, how did those decisions turn out compared to decisions where I followed the recommendation?" These questions have profound implications for how much the operator trusts their own analytical function and how they weigh intelligence against other factors.

  • Years 15-30: If succession occurs, the decision-support interface becomes genuinely dyadic -- a real analyst briefing a real decision-maker. The protocols designed for self-briefing translate directly to this scenario. The intelligence archive and decision log provide the successor with a comprehensive record of how the institution has thought about and acted on its challenges. This institutional memory is irreplaceable.

  • Years 30-50+: The decision-intelligence interface should be a mature institutional function with decades of calibration data, a rich archive of precedent, and established procedures that successive operators follow naturally. The specific decisions will change as the institution's circumstances evolve, but the discipline of intelligence-informed decision-making should be ingrained in the institution's culture.

  • Signpost for revision: If the annual calibration review shows that intelligence-supported decisions do not produce better outcomes than unsupported decisions, either the intelligence function is failing (producing inaccurate analysis) or the decision-support interface is failing (good analysis is not reaching the decision in a useful form). Investigate both possibilities. If the intelligence function is failing, the problem lies in D7-004 (methods) or D7-005 (bias). If the interface is failing, the problem lies here.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The self-briefing protocol is the part of this article I expect to be challenged most often, especially by myself. When I know the answer and I know the decision, producing a formal intelligence product before acting feels like writing a letter to myself. But the protocol exists for three reasons, and all three are important.

First, the product disciplines my thinking. The act of writing down evidence, stating confidence, listing assumptions, and arguing the counter-case forces me to think more carefully than I would if I just acted on my initial impression. I have already found, in writing the first intelligence products for this institution, that the devil's advocate exercise changed my mind about two assessments I was initially confident about.

Second, the product creates the record. When I review decisions in five years, I will want to know what I thought I knew at the time, not what I think I remember thinking. The product captures the state of knowledge at the moment of decision -- something memory cannot do reliably.

Third, the product serves the successor. The person who takes over this institution will inherit a rich archive of how decisions were made and what intelligence supported them. This is the closest thing to apprenticeship across time -- learning from the predecessor's thinking, not just from their outcomes.

R-D7-10-07 (the time-pressure exception) will be my most-used escape hatch. I put it in because some decisions cannot wait for a full intelligence product. But I also put in the monitoring rule about the 25% threshold because I know I will be tempted to use the exception routinely. The exception exists for genuine urgency, not for convenience.
