D20-007 — Context Recovery Procedures

Document ID
D20-007
Domain
20 — Institutional Memory
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D20-002 (Decision Log), D20-005 (Oral History), D20-003 (Timeline)
Depended Upon By
D20-013 (Memory as Immune System)

Purpose

This article provides step-by-step procedures for recovering the context behind institutional decisions and artifacts when the original participants are unavailable and the existing documentation is insufficient. It operationalizes the four-channel context recovery model introduced in D20-001 (Section 4.2) and provides practical guidance for the future maintainer who encounters something they do not understand and cannot ask anyone about.

Context recovery is the moment of truth for institutional memory. All of the institution's memory infrastructure -- the decision log, the oral histories, the timeline, the commentary sections, the legacy profiles, the attribution records -- exists so that this moment can be resolved successfully. When a future maintainer asks "why is it this way?" and finds an answer in the record, every investment in institutional memory is justified. When they ask and find nothing, every gap in the record is exposed.

This article is written for the person standing in front of a puzzle. They have found a configuration they do not understand, a policy that seems arbitrary, a system design that appears suboptimal, or a decision that contradicts what they would have decided. They need to know whether the decision was made for reasons that still apply, for reasons that have expired, or for no documented reason at all. This article tells them how to find out.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The four-channel context recovery model: decision log, commentary, timeline, and forensic reconstruction.
  • Step-by-step procedures for each channel.
  • How to determine when context recovery is necessary.
  • How to document the results of context recovery.
  • How to act when context recovery fails (when the reason cannot be determined).
  • How to use context recovery results to improve the memory system.

This article does not cover:

  • The philosophy of why context recovery matters (see D20-001).
  • How to maintain the decision log (see D20-002).
  • How to conduct oral history sessions (see D20-005).
  • How to build or navigate the timeline (see D20-003).
  • The quality audit of context recovery procedures (covered by Domain 19).

Background

3.1 When Context Recovery Is Needed

Context recovery is needed whenever a current member encounters an institutional artifact -- a decision, a configuration, a policy, a design choice, a document, a practice -- and cannot determine why it exists or why it takes the form it does. This situation arises in several common scenarios:

  • A new member joins the institution and encounters established practices for the first time.
  • A member takes over responsibility for a domain or system that was previously managed by someone who has departed.
  • A routine review raises questions about a practice whose rationale was not documented.
  • A proposed change requires understanding the original intent behind the current state.
  • An audit reveals a discrepancy between documentation and practice, and the reason for the discrepancy is unknown.

3.2 The Cost of Guessing

When context is unavailable, the maintainer faces a choice: guess at the reason and act accordingly, or leave the artifact unchanged out of caution. Both options carry risk. Guessing may lead to changes that break things that work for reasons the guesser did not anticipate. Caution may lead to ossification, where the institution cannot evolve because no one dares change anything they do not fully understand.

Context recovery provides a middle path: a structured investigation that reduces uncertainty before action is taken. It will not always produce a definitive answer, but it will produce the best available answer, and it will document the investigation itself so that the next person who asks the same question does not start from zero.

3.3 The Four Channels

D20-001 introduced the four-channel model for context recovery. This article provides the operational procedures for each channel, in order of preference:

  1. Channel 1: Decision Log. The fastest and most reliable channel. Search the decision log for entries related to the artifact in question.
  2. Channel 2: Commentary Sections. Informal context that fills gaps in the formal record. Search the Commentary sections of relevant articles.
  3. Channel 3: Institutional Timeline. Contextual information about what else was happening when the decision was made. Consult the timeline for the relevant period.
  4. Channel 4: Forensic Reconstruction. The last resort. Reconstruct context from indirect evidence: configuration files, data patterns, system architecture, physical artifacts, and the recollections of current members who may have secondhand knowledge.

System Model

4.1 The Context Recovery Workflow

TRIGGER: "Why is it this way?"
  |
  v
CHANNEL 1: Search Decision Log
  |-- Found? --> Document finding. Assess whether reasons still apply. Done.
  |-- Not found? --> Continue.
  v
CHANNEL 2: Search Commentary Sections
  |-- Found? --> Document finding. Cross-reference with Channel 1 if partial. Assess. Done.
  |-- Not found? --> Continue.
  v
CHANNEL 3: Consult Institutional Timeline
  |-- Context found? --> Document finding. Assess. Done.
  |-- No useful context? --> Continue.
  v
CHANNEL 4: Forensic Reconstruction
  |-- Reconstruct from indirect evidence.
  |-- Document reconstruction with confidence level.
  |-- Assess whether reasons still apply.
  v
DOCUMENT RESULTS
  |-- Record the context recovery attempt in the decision log.
  |-- If context was found: annotate the original artifact with recovered context.
  |-- If context was NOT found: record the gap. Decide whether to maintain, modify, or reverse
  |   the artifact based on best available judgment.
  v
IMPROVE THE SYSTEM
  |-- Identify why the context was missing (which memory processes failed?).
  |-- Update procedures to prevent similar gaps.

4.2 Channel 1 Procedures: Decision Log Search

When searching the decision log for context:

  1. Identify the artifact in question. Note its creation date (if known), its domain, and any names associated with it.
  2. Search the decision log by date range: start with the probable creation date and extend 6 months in both directions.
  3. Search by keyword: use the artifact's name, its domain, and related technical terms.
  4. Search by contributor: if the creator is known (via attribution records, D20-006), search for all decisions made by that contributor in the relevant time period.
  5. If a relevant entry is found, verify that it addresses the specific question. A decision log entry may document that something was decided without fully explaining why. If the "why" is absent, note what was found and continue to Channel 2.

4.3 Channel 2 Procedures: Commentary Section Search

Commentary sections contain informal, dated entries from maintainers. They often capture reasoning that was too informal or speculative for the decision log:

  1. Identify the articles most likely to be related to the artifact. Start with the article in the artifact's domain, then check related domain articles.
  2. Read the Commentary sections of those articles, focusing on entries from the probable creation period.
  3. Look for indirect references: a commentary entry may not mention the artifact by name but may discuss the problem it was designed to solve.
  4. If oral histories exist for the probable creator (check D20-006 contributor registry for oral history session references), search the oral history transcripts.

4.4 Channel 3 Procedures: Timeline Consultation

The institutional timeline (D20-003) provides contextual information that may explain the decision:

  1. Locate the probable creation date on the timeline.
  2. Examine events in the surrounding period: what else was happening? Was there a crisis, a hardware change, a personnel transition, a policy shift?
  3. Look for causal chains: did a preceding event create conditions that explain the artifact? Did the artifact enable or respond to a subsequent event?
  4. Note any relevant events for the context recovery documentation, even if they do not definitively explain the artifact.

4.5 Channel 4 Procedures: Forensic Reconstruction

Forensic reconstruction is the most labor-intensive channel and produces results with the lowest confidence. Use it only when Channels 1-3 have been exhausted:

  1. Examine the artifact itself. Configuration files, code comments, file metadata (creation dates, modification dates, file names), directory structure, and naming conventions may contain clues.
  2. Examine related artifacts. What was created at the same time? What does the artifact interact with? What would break if it were removed?
  3. Consult current members. Even if no one was present for the original decision, someone may have secondhand knowledge or a plausible theory.
  4. Construct hypotheses. Based on the evidence, generate plausible explanations. Rate each hypothesis by confidence (high, moderate, low).
  5. Test non-destructively. If possible, verify hypotheses without altering the artifact. For example, if the hypothesis is "this configuration prevents a specific failure mode," test whether the failure mode exists in a non-production environment.
  6. Document everything. The reconstruction process itself is valuable institutional memory. Future investigators may find your notes useful even if your conclusions prove wrong.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D20-07-01: Document Every Recovery Attempt. Every context recovery attempt must be documented, regardless of whether it succeeds. The documentation includes: what question was asked, which channels were searched, what was found (or not found), what conclusion was reached, and what confidence level was assigned. This documentation is filed in the decision log as a "CONTEXT RECOVERY" entry.

  • R-D20-07-02: Follow Channel Order. Context recovery must proceed through the channels in order: Decision Log, Commentary, Timeline, Forensic. Do not skip to forensic reconstruction without first exhausting the documented channels. The earlier channels are faster and more reliable; skipping them wastes effort and risks missing available information.

  • R-D20-07-03: Confidence Levels Are Mandatory. Every context recovery conclusion must carry a confidence level: HIGH (the reason was explicitly documented and verified), MODERATE (the reason was inferred from multiple consistent sources), or LOW (the reason was reconstructed from indirect evidence and may be incorrect). The confidence level determines how much weight the conclusion should carry in subsequent decision-making.

  • R-D20-07-04: Recovery Feeds Improvement. Every context recovery attempt that fails to find context in Channels 1-3 (requiring forensic reconstruction) must be followed by a brief analysis of why the context was missing. Was the decision log incomplete? Was the commentary section empty? Was the timeline too coarse? This analysis feeds into improvements to the memory system, creating a feedback loop between context recovery and memory maintenance.

  • R-D20-07-05: No Silent Reversals. If context recovery fails and the maintainer decides to change or reverse the artifact, the change must be recorded in the decision log with a note explaining that the original context could not be recovered. Silent reversals -- changing things without documenting why or acknowledging the uncertainty -- are prohibited.

Failure Modes

  • All channels fail. The context cannot be recovered by any means. The maintainer is left with an artifact they do not understand and no way to find out why it exists. Severity: variable (depends on the artifact's importance). Likelihood: increases with institutional age and decreases with memory system quality. Detection: the context recovery documentation itself records the failure. Mitigation: the memory improvement feedback loop (R-D20-07-04) reduces the likelihood of future total failures.

  • False confidence. The maintainer finds what appears to be the context but has misidentified the relevant decision log entry or misinterpreted a commentary entry. They act on a false understanding. Severity: variable. Likelihood: moderate (especially when multiple similar decisions exist in the log). Detection: difficult to detect until the consequences of the action reveal the misunderstanding. Mitigation: confidence levels (R-D20-07-03) signal uncertainty. When confidence is moderate or low, the maintainer should proceed cautiously and reversibly.

  • Channel skipping. The maintainer skips Channels 1-3 and goes directly to forensic reconstruction, either because they are impatient or because they do not know how to search the decision log effectively. Severity: moderate (wastes effort; may miss available information). Likelihood: moderate. Detection: audit of context recovery documentation. Mitigation: R-D20-07-02 mandates channel order; training includes search technique.

  • Recovery without documentation. The maintainer recovers the context (or believes they have) but does not document the recovery. The next person who asks the same question starts from scratch. Severity: moderate. Likelihood: high (documentation is easy to skip under time pressure). Detection: audit of decision log for context recovery entries. Mitigation: R-D20-07-01 mandates documentation; the operational review checks for undocumented recovery attempts.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If all channels fail: Document the failure thoroughly. Record what was searched, what was not found, and what hypotheses were considered. Then make a pragmatic decision: if the artifact appears to be functioning correctly and poses no known risk, leave it in place and document the uncertainty. If the artifact appears dysfunctional or risky, propose a replacement through the governance process (GOV-001), noting explicitly that the original context could not be recovered. The new decision then has full context, even though the old one did not.

  2. If false confidence led to a bad change: Reverse the change if possible. Document the sequence of events: the context recovery, the misidentification, the change, the consequences, and the reversal. This becomes a lesson learned (D20-004). Update the decision log with both the original (misidentified) context and the corrected understanding. If the original context remains unknown, note that as well.

  3. If systematic channel skipping is detected: Provide additional training on decision log search techniques. Review the decision log's indexing and search capabilities (in coordination with Domain 16): is the log hard to search? If so, the problem may be infrastructure rather than discipline.

  4. If undocumented recoveries are detected: Conduct retrospective documentation: ask the person who performed the recovery to document it now, even if their memory of the process has faded. A late documentation is better than none. Add a reminder to the operational checklist that context recovery documentation is mandatory.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Context recovery is rarely needed because the original participants are still present. Use this period to test the procedures: conduct practice context recovery exercises against recent decisions, verifying that the documentation is sufficient. Identify and fix gaps while the people who made the decisions are still available to confirm or deny the recovered context.

  • Years 5-15: Context recovery becomes real. The first departures have occurred. The first genuine context gaps appear. The procedures are tested in earnest. Refine the procedures based on experience: are the channel-order rules effective? Are the confidence levels calibrated correctly? Is the documentation burden proportionate to the value?

  • Years 15-30: Context recovery is a routine operation. The institution has accumulated enough memory that most recoveries succeed through Channels 1-3. Forensic reconstruction (Channel 4) is used only for the oldest or most poorly documented artifacts. The quality of context recovery is a direct measure of the quality of the institutional memory system.

  • Years 30-50: Context recovery for founding-era decisions is the critical test. Can a third-generation maintainer understand why the institution was built the way it was? The answer depends on the quality of decisions made in Years 0-5 about what to record and how. If those early decisions were good, context recovery for founding-era artifacts will succeed. If they were poor, forensic reconstruction will be the only option, and much will be permanently lost.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: This article is, in some ways, the most important article in Domain 20. The decision log, the oral histories, the timeline, the attribution records -- they are all infrastructure. Context recovery is the use case. It is the moment when a future maintainer reaches into the institution's memory and either finds what they need or finds nothing.

I have tried to make the procedures concrete enough to be useful without being so rigid that they become a burden. The four-channel model is a search strategy, not a ritual. If you are a future maintainer and you find the answer in the decision log on your first search, you do not need to ceremonially consult the timeline before acting on what you found. The channel order is a heuristic for efficiency, not a compliance requirement.

The hardest part of context recovery is not finding information. It is accepting when information cannot be found. The temptation to invent a plausible story and treat it as truth is strong. Resist it. A documented "I do not know why this exists" is more honest and more useful than a fabricated explanation. Future maintainers will respect your honesty. They will not respect your fiction.
