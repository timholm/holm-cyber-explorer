D11-009 — Administrative Forms, Ledgers, and Templates

Document ID
D11-009
Domain
11 — Administration
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Draft

Purpose

This article defines the canonical forms, ledgers, and templates used for all administrative processes across the institution. Forms are the interface between process and record: they ensure that administrative actions capture the right information, in the right format, every time. Without standardized forms, each administrative action requires the operator to decide in the moment what to record, how to structure it, and where to store it — decisions that consume attention and invite inconsistency.

A well-designed form is not bureaucratic overhead. It is a checklist that prevents omissions, a template that ensures consistency, and a record structure that enables indexing and retrieval. This article defines the core set of forms the institution requires, establishes design principles for form creation, and defines the version control process that keeps forms aligned with the processes they serve.

Scope

In scope:

  • Canonical form definitions for all administrative processes (procurement, maintenance, budget, personnel, audit, reporting).
  • Ledger definitions for ongoing tracking (expense ledger, maintenance ledger, consumable ledger).
  • Template definitions for recurring documents (reports, reviews, plans).
  • Form design principles: what makes a good institutional form.
  • Version control for forms: how forms are updated when processes change.
  • Physical and digital form formats.

Out of scope:

  • The administrative processes themselves (defined in respective D11 articles).
  • Documentation templates for non-administrative articles (meta-framework).
  • Technical forms for system configuration or deployment (domain-specific).

Background

Forms as Cognitive Prosthetics

A form is a cognitive prosthetic: it externalizes the memory of what needs to be captured so that the operator's attention can focus on the content rather than the structure. When conducting a procurement event without a form, the operator must simultaneously remember what to buy, track what was actually purchased, note prices, record serial numbers, verify quantities, and document the vendor — all while navigating a potentially unfamiliar commercial environment. With a procurement form, the structure is already defined. The operator fills in the blanks. The cognitive load drops dramatically, and the probability of capturing complete information rises equally dramatically.

This cognitive-prosthetic function is especially important in stressful or unusual situations. During an incident response, the operator is under cognitive load from the incident itself. An incident report form ensures that critical information (timeline, root cause, affected systems, actions taken) is captured even when the operator is too stressed to think about documentation structure.

The Tension Between Standardization and Flexibility

Forms impose standardization, which has both benefits and costs. The benefit is consistency: every procurement record has the same fields, making comparison and analysis possible. The cost is rigidity: a form designed for routine procurement may not accommodate an unusual acquisition (a salvaged component with no receipt, a barter exchange with no monetary value). The form system must balance these by providing standard fields for common cases and flexible extension mechanisms for unusual cases.

Physical and Digital Coexistence

The institution must be able to operate with or without functioning digital systems. All forms must therefore exist in both digital and physical formats. The digital format is the primary working copy; the physical format is the backup and the field copy. When the operator is away from digital systems (during a procurement trip, during outdoor maintenance, during a power outage), physical forms ensure that information is captured in real-time rather than reconstructed from memory later.

System Model

Form Design Principles

All institutional forms follow these design principles:

  1. Minimal completeness: A form includes every field necessary to create a complete record, and no fields that are not necessary. Every field must justify its existence by answering: what decision or process depends on this information?
  2. Self-documenting: Each field includes a brief description or example so that a new operator can complete the form without consulting a separate instruction manual.
  3. Consistent structure: All forms share a common header (form ID, form version, date, operator) and a common footer (notes/comments, signature/confirmation). Body sections vary by form type.
  4. Machine-parseable: Digital forms use structured plain-text formats (YAML or CSV) so that data can be extracted and analyzed programmatically when tools are available.
  5. Printable: Every form can be printed on a single sheet of standard paper (or, for complex forms, two sheets front-and-back). Physical forms use large enough fields for handwritten entries.
  6. Versioned: Every form carries a version number. When a process changes and the form must be updated, a new version is issued. Old versions are retained in the form archive. Records created with old form versions are valid and do not need to be re-created.

Ledger Definitions

Ledgers are running records that accumulate entries over time. Unlike forms (which are completed once per event), ledgers grow continuously:

  • Expense Ledger: A running CSV file recording every monetary expenditure. Fields: date, amount, category, description, budget line reference, receipt reference. One file per fiscal year.
  • Maintenance Ledger: A running log of all maintenance actions across all assets. Fields: date, asset ID, action, result, duration, next due date. One file per fiscal year.
  • Consumable Ledger: A running record of consumable additions and depletions. Fields: date, item, quantity added/removed, current stock level, reorder threshold. One file per fiscal year.
  • Procurement Ledger: A running record of all procurement events. Fields: date, items acquired, vendor, total cost, procurement window reference. One file per fiscal year.

Version Control for Forms

Forms are versioned using a two-part scheme: major.minor. Major version changes occur when fields are added or removed (the structure of the form changes). Minor version changes occur when instructions, descriptions, or formatting change without affecting the data structure. When a new major version is issued:

  1. The new version is published with a changelog documenting what changed and why.
  2. The old version is moved to the form archive but remains valid for records already created with it.
  3. All future records must use the new version.
  4. The master index (D11-007) is updated to reference the current form version.

Rules and Constraints

  • R-FRM-01: All administrative processes must use the canonical forms defined in this article. Ad-hoc record formats are prohibited for any process that has a defined form.
  • R-FRM-02: Every form must exist in both digital (structured plain-text) and physical (printable) formats. The two formats must contain identical fields.
  • R-FRM-03: Form fields marked as required must be completed for the record to be considered valid. A form with empty required fields is incomplete and must be corrected within 48 hours.
  • R-FRM-04: Ledgers must be updated within 24 hours of the event they record. A ledger entry that is more than one week late is in violation and the gap must be investigated to ensure no entries were lost.
  • R-FRM-05: When a process changes (as defined in its respective article), the corresponding form must be reviewed and updated if necessary. Form updates must not lag process changes by more than one quarter.
  • R-FRM-06: A current copy of every active form (both digital and physical) must be accessible from the institutional operations area. The operator should never need to search for the correct form; it should be immediately at hand.
  • R-FRM-07: Forms must not be modified informally. If an operator discovers that a form is missing a needed field or contains an unnecessary field, the modification goes through the version control process. Using a hand-modified form creates records that are inconsistent with the canonical format.

Failure Modes

  • Form proliferation. New forms are created for every slight variation of a process, resulting in dozens of forms that are nearly identical and confusing to select among. Mitigation: the Lightness Principle applies. Each form must serve a distinct process. Variations within a process should be handled through optional fields, not separate forms. The core form set defined in this article is intentionally limited.
  • Form obsolescence. Processes evolve but forms are not updated. Operators complete forms that no longer match the actual process, either omitting new requirements or filling in fields that are no longer relevant. Mitigation: R-FRM-05 ties form updates to process changes. The quarterly administrative review should include a form-process alignment check.
  • Informal modification. An operator adds handwritten fields to a physical form or edits the digital template without going through version control. Records created with modified forms are inconsistent with the canonical format and may be partially incompatible with analysis tools. Mitigation: R-FRM-07 prohibits informal modification. The version control process is deliberately lightweight to reduce the temptation to bypass it.
  • Ledger gaps. The 24-hour update requirement for ledgers is not met. Entries accumulate as "I will do it later" items and are eventually lost. Mitigation: R-FRM-04 defines the requirement. The weekly status note (D11-008) should include a ledger currency check: are all ledgers up to date? If not, update them before writing the status note.
  • Physical/digital divergence. The physical and digital forms get out of sync. The physical forms are a previous version while the digital forms have been updated, or vice versa. Records created with mismatched forms have structural inconsistencies. Mitigation: when a form version is updated, both formats must be updated simultaneously. R-FRM-02 requires identity between formats.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If forms have diverged from processes: Conduct a form-process alignment audit. For each active form, compare its fields to the requirements of the corresponding process article. Identify additions, removals, and modifications needed. Issue new form versions for all misaligned forms. Distribute both physical and digital copies.
  2. If ledgers have gaps: Reconstruct what can be reconstructed from receipts, records, memory, and other sources. For entries that cannot be reconstructed, note the gap in the ledger with a "RECONSTRUCTED" or "GAP" flag. Adjust the ledger update workflow to prevent recurrence — consider whether the update cadence or process is too burdensome.
  3. If forms have been informally modified: Identify all records created with modified forms. Determine whether the modifications captured essential information not in the canonical form (in which case, update the canonical form through version control) or whether the modifications were ad-hoc and the canonical form is sufficient. For records created with modified forms, transcribe any non-standard data into the notes field of a canonical-format record.
  4. If the physical form supply is exhausted: Print new copies from the digital masters. If printing is unavailable, hand-copy the form structure onto blank paper. The critical requirement is that the field structure is preserved, not the exact layout. A hand-drawn form with the correct fields is far better than no form at all.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: The core form set is being deployed for the first time. Some forms will prove to have too many fields; others will be missing critical fields. Expect at least one major version revision of most forms within the first two years. This is normal and expected. The forms are calibrated through use.
  • Years 3-10: The forms are stable. Major version changes are rare. Ledgers are growing and providing useful longitudinal data. The form system is an established part of institutional routine. New forms may be needed as the institution adds processes, but the core set should be essentially stable.
  • Years 10-25: Multiple form versions exist in the archive. Records created with different form versions must be reconcilable. The version control system proves its value: the changelog for each form provides a history of how processes evolved over time.
  • Years 25-50+: The form archive is a meta-record of institutional administrative evolution. How the forms changed over time tells the story of how the institution's operations changed. For successor operators, the current forms are the most practical orientation tool: they define exactly what information the institution captures for each process.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 — Founding Entry: I have always hated filling out forms. They feel like compliance exercises designed by someone who does not do the actual work. But I have also experienced the consequence of not having forms: inconsistent records that are missing critical information because I did not think to capture it at the time. The forms in this article are not designed by a distant bureaucrat. They are designed by me, for me, based on what I know I need to record and what I know I will forget to record if I do not have a checklist. Every field in every form exists because I have already forgotten it at least once. The forms are my memory of my own forgetfulness.
