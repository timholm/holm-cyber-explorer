D9-010 — Degraded-Capacity Education Plan

Document ID
D9-010
Domain
9 -- Education & Training
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D9-001, D9-002, D9-008, D8-013, GOV-001, OPS-001, D12-001
Depended Upon By
D10 (degraded operations depend on degraded-capacity training), D12-001 (disaster recovery references degraded education plans)

Purpose

This article defines how the holm.chat Documentation Institution maintains its capacity for knowledge transfer when operating with minimal personnel. It addresses the most extreme scenario in institutional education: what happens when the institution is reduced to a single person who must simultaneously operate the institution, learn its systems, and preserve its capacity to train their own eventual successor?

The degraded-capacity education plan is the last line of defense against institutional knowledge death. It is activated when normal education mechanisms -- mentorship, structured curricula, scheduled training -- are unavailable because the people who would deliver them are unavailable. In this scenario, the institution's documentation must function as the sole teacher, and the learning process must be integrated into operational necessity rather than conducted as a separate activity.

This article is intentionally written to be the most accessible, most clearly written article in Domain 9. Per the Domain 9 maintenance plan, it must be readable and actionable by someone who has had no prior exposure to the institution. If a person inherits this institution with no preparation and no mentor, this article -- together with D9-002 (the onboarding guide) -- must be sufficient to start the learning process. Everything else in Domain 9 describes the education system as it should work. This article describes how it works when everything else has failed.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The definition of degraded-capacity states and their triggers.
  • Emergency training priorities: what to learn first when time and resources are scarce.
  • The "one person left" scenario: how a sole inheritor achieves operational competence.
  • The integration of learning with operation: how to learn by doing when there is no time for separate study.
  • The minimum viable education: the absolute minimum knowledge required to keep the institution alive.
  • Recovery from degraded capacity: how to restore normal education operations as capacity returns.

This article does not cover:

  • Normal-capacity education (see all other D9 articles).
  • The operational aspects of degraded-capacity operation (see D10, OPS-001).
  • Disaster recovery for systems and data (see D12-001); this article addresses the human knowledge dimension only.
  • The governance aspects of succession (see GOV-001).

Background

3.1 Degraded-Capacity States

The institution's educational capacity exists on a continuum. At full capacity, multiple trained people can teach, multiple curricula are active, mentorship is available, and training can proceed on its normal schedule. At zero capacity, the last knowledgeable person is gone and only the documentation remains. This article defines three degraded states between those extremes:

  • State 1 -- Reduced Mentorship: The institution has multiple members, but the most experienced person (the primary trainer) is unavailable. Remaining members must train each other or learn from documentation. This is the mildest degradation. The cross-training matrix (D9-008) should prevent this from being critical.
  • State 2 -- Solo Operator: The institution has exactly one person. There is no one to learn from and no one to teach. The operator must self-train from documentation and maintain their own competence through deliberate practice. This is the institution's founding state and its most common degraded state.
  • State 3 -- Cold Start: The institution has been inherited by someone with no prior involvement. The previous operator is unavailable. The inheritor must learn everything from the documentation. This is the worst case -- the scenario that tests whether the institution's educational design truly works.

3.2 The Triage Principle

When educational capacity is degraded, not everything can be learned at once. The institution must triage: what must be learned immediately to prevent catastrophic failure? What can wait days? What can wait weeks? What can wait months?

The triage order is derived from operational criticality. A function that must be performed daily (backup verification, system health check) must be learned before a function that is performed monthly (documentation audit, quarterly review). A function whose failure causes data loss must be learned before a function whose failure causes inconvenience. The emergency training priorities in Section 4 codify this triage.

3.3 Learning by Operating

In degraded capacity, there is no separate "study time." The operator learns by doing. Every operational task is also a learning opportunity. The key insight is that the documentation's self-teaching requirement (D9-001, R-D9-01) means that following a procedure from the documentation is simultaneously operating and learning. The operator who performs the daily backup while reading the backup procedure for the first time is both keeping the institution alive and training themselves.

This learning-by-operating model has limitations. It produces Stage 2 competence (Guided Execution) efficiently but does not reliably produce Stage 3 (Independent Execution) or Stage 4 (Adaptive Mastery). For those higher stages, deliberate study of the Background and System Model sections is needed -- understanding why, not just how. But Stage 2 is sufficient for survival. Higher stages can be pursued once the immediate operational crisis is past.

System Model

4.1 Emergency Training Priorities

When degraded-capacity education is activated, learning proceeds in the following priority order. Each priority level must be achieved before significant time is invested in the next. The time estimates assume a State 3 (Cold Start) scenario; States 1 and 2 will typically be faster.

Priority 0: Orientation (Hours 1-8). Read this article. Read D9-002 (onboarding guide) Phase 0. Read CON-001 (founding mandate) and ETH-001 (ethical foundations). The goal is to understand what this institution is, why it exists, and what you are responsible for. Do not attempt to operate any systems yet. Read first.

Priority 1: System Survival (Days 1-7). Learn the daily operational cycle: backup execution, backup verification, system health monitoring. These are Tier A functions (D9-008). Follow the documented procedures exactly, step by step, consulting the documentation at every step. The goal is to keep the systems running and data safe while you learn everything else. Key documents: OPS-001 (daily cycle), Domain 4 operational articles (hardware health), Domain 6 operational articles (backup procedures).

Priority 2: Security Posture (Days 7-14). Learn the security architecture: the air gap, the trust model, the import pipeline. Read SEC-001 (threat model). Understand what you must not do (connect to networks, bypass the quarantine process). The goal is to avoid making a security mistake that compromises the institution while you are still learning it. Key documents: SEC-001, D18-002 (import pipeline).

Priority 3: Governance and Documentation (Days 14-30). Learn the decision log, the governance tiers, and the documentation system. Begin maintaining the decision log for your own decisions. Read GOV-001 (authority model). The goal is to begin creating institutional records of what you are doing and why, so that your learning process itself becomes part of the institutional memory. Key documents: GOV-001, D20-002 (decision log operations).

Priority 4: Comprehensive Understanding (Days 30-90). Complete the onboarding sequence (D9-002). Read the domain philosophy articles. Study the System Model and Background sections of operational articles you have been following procedurally. The goal is to transition from Stage 2 (Guided Execution) to Stage 3 (Independent Execution) for Tier A and Tier B functions.

Priority 5: Full Competence (Months 3-12). Pursue Stage 3 competence across all institutional functions. Begin developing Stage 4 capability in your primary areas. Create or update training materials based on your learning experience. The goal is to achieve the competence level where you can not only operate the institution but train your own successor.

4.2 The Minimum Viable Education

If time is extremely scarce -- if the institution must be operated and there is barely time to learn -- the absolute minimum viable education consists of three capabilities:

  1. Keep the data safe: Execute the daily backup and verify its integrity. This single function prevents irreversible loss.
  2. Do not compromise security: Understand and maintain the air gap. Do not connect isolated systems to networks. Do not bypass the import quarantine.
  3. Record what you do: Maintain the decision log. Even if your entries are brief and uncertain, they create a record that future operators (including your future self) can use.

These three capabilities can be learned in a single day from the documentation. They do not constitute operational competence. They constitute survival. Everything else builds on this foundation.

4.3 The Learning Journal

During degraded-capacity education, the learner must maintain a learning journal. This is a dated, chronological record of: what was studied each day, what was confusing, what questions remain unanswered, what documentation gaps were discovered, and what operational decisions were made and why. The learning journal serves three purposes:

  • Self-monitoring: The learner can track their own progress and identify areas that need more attention.
  • Documentation improvement: Every confusion point is a potential documentation gap. The learning journal is a systematic collection of improvement data for the education system.
  • Institutional memory: The journal documents the degraded-capacity period itself -- what happened, how the institution was maintained, what was learned. This becomes part of the institutional record and is invaluable for future operators who may face similar situations.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D9-10-01: This article must be written in the plainest possible language. It must assume no prior knowledge of the institution beyond basic literacy and general technical aptitude. It is the entry point for someone who has never seen this institution before. Jargon must be defined. References to other articles must include enough context that the reader understands what they will find there without having read it yet.
  • R-D9-10-02: The emergency training priorities defined in Section 4.1 must be followed in order during State 3 (Cold Start) activation. A person in a cold start must not attempt Priority 2 tasks until Priority 1 capabilities are established. The triage order exists because some mistakes are irreversible (data loss from missed backups, security compromise from bypassed air gap) and others are recoverable (governance errors, documentation gaps).
  • R-D9-10-03: During degraded-capacity operation, the operational tempo is reduced to the minimum necessary. Per OPS-001, functions are triaged: Tier A functions are performed daily, Tier B functions are performed as time allows, Tier C functions are suspended. This creates time for learning. A degraded-capacity operator who spends all their time operating and none learning will never achieve the competence needed to restore full capacity.
  • R-D9-10-04: The learning journal (Section 4.3) is mandatory during all degraded-capacity states. It is maintained alongside the operational log and the decision log. Entries need not be formal or polished. They must be honest: recording what the learner does not understand is as important as recording what they do.
  • R-D9-10-05: When degraded capacity is caused by personnel departure, the departing person (if available) must prioritize knowledge transfer over all other activities during their remaining time. Per D9-008 recovery procedures, the departing person's primary responsibility shifts to training their replacement, not to ongoing operations.
  • R-D9-10-06: This article must be reviewed annually, and must be reviewed by someone who did not write it. The reviewer must attempt to follow the emergency training priorities as if they were a cold-start inheritor. If the article fails this test, it must be revised until it passes.
  • R-D9-10-07: During degraded-capacity education, no architectural changes, no system redesigns, and no governance policy changes are permitted until Priority 4 (comprehensive understanding) is achieved. The operator must understand the institution as it is before attempting to change it.

Failure Modes

  • The panic operator. A cold-start inheritor begins making changes immediately without reading the documentation, motivated by urgency or anxiety. They accidentally compromise the air gap, overwrite data, or make governance decisions they do not understand. Mitigation: R-D9-10-02 requires following the priority order. R-D9-10-07 prohibits architectural changes until comprehensive understanding is achieved. The article's first instruction is: "Read first. Do not operate any systems yet."

  • The documentation desert. The documentation does not actually meet the self-teaching requirement. The cold-start inheritor cannot learn critical functions from it. The degraded-capacity plan assumes capabilities the documentation does not deliver. Mitigation: R-D9-10-06 requires annual testing of this article by a non-author reviewer following the priorities. D9-009 (training material standards) and D9-012 (training effectiveness evaluation) provide ongoing quality assurance.

  • The learning plateau. The operator achieves Stage 2 competence through learning-by-operating but never progresses to Stage 3 because there is no time for deliberate study. The operator can follow procedures but cannot adapt when procedures are insufficient. Mitigation: R-D9-10-03 requires reduced operational tempo to create learning time. Priority 4 explicitly includes studying the "why" (System Model and Background sections), not just the "how" (procedures).

  • The isolation despair. A cold-start inheritor, working alone with no mentor and no support, becomes overwhelmed and disengaged. The institution is technically accessible but psychologically burdensome. Mitigation: this is acknowledged but not fully solvable by documentation alone. The learning journal provides a sense of progress. The Priority 0 orientation documents (CON-001, ETH-001) explain why the institution matters, which may provide motivation. The Commentary sections throughout the documentation provide a human voice.

  • The perpetual emergency. The operator never exits degraded-capacity mode. They remain at Priority 1 or 2 indefinitely because operational demands consume all available time. The institution survives but never thrives. Mitigation: the priority structure is designed so that each level, once achieved, requires progressively less time to maintain. Daily backups become routine. Security practices become habitual. The time freed up is explicitly allocated to learning (R-D9-10-03).

  • Incomplete succession preparation. The previous operator never created the tacit knowledge artifacts, never tested the documentation's self-teaching capability, and never prepared for the scenario this article addresses. The cold-start inheritor faces not just a learning challenge but a documentation quality crisis. Mitigation: D9-007 (tacit knowledge capture), D9-008 (cross-training), and D9-009 (training material standards) are preventive measures. If they were not followed, the cold-start inheritor must work with what exists and rebuild what is missing, starting with the learning journal as the reconstruction record.

Recovery Procedures

  1. Entering degraded capacity (State 1 -- Reduced Mentorship): Consult the cross-training matrix (D9-008). Identify which functions are now below minimum coverage. Assign remaining members to cross-train on the highest-priority uncovered functions. Reduce the operational tempo for non-critical functions to create training time. Record the state change in the decision log.

  2. Entering degraded capacity (State 2 -- Solo Operator): Confirm that all Tier A functions can be performed. Review all documentation for Tier A functions to ensure self-teaching quality. Begin maintaining the learning journal if the solo operator is newly sole (i.e., they did not build the systems). Begin the quarterly self-exercise schedule (D9-008, R-D9-08-07) for all functions to prevent atrophy. Record the state change in the decision log.

  3. Entering degraded capacity (State 3 -- Cold Start): Follow the emergency training priorities (Section 4.1) exactly. Begin the learning journal immediately. Do not attempt to operate systems until Priority 0 (orientation) reading is complete. Proceed through the priorities in order. Contact information for institutional advisors or emergency contacts, if any exist, should be stored in a clearly labeled file at the top level of the documentation system. Record all activities in the learning journal.

  4. Exiting degraded capacity: Degraded capacity is exited when the cross-training matrix (D9-008) shows minimum coverage restored for all Tier A and Tier B functions. The exit is recorded in the decision log. The learning journal is archived as an institutional memory artifact. A post-degradation review is conducted: what documentation gaps were discovered? What training materials need to be created or revised? What failed? The review findings are used to improve the degraded-capacity plan for future activations.

  5. If the documentation proves insufficient during cold start: The inheritor must create RECONSTRUCTED documentation as they figure out how systems work through direct inspection. These reconstructed documents are flagged as "unverified -- derived from system inspection, not from authoritative source." They are better than nothing and become the basis for proper documentation once the inheritor achieves sufficient understanding to verify them.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: This article is the institution's current reality. With a single operator, the institution is permanently in State 2. The primary work is ensuring that the documentation quality is sufficient for a future State 3 activation. The operator should periodically re-read this article and test it: can you imagine a stranger following these instructions and succeeding? If not, the article or its referenced documentation needs improvement.

  • Years 5-15: If the institution has grown beyond one person, this article moves from describing the present to describing a contingency. It should be tested during this period -- ideally by having a new member attempt the cold-start learning path (or a simulation of it) to verify that the emergency training priorities and the documentation quality are adequate. The first real test will reveal weaknesses that no amount of theoretical planning can anticipate.

  • Years 15-30: The article should have been tested at least once in some form. Its revisions should reflect real experience. The institution may have survived a degraded-capacity episode and learned from it. The priority order may need adjustment based on how the institution's systems have evolved. The minimum viable education may need revision as the institution's complexity changes.

  • Years 30-50+: This article must remain the most accessible document in Domain 9. Even as the institution grows in sophistication, the degraded-capacity plan must remain readable by someone with no institutional context. The temptation will be to assume that degraded capacity is unlikely because the institution is now well-established. Resist that temptation. The fifty-year horizon includes scenarios that are difficult to predict. This article is insurance against the unpredictable.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: This article is addressed to a person I have never met, who may not yet exist, in a situation I hope never occurs. If you are reading this because you have inherited this institution without preparation, without a mentor, and without prior knowledge of what it contains or how it works: I am sorry that the transition was not smoother. I tried to prepare for you.

Here is what matters right now: the institution's data is safe as long as the backups continue. The backups are the first thing to learn. Everything else can wait hours, days, even weeks. But if the backups stop and a disk fails, data may be lost that cannot be recovered. Start with the backups.

The second thing that matters: do not connect any of the institution's systems to a network. They are deliberately isolated. The isolation is not an accident or a limitation -- it is a security architecture. Read SEC-001 when you have time to understand why. For now, just maintain the isolation.

The third thing: write down what you do. Every day, write a few sentences in the learning journal about what you studied, what you did, what confused you. This record will help you later, and it will help whoever comes after you.

Everything else -- the governance model, the documentation standards, the quality audits, the training curricula -- can be learned over weeks and months. The three things above keep the institution alive while you learn. Focus on them first.

I wrote this institution to outlast me. If you are reading this, it has succeeded at least partially: the documentation survived to reach you. Now it is your turn to decide whether it continues. I believe it is worth continuing. The rest of the documentation explains why.
