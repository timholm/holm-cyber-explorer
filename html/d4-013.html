D4-013 — Infrastructure Documentation Standards

Document ID
D4-013
Domain
4 -- Infrastructure & Power
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D4-001, CON-001
Depended Upon By
All Domain 4 articles (documentation standard for all infrastructure records)

Purpose

This article defines the standards, formats, and requirements for all physical infrastructure documentation within the holm.chat Documentation Institution. Infrastructure documentation is the bridge between the physical world and the institutional memory. Without it, knowledge of the infrastructure exists only in the operator's head, and that knowledge dies or degrades when the operator changes, forgets, or is unavailable.

The institution is, at its core, a documentation project. It would be deeply ironic -- and operationally fatal -- if the institution's own physical infrastructure were poorly documented. This article ensures that the same rigor applied to the documentary corpus is applied to the records of the physical systems that house, power, and connect that corpus.

This article defines six categories of infrastructure documentation: the hardware register, the network diagram, the power diagram, the maintenance log, the spare parts register, and the decommissioning log. For each category, this article specifies the required fields, the format, the update triggers, the storage location, and the retention period. The goal is that any competent person, armed with these documents and nothing else, could understand the institution's entire physical infrastructure.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The six categories of infrastructure documentation and their purposes.
  • Required fields and format for each documentation category.
  • Update triggers: when and why each document is updated.
  • Storage and backup requirements for infrastructure documentation.
  • The documentation audit process: how documentation is verified for accuracy and completeness.
  • Labeling standards for physical infrastructure components.

This article does not cover:

  • The content of the institutional documentary corpus (see Domain 6).
  • Software configuration documentation (see Domain 5).
  • The specific maintenance procedures that generate maintenance log entries (see D4-011).

Background

CON-001 mandates that the institution be self-built, meaning the operator understands every component. But understanding fades over time. The details of a cable run installed three years ago are no longer fresh. The reason a particular switch port was assigned to a particular machine is forgotten. The date a UPS battery was last replaced is uncertain. Documentation transforms ephemeral understanding into permanent record.

D4-001 introduces the requirement that any competent person can understand the physical layout from documentation alone. This article makes that requirement concrete. It is not sufficient to have documentation that the original operator can understand with the aid of memory. The documentation must stand alone: complete, current, and interpretable by someone who has never seen the physical site.

This standard serves three critical functions. First, it supports daily operations by providing the operator with reliable reference material for maintenance, troubleshooting, and modifications. Second, it supports continuity by ensuring that a successor operator can take over without oral transmission of undocumented knowledge. Third, it supports auditing by providing a verifiable record against which the physical infrastructure can be compared.

System Model

13.1 The Hardware Register

The hardware register is the authoritative record of every piece of hardware in the institution. Every hardware item, from the primary server to the smallest sensor, has an entry. Required fields:

FieldDescription
Register IDA unique identifier assigned sequentially (HW-001, HW-002, etc.)
TypeCategory: server, storage, switch, UPS, sensor, cable, etc.
Make and ModelManufacturer and model number
Serial NumberManufacturer's serial number (if applicable)
SpecificationsKey specifications (capacity, speed, voltage, etc.)
Date AcquiredThe date the hardware was purchased or received
Date DeployedThe date the hardware was placed into active service
RoleThe hardware's function within the institution (e.g., "primary storage server," "equipment room temperature sensor")
LocationPhysical location (e.g., "equipment room, rack position 3," "south wall, cable tray")
IP AddressIf networked, the assigned IP address
Power CircuitWhich power circuit or UPS the hardware is connected to
StatusActive, spare, decommissioned
NotesAny additional information (firmware version, configuration notes, known issues)

The hardware register is updated within 24 hours of any hardware acquisition, deployment, role change, relocation, or decommissioning.

13.2 The Network Diagram

The network diagram is a visual representation of all network connections within the institution. It includes:

  • Every networked device, identified by register ID and hostname.
  • Every switch, with port numbers labeled.
  • Every cable run, showing source device, source port, destination device, destination port, cable type, and cable length.
  • The IP address of every device.
  • VLAN assignments, if VLANs are in use.
  • The quarantine machine shown as explicitly disconnected (a clear visual indicator that it is not on the network).

The diagram is maintained in a format that can be reproduced without proprietary software. Acceptable formats include: hand-drawn diagrams (photographed or scanned for backup), diagrams created in open-format tools (SVG, plain text ASCII diagrams), or diagrams in widely-supported formats (PDF). The diagram is updated within 24 hours of any network change. The previous version is retained for historical reference.

13.3 The Power Diagram

The power diagram shows how power flows from generation to consumption:

  • Power sources (grid connection, solar panels, generator, battery banks).
  • Distribution components (main panel, sub-panels, transfer switches, inverters, charge controllers).
  • UPS units and their loads.
  • Individual circuits and which equipment each circuit serves.
  • Circuit breaker ratings and locations.
  • Grounding and bonding connections.

The power diagram follows the same format requirements as the network diagram. It is updated within 24 hours of any change to the power infrastructure.

13.4 The Maintenance Log

The maintenance log (defined operationally in D4-011) records all maintenance activities. Required fields per entry:

  • Date and time
  • Maintenance type (daily, weekly, monthly, quarterly, annual, or unscheduled)
  • Tasks performed (reference to D4-011 task identifiers)
  • Findings (nominal, or specific observations)
  • Actions taken (if any issues found)
  • Follow-up required (yes/no, and description if yes)
  • Operator signature or identifier

13.5 The Spare Parts Register

The spare parts register (defined operationally in D4-012) tracks all spare inventory. Required fields per item:

  • Inventory ID (SP-001, SP-002, etc.)
  • Component type, make, model
  • Serial number (if applicable)
  • Date acquired
  • Storage location
  • Intended use (which production system it serves as spare for)
  • Status (available, deployed, retired)
  • Shelf life notes (next required maintenance date)

13.6 The Decommissioning Log

The decommissioning log (defined operationally in D4-010) records all hardware retirements. Required fields per entry:

  • Hardware register ID of the decommissioned item
  • Date of decommissioning
  • Reason (failure, obsolescence, replacement, security concern)
  • Data classification of any media (Tier A, B, or C per D4-010)
  • Sanitization method used
  • Physical destruction method used (if applicable)
  • Disposal method for remaining components
  • Operator confirmation of completeness

13.7 Physical Labeling Standards

In addition to the registers and diagrams, the physical infrastructure itself is labeled:

  • Equipment: Every piece of hardware bears a label with its register ID (HW-001, etc.) and its hostname or role name. Labels are printed, not handwritten, and use durable adhesive appropriate for the environment.
  • Cables: Every cable is labeled at both ends with the source and destination (e.g., "HW-003 eth0 -> SW-001 port 5"). Cable labels use wrap-around labels or adhesive flags.
  • Circuit breakers: Every circuit breaker is labeled with the circuit it protects and the equipment on that circuit.
  • Storage locations: Spare parts storage bins or shelves are labeled with the inventory ID range they contain.

Labels are checked during the quarterly maintenance audit (D4-011) and replaced if faded, damaged, or detached.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D4-70: Documentation Is Authoritative. When there is a discrepancy between documentation and physical reality, the physical reality is correct and the documentation is wrong. The documentation is updated immediately. But the discrepancy itself is investigated: how did the documentation become inaccurate? Was a change made without updating documentation? Was documentation updated incorrectly?
  • R-D4-71: 24-Hour Update Window. All infrastructure documentation is updated within 24 hours of any change to the infrastructure it describes. Changes made without corresponding documentation updates are violations of this standard.
  • R-D4-72: Standalone Comprehensibility. All infrastructure documentation must be comprehensible to a competent technical person who has never visited the site. Documentation that relies on the reader's prior knowledge of the site ("the switch near the window," "the drive on the left") is inadequate. Use register IDs, rack positions, and physical coordinates.
  • R-D4-73: Open Formats. Infrastructure documentation is stored in formats that do not require proprietary software to read or edit. Plain text, PDF, SVG, and standard image formats (PNG, JPEG) are acceptable. Proprietary formats (Visio, proprietary database files) are acceptable only if an export to an open format is maintained alongside.
  • R-D4-74: Documentation Is Backed Up. All digital infrastructure documentation is included in the institution's backup procedures (Domain 12). Physical documentation (handwritten logs, printed diagrams) is photographed or scanned and included in digital backups.
  • R-D4-75: Annual Documentation Audit. During the annual maintenance session (D4-011), every infrastructure document is audited for accuracy and completeness. The audit compares each document to physical reality and flags discrepancies.

Failure Modes

  • Documentation rot. Documentation gradually falls out of date as small changes accumulate without updates. After months or years, the documentation no longer reflects reality. Mitigation: the 24-hour update window (R-D4-71) prevents individual instances of rot. The quarterly and annual audits (D4-011, R-D4-75) catch accumulated drift.
  • Single point of documentation failure. Documentation is stored in only one location (a single notebook, a single file on a single drive). If that location is destroyed, all infrastructure knowledge is lost. Mitigation: R-D4-74 requires backup. Digital documentation is included in the institution's regular backup cycle. Physical documentation is digitized and backed up.
  • Excessive documentation burden. The documentation requirements become so onerous that the operator spends more time documenting than maintaining. Documentation quality drops because the operator is cutting corners to save time. Mitigation: the documentation standards in this article are designed to be proportionate. Each register has a defined set of required fields, not an unlimited set. The focus is on the minimum information needed for standalone comprehensibility.
  • Labeling failure. Physical labels fall off, fade, or become unreadable. The operator can no longer identify which cable goes where by looking at the labels. Mitigation: quarterly label inspections (D4-011). Use of durable label materials rated for the environment. Replacement of degraded labels is a maintenance task, not a discretionary action.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If documentation is found to be significantly out of date: Schedule a documentation recovery session. Walk through the entire physical infrastructure with the relevant register or diagram. Update every entry to match physical reality. This may take a full day for a comprehensive recovery. Once complete, resume the regular 24-hour update discipline. Investigate the root cause of the documentation failure.
  2. If documentation is lost (digital file corruption, physical document destruction): Reconstruct the documentation from physical inspection of the infrastructure. For the hardware register: inspect every piece of hardware and create entries from what is observable (labels, serial number plates, visual identification). For the network diagram: trace every cable physically. For the power diagram: trace every circuit from the panel. This reconstruction is time-consuming but possible if the physical infrastructure is intact. Prioritize the hardware register and network diagram as the most operationally critical documents.
  3. If labels are found to be missing or unreadable across multiple components: Conduct a systematic relabeling session. Use the hardware register and network diagram to identify each component and apply new labels. If the register is also inaccurate, perform the documentation recovery (procedure 1) simultaneously.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: The documentation standards are established alongside the initial infrastructure deployment. The first generation of documents sets the format and level of detail. The operator discovers which fields are most useful in practice and which are rarely referenced. The annual documentation audit produces refinements.
  • Years 3-10: The documentation becomes the primary reference for maintenance and troubleshooting. The operator relies on it daily. The value of thorough documentation becomes apparent whenever a non-obvious connection or configuration must be recalled. Historical documentation (old versions of diagrams, past maintenance logs) proves its value when investigating long-term trends or understanding why a past decision was made.
  • Years 10-25: If the institution transitions to a successor operator, the documentation's standalone comprehensibility is tested for the first time. Any gaps are identified and filled. The documentation standards may be refined based on the successor's experience reading and using documentation they did not create.
  • Years 25-50: The documentation has been maintained through multiple hardware generations, potentially multiple operators, and possibly a site migration. The specific formats may have evolved (digital tools change, labeling materials improve). The underlying principles -- authoritative, current, standalone, backed up -- endure because they serve the same human needs regardless of the technology documented.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry:

Documentation is the institutional memory of the physical world. The documentary corpus is the institution's purpose; infrastructure documentation is the institution's self-awareness. Without it, the institution does not know what it is made of, how it is connected, or what state it is in.

I have seen the alternative. I have inherited systems where the only documentation was the system itself. "Just trace the cables." "Just look at the labels." But the labels were faded, and the cables ran through a ceiling where no one could see them, and the operator who installed them was long gone. Every troubleshooting session began with thirty minutes of archaeology before the actual problem could be addressed.

The time to document is now -- at the moment of installation, at the moment of change. Not "later." Not "when I have time." Now. Because "later" becomes "never," and "when I have time" becomes "when a crisis forces me to reconstruct what I should have recorded." Record it now. Your future self -- or your successor -- will be grateful.
