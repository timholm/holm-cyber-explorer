D14-013 — Research Archive and Knowledge Preservation

Document ID
D14-013
Domain
14 -- Research & Theory
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
ETH-001, CON-001, OPS-001, D14-001, D14-002, D14-004, D14-005, D14-008, D13-001, D11-001
Depended Upon By
All Domain 14 articles that produce research outputs requiring archival. Referenced by Domain 6 (Data & Archives) for research data archival standards. Referenced by Domain 12 (Disaster Recovery) for research archive as critical asset. Referenced by Domain 13 (Evolution) for archive format decisions.

Purpose

This article defines how research outputs -- positive results, negative results, inconclusive results, experiment logs, Technology Decision Records, Validation Records, Replication Records, the Living Research State Document, and all associated raw data -- are archived, indexed, and made accessible to current and future operators. The research archive is the institution's intellectual memory. Without it, every insight, every failure, every painstaking investigation eventually vanishes into the fog of time, leaving no trace except whatever operational changes it might have influenced.

The archive is not a passive storage system. It is an active resource that must be organized, indexed, maintained, and periodically reviewed. A pile of experiment logs in a directory is not an archive any more than a pile of books on a floor is a library. The difference is structure: the index that makes items findable, the relationships that connect related items, the metadata that provides context, and the preservation practices that ensure the items remain readable across decades.

This article also addresses the specific challenge of research archive preservation in an air-gapped, off-grid environment over a 50-year horizon. The storage media will change. The file systems will change. The formats may become obsolete. The archive must be designed from the start to survive these changes, and the maintenance plan must include regular migration to current storage technologies and format verification.

Scope

In scope: - The research archive structure: organization, hierarchy, and naming conventions. - The archive index: how to find items in the archive. - Archival format requirements: format longevity, readability, and compliance with 50-year continuity rules. - Archive maintenance: integrity verification, format migration, and redundancy management. - Access and retrieval procedures: how to find and use archived research outputs. - The relationship between the research archive and the knowledge registry (D14-004). - Archive backup and disaster recovery provisions.

Out of scope: - General data storage and backup procedures (Domain 6, though the research archive is stored within the general data infrastructure). - The research methodology that produces archive content (D14-002, D14-003). - Knowledge classification (D14-004, though the archive stores classified knowledge). - The general institutional archive (Domain 6 series).

Background

3.1 The Archive as Institutional Asset

In a single-operator institution with a 50-year horizon, the research archive has a unique value: it is the institution's conversation with its own future. The experiment conducted in 2028 may inform a decision in 2048. The technology evaluation from 2035 may prevent a mistake in 2055. The negative result recorded in 2030 may save weeks of wasted effort in 2060. Every item in the archive is a message from the past to the future, and its value increases with time because the future has less access to the context in which the research was conducted.

This increasing value makes archive maintenance a compounding investment. Every year the archive is maintained, its total value grows. Every year it is neglected, its total value degrades. An unmaintained archive is a slowly dying memory.

3.2 The Format Longevity Challenge

The research archive must remain readable for the lifetime of the institution. This means that file formats, storage media, and access tools must either be permanent (which nothing in computing is) or must be periodically migrated to current technologies. The 50-year continuity rules established in CON-001 and the meta-framework address this challenge for the institution's general data. The research archive has additional requirements because its content includes not just text documents but structured data (experiment measurements, statistical analyses, equipment configurations) that must be preserved with semantic fidelity, not just bitwise fidelity.

A CSV file can be preserved bitwise for 50 years. But if the column headers are abbreviated and the units are not specified, the file may be bitwise perfect and semantically useless in 2076 because nobody remembers that "temp_c" meant "temperature in Celsius measured at the south wall sensor." Semantic preservation -- ensuring that future operators can understand the meaning of the data, not just read its bits -- is the deeper challenge.

3.3 The Findability Problem

An archive that cannot be searched is an archive that does not exist, functionally. If the operator must browse through hundreds of files to find a relevant experiment log, they will not search the archive -- they will rely on memory, which is precisely the failure mode the archive is meant to prevent. The archive must be indexed in a way that supports multiple search strategies: by date, by topic, by knowledge category, by experiment ID, by technology name, and by keyword. The index must be maintained as new items are added and must itself be stored in a format that survives the 50-year continuity requirement.

System Model

4.1 Archive Structure

The research archive is organized into a hierarchical directory structure that reflects the types of research output the institution produces:

  • /research-archive/experiments/ -- Experiment logs (D14-002), organized by year (YYYY/) and experiment ID (EXP-YYYY-NNN/). Each experiment directory contains: the pre-registration document, the experiment log, the raw data files, the analysis files, the Chain of Custody Records (D14-008), and the Validation Record (D14-009).
  • /research-archive/technology-decisions/ -- Technology Decision Records (D14-003), organized by year. Each TDR directory contains: the TDR document, the rubric scoring sheets, proof-of-concept documentation, and the final decision record.
  • /research-archive/negative-results/ -- The Negative Results Registry (D14-005) master file, plus individual detailed entries organized by year.
  • /research-archive/knowledge-registry/ -- The Knowledge Registry (D14-004) master file, plus supporting documentation for individual entries.
  • /research-archive/replications/ -- Replication Records (D14-012), organized by year and linked to the original experiments they replicate.
  • /research-archive/validations/ -- Validation Records (D14-009), organized by year.
  • /research-archive/integration/ -- Integration Decision Records (D14-010) and post-integration assessments.
  • /research-archive/lrsd/ -- Living Research State Document (D14-006) versions, organized chronologically. Each monthly update is preserved as a separate version.
  • /research-archive/plans/ -- Annual Research Plans (D14-007), Ethics Pre-Checks (D14-011), and other planning documents.
  • /research-archive/index/ -- The archive index files (Section 4.2).

4.2 The Archive Index

The archive index is a set of plain-text files that provide multiple access paths to archive content:

Chronological index: A master list of all archive items in date order. For each item: date, type (experiment, TDR, negative result, etc.), ID, title, and file path within the archive. This index supports browsing the institution's research history in sequence.

Topic index: A categorized list of archive items organized by subject area. Subject areas are derived from the knowledge taxonomy (D14-004) categories and subcategories. Each entry includes: topic, item type, ID, title, and file path. This index supports searching by subject.

Technology index: A list of all archive items related to specific technologies. Each technology that has been evaluated, adopted, or referenced in research has an entry listing all related TDRs, experiments, negative results, and knowledge registry items. This index supports technology-specific research.

Cross-reference index: A list of relationships between archive items. For each item, the index records: what it references, what references it, what it supersedes, and what it contradicts. This index supports navigating the web of relationships between research outputs.

Keyword index: A manually maintained keyword index for archive items. Each item is assigned 3-10 keywords at the time of archival. The keywords are drawn from a controlled vocabulary that is maintained and extended as the archive grows. The keyword index supports free-text-like searching without requiring full-text search software.

All index files are in plain text format (one entry per line, tab-separated fields), readable without any specialized software. The index is updated whenever a new item is added to the archive.

4.3 Archival Format Requirements

All research archive content must comply with the following format requirements:

Text documents: Plain text (UTF-8), Markdown, or HTML. No proprietary word processing formats. Documents that require formatting may use HTML or Markdown, which can be rendered by any web browser or text editor.

Structured data: CSV (comma-separated values) or TSV (tab-separated values) with header rows that include full field names and units. Column abbreviations are prohibited unless a legend is included in the file header. Each data file must include a metadata header (as comments or in a companion .meta file) that describes: what the data represents, when it was collected, what equipment produced it, what units are used, and any known limitations or caveats.

Images and diagrams: PNG for raster images, SVG for vector diagrams. JPEG is acceptable for photographs. All images must have accompanying text descriptions that provide the essential information the image conveys -- a future operator who cannot render the image format must still be able to understand what it showed.

Raw data: In addition to the format requirements above, raw data files must include a README.txt in the same directory that describes the data format, collection method, and any processing that would be required to interpret the data.

4.4 Archive Maintenance

The research archive requires periodic maintenance to remain usable over the institution's lifetime:

Monthly: Index update. Whenever new items are added to the archive, the relevant index files are updated. This should be done at the time of archival, not deferred.

Quarterly: Integrity verification. A random sample of at least 5% of archived files is verified against their stored integrity hashes (D14-008). Any failures trigger a full integrity check and investigation.

Annually: Archive audit. A comprehensive review that checks: Is the archive complete? Are all experiment logs, TDRs, negative results entries, and other required documents present? Are the indexes current? Are all formats still readable with currently available tools? Are redundant copies in sync? The audit results are documented in the archive itself.

Every 5 years: Format review. Are the archive formats still widely supported? Are any formats showing signs of obsolescence? Are there new formats that would be more appropriate? If format migration is needed, it is planned and executed as a formal project, with the migration itself documented in the archive.

Every 10 years: Storage media review. Is the physical storage media still reliable? Are the storage technologies still maintainable? Should the archive be migrated to new media? Storage media migration is one of the most critical archive maintenance tasks because media degradation can be silent until it produces data loss.

4.5 Archive Backup and Disaster Recovery

The research archive is classified as a critical institutional asset and is subject to the backup and disaster recovery provisions of Domain 6 and Domain 12. In addition to the general requirements:

  • The archive must be backed up to at least two physically separate storage locations.
  • At least one backup copy must be stored on different media technology than the primary (e.g., if the primary is on SSDs, one backup should be on HDDs or optical media).
  • The archive must be included in all disaster recovery scenarios. The ability to restore the research archive from backup must be verified annually as part of disaster recovery testing.
  • If the institution experiences a disaster that destroys the primary archive, the recovery plan must prioritize the research archive alongside operational data. Research knowledge that is lost cannot be regenerated -- unlike system configurations, which can be rebuilt from documentation.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-RAK-01: All research outputs (experiment logs, TDRs, negative results entries, Validation Records, Replication Records, Integration Decision Records, LRSD versions, Annual Research Plans, Ethics Pre-Checks, and associated raw data) must be filed in the research archive within 30 days of completion.
  • R-RAK-02: All archive content must comply with the format requirements in Section 4.3. Proprietary formats are prohibited for primary archive content. If data is received in a proprietary format, conversion must occur before or at the time of archival.
  • R-RAK-03: The archive index must be updated whenever new content is added. An archive item that is not in the index is functionally invisible and may as well not exist.
  • R-RAK-04: Archive content may not be deleted or modified after archival. If an error is discovered in an archived document, a correction is filed as a separate document that references the original. The original is retained with a note pointing to the correction. This append-only policy ensures that the archive is a reliable historical record.
  • R-RAK-05: The quarterly integrity verification (Section 4.4) is mandatory and must cover at least 5% of archived files. Verification failures must be investigated within 7 days.
  • R-RAK-06: The annual archive audit (Section 4.4) is mandatory. Audit results must be documented and filed in the archive. The audit must verify completeness, index currency, format readability, and backup synchronization.
  • R-RAK-07: The research archive must be stored in at least two physically separate locations on different media technologies. Single-copy archive content is a critical vulnerability that must be remediated within 7 days of discovery.
  • R-RAK-08: Structured data files must include metadata headers or companion .meta files that describe the data's meaning, units, collection method, and limitations. A data file without metadata is a liability, not an asset, because it may be misinterpreted by future operators.

Failure Modes

  • Archive neglect. Research outputs are produced but not filed in the archive. They exist on the operator's working storage but are not organized, indexed, or backed up as archive content. Over time, the archive becomes incomplete, and the unfiled items become increasingly difficult to locate and integrate retroactively. Mitigation: R-RAK-01 establishes a 30-day filing deadline. The annual archive audit verifies completeness by cross-referencing the archive against the experiment log register, TDR register, and other tracking lists.
  • Index rot. The archive index is not updated when new items are added. Over time, the index becomes incomplete and unreliable. Operators stop using it because it does not reflect the archive's actual content, and the archive becomes effectively unsearchable. Mitigation: R-RAK-03 requires index update at the time of archival. The annual audit checks index currency.
  • Format obsolescence. Archive content is stored in formats that become unreadable as software evolves. The data exists physically but is semantically inaccessible. Mitigation: R-RAK-02 requires open, widely-supported formats. The 5-year format review (Section 4.4) provides early warning of format obsolescence.
  • Silent media degradation. Storage media degrades over years or decades, corrupting archived data without producing visible errors until the data is accessed. Mitigation: the quarterly integrity verification (R-RAK-05) detects corruption before it becomes critical. The 10-year media review ensures that aging media is replaced proactively.
  • Semantic loss. Data files are preserved perfectly at the bit level, but their meaning is lost because the metadata is insufficient. A CSV file with column headers like "v1, v2, v3" is useless to a future operator who does not know what v1, v2, and v3 represent. Mitigation: R-RAK-08 requires metadata headers or companion files. The archive audit checks for metadata completeness.
  • Single-point-of-failure storage. The archive exists in only one physical location. A hardware failure, environmental event, or theft destroys the only copy. Mitigation: R-RAK-07 requires redundant storage on different media. The annual audit verifies backup synchronization.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If archive items have not been filed: Conduct an archive completion sprint. Cross-reference the experiment log register, TDR register, negative results register, and other tracking lists against the archive contents. Identify all missing items and file them. For items that have been lost from working storage, attempt recovery from backups or operational logs. Document any items that are irrecoverably lost.
  2. If the index has fallen out of date: Rebuild the index from the archive contents. Walk the archive directory structure and regenerate all index files (chronological, topic, technology, cross-reference, keyword). This is labor-intensive but is a one-time recovery that, once completed, is maintained incrementally.
  3. If format obsolescence is discovered: Identify all affected files. Locate format conversion tools. If conversion tools exist, convert the files and store the converted versions alongside the originals (the originals are retained per R-RAK-04). If conversion tools do not exist, attempt to locate the original software or reverse-engineer the format. Document the format obsolescence event and update the format review cycle to monitor the affected format family more closely.
  4. If media degradation has caused data loss: Restore from redundant copies. If redundant copies are also degraded, attempt data recovery from the best available copy. Document the loss in the archive. Assess the impact: what knowledge was lost? Can the underlying experiments be re-run? Update the media review cycle to inspect the affected media technology more frequently.
  5. If semantic loss is discovered: Attempt to reconstruct the metadata from experiment logs, operational logs, the operator's memory, and any contextual clues in the data itself. File the reconstructed metadata with an explicit note that it is reconstructed and may be incomplete. Going forward, enforce R-RAK-08 rigorously to prevent further semantic loss.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The archive is small and manageable. The directory structure and index system are being established. The operator is developing the habit of filing research outputs promptly and maintaining the index. The early archive is simple enough that findability is not yet a challenge -- but the habits established now will determine whether the archive remains usable as it grows.
  • Years 5-15: The archive grows to hundreds of items. The index becomes genuinely necessary for finding things. The first format review may identify formats that need updating. The first media review may prompt migration to newer storage technology. The keyword vocabulary stabilizes as the operator develops consistent terminology.
  • Years 15-30: If succession occurs, the archive is the successor's primary window into the institution's intellectual history. The quality of the index, the completeness of the metadata, and the readability of the formats determine how effectively the successor can use the predecessor's research. A well-maintained archive accelerates the succession; a poorly-maintained one turns it into an archaeological expedition.
  • Years 30-50+: The archive spans decades. It has survived media migrations, format conversions, and possibly multiple operators. It contains the institution's complete research history -- every question asked, every experiment conducted, every conclusion reached, every failure recorded. It is, by this point, one of the institution's most valuable and irreplaceable assets. The maintenance practices established in the early years have compounded into a robust, navigable record of institutional knowledge.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: I am building an archive for research that has not yet been conducted, in formats designed to be readable by people who have not yet been born, on storage media that will certainly need to be replaced multiple times. The absurdity of planning a 50-year archive from scratch is matched only by the absurdity of not planning it. An institution that does not archive its research is an institution that repeats itself endlessly, rediscovering what it already knew, repeating failures it already documented, and losing the compounding value of accumulated knowledge.

The thing I am most concerned about is semantic loss. Bits are relatively easy to preserve. Meaning is hard. I can store a CSV file for 50 years, but if the person who reads it in 2076 does not know what the columns mean, the bits are useless. Every data file in this archive will have a metadata companion that explains what the data is, where it came from, and what it means. This is tedious to create and will feel like overhead in the early years when I remember perfectly well what every file contains. It will feel like a lifesaving act of foresight in the later years when I do not, or when someone else is reading my files for the first time.

The append-only policy matters to me for a reason beyond technical integrity. The archive should be honest. It should show what I thought at the time, not what I wish I had thought in retrospect. If I wrote something wrong, the correction lives alongside the original, not instead of it. The full record -- mistakes and all -- is more valuable than a curated highlight reel.
