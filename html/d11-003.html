D11-003 — Budget and Resource Accounting Framework

Document ID
D11-003
Domain
11 — Administration
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Draft

Purpose

This article defines how the holm.chat Documentation Institution tracks, allocates, forecasts, and audits all resources — monetary and non-monetary — required for its continued operation over a multi-decade horizon. A budget, in this context, is not a spreadsheet reviewed once a year. It is a continuously maintained model of what the institution consumes, what it holds in reserve, what it will need, and what it can afford. Without this model, every procurement decision is a guess, every maintenance deferral is a hidden debt, and every planning exercise is fiction.

The framework described here is designed to function whether the institution operates with significant financial resources or with almost none. It accounts for monetary expenditure, labor-hours, consumable depletion, asset depreciation, and opportunity costs. It is deliberately simple enough to maintain by hand in a paper ledger, yet structured enough to support meaningful analysis when computational tools are available. The goal is not accounting precision to the penny. The goal is resource awareness sufficient to prevent surprise shortfalls and to enable confident long-term planning.

Scope

In scope:

  • Definition of resource categories tracked by the institution (monetary funds, hardware assets, consumables, labor-hours, energy capacity, storage capacity, knowledge capital).
  • Budget creation, approval, and revision procedures.
  • Resource forecasting methods for planning horizons of 1 year, 5 years, and 15+ years.
  • Non-monetary resource accounting: barter, labor exchange, donated goods, salvaged components.
  • Reserve policies: minimum thresholds for critical resources, emergency fund requirements.
  • Audit procedures for verifying budget accuracy against actual expenditure and asset state.
  • Integration with the asset registry (D11-002) for depreciation tracking and replacement cost estimation.

Out of scope:

  • Procurement execution procedures (D11-004).
  • Tax compliance or external financial reporting (outside institutional scope).
  • Investment strategy or wealth management (not an institutional function).
  • Specific vendor pricing or market analysis (ephemeral data, not documentation).

Background

The Problem of Invisible Resource Consumption

Most self-built technical systems fail financially not through a single catastrophic expense but through the slow, invisible accumulation of costs that were never tracked. A hard drive reaches end-of-life and needs replacement. A UPS battery degrades below useful capacity. A software license expires that was never budgeted for renewal. A cable corrodes in a damp environment and must be sourced from a specialty supplier. None of these events are individually ruinous. But when three or four arrive in the same quarter, and no reserves have been set aside, the operator faces a resource crisis that could have been prevented by simple forecasting.

The air-gap and off-grid nature of this institution intensifies this problem. In a connected, on-grid environment, procurement can be reactive — order what you need when you need it, and it arrives in days. In this institution, procurement requires crossing the air gap, which means travel, planning, and lead time. Every procurement event has overhead costs beyond the item price: time, fuel, exposure to external networks, and disruption to the operational routine. This overhead means that unplanned procurement is not merely inconvenient — it is structurally expensive in ways that do not appear on a receipt.

Why Non-Monetary Accounting Matters

This institution cannot be understood through monetary accounting alone. Many of its most critical resources are not purchased — they are generated, salvaged, or traded. Solar energy is produced on-site but is finite and seasonal. Labor-hours are the operator's most constrained resource and cannot be purchased at any price. Storage capacity depreciates through wear. Knowledge, once lost, cannot be rebought.

A budget framework that tracks only money will systematically undervalue these resources and will fail to detect the most dangerous kinds of depletion. The framework defined here therefore treats all resource categories as first-class accounting objects, each with its own unit of measure, its own depletion rate, and its own reserve threshold.

Historical Precedent

The concept of non-monetary resource accounting is not new. Military logistics has tracked materiel, labor, and transport capacity alongside financial budgets for centuries. Homestead and off-grid communities have long maintained inventories of seeds, fuel, tools, and preserved food as a form of budgeting that has nothing to do with money. This framework draws on both traditions, adapted for the specific needs of a documentation institution.

System Model

Resource Categories

The institution recognizes seven primary resource categories, each tracked independently:

  1. Monetary Funds: Cash or equivalent financial instruments available for procurement. Tracked in the local currency. Subdivided into operational budget (current-year spending authority) and reserve fund (emergency and long-term replacement savings).
  2. Hardware Assets: Physical equipment with a useful life greater than one year. Tracked through the asset registry (D11-002) with current replacement cost estimates updated annually. Depreciation is tracked as a budget line item even though no money changes hands — it represents the future procurement obligation the asset creates.
  3. Consumables: Items with a useful life less than one year or that are depleted through use. Includes storage media with limited write cycles, cleaning supplies, cable ties, thermal paste, documentation paper, ink, and similar. Tracked by quantity and estimated months of supply remaining.
  4. Labor-Hours: The operator's available working time. This is the institution's most constrained and most valuable resource. The budget must account for the fact that administrative tasks, maintenance, documentation, and daily operations all compete for the same finite pool of hours. Labor-hour budgeting is approximate by nature but must be done to prevent chronic overcommitment.
  5. Energy Capacity: Available electrical power, measured in watt-hours per day. In an off-grid institution, energy is a hard constraint. The budget must track seasonal variation in solar production, battery storage capacity degradation over time, and the energy cost of every operational system.
  6. Storage Capacity: Available data storage across all systems, measured in terabytes. Tracks current utilization, growth rate, and the gap between available and projected needed capacity.
  7. Knowledge Capital: The institutional knowledge held by current operators, measured qualitatively. Tracked through skills inventories and documentation coverage assessments. Knowledge capital depreciates when documentation becomes stale or when skilled operators depart without transferring knowledge.

Budget Cycle

The institutional budget operates on an annual cycle with quarterly reviews:

  • Annual Budget Creation (Q4 of preceding year): Review the previous year's actual expenditure and resource consumption against budget. Identify variances. Project the coming year's needs based on the asset lifecycle calendar, scheduled maintenance, planned projects, and known replacements. Set spending authority for each category. Establish or confirm reserve thresholds.
  • Quarterly Reviews (end of Q1, Q2, Q3): Compare actual to budgeted figures. Adjust forecasts if consumption patterns differ from projections. Flag any category approaching its reserve threshold. Identify procurement needs for the next quarter.
  • Year-End Reconciliation (Q4): Final accounting of all resource categories against the annual budget. Document variances with explanations. Feed learnings into the next annual budget creation.

Forecasting Horizons

The institution maintains three forecasting horizons simultaneously:

  • Operational Forecast (1 year): Detailed, line-item projections for the current budget year. High confidence. Used for procurement timing and scheduling.
  • Strategic Forecast (5 years): Category-level projections based on known asset lifecycles, technology replacement cycles, and trend extrapolation. Medium confidence. Used for reserve fund sizing and major procurement planning.
  • Horizon Forecast (15+ years): Order-of-magnitude estimates for major technology transitions, infrastructure replacement, and institutional evolution scenarios. Low confidence but high value — identifies the large-scale resource commitments that can only be met through long-term saving and planning.

Rules and Constraints

  • R-BUD-01: A budget must exist and be current at all times. An institution operating without a budget is in administrative emergency and must create one before any non-essential procurement occurs.
  • R-BUD-02: All seven resource categories must be tracked. It is permissible to track some categories with less precision than others (labor-hours, for example, are inherently approximate), but no category may be omitted entirely.
  • R-BUD-03: The monetary reserve fund must maintain a minimum balance equal to the estimated cost of replacing the single most expensive critical asset in the institution. This ensures that a catastrophic single-asset failure does not create an institutional crisis.
  • R-BUD-04: Consumable inventories must maintain a minimum of three months' supply for all items classified as critical. Items are classified as critical if their absence would force a system shutdown or significantly degrade institutional capability.
  • R-BUD-05: Every budget line item must have a named justification. "Miscellaneous" categories are permitted but must not exceed 10% of any resource category's total budget. If miscellaneous spending consistently exceeds this threshold, the items must be broken out into named categories.
  • R-BUD-06: Budget variances exceeding 20% in any category trigger a mandatory review and documented explanation. Repeated variances in the same direction indicate a systemic forecasting error that must be corrected.
  • R-BUD-07: Depreciation of hardware assets must be tracked as a budget liability even though it does not represent a current cash outflow. The accumulated depreciation for any asset represents the minimum amount that should be held in reserve for its eventual replacement.
  • R-BUD-08: All budget records must be stored in plain-text or simple structured formats compliant with the 50-year continuity rules. No budget record may depend on proprietary software for interpretation.
  • R-BUD-09: Non-monetary resources acquired through barter, donation, or salvage must be recorded at their estimated replacement cost for budgeting purposes. This ensures that the institution understands the true value of resources it did not purchase.

Failure Modes

  • Optimistic budgeting. The operator consistently underestimates costs and overestimates available resources because the budget is aspirational rather than empirical. The annual reconciliation reveals persistent shortfalls, but forecasts are not adjusted because admitting the real costs feels discouraging. Mitigation: R-BUD-06 requires mandatory investigation of persistent variances. The budget must be based on historical actuals, not hopes. When historical data is unavailable (early years), estimates should include a contingency margin of at least 25%.
  • Budget abandonment. The budget exists on paper but is not consulted for actual decisions. Procurement happens based on immediate need and available cash, not on budgeted allocations. The budget becomes a historical document rather than a planning tool. Mitigation: the quarterly review cycle creates regular touchpoints. Procurement requests (D11-004) must reference the relevant budget line item, creating a structural dependency between spending and budgeting.
  • Single-category blindness. The operator tracks monetary resources carefully but neglects labor-hours, energy capacity, or consumables. A project is "within budget" financially but consumes three months of labor-hours that were needed for maintenance. Mitigation: R-BUD-02 requires all seven categories to be tracked. Cross-category impact assessment should be part of every significant resource allocation decision.
  • Reserve erosion. The reserve fund is gradually spent on operational expenses because it is the only available cash when budget overruns occur. The reserve exists in name but not in practice. Mitigation: R-BUD-03 defines a minimum reserve threshold. Spending from reserves requires a documented justification and a replenishment plan.
  • Forecast horizon collapse. The operator stops maintaining the strategic and horizon forecasts because the future feels too uncertain to plan for. The institution loses the ability to anticipate major expenses years before they arrive. Mitigation: even low-confidence forecasts have value. The horizon forecast need not be precise — it must exist. Annual review ensures it is updated with the best current understanding.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If no budget exists: Begin with a resource census. For each of the seven categories, determine the current state: how much money is available, what assets exist and their condition (from the asset registry, D11-002), what consumables are on hand, what the current energy production is, what storage is available. This census becomes the opening balance. Then create a 12-month operational forecast based on known obligations and historical patterns. This minimal budget is better than no budget and can be refined over subsequent quarters.
  2. If the budget has diverged from reality: Conduct a full reconciliation. For every budget category, compare the budgeted figure to the actual figure. Document the variance. Determine whether the error is in the budget (bad forecast) or in the tracking (missed transactions). Correct both. Adjust the remaining quarters of the current budget to reflect the corrected understanding.
  3. If reserves have been depleted: Declare an austerity period. All non-essential procurement is suspended. A replenishment plan is created that specifies how much will be set aside each month until the reserve minimum (R-BUD-03) is restored. The replenishment plan is a Tier 2 governance decision (GOV-001) because it affects the institution's risk posture.
  4. If forecasts have collapsed: Start with the asset registry. Every asset has an estimated end-of-life date. Plot those dates on a timeline. This simple exercise reconstructs the strategic forecast for the most expensive resource category (hardware). Extend the method to consumables and energy. The horizon forecast can be reconstructed from technology trend analysis and the evolution path documents in Domain 13.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: The budget framework is being established for the first time. Historical data is limited or nonexistent. Forecasts will be rough estimates. The primary goal is to establish the tracking discipline — recording every resource inflow and outflow so that future budgets have an empirical foundation. Expect significant variances in the first two years. Do not abandon the framework because early estimates are wrong; correct them and continue.
  • Years 3-10: Sufficient historical data exists to make meaningful forecasts. The budget becomes a genuine planning tool rather than a recording exercise. Patterns emerge: seasonal energy variation, consumable depletion rates, the real (as opposed to manufacturer-estimated) lifespan of hardware components. The strategic forecast becomes increasingly reliable.
  • Years 10-25: The budget framework has survived at least one major technology transition (hardware generation change, storage format migration). The horizon forecast has been tested against reality. Adjustments to the framework itself may be needed to accommodate new resource categories or changed operational patterns. The framework must evolve, but the underlying principles — track everything, maintain reserves, forecast ahead — remain constant.
  • Years 25-50+: The budget records are now a multi-decade archive of institutional resource consumption. They reveal long-term trends invisible in any single year's data. They are invaluable for successor operators who need to understand the institution's material history and project its future needs. The archive itself becomes a resource worth preserving and indexing.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 — Founding Entry: I have never maintained a formal budget for a personal technology project. I have always operated on a combination of intuition and checking my bank balance. This works well enough when the time horizon is measured in months. It fails completely when the time horizon is measured in decades. The most dangerous illusion in resource management is that you can "feel" whether you are on track. You cannot. Feelings do not account for depreciation. Feelings do not model the compounding cost of deferred maintenance. Feelings do not warn you, three years in advance, that four major components will reach end-of-life in the same quarter. Numbers do. This framework exists because I trust numbers more than I trust my intuition about money and resources, and I trust my future self to need these records even if my present self finds them tedious to maintain.
