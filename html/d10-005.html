D10-005 — Incident Classification & Response Framework

Document ID
D10-005
Domain
10 -- User Operations
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
OPS-001, D10-002, D10-003, SEC-001, SEC-002, SEC-003, D6-001, D6-002, D6-003, D8-009
Depended Upon By
D10-010 (degraded operations triggered by incidents), D10-013 (post-incident review), all domain articles that reference incident escalation.

Purpose

This article extends D10-003 by providing the comprehensive framework for incident classification across all operational domains and defining the escalation paths that connect operational incidents to governance decisions. While D10-003 established the core incident response procedure and severity classification, this article addresses the broader question: how does the institution systematically categorize, route, escalate, and learn from the full spectrum of events that threaten its operation?

The distinction between D10-003 and D10-005 is one of scope. D10-003 is the field manual: when something goes wrong, what do you do right now? This article is the strategic framework: how are incidents classified across domains, when does an operational incident become a governance matter, how do escalation paths work, and how does the institution's incident history inform its evolution? D10-003 is what you reach for in the moment. D10-005 is what you consult when designing the systems that prevent and manage incidents over decades.

An institution that merely responds to incidents is reactive. An institution that classifies, escalates, and learns from incidents is adaptive. This article provides the machinery of adaptation.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The multi-dimensional classification taxonomy for incidents: by severity (from D10-003), by domain, by type, and by recurrence pattern.
  • Escalation paths from operational response to governance decisions.
  • The relationship between incident classification and resource allocation.
  • Cross-domain incident coordination when an incident affects multiple domains simultaneously.
  • Aggregate incident analysis: patterns, trends, and systemic vulnerabilities.
  • The threshold criteria for when an incident triggers structural change versus procedural adjustment.

This article does not cover:

  • The step-by-step incident response procedure (see D10-003).
  • Specific technical recovery procedures for data, security, or hardware incidents (see D6-002, D6-003, SEC-002, SEC-003).
  • The post-incident review process in detail (see D10-013).
  • Full disaster recovery (see Domain 12).

Background

3.1 From Response to Framework

D10-003 provides the immediate response capability. An incident occurs, the operator follows the seven-step procedure, the incident is resolved. But response alone is insufficient for long-term institutional health. Without a framework that categorizes incidents, identifies patterns, and connects operational events to strategic decisions, the institution is condemned to fight the same fires repeatedly.

The classification framework serves three audiences across time. In the present, it helps the operator route an incident to the correct response procedure. Over the medium term, it enables pattern recognition: are hardware incidents increasing? Are security incidents concentrated in a particular subsystem? Over the long term, it informs the institution's evolution: which systems need replacement, which procedures need redesign, which architectural assumptions have proven wrong?

3.2 The Escalation Problem

Not every incident can be resolved at the operational level. Some incidents reveal flaws that require governance decisions: a recurring hardware failure that necessitates infrastructure redesign, a security vulnerability that requires architectural change, a data integrity issue that demands a new verification strategy. The operator must know when an incident has exceeded operational authority and requires escalation to the appropriate governance tier per GOV-001.

In a single-operator institution, escalation is not about transferring responsibility to another person. It is about elevating the decision from "what do I do right now?" to "what structural change does the institution need?" The escalation path is a cognitive shift from tactical to strategic, accompanied by the documentation and deliberation requirements of the appropriate governance tier.

3.3 Cross-Domain Incidents

Many incidents do not respect domain boundaries. A power failure (Domain 4) may cause data corruption (Domain 6) that triggers a security concern (Domain 3) and requires a change to automation behavior (Domain 8). The classification framework must accommodate multi-domain incidents without requiring the operator to file separate incident reports for each domain. A single incident with a single root cause receives a single classification, with cross-domain impacts noted and tracked.

System Model

4.1 Classification Taxonomy

Every incident is classified along four dimensions:

Dimension 1: Severity. The four-level severity system defined in D10-003 Section 4.1 (S1 Critical, S2 High, S3 Medium, S4 Low). This dimension determines response urgency.

Dimension 2: Domain. The primary domain affected by the incident. Categories: Infrastructure (D4), Platform (D5), Data (D6), Intelligence (D7), Automation (D8), Security (D3), Operations (D10), or Multi-Domain. This dimension determines which domain expertise and procedures are invoked.

Dimension 3: Type. The nature of the incident within its domain:

  • Hardware failure (component degradation, physical damage, environmental damage).
  • Software failure (service crash, configuration error, dependency failure).
  • Data integrity failure (corruption, loss, unauthorized modification).
  • Security breach (unauthorized access, key compromise, physical breach).
  • Automation failure (agent malfunction, runaway process, incorrect output).
  • Environmental failure (power, cooling, physical environment).
  • Procedural failure (operator error, documentation gap, process gap).
  • External event (upstream service failure, supply chain disruption, regulatory change).

Dimension 4: Recurrence. The incident's relationship to previous incidents:

  • Novel: no prior incident of this type in the institutional record.
  • Recurrent: the same or substantially similar incident has occurred before.
  • Chronic: the incident recurs frequently and represents a systemic weakness.
  • Escalated: a previously lower-severity incident has worsened.

4.2 Escalation Paths

Incidents are escalated from operational response to governance decision when they meet any of the following criteria:

Escalation TriggerGovernance TierAction Required
S1 incident of any typeTier 2 minimumPost-resolution governance review of the root cause and structural implications
Recurrent incident (3+ occurrences within 12 months)Tier 2Structural analysis: why is this recurring? What systemic change is needed?
Chronic incident (ongoing pattern over 6+ months)Tier 3Architectural or policy change proposal
Incident revealing undocumented system or procedureTier 2Documentation sprint per CON-001
Incident requiring budget expenditure (hardware replacement, new tools)Tier 2Resource allocation decision per GOV-001
Incident revealing a flaw in governance or policyTier 3 or Tier 4Policy revision per GOV-001 amendment process
Incident affecting institutional mission capabilityTier 3Mission impact assessment per CON-001

4.3 Cross-Domain Incident Coordination

When an incident spans multiple domains, the following procedure applies:

  1. Classify the incident by its root cause domain. A power failure that causes data corruption is an Infrastructure incident with Data impact, not a Data incident.
  2. Record all affected domains in the incident log entry.
  3. Apply the response procedure for the root cause domain first (contain the source), then address downstream impacts in order of severity.
  4. The post-incident review must examine the cross-domain propagation path: how did a failure in one domain cascade to others? What isolation or containment mechanisms should have prevented the cascade?
  5. If the cascade reveals missing isolation between domains, escalate per Section 4.2 for architectural review.

4.4 Aggregate Analysis

Individual incident response is necessary but insufficient. The classification framework enables aggregate analysis that reveals patterns invisible at the individual incident level:

Quarterly pattern review. During the OPS-001 quarterly operations cycle, review all incidents from the quarter. Classify by dimension. Look for:

  • Increasing frequency in any category.
  • Concentration of incidents in a single domain or system.
  • Recurrence of previously resolved issues.
  • Incidents detected by monitoring versus incidents detected by accident.
  • Average time between detection and resolution by severity level.

Annual trend analysis. During the OPS-001 annual operations cycle, analyze the full year's incident history. Compare with previous years. Identify long-term trends: is the institution becoming more or less stable? Are new categories of incident emerging? Are old categories declining?

The aggregate analysis output is a brief written report appended to the operational log, summarizing findings and recommending any structural changes. This report feeds into the annual strategic planning process.

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D10C-01: Every incident must be classified along all four dimensions defined in Section 4.1. Incomplete classification makes aggregate analysis impossible.
  • R-D10C-02: Escalation triggers defined in Section 4.2 are mandatory. Meeting an escalation criterion requires governance review at the specified tier. The operator may not resolve structural issues at the operational level without governance deliberation.
  • R-D10C-03: Cross-domain incidents must be classified by root cause, not by the most visible symptom. Misclassification leads to treating symptoms rather than causes.
  • R-D10C-04: The quarterly pattern review is mandatory. It must be conducted even if no incidents occurred during the quarter (the absence of incidents is itself a finding worth recording).
  • R-D10C-05: The annual trend analysis must compare the current year with all previous years for which data exists. Trends visible only over multi-year timescales are the most strategically important.
  • R-D10C-06: Chronic incidents (Dimension 4) that persist for more than 12 months without structural resolution must be escalated to Tier 3 governance regardless of their individual severity. A chronic S3 incident is, in aggregate, more damaging than a single S1 incident because it represents an unresolved systemic weakness.
  • R-D10C-07: The classification taxonomy may be extended with new types or categories as the institution encounters incidents that do not fit the existing taxonomy. Extensions are Tier 1 governance decisions (low-ceremony) and must be documented.

Failure Modes

  • Classification fatigue. Four dimensions of classification for every incident feels bureaucratic, especially for S4 events. The operator starts classifying carelessly or not at all. Mitigation: for S4 events, classification can be abbreviated (severity and domain only; type and recurrence added during quarterly batch review). The full four-dimension classification is required only for S1-S3 events.

  • Symptom-based classification. The operator classifies a cross-domain incident by its most dramatic symptom rather than its root cause. Data corruption caused by a power failure is classified as a Data incident. Resources are spent hardening data systems when the real vulnerability is in power infrastructure. Mitigation: R-D10C-03 and the cross-domain procedure in Section 4.3. The post-incident review should always trace back to root cause.

  • Escalation avoidance. The operator encounters an escalation trigger but resolves the issue operationally to avoid the overhead of governance review. The structural vulnerability persists. Mitigation: R-D10C-02 makes escalation mandatory. The quarterly pattern review will detect unescalated recurrent incidents.

  • Analysis without action. The quarterly and annual reviews produce insightful analysis, but the findings do not result in structural changes. The institution understands its problems but does not fix them. Mitigation: every pattern review must produce specific, actionable recommendations with governance tier assignments and deadlines. These are tracked in the decision log per GOV-001.

  • Taxonomy sprawl. The classification taxonomy grows to accommodate every edge case, becoming so fine-grained that it hinders rather than helps analysis. Mitigation: R-D10C-07 allows extensions but does not require them. The taxonomy should remain at a level of granularity that supports meaningful pattern recognition. If a category has fewer than three incidents over two years, it may be too specific.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If incident classification has been neglected: During the next quarterly review, retrospectively classify all unclassified incidents from the incident log. Use the information recorded during response (D10-003) to assign dimensions. Record the retroactive classification and the gap in the maintenance log. Going forward, integrate classification into Step 2 (Assess and Classify) of the D10-003 procedure.

  2. If escalation triggers have been missed: During the quarterly pattern review, scan for unescalated triggers. For each, determine whether the operational resolution was adequate or whether the structural issue persists. If it persists, escalate now. The delay in escalation is itself a finding to record.

  3. If the quarterly or annual review has been skipped: Conduct it as soon as possible. The value of the review compounds over time; a skipped review means a quarter or year of pattern data goes unanalyzed. Do not attempt to combine multiple skipped reviews into one session. Conduct each skipped review independently, in chronological order, so that patterns emerge naturally.

  4. If the classification taxonomy has become unwieldy: Conduct a taxonomy consolidation during the annual review. Merge categories with fewer than three incidents over two years. Simplify type classifications that have proven unhelpful for pattern recognition. Document the consolidation and map old categories to new ones so that historical analysis remains possible.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The classification taxonomy is provisional. Real incidents will reveal categories and types not anticipated by the initial design. The quarterly reviews during this period are as much about refining the taxonomy as about analyzing incidents. The escalation paths will be tested for the first time and may need adjustment.
  • Years 5-15: The taxonomy should be mature and stable. Aggregate analysis should produce meaningful strategic insights. The institution should be making proactive infrastructure and architectural decisions informed by incident trend data rather than reacting to individual events.
  • Years 15-30: A decade or more of classified incident data enables predictive analysis. Patterns that take years to emerge become visible: the true reliability characteristics of hardware platforms, the long-term effects of architectural decisions, the types of incidents that new operators encounter versus experienced ones. This data is a strategic asset.
  • Years 30-50+: The incident classification archive spanning decades is unprecedented for a small institution. It constitutes a longitudinal study of institutional resilience. The framework itself may have been revised multiple times, but the underlying principle -- systematic classification, aggregate analysis, and escalation to governance -- should remain the foundation of the institution's adaptive capacity.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The temptation in writing this article was to create an elaborate classification system with dozens of categories, sub-categories, and decision matrices. I have resisted that temptation. The four dimensions are sufficient. Severity tells me how urgently to respond. Domain tells me where to look. Type tells me what kind of problem it is. Recurrence tells me whether it is new or familiar. That is enough to route a response and to enable pattern analysis.

The escalation paths are the most important part of this article, and also the part I expect to be most tempted to ignore. When I fix a problem, I want to move on. I do not want to elevate it to a governance decision and go through the deliberation process. But the problems I fix and move on from are the problems that come back. The escalation triggers exist to force me to address root causes, not just symptoms.

The quarterly pattern review is where the real value of this framework emerges. Individual incidents are events. Patterns of incidents are knowledge. I have committed to conducting these reviews even when -- especially when -- there is nothing dramatic to review. The absence of incidents in a previously active category is just as informative as the presence of incidents in a new one.
