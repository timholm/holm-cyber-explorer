D18-010 — Batch Import Procedures

Document ID
D18-010
Domain
18 -- Import & Quarantine
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D18-002 (Validation Pipeline), D18-007 (Format Recognition), D18-005 (Provenance Tracking)
Depended Upon By
D18-014 (Operator Training)

Purpose

This article defines the procedures for batch import -- the intake of large collections of content in a single import event. While the standard import pipeline (D18-002) is designed for individual items or small sets, batch imports present unique challenges of scale: hundreds or thousands of items arriving together, potentially on multiple media, potentially in heterogeneous formats, and potentially exceeding the quarantine zone's capacity for individual examination.

Batch imports occur when the institution acquires a large collection (a donated library, an archival dump, a database export from a retiring system), when a federated node performs a major synchronization, or when an operator returns from an external acquisition trip with a full drive of collected material. These events are infrequent but high-impact, and they require procedures that the standard pipeline does not address.

The fundamental tension in batch import is between thoroughness and practicality. The institution cannot individually examine every item in a 10,000-file collection with the same rigor applied to a single incoming document. But it also cannot admit 10,000 items without validation simply because examining them all would take too long. This article defines the strategies that balance these concerns: sampling, batch-level validation, segmentation, and progressive admission.

Scope

This article covers:

  • Definition of what constitutes a batch import versus standard import.
  • Pre-import planning procedures for anticipated large acquisitions.
  • Sampling strategies for batch-level quality assessment.
  • Batch-level validation versus item-level validation: when each applies.
  • Segmentation: how to break a large batch into manageable processing units.
  • Progressive admission: admitting validated segments while validation continues on others.
  • Progress tracking and status reporting for multi-day or multi-week import operations.
  • Batch provenance: how provenance is recorded when a single source provides thousands of items.

This article does not cover:

  • The individual validation checks applied to sampled items (see D18-002).
  • Format recognition for individual items within the batch (see D18-007).
  • Inter-node synchronization, which is a specialized form of batch import (see D18-011).
  • Quarantine zone capacity management (see D18-006, though this article references capacity constraints).

Background

The Scale Problem

The standard import pipeline assumes a manageable number of items that can each receive individual attention. A single document is quarantined, examined, scored, and decided upon. This scales to perhaps a few dozen items per import session. When the number climbs to hundreds or thousands, individual examination becomes impractical. An operator examining each file in a 5,000-file collection at an average of five minutes per file would need 417 hours -- over ten weeks of full-time work on a single import. The institution needs a way to assess large collections without examining every item, while maintaining an acceptable level of assurance.

Sampling as Assurance

Statistical sampling is the standard approach to quality assurance for large populations. Instead of examining every item, the institution examines a representative sample and infers the quality of the whole from the quality of the sample. This is not a compromise on quality -- it is a well-established methodology used in manufacturing, auditing, and every field where exhaustive examination is impractical. The key is choosing the sample correctly: it must be large enough to be statistically meaningful, random enough to be unbiased, and stratified enough to represent the diversity of the collection.

The Batch Provenance Challenge

When a single carrier arrives with a hard drive containing 5,000 documents from 200 different sources, the provenance situation is complex. The carrier provenance is shared across all items (the same person brought them, on the same medium, at the same time). The source provenance varies per item. Recording item-level provenance for every item in a large batch may be impractical. The institution needs a batch provenance model that captures the common elements efficiently while allowing item-level provenance where it is available and important.

Sampling Methodology

Stratified Random Sampling: The batch is divided into strata based on observable characteristics (format, source, size, directory location). Within each stratum, items are selected randomly. This ensures that every type of content in the batch has a chance of being examined, rather than allowing a random sample to accidentally skip an entire category.

Batch Provenance Model

A batch provenance record captures the shared provenance elements:

  • Batch ID: Unique identifier for this import batch.
  • Carrier: Who brought the batch.
  • Acquisition Context: How and why the batch was acquired.
  • Medium Description: Physical media details.
  • Aggregate Source: The source of the collection as a whole (e.g., "University X digital library export").
  • Date Received: When the batch arrived.
  • Item Count: Total items in the batch.
  • Format Distribution: Summary of formats found during batch-level assessment.

Individual items within the batch inherit the batch provenance and may have additional item-level provenance if available (e.g., individual authorship, individual publication dates extracted from metadata).

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D18-B01: Batch Registration Required. Every batch import MUST be registered before processing begins. Registration creates the batch provenance record and triggers the appropriate size-category procedures.

  • R-D18-B02: No Batch Bypasses Quarantine. The entire batch MUST enter quarantine before any item is admitted to production. A batch is not a reason to bypass quarantine; it is a reason to use quarantine efficiently.

  • R-D18-B03: Minimum Sample Requirements. The minimum sample sizes specified in the sampling methodology MUST be met. An operator MAY examine more items than the minimum but MUST NOT examine fewer.

  • R-D18-B04: Sample Failure Escalation. If more than 10% of sampled items fail validation, the entire segment from which the sample was drawn MUST be subjected to expanded examination (double the sample size) or full item-by-item validation. If more than 25% of expanded-sample items fail, the segment SHOULD be rejected as a whole.

  • R-D18-B05: Major Acquisitions Require Governance Approval. Any batch import of 10,001 or more items MUST receive governance-level approval before processing begins. The approval must include a resource allocation plan (operator hours, quarantine storage, processing timeline).

  • R-D18-B06: Progress Tracking. For any batch import expected to take more than one working day, the operator MUST maintain a progress log that records: segments processed, sample results, items admitted, items rejected, and estimated time remaining. This log is part of the import record.

  • R-D18-B07: Batch-Level Rejection Is Permitted. If batch-level assessment reveals systemic problems (e.g., the entire batch is corrupted, the entire batch is in an unrecognized format, the batch provenance is irrecoverably deficient), the entire batch MAY be rejected without item-level examination. The rejection must be documented per D18-009 with the batch-level findings as justification.

Failure Modes

  • Sampling bias. The sample is not representative of the batch. The sampled items pass validation, but unsampled portions contain problems. Likelihood: Moderate if sampling is not truly random or not stratified. Severity: High -- bad content is admitted based on a misleading sample. Detection: Post-admission spot-checking of non-sampled items. Anomaly detection in admitted content.

  • Quarantine capacity exceeded. A large batch fills quarantine storage, blocking other import operations. Likelihood: High for major acquisitions if capacity planning was inadequate. Severity: Medium -- import operations halt but no data is at risk. Detection: Quarantine storage monitoring per D18-006. Mitigation: Segmented processing with progressive admission clears quarantine space as segments are processed.

  • Batch processing abandonment. A multi-week batch import is interrupted (operator illness, hardware failure, institutional priority change) and not resumed. The batch sits partially processed in quarantine, consuming storage and creating ambiguity about which items have been validated. Likelihood: Moderate for very large batches. Severity: Medium. Detection: Quarantine residency time monitoring. Progress log review. Mitigation: The progress log enables a different operator to resume where processing stopped.

  • Provenance loss for individual items. The batch provenance record captures the collection-level information, but item-level provenance that was available (e.g., in file metadata, directory names, or accompanying manifests) is not captured during batch processing. Over time, the institution knows that an item came from "batch B-2031-014" but not which specific source within the batch. Likelihood: Moderate if batch processing prioritizes speed over thoroughness. Severity: Medium over time as source-level provenance becomes important for trust reassessment. Detection: Periodic review of provenance completeness for batch-imported content.

  • Duplicate admission. A batch contains items that already exist in the institution. Without duplicate detection, the same content is admitted multiple times, wasting storage and creating confusion. Likelihood: High, especially for inter-node synchronization batches. Severity: Low (duplicate content is wasteful but not harmful). Detection: Hash-based duplicate checking during batch-level assessment.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If a batch sample was biased and bad content was admitted: Identify the batch. Conduct a full item-level validation of all non-sampled items from the affected segment(s). Quarantine any items that fail. Revise the sampling methodology to prevent recurrence -- was the sample too small? Not stratified? Not random? Document the incident and the sampling methodology correction.

  2. If a batch import is interrupted: Review the progress log. Identify which segments have been fully processed, which are partially processed, and which have not been started. For fully processed segments, no action is needed. For partially processed segments, resume processing where the log indicates. For unstarted segments, begin processing normally. If the progress log is missing or incomplete, re-process the batch from the beginning of the earliest incomplete segment.

  3. If quarantine capacity is exceeded during batch processing: Suspend batch processing. Process and disposition any non-batch content in quarantine. Admit validated batch segments to production to free quarantine space. If insufficient space can be freed, provision additional quarantine storage per D18-006 R-D18-Q08. Resume batch processing once adequate space is available.

  4. If duplicates were admitted: Run a deduplication scan across all batch-imported content, comparing hashes against existing institutional content. For each duplicate, determine which copy has better provenance and retain that one. Remove duplicates from the institutional catalog. This is a cleanup operation, not a security concern.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: Batch imports are infrequent. The procedures are tested with small and medium batches. Sampling methodology is calibrated against actual results -- do the minimum sample sizes produce reliable quality assessments? The batch provenance model is refined based on the types of batch metadata actually available in practice.

  • Years 5-15: As the institution grows, batch imports become more common. Federated node synchronizations (D18-011) are a regular form of batch import. Tooling for batch-level assessment (format distribution analysis, duplicate detection, automated sampling) matures. The segmentation strategies are refined based on operational patterns.

  • Years 15-30: Major acquisitions may bring in content from defunct institutions, retired systems, or archival repositories. These imports are not just large in scale but complex in provenance -- the original curators are no longer available to explain the collection's organization or history. Batch provenance procedures must accommodate these "orphan collection" imports.

  • Years 30-50+: The institution may itself become a major repository, receiving large donations from other organizations or inheriting entire collections. The batch import procedures become one of the institution's core operational capabilities. The sampling methodology, refined over decades, represents significant institutional knowledge about how to efficiently assess large collections.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The minimum sample sizes in this article are informed by general quality assurance practice but are not derived from rigorous statistical power analysis. They are starting points. After the institution has processed several batch imports, the sample sizes should be validated empirically: did the sample results accurately predict the quality of the non-sampled portions? If not, the minimum sample sizes need adjustment.

The 10% and 25% failure thresholds for sample escalation are also starting points. They should be calibrated against the institution's risk tolerance. A more risk-tolerant institution might set higher thresholds; a more cautious one might set lower. The thresholds should be reviewed after the first year of batch import operations.

One open question: should the institution accept batch imports where no manifest or inventory accompanies the batch? A batch that arrives as "here's a hard drive, I don't know exactly what's on it" is harder to assess than one that arrives with a manifest listing every file, its source, and its format. The current procedures do not require a manifest, but future operators may want to make one a prerequisite for large and major batches, as it significantly improves the efficiency of batch-level assessment and segmentation.
