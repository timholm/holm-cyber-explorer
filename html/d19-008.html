D19-008 — Defect Tracking & Resolution Lifecycle

Document ID
D19-008
Domain
19 -- Quality Assurance
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
D19-005 (Quality Standards Taxonomy)
Depended Upon By
D19-011 (Continuous Improvement Process), D19-013 (Quality Degradation Detection). Referenced by all domains that report or resolve quality defects.

Purpose

This article defines how quality defects are identified, reported, classified, prioritized, assigned, tracked, resolved, and verified within the institution. A defect, in this context, is any discrepancy between the documented standard and the actual state of an institutional artifact -- a factual error in a document, a broken cross-reference, an outdated procedure, a missing section, a failed integrity check, or any other condition that reduces the quality of an institutional output below its defined standard.

Without a defect tracking system, defects are discovered and then forgotten, discovered and fixed in one place but not in others, or discovered but deprioritized indefinitely. The defect accumulates companions, and the institution's quality degrades invisibly. A tracking system makes defects visible, assigns them owners, and ensures they are resolved or consciously accepted.

This article is addressed to everyone who may discover, report, or be responsible for resolving a quality defect. It is addressed with particular emphasis to the QA function, which maintains the defect registry, and to domain leads, who are responsible for resolving defects within their domain.

Scope

This article covers:

  • The definition and classification of quality defects.
  • The defect lifecycle from detection through resolution and verification.
  • Severity and priority classification.
  • The defect registry -- what is recorded for each defect.
  • Assignment and ownership rules.
  • Escalation procedures for unresolved defects.
  • Defect metrics and trend analysis.

This article does not cover:

  • Security incidents (see Security domains; though a security incident may also be a quality defect, the security response takes precedence).
  • Continuous improvement processes that use defect data (see D19-011).
  • The audit procedures through which defects are systematically discovered (see D19-002).

Background

3.1 Defects in a Documentation Institution

In a software organization, a defect is a bug -- code that does not behave as specified. In a documentation institution, defects are more varied and often more subtle. A factual error is the most obvious defect, analogous to a software crash. But a document that is factually correct yet incomprehensible to its intended audience is also defective. A document that was correct when written but no longer reflects current reality is defective. A cross-reference that points to a retired document is defective. A procedure that works in theory but has never been tested in practice is potentially defective. The defect classification must be broad enough to capture all of these.

3.2 The Cost of Untracked Defects

An untracked defect is a silent burden on the institution. Someone discovers it, works around it, and moves on. The next person encounters the same defect and spends time puzzling over it before also working around it. Over years, the accumulated time lost to untracked defects can exceed the time that would have been required to fix them. More dangerously, some defects are not merely inconvenient -- they are misleading. A factual error in a procedure can lead to incorrect actions. An outdated security procedure can create vulnerabilities. An incorrect cross-reference can lead a reader to the wrong policy. These defects do not merely waste time. They cause harm.

3.3 Design Decisions

The defect tracking system described here is deliberately simple. It uses a text-based registry rather than a specialized database, because the institution is air-gapped and cannot depend on any particular software tool being available in the future. The lifecycle states are few and clearly defined. The classification system uses two axes (severity and priority) rather than a single axis, because severity (how bad is it?) and priority (how urgently should it be fixed?) are independent dimensions.

System Model

4.1 Defect Classification

Severity measures the impact of the defect if left unresolved:

SeverityDefinitionExamples
CriticalThe defect creates a risk of institutional harm -- data loss, security vulnerability, or actions based on incorrect information.Factual error in a security procedure; corrupted integrity check; incorrect disaster recovery step.
MajorThe defect significantly impairs the usefulness of the affected artifact but does not create immediate institutional risk.Missing required section in a published article; broken cross-references to active documents; procedure that omits a necessary step.
MinorThe defect reduces quality but the artifact remains usable for its primary purpose.Typographical errors; formatting inconsistencies; outdated but non-critical metadata.
CosmeticThe defect has no impact on the artifact's usefulness but does not meet the defined standard.Style inconsistencies; non-standard heading capitalization; minor formatting deviations.

Priority measures how urgently the defect should be resolved:

PriorityDefinitionTarget Resolution Time
ImmediateMust be resolved before any other work. The defect is actively causing harm or blocking critical operations.Within 24 hours
HighShould be resolved as soon as practical. The defect is a significant quality issue that will cause harm if left unresolved.Within 7 days
NormalShould be resolved in the normal course of maintenance work.Within 30 days
LowResolve when convenient. The defect is acknowledged but does not warrant displacing other work.Within 90 days or next scheduled review

4.2 Defect Lifecycle States

Every defect passes through the following states:

  1. Reported. The defect has been identified and entered into the registry. It has not yet been evaluated.
  2. Triaged. The defect has been evaluated for severity and priority. It has been assigned an owner responsible for resolution.
  3. In Progress. The owner is actively working on resolving the defect.
  4. Resolved. The owner believes the defect is fixed. The fix has not yet been verified.
  5. Verified. A person other than the owner has confirmed that the defect is resolved. The fix is adequate.
  6. Closed. The defect is fully resolved and verified. It is retained in the registry for historical analysis.
  7. Deferred. The defect has been triaged but a deliberate decision has been made to defer resolution. The deferral reason and a re-evaluation date are recorded.
  8. Rejected. Upon triage, the reported condition was determined not to be a defect (the behavior is as intended, or the standard cited does not apply). The rejection rationale is documented.

4.3 Defect Registry Record Structure

Each defect record contains:

DEFECT RECORD
=============
Defect ID:        DEF-[YYYY]-[SEQUENCE] (e.g., DEF-2026-001)
Date Reported:    [date]
Reporter:         [person or process that identified the defect]
Affected Artifact:[document ID or system component]
Description:      [clear description of the discrepancy]
Standard Violated:[reference to the specific standard, or "implicit"]
Severity:         [Critical / Major / Minor / Cosmetic]
Priority:         [Immediate / High / Normal / Low]
Status:           [Reported / Triaged / In Progress / Resolved /
                   Verified / Closed / Deferred / Rejected]
Owner:            [person responsible for resolution]
Resolution:       [description of the fix, when resolved]
Verified By:      [person who verified the fix]
Date Closed:      [date]
Notes:            [any additional context, related defects, etc.]

4.4 Defect Metrics

The following metrics are tracked and reviewed quarterly:

  • Open defect count by severity and priority.
  • Defect age distribution -- how long defects remain open.
  • Defect inflow rate -- how many new defects are reported per period.
  • Defect resolution rate -- how many defects are closed per period.
  • Defect backlog trend -- is the backlog growing, stable, or shrinking?
  • Defects by domain -- which domains produce the most defects?
  • Defects by quality dimension -- which quality dimensions are most commonly deficient?

Rules & Constraints

  • R-D19-08-01: Anyone May Report a Defect. Any person who identifies a quality defect may report it. There is no threshold of certainty required. If in doubt, report. Triage will determine whether the report constitutes a genuine defect.

  • R-D19-08-02: Critical Defects Must Be Triaged Within 24 Hours. A reported defect classified as potentially Critical severity must be triaged (evaluated and assigned) within 24 hours of being reported. Non-critical defects must be triaged within 7 days.

  • R-D19-08-03: Defects Must Not Be Deleted. A defect record, once created, may change status but may not be deleted from the registry. Even rejected defects are retained -- they may indicate a misunderstanding that needs to be addressed, and they contribute to trend analysis.

  • R-D19-08-04: Resolution Must Be Verified. A defect may not move to Closed status without verification by someone other than the resolver. In a single-operator institution, verification may be conducted by the same person after a temporal gap of at least 24 hours, using a checklist.

  • R-D19-08-05: Deferred Defects Must Have Re-evaluation Dates. A defect may be deferred, but the deferral must include a reason and a date by which the deferral decision will be re-evaluated. Indefinite deferral is not permitted.

  • R-D19-08-06: No Blame. Per D19-001 R-D19-08, defect reports are for learning, not punishment. A defect report describes a condition, not a failing. The person who created the defective artifact is not the subject of the report. The defect is.

Failure Modes

  • Defect backlog growth. Defects are reported faster than they are resolved. The backlog grows until it becomes overwhelming and people stop reporting because they believe nothing will be fixed. Likelihood: high in a resource-constrained institution. Severity: high. Detection: the backlog trend metric. If the backlog grows for three consecutive quarters, intervention is required.

  • Triage neglect. Defects are reported but sit in the Reported state without triage. They accumulate without being evaluated or assigned. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: moderate. Detection: count of defects in Reported state older than 7 days.

  • Priority inflation. Everything is marked Immediate or High. The priority system becomes meaningless. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: moderate. Detection: if more than 20% of defects are classified as Immediate or High, the classification standards may be too loose, or the institution has a genuine quality crisis.

  • Defect avoidance. People stop reporting defects because the process is too burdensome, because they do not believe defects will be fixed, or because they fear the cultural implications of reporting. Likelihood: moderate. Severity: critical (a system that stops receiving reports appears to have no defects, which is never true). Detection: if the defect inflow rate drops to near zero without a corresponding improvement in quality scores, reporting avoidance is likely.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If the backlog is growing unsustainably: Declare a defect triage day. Review the entire backlog. Reclassify severity and priority based on current institutional conditions (a defect that was High priority six months ago may now be Low). Close any defects that have been resolved by other changes. Defer any defects that genuinely cannot be addressed with current resources, but set realistic re-evaluation dates. After triage, if the remaining actionable backlog is still unsustainable, raise the issue to governance as a resource allocation concern.

  2. If triage is being neglected: Assign a specific person as the defect triage owner on a rotating schedule. Triage is not optional work that gets done "when there is time." It is a scheduled duty. If the institution cannot sustain regular triage, the reporting process may be too heavy and should be simplified.

  3. If priorities are inflated: Recalibrate by reviewing the priority definitions against actual institutional impact. If a defect classified as Immediate has been open for two weeks without causing observable harm, its priority was incorrect. Reclassify it. Use the corrected examples to recalibrate future classification.

  4. If people have stopped reporting: Investigate why. Lower the barrier to reporting -- a defect report can be as simple as a one-line description and the affected artifact's ID. Demonstrate that reports lead to action by publicly resolving a batch of recently reported defects. Address any cultural factors that discourage reporting.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-3: The defect registry will be small. Use this period to establish the habit of reporting, triaging, and resolving defects. The process matters more than the volume. Even one defect per month, tracked through its full lifecycle, builds the institutional muscle.

  • Years 3-10: The registry accumulates enough data for trend analysis. Begin using defect metrics to identify systemic quality issues. Are most defects in one domain? In one quality dimension? From one type of change? This data feeds D19-011 (Continuous Improvement).

  • Years 10-25: The registry is a historical record. It shows how the institution's quality has evolved over time. Defect patterns from the early years should inform training for new participants. Old defects should not be purged -- they are institutional memory.

  • Years 25-50+: The defect tracking system must have survived at least one technology migration by this point. Ensure that the registry's format remains readable and that the lifecycle process remains functional. The defect ID namespace (DEF-YYYY-NNN) should not have been exhausted, but if it has, extend it rather than reusing old IDs.

Commentary Section

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: The biggest risk to a defect tracking system in a small institution is not that it will be too complex. It is that it will be too earnest. Every quality imperfection will be logged, triaged, prioritized, assigned, and tracked with the same solemnity. The result is a backlog of 200 cosmetic defects, each dutifully tracked, none resolved, each reminding the operator that they are falling behind. The antidote is honest triage. Most cosmetic defects should be resolved immediately (fix the typo when you see it) or not tracked at all. Reserve the formal tracking system for defects that require deliberate attention -- things you cannot fix in the moment, things that require coordination, things that might recur. Let the formal system serve its purpose: making the important visible and ensuring it gets done.
