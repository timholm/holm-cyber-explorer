D13-013 — Post-Migration Verification and Stabilization

Document ID
D13-013
Title
Post-Migration Verification and Stabilization
Domain
13 -- Evolution & Adaptation
Version
1.0.0
Date
2026-02-17
Status
Ratified
Depends On
ETH-001, CON-001, D13-001, D13-002, D13-007, D13-012, D6-002, D12-001
Depended Upon By
D13-004 (sunset depends on verification completion). Referenced by all articles involving technology transitions.

Purpose

This article defines how to verify that a technology migration has succeeded and how to stabilize the new system before the old system is decommissioned. Verification is the final gate in the Evolution Decision Framework (D13-001 Section 4.1, Stage 4). A migration is not complete when the new system is deployed. It is not complete when the new system appears to work. It is complete only when the new system has been verified against specific, documented criteria and has operated stably for a defined period under real conditions.

D13-002 provides the universal migration framework including Phase 6 (post-migration verification) and Phase 7 (stabilization and decommissioning). This article expands those phases into detailed procedures covering: what to verify, how to verify it, what constitutes a passing result, what constitutes a failing result, how long to stabilize, what to monitor during stabilization, and when and how to make the final decision to decommission the old system or roll back to it.

Post-migration verification is the institution's defense against the most insidious class of migration failure: the migration that appears to succeed but has introduced subtle problems that manifest only under sustained operation. A storage migration that passes initial checksums but introduces intermittent read errors under load. An OS upgrade that works perfectly except when a specific quarterly process runs. A data format conversion that preserved 99.9% of content but silently truncated the remaining 0.1%. These are the failures that verification and stabilization exist to catch.

Scope

In scope: - The verification framework: categories of verification, methods, and success criteria. - The stabilization period: duration, monitoring activities, and decision points. - Rollback decision criteria: when to abandon the new system and return to the old. - The verification report: format, content, and storage. - Burn-in testing: extended operational testing designed to surface latent defects. - Regression testing: verifying that capabilities present in the old system are present in the new. - The final decommissioning decision: criteria and procedure.

Out of scope: - The migration procedure itself (D13-002). - Pre-migration testing (D13-002 Phase 3). - Legacy coexistence management during stabilization (D13-012). - Old system decommissioning procedures (D13-004).

Background

3.1 The Latent Defect Problem

The most dangerous migration failures are the ones that pass initial verification. They appear successful because the verification tested the most common scenarios. But real-world operation is not a controlled test. It includes edge cases, unusual workloads, time-dependent processes, and interactions between components that cannot all be anticipated. Latent defects hide in these untested spaces and emerge only when the specific conditions that trigger them occur. The stabilization period exists to give latent defects time to manifest before the fallback system is decommissioned.

3.2 The Verification Asymmetry

Verification can prove the presence of defects but cannot prove their absence. Passing all verification tests means no defects were found by these tests -- not that no defects exist. This asymmetry demands humility: no matter how thorough the verification, the stabilization period must be honored because it provides an additional, fundamentally different form of testing -- testing by real operation over time.

3.3 The Cost of Late Rollback

Rolling back a migration during the stabilization period is relatively straightforward: the old system is still available and intact. Rolling back after the old system has been decommissioned is a disaster recovery event. The difference in cost and risk is enormous. This asymmetry means that the decision to decommission the old system must be made with great care, and the verification and stabilization process must be thorough enough to justify that decision.

System Model

4.1 The Verification Framework

Post-migration verification covers five categories. All five must pass before the migration is considered verified. A failure in any category triggers the issue resolution process (Section 4.4).

Category 1: Data Integrity Verification. All data that was migrated or converted is verified against the pre-migration baseline.

  • Checksum comparison: every migrated file's checksum is compared against the pre-migration manifest. Any mismatch is a finding.
  • Record count verification: total data records in the new system match the pre-migration count.
  • Content spot-check: a random sample of data (minimum 2% or 50 items, whichever is greater) is manually inspected for correctness.
  • Metadata preservation: metadata (timestamps, permissions, ownership, tags) is verified on a sample basis.

Category 2: Service Functionality Verification. Every service that was affected by the migration is tested for correct operation.

  • Service startup: each affected service starts correctly.
  • Core function test: each service's primary function is exercised and verified to produce correct output.
  • Configuration verification: service configurations match the intended post-migration state.
  • Inter-service communication: services that communicate with each other are tested end-to-end.

Category 3: Performance Verification. The new system performs comparably to the old system.

  • Throughput comparison: key operations are timed and compared against pre-migration baselines. Degradation exceeding 20% is a finding requiring investigation.
  • Resource utilization: CPU, memory, storage, and power consumption are measured and compared against baselines.
  • Latency measurement: response times for interactive operations are measured.

Category 4: Security Verification. The migration has not compromised the institution's security posture.

  • Air gap integrity: network interfaces are verified to be absent or disabled.
  • Encryption verification: encrypted data remains encrypted and decryptable with current keys.
  • Access control verification: file permissions and user access levels are correct.
  • Security configuration audit: security-relevant configurations (firewall rules, service exposure, logging) match the intended state.

Category 5: Operational Verification. The institution's daily operations can be performed on the new system.

  • Daily operations checklist: execute the standard daily operations checklist (OPS-001) and verify that all items can be completed.
  • Backup verification: create a backup on the new system and verify it can be restored.
  • DR readiness: verify that disaster recovery procedures (D12-001) are functional on the new system.
  • Documentation currency: verify that operational procedures accurately describe the post-migration system.

4.2 The Stabilization Period

After verification passes, the system enters the stabilization period. During stabilization, the new system is in production use but the old system remains available for rollback.

Migration TypeMinimum Stabilization Period
OS minor update (Tier 3)7 days
Hardware drop-in replacement (Tier 3)7 days
OS major upgrade (Tier 2)30 days
Hardware platform migration (Tier 2)90 days
Data format conversion, Tier 1 data90 days
Data format conversion, Tier 2/3 data30 days
Distribution change (Tier 2)90 days

During stabilization, the operator monitors for:

  • Intermittent errors that did not appear during verification testing.
  • Performance degradation that develops over time (e.g., memory leaks, storage fragmentation).
  • Edge case failures that only occur during specific operational scenarios.
  • Compatibility issues with processes that run less frequently than the verification period (weekly, monthly, quarterly).

A stabilization journal is maintained: a daily log of observations about the new system's behavior. Positive observations ("system running normally") and negative observations ("encountered error X during operation Y") are both recorded. The journal is the primary input to the decommissioning decision.

4.3 The Regression Test Suite

For each critical operational scenario, a regression test is documented and executed during verification and periodically during stabilization. The regression test suite is built from the institution's operational procedures and includes:

  • Every operation in the daily checklist.
  • Every operation in the weekly and monthly checklists.
  • At least one backup-and-restore cycle.
  • At least one DR scenario (simplified).
  • Every data access pattern used in normal operations.

The regression test suite is a permanent artifact that is updated with each migration to capture new operational scenarios. Over time, it becomes a comprehensive test of institutional functionality.

4.4 Issue Resolution During Verification and Stabilization

When a verification failure or stabilization issue is discovered:

  1. Classify the issue. Critical (data integrity, security, or service availability affected), major (performance or functionality degraded), or minor (cosmetic or inconvenience).
  2. Assess rollback necessity. Critical issues trigger an immediate rollback assessment. Major issues trigger investigation with a 72-hour resolution deadline. Minor issues are documented and may be resolved in place.
  3. Resolve or roll back. If the issue can be resolved without affecting data integrity or requiring changes to the new system's core configuration, resolve it. If resolution would require significant changes, assess whether the migration should be rolled back and reattempted after the issue is addressed.
  4. Document the issue. Record the issue, its classification, its resolution (or the rollback decision), and any lessons learned in the verification report and the stabilization journal.

4.5 The Decommissioning Decision

At the end of the stabilization period, the operator makes the decommissioning decision: should the old system be retired, or should the stabilization period be extended?

The decision is based on three inputs:

  • Verification report: Did all five verification categories pass?
  • Stabilization journal: Were any significant issues discovered during stabilization? If yes, have they been resolved?
  • Regression test results: Did all regression tests pass during stabilization?

If all three inputs are positive, the old system is approved for decommissioning per D13-004. If any input is negative, the stabilization period is extended by the minimum duration for the migration type, and the unresolved issues are addressed. If issues persist after one extension, the decision escalates to Tier 2 review: continue the migration (accepting known issues), roll back, or seek an alternative approach.

The decommissioning decision is documented in the migration plan (D13-002 Section 4.5) and recorded in the decision log (GOV-001).

Rules & Constraints

  • R-PMV-01: All five verification categories (Section 4.1) must be completed and documented before a migration is considered verified. A failure in any category must be resolved before proceeding to stabilization.
  • R-PMV-02: The minimum stabilization periods in Section 4.2 must be honored. Shortening the stabilization period requires Tier 2 approval with documented justification.
  • R-PMV-03: A stabilization journal must be maintained for the duration of the stabilization period. Daily entries are required, even if the entry is "no issues observed."
  • R-PMV-04: The regression test suite (Section 4.3) must be executed in full at least twice during the stabilization period: once at the beginning and once at the end.
  • R-PMV-05: Critical issues discovered during verification or stabilization trigger an immediate rollback assessment. The assessment must be documented regardless of whether rollback is executed.
  • R-PMV-06: The decommissioning decision (Section 4.5) must be based on documented evidence from the verification report, stabilization journal, and regression test results. Decommissioning without this evidence is prohibited.
  • R-PMV-07: The verification report is a Tier 2 data artifact preserved as part of the migration record. It must include: verification date, each category's results, any issues found, and the final verification determination.
  • R-PMV-08: The old system must remain intact and available for rollback until the decommissioning decision is made. Per D13-002 R-D13-02-05, premature decommissioning requires Tier 2 approval.

Failure Modes

  • Verification theater. Verification is performed perfunctorily -- checklists are marked as complete without thorough testing. Issues that would have been caught by genuine verification are missed. Mitigation: R-PMV-01 requires documented results for each category. The verification report must include specific evidence (checksums, test outputs, measurements), not just assertions.
  • Stabilization impatience. The operator truncates the stabilization period because the system "seems fine" and the old system is consuming resources. Latent defects that would have manifested during the full stabilization period are missed. Mitigation: R-PMV-02 mandates minimum periods. R-PMV-08 prevents premature decommissioning.
  • Journal neglect. The stabilization journal is not maintained. When the decommissioning decision arrives, there is no record of how the system behaved during stabilization. The decision is made on impression rather than evidence. Mitigation: R-PMV-03 mandates daily entries. The journal is an input to the decommissioning decision.
  • Regression test staleness. The regression test suite was written for a previous migration and does not cover new operational scenarios. Tests pass but do not exercise the full operational surface. Mitigation: the regression test suite is updated with each migration to capture new scenarios. It is reviewed as part of migration planning.
  • Rollback delay. A critical issue is discovered but the rollback decision is delayed by hope that the issue can be resolved. Meanwhile, the situation worsens. Mitigation: R-PMV-05 mandates an immediate rollback assessment for critical issues. The assessment may conclude that rollback is not needed, but it must be performed promptly.

Recovery Procedures

  1. If a verification failure is discovered after stabilization began: Halt stabilization. Determine whether the failure affects data integrity. If yes, assess rollback immediately. If no, resolve the failure and restart the affected verification category. Do not count the pre-failure stabilization time -- the stabilization clock resets when verification is re-completed.
  2. If a latent defect manifests during stabilization: Classify per Section 4.4. If the defect is critical, execute the rollback procedure (D13-002 Phase 4). If major, extend stabilization and monitor. If minor, resolve in place and document.
  3. If the decommissioning decision was made prematurely and problems emerge: If the old system has not yet been fully decommissioned, reactivate it. If the old system has been decommissioned, this becomes a disaster recovery event -- restore the old system from backup (D6-002) or address the problems on the new system using available resources. Document the premature decision and its consequences in the lessons learned framework (D20-004).
  4. If the verification report is missing or incomplete: Reconstruct what can be reconstructed from the stabilization journal, operational logs, and current system state. Mark the reconstruction as "RETROACTIVE -- original verification incomplete." Use the gap to reinforce the verification discipline for future migrations.
  5. If the stabilization journal was not maintained: The decommissioning decision cannot be made on the basis of documented evidence as required by R-PMV-06. Extend the stabilization period by the minimum duration and begin maintaining the journal immediately. The decision requires a full stabilization period with journal evidence.

Evolution Path

  • Years 0-5: The first migrations produce the first verification reports and stabilization journals. The regression test suite is created. The stabilization periods may prove too long or too short -- experience will calibrate. The verification categories may need refinement as the operator discovers what tests are most discriminating.
  • Years 5-15: The verification framework has been refined by multiple migrations. The regression test suite has grown to cover a comprehensive range of operational scenarios. The stabilization journal archive provides historical data on how long latent defects typically take to manifest, informing stabilization period decisions.
  • Years 15-30: Post-migration verification is routine. The operator has a refined sense of what to look for, which verification tests are most valuable, and what stabilization period is actually needed. The accumulated verification reports tell the story of the institution's migration reliability -- how many migrations succeeded cleanly, how many required issue resolution, and how many were rolled back.
  • Years 30-50+: The verification and stabilization procedures have been tested by dozens of migrations across multiple technology generations. The regression test suite is comprehensive. The institutional knowledge of how to verify a migration is deep and documented. New operators can study past verification reports and stabilization journals to understand the institution's migration history and learn from it.

Founder Commentary

This section is reserved for dated entries by current and future operators.

2026-02-17 -- Founding Entry: Verification and stabilization are the parts of migration that are most tempting to rush. The hard work of the migration is done. The new system is up. It seems to work. The old system is taking up space and power. Everything in me wants to declare victory and move on. This article is my commitment to resist that temptation. The stabilization journal requirement is particularly important to me because it forces daily attention. Even writing "no issues observed" takes a few minutes of deliberate observation that might catch something I would otherwise miss. And the accumulated journal entries create an evidence base for the decommissioning decision that is much more reliable than my impression of how things went. I know from experience that my impression of a migration's success is colored by the relief of having completed it. The journal provides a corrective to that bias.
